## Overview

Sunlight appears white, but it contains all colors mixed together. A spectrometer separates white light into its spectrum: red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, violet.

More importantly, hot elements emit light at specific wavelengths (discrete lines), forming a unique fingerprint. By analyzing which lines are present, you can identify which elements are in a star, planet, or gas flame.

A spectrometer reveals:
- What elements are in a distant star
- Whether a star is moving toward or away (redshift/blueshift)
- Composition of nebulae and galaxies
- Whether a chemical sample contains a particular element

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of light, waves, and wavelengths (Vol 29, Physics)
- Understanding of diffraction and interference (Vol 29, Ch 3 or equivalent)
- Ability to mount and align optical components

## Materials & Tools

### For a Basic Prism Spectrometer

- **Prism:** Optical prism (or home-made from glass) to refract light into a spectrum
- **Collimator:** A lens that creates parallel light from a source
- **Viewing telescope or eyepiece:** To observe the spectrum
- **Light source:** Sun, lamp, or element flame to be analyzed
- **Slit:** A narrow opening to define the light beam
- **Wavelength reference:** Known elements (hydrogen, helium) for calibration

### For a Diffraction Grating Spectrometer (Simpler)

- **Diffraction grating:** Multiple parallel slits or reflective lines (can be made from a CD or created on glass)
- **Light source**
- **Observation screen or eyepiece**

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Prism Spectrometer Design

A prism bends light, with different wavelengths bending at slightly different angles.

**Step 1: Obtain or make a prism.**

A prism is a piece of optical glass with specific angles, typically a 45-90-45 degree triangle.

**Making a prism (if none is available):**
1. Grind and polish a piece of optical glass to the right shape and angles
2. The prism must have smooth, polished surfaces (Vol 32, Ch 1)
3. Typical prism material: crown glass (less dispersion) or flint glass (more dispersion)
4. Flint glass separates colors more, making faint lines easier to see

**Step 2: Build the collimator.**

The collimator creates parallel light from the light source.

**Simple collimator:**
1. A narrow slit (roughly 0.5 mm wide) is placed at the focal point of a lens
2. The lens (focal length 50–100 mm) creates parallel light from the slit
3. This parallel light enters the prism
4. The slit width determines the spectral resolution (narrower slit = sharper lines, but dimmer image)

**Assembly:**
1. Mount the slit in front of the lens
2. The light source should be just in front of the slit
3. Adjust the distance between slit and lens so light emerging from the lens is parallel (test by observing if the light beam stays constant width at a distance)

**Step 3: Mount the prism.**

The prism must be positioned and angled correctly.

**Alignment:**
1. The prism should be tilted so that light from the collimator enters one face at about 45 degrees
2. Light is refracted twice (entering and exiting the prism), spreading into a spectrum
3. The prism should be rotated so the spectrum is visible (roughly 45 degrees to the incident light)

**Mounting:**
1. Use a prism holder (brass or wood block with angled surfaces) to hold the prism
2. Include fine adjustment screws to rotate the prism slightly for optimal angle
3. The prism should not be touched by bare hands (oils on skin damage the optical surface)

**Step 4: Create the viewing stage.**

The spectrum from the prism is observed through a telescope or magnifying lens.

**Option A: Viewing screen**
1. Place a screen (white paper or translucent glass) beyond the prism to catch the spectrum
2. The spectrum appears as colored bands (continuous spectrum) or colored lines (emission spectrum)
3. Simple but no magnification; hard to see faint lines

**Option B: Observation telescope**
1. A small telescope positioned to view the spectrum produced by the prism
2. This magnifies the spectrum, making faint lines visible
3. The telescope can be adjusted for focus (just as in Vol 32, Ch 3)

**Step 5: Calibrate against known elements.**

The position of spectral lines depends on the prism angle and the wavelength.

**Calibration procedure:**
1. Observe the spectrum of a known element (hydrogen lamp, or a flame with a salt such as sodium, which glows yellow)
2. Note the positions of the known spectral lines
3. Create a reference chart (wavelength vs. position on your spectrum)
4. Future spectra can be analyzed by matching lines to the reference chart

### Part 2: Diffraction Grating Spectrometer (Simpler Alternative)

A diffraction grating uses the wave nature of light to separate wavelengths.

**Theory (simplified):**
When light passes through many parallel, closely-spaced slits, different wavelengths are diffracted (bent) at different angles. This separates the spectrum without a prism.

**Step 1: Create or obtain a diffraction grating.**

**Method A: Using a CD**
1. A CD has microscopic grooves (tracks) that act as a diffraction grating
2. The surface reflects light in different wavelengths at different angles
3. Inexpensive and readily available (old CDs work fine)

**Method B: Creating a grating on glass**
1. Coat glass with a thin layer of resist (a light-sensitive material)
2. Expose the resist to narrow parallel lines of light (using a laser or pattern)
3. Develop the resist (wash away the exposed areas)
4. Etch the glass beneath the resist with hydrofluoric acid (creates grooves)
5. The resulting grooves act as a diffraction grating

**Grating spacing requirements:**
- Grating spacing should be roughly equal to the wavelength of visible light (around 0.5 micrometers)
- A CD's track spacing is about 1.6 micrometers, which works well for visible light

**Step 2: Set up the observation setup.**

**Simple setup:**
1. A light source (sun, lamp, or flame) shines through a narrow slit
2. The light reflects from or passes through the diffraction grating
3. The light spreads into a spectrum visible from different angles

**Viewing options:**
- **Reflection grating:** View the spectrum reflected from the grating surface (good for CD-based gratings)
- **Transmission grating:** View the spectrum passing through the grating (if grating is transparent)

**Step 3: Observe and analyze the spectrum.**

1. Point the grating toward the light source
2. Move your eye to different angles relative to the grating
3. At certain angles, you see the spectrum (different wavelengths at different angles)
4. The relationship is: sin(angle) ≈ (order × wavelength) / (grating spacing)

**Higher orders:**
- At different angles, you see multiple copies of the spectrum (first order, second order, etc.)
- First order is brightest; higher orders are progressively dimmer

### Part 3: Analyzing Spectral Lines

**Step 1: Understand emission vs. absorption spectra.**

**Emission spectrum (bright lines on dark background):**
- Hot, glowing elements emit light
- Each element emits at specific wavelengths
- Appears as bright colored lines on a dark background

**Absorption spectrum (dark lines on bright background):**
- Cooler elements absorb light from a bright background source
- Each element absorbs at the same wavelengths it emits
- Appears as dark lines in a continuous rainbow spectrum

**Example:** Hydrogen emits red, cyan, blue, and violet lines (at specific wavelengths). If sunlight (which contains all wavelengths) passes through hydrogen gas, these same wavelengths are absorbed, creating dark lines in the solar spectrum.

**Step 2: Measure wavelengths from line positions.**

If you have calibrated your spectrometer (Step 5 above), you can measure unknown spectra.

**Procedure:**
1. Observe an unknown light source with your spectrometer
2. Note the positions of spectral lines (how far from the center, measured in degrees or distance)
3. Use your calibration curve (from known elements) to convert line position to wavelength
4. Look up the wavelengths in a reference table to identify the element

**Example wavelengths (key emission lines):**
- Hydrogen: Red (656 nm), Cyan (486 nm), Blue (434 nm), Violet (410 nm)
- Helium: Red (668 nm), Orange (588 nm), Green (501 nm), Blue (471 nm)
- Sodium: Bright yellow (589 nm doublet)
- Calcium: Red (393 nm), Violet (396 nm)

**Step 3: Analyze redshift for velocity measurement.**

If a star is moving, its spectral lines shift slightly (Doppler effect).

**Redshift (star moving away):**
- Lines shift toward the red end of the spectrum (longer wavelengths)
- The amount of shift indicates how fast the star is moving away

**Blueshift (star moving toward):**
- Lines shift toward the blue end of the spectrum (shorter wavelengths)
- The amount of shift indicates how fast the star is moving toward

**Measurement:**
1. Observe the spectrum of a moving star
2. Measure the shift in a known spectral line (difference from the expected wavelength)
3. Use the formula: velocity ≈ (shift / original wavelength) × speed of light
4. This gives the star's velocity toward or away from Earth

### Part 4: Applications of Spectroscopy

**Identifying stellar composition:**
- Observe the spectrum of a star (use a spectroscope attached to a telescope)
- Identify the bright emission lines or dark absorption lines
- Determine which elements are present in the star's atmosphere

**Measuring stellar temperature:**
- Hot stars emit more blue and ultraviolet light
- Cool stars emit more red and infrared light
- The overall shape of the spectrum indicates temperature

**Measuring stellar rotation:**
- A spinning star's spectrum lines are broadened (Doppler effect from rotation)
- The width of the lines indicates rotation speed

**Detecting exoplanets:**
- If a star is orbited by a planet, the star's spectral lines shift slightly due to the planet's gravitational pull
- Measuring these shifts reveals the presence and mass of planets

**Identifying elements in flames:**
- Heat a chemical sample in a flame
- Observe the emission spectrum
- Each element produces a characteristic set of lines
- This is how chemists identify unknown samples

## Verification

A functional spectrometer shows:

- **Clear spectrum:** Spectral lines (or continuous spectrum) are visible and well-defined
- **Proper dispersion:** Colors are spread across the full viewing area
- **Bright lines:** Emission lines are bright enough to see easily
- **Stable image:** The spectrum does not drift or vibrate

A poorly functioning spectrometer shows:

- **No spectrum visible:** No lines or continuous spectrum appears
- **Dim spectrum:** Lines are barely visible
- **Blurred lines:** Spectral lines are fuzzy, not sharp
- **Poor separation:** All colors blend together instead of separating
- **Vignetting:** The spectrum is bright in the center but dark at the edges

## Common Mistakes

- **Slit too wide:** A wide slit produces a bright spectrum but fuzzy lines (poor resolution). Keep the slit narrow, even if the spectrum is slightly dimmer.
- **Poor collimation:** If light from the slit is not parallel when entering the prism, the spectrum will be blurry. Check that light emerging from the collimator lens is parallel (test by observing beam width at a distance).
- **Prism angle incorrect:** The angle at which light enters the prism affects both the position and clarity of the spectrum. Adjust the prism angle until the spectrum is centered and sharp.
- **Dirty optics:** Dust on the prism or grating severely degrades the image. Keep optics covered when not in use.
- **Light source too bright:** An overly bright source can saturate the spectrum, washing out faint lines. Use a moderate light source and adjust with neutral-density filters if needed.
- **Misidentifying lines:** Spectral lines are easy to misidentify if not carefully compared to reference data. Always check against known elements before claiming identification of an unknown.
- **Ignoring wavelength reference:** Without calibration against known elements, you cannot determine actual wavelengths. Always include a reference element in your observations.
