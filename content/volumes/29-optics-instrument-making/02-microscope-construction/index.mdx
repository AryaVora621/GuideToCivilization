## Overview

A microscope reveals the invisible: bacteria, parasites, blood cells, pollen. Two designs exist:

1. **Simple microscope (single lens):** High magnification (around 200x) but limited field of view and clarity
2. **Compound microscope (multiple lenses):** Even higher magnification (400x–1000x) with better image quality

Both are buildable with hand tools and properly ground lenses.

## Prerequisites

- Ability to grind optical lenses (Vol 32, Ch 1)
- Basic mechanical skills (drilling, threading, assembly)
- Access to metal (brass, steel) or wood for the body

## Materials & Tools

### For a Simple Microscope

- **Single lens:** Ground biconvex lens, focal length 10–20 mm (strong magnification)
- **Lens holder:** Brass tube or wooden frame to hold the lens
- **Light source:** Candle, oil lamp, or sunlight reflected by a mirror
- **Mirror (optional):** Small mirror to direct light through the specimen
- **Stage:** Flat surface (brass plate, glass slide) to hold the specimen

### For a Compound Microscope

- **Objective lens:** Strong lens (short focal length, 5–10 mm), ground to high precision
- **Eyepiece lens:** Weaker lens (longer focal length, 25–50 mm)
- **Tube:** Brass or metal tube to hold both lenses separated by the correct distance
- **Stage:** Platform to hold the glass slide with the specimen
- **Focusing mechanism:** Screw to move the objective up and down (for sharp focus)
- **Light source and mirror:** As above

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Simple Microscope (Leeuwenhoek Design)

A simple microscope is just a strong lens held at the right distance from the specimen.

**Step 1: Prepare the lens.**

- The lens must be a strong biconvex lens (both surfaces curved outward)
- Focal length: 10–15 mm (the distance from the lens where light rays converge)
- Such lenses bend light powerfully, creating high magnification (around 200x)

**Step 2: Build a lens holder.**

**Option A: Brass tube design**
1. Obtain or make a brass tube, roughly 3 cm long and 1 cm diameter
2. Drill a hole through the tube (sized to the lens diameter)
3. Create a retaining ring (a brass collar that screws down to hold the lens snugly)
4. The lens should be recessed slightly into the tube (about 2 mm from the front)

**Option B: Wooden frame design**
1. Carve or whittle a small wooden block with a hole sized to the lens
2. Glue the lens into the hole with a strong adhesive (hide glue or plant-based resin)
3. Allow 24 hours to cure fully
4. The lens should be flush or slightly recessed

**Step 3: Create a specimen mount.**

- A glass slide (a thin piece of glass, roughly 5 cm × 2 cm) holds the specimen
- The specimen is placed on the slide, usually with a tiny drop of water or oil
- Cover the specimen with a thin glass coverslip (to protect it and reduce water evaporation)

**Step 4: Assemble the microscope.**

1. Hold the lens holder about 2–3 cm in front of the eye
2. Position the specimen slide just in front of the lens, starting about 5 mm away
3. Move the specimen closer or farther until it comes into sharp focus
4. The closer the specimen, the higher the magnification (but focus becomes difficult)

**Step 5: Direct light through the specimen.**

- Position a light source (candle or sunlight) behind and slightly to the side of the specimen
- Use a small mirror to reflect light upward through the specimen toward the lens
- Adjust the light direction until the specimen is well-illuminated

**Step 6: Observe.**

- Look through the lens at a distance of about 2–3 cm (varies with lens strength)
- The specimen will appear magnified and inverted (upside down and reversed)
- Small movements of the specimen slide create large movements in the view (difficult to control)

### Part 2: Compound Microscope (Multiple Lenses)

A compound microscope uses two lenses: the objective (near the specimen) and the eyepiece (near the eye).

**Step 1: Calculate lens spacing.**

The distance between the lenses determines the magnification and the working distance.

**Simple formula (approximate):**
- Magnification ≈ (lens separation) / (focal length of objective)
- Example: Objective focal length 10 mm, eyepiece focal length 30 mm, lens separation 160 mm
- Magnification ≈ 160 / 10 = 16× objective magnification
- Total magnification ≈ 16× × eyepiece power ≈ 16× × 5 = 80× total

**More precise formula (using lens equation):**
For a compound microscope:
- Magnification = (separation - focal length of objective - focal length of eyepiece) / (focal length of objective × focal length of eyepiece)

Start with a spacing of around 140–160 mm and adjust by trial.

**Step 2: Build the tube assembly.**

1. Obtain a metal or wood tube (roughly 5–8 cm diameter, 20–30 cm long)
2. At one end, thread or create a fitting for the objective lens holder
3. At the other end, thread or create a fitting for the eyepiece lens holder
4. Both lens holders should align the lenses along the tube's axis
5. The stage (specimen holder) should be between the objective and the eyepiece, closer to the objective

**Step 3: Create a focusing mechanism.**

The objective lens must be moved up and down to bring the specimen into focus.

**Simple screw mechanism:**
1. Thread a brass rod with fine threads (roughly 1 mm pitch)
2. Attach the objective lens holder to the rod
3. Use a threaded brass block as a nut, fixed to the tube
4. Turning the rod moves the objective lens up or down
5. One full rotation might move the lens 1 mm; allows fine focusing

**Alternative: rack and pinion (more sophisticated)**
1. A gear (pinion) meshes with a toothed rail (rack)
2. Turning the gear moves the rack (and the objective) up or down
3. More stable and smooth than threads, but requires machine tools to create

**Step 4: Prepare the stage.**

- A flat brass or glass plate below the objective lens holds the specimen
- The stage should have a hole (roughly 1 cm diameter) to allow light to pass through
- The specimen is placed on a glass slide on the stage

**Step 5: Direct light through the specimen.**

**Critical for clarity:** The light must pass through the specimen and through the objective lens.

- Place a light source (candle, oil lamp, sunlight) below the stage
- Alternatively, use a mirror to reflect light upward through the hole in the stage
- Position the light so it passes directly through the specimen and the objective lens

**Advanced: Use a condenser lens**
- A weak lens below the stage can focus light onto the specimen, improving brightness
- The condenser should be positioned about 1–2 cm below the slide

**Step 6: Focus and observe.**

1. Use the coarse focusing knob to bring the objective lens very close to the slide (almost touching)
2. Look through the eyepiece
3. Slowly turn the fine focus knob, moving the objective lens away from the slide
4. When the specimen comes into focus, stop
5. Adjust the light and fine focus for maximum clarity

### Part 3: Achieving High Magnification and Clarity

**Step 1: Understand resolution vs. magnification.**

- **Magnification:** How large the image appears (in multiples of the original size)
- **Resolution:** The ability to see fine details (how close together two points can be and still appear separate)

A simple lens can magnify 200×, but resolution beyond about 100× is limited by the physics of light (diffraction limit). To see greater detail, you need:
1. Better lenses (minimal aberrations, higher quality grinding)
2. More lenses in the optical path (compound microscopes)
3. Shorter wavelengths of light (blue light has shorter wavelength than red; it resolves finer detail)

**Step 2: Reduce optical aberrations.**

Aberrations are imperfections in the lens that blur the image or distort colors.

**Common aberrations and how to reduce them:**
- **Spherical aberration:** The lens is not perfectly spherical; edges focus at different points than the center. Solution: Grind the lens to higher precision (Part 1, Vol 32, Ch 1).
- **Chromatic aberration:** Different colors focus at different distances. Solution: Use multiple lenses (compound design) with different glasses; the second lens corrects the error.
- **Coma and astigmatism:** Off-axis image distortion. Solution: Use smaller aperture (limit the light beam to the center of the lens) or grind the lens asymmetrically (advanced).

**Step 3: Optimize illumination.**

Proper lighting is as important as good lenses.

- **Brightness:** The specimen must be well-lit. Dim light makes it hard to see detail.
- **Contrast:** If the specimen is nearly transparent, the image is hard to see. Use stains (many microorganisms absorb dyes) or let the specimen dry on the slide.
- **Angle of light:** Light slightly off-axis (not perfectly straight through) can increase contrast and reveal surface texture.

### Part 4: Maintaining and Using the Microscope

**Step 1: Care of lenses.**

- Keep lenses clean. Dust or oil on the lens severely degrades the image.
- Clean with soft, lint-free cloth (old linen works well).
- Use only gentle, circular motions; do not press hard.
- For stubborn spots, breathe gently on the lens (creating moisture) and wipe.

**Step 2: Care of the mechanical parts.**

- Keep the focusing mechanism (screws, gears) clean and lightly oiled
- Dust tends to bind moving parts; wipe clean weekly
- Check that the lens holders are tight; vibration blurs the image

**Step 3: Preparing specimens.**

**For bacteria and single-celled organisms:**
- Suspend the organism in water on a glass slide
- Cover with a coverslip
- Observe within a few minutes (water evaporates quickly)

**For tissue samples:**
- Slice thin (50–100 micrometers; almost transparent)
- Place on a slide with a drop of water or oil
- Cover with a coverslip

**For staining:**
- Many biological stains improve contrast
- Simple example: Gram's stain (for bacteria)
- Another example: Methylene blue (a dye that colors some structures)
- Staining requires chemicals; in a post-collapse setting, use natural dyes (madder root for red, indigo for blue, etc.)

## Verification

A functional microscope shows:

- **Clear image:** The specimen appears sharp, not blurry
- **Magnification:** Structures are visibly enlarged; you see detail invisible to the naked eye
- **Adequate brightness:** The field of view is well-lit; you can see the entire specimen
- **Stable image:** The image does not vibrate or drift when you look

A poorly functioning microscope shows:

- **Blurry image:** Even with focusing, the image is fuzzy; indicates optical aberrations or poor lens quality
- **Dim image:** Hard to see detail; indicates inadequate lighting or dirty lenses
- **Unstable image:** Vibrations or focus drift when you look; indicates mechanical looseness or vibration from the light source
- **Chromatic aberration:** Rainbow colors around the specimen; indicates color separation (fixable by adding more lenses)

## Common Mistakes

- **Using weak lenses:** A lens that is not strongly curved will not magnify enough. The objective lens must be ground with a short focal length (under 10 mm for good magnification).
- **Incorrect lens spacing:** Lenses too close or too far apart will not focus properly. Start with 140–160 mm spacing and adjust by trial.
- **Poor light management:** Inadequate or misdirected light makes observation nearly impossible. Spend time optimizing light position and angle.
- **Specimen too thick:** If the specimen is too thick, the objective cannot focus through the entire thickness. Slice tissue thinly.
- **Dirty or scratched lenses:** Dust or scratches on the lens scatter light and severely degrade the image. Keep lenses clean.
- **Loose mechanical parts:** Vibration from loose fittings blurs the image. Check all screws and holders regularly.
- **Wrong eyepiece power:** A weak eyepiece (too low magnification) wastes the objective's power. Match eyepiece strength to objective strength (rule of thumb: objective magnification × eyepiece magnification = total).
