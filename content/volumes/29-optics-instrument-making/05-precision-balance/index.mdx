## Overview

A balance measures mass by comparing an unknown weight to known standard weights. A mechanical balance with a knife-edge pivot can measure to 0.1 gram accuracy or better—sufficient for chemistry, pharmacy, and laboratory work.

Unlike modern electronic scales (which require electricity), a mechanical balance works indefinitely with no power source.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of mechanical equilibrium (balance and levers)
- Access to precision calibration masses (or ability to create them)
- Metalworking skills for the balance beam
- Understanding of accuracy and systematic errors

## Materials & Tools

### For Building a Precision Balance

- **Balance beam:** Steel or iron rod, 30–60 cm long
- **Knife-edge:** Sharp steel blade (the pivot point)
- **Pan holders:** Metal rods to hold sample and reference pans
- **Pans:** Metal or ceramic platforms for samples and weights
- **Calibration masses:** Known standard weights (1 g, 10 g, 100 g, etc.)
- **Damping mechanism:** Oil or air to reduce oscillation
- **Pointer and scale:** To indicate balance position

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Understanding the Principle

A lever (the balance beam) balances when the torques (rotational forces) are equal:

Left torque = Right torque
(Left mass × Left arm length) = (Right mass × Right arm length)

If the arms are equal length, left mass = right mass.

**Precision:** The knife-edge pivot must be extremely sharp (minimal friction) and the beam must be straight (uniform arm length).

### Part 2: Building the Balance Beam

**Step 1: Prepare the beam.**

1. Machine a steel rod perfectly straight (use a lathe if available)
2. Length: 30–60 cm (longer = more sensitivity but harder to balance)
3. Cross-section: Roughly square or rectangular (2–4 cm wide) for strength
4. Surface: Polish smooth to reduce friction

**Step 2: Install the knife-edge pivot.**

The pivot must be:
1. Extremely sharp (acute angle, like a knife blade)
2. Made of hardened steel (resists wear)
3. Perfectly centered on the beam
4. Horizontal and level

**Knife-edge installation:**
1. Drill a hole perpendicular to the beam
2. Machine a hardened steel blade to fit the hole
3. The blade should be removable (for maintenance and storage)
4. Test balance: The beam should balance perfectly when no pans are attached

**Step 3: Attach the pan supports.**

1. Drill holes near each end of the beam (equal distance from the pivot)
2. Install threaded inserts or threaded rods
3. Hang metal rods below the beam
4. Attach pans (metal or ceramic) to the rods

**Symmetry:** The left and right pan supports must be identical (same length, same weight) so the beam is balanced.

### Part 3: Creating Calibration Masses

**Step 1: Understand mass standards.**

Calibration masses must be:
1. **Known precisely:** Manufactured or calibrated to a known standard
2. **Stable:** Don't change over time (no rust, no loss of material)
3. **Corrosion-resistant:** Metal that doesn't tarnish or oxidize
4. **Properly marked:** Engraved or etched with the mass value

**Step 2: Obtain or create standard masses.**

**Option A: Borrow from an existing laboratory** (if available)
- School or university might have calibrated mass sets
- These are accurate and reliable

**Option B: Create from a known reference**
1. Find a known accurate mass (e.g., a commercial balance at a pharmacy)
2. Measure copies of known reference objects
3. Example: If a liter of water weighs 1000 grams, measure water in a container to create a 100-gram reference

**Option C: Iterative calibration**
1. Start with a rough estimate (e.g., a carved wooden block you think is 100 grams)
2. Use a borrowed accurate balance to refine it
3. Repeat until accurate

**Step 3: Create a set of standard masses.**

A useful set includes:
- 1 g (for fine adjustments)
- 5 g
- 10 g (for most measurements)
- 20 g
- 50 g
- 100 g (for larger samples)
- Higher denominations (500 g, 1 kg, etc.) as needed

**Material:** Brass or stainless steel (resists corrosion and tarnishing).

**Storage:** Keep masses in a box, isolated from moisture and dust. Handle only with cloth or gloves (skin oils can affect mass).

### Part 4: Calibration and Testing

**Step 1: Test the balance sensitivity.**

Place a single grain of salt (or a small standard mass like 0.1 g) on one pan. Does the beam noticeably move?

**Sensitivity:** The beam should deflect by at least 1 cm when 0.1 g is added to one side. Higher sensitivity = finer measurements.

**Step 2: Check accuracy against known standards.**

1. Place a known 100-gram mass on the left pan
2. Add standard masses to the right pan until the beam is level
3. The right pan should require exactly 100 grams
4. If not, adjust the balance (slightly move the knife-edge or adjust pan hangers)

**Step 3: Test linearity across the range.**

1. Test with various known masses (10 g, 50 g, 100 g, etc.)
2. Does the balance measure them correctly?
3. Small errors are normal; systematic errors indicate a problem

**Step 4: Identify and correct systematic errors.**

**Unequal beam arms:**
- Measure both arm lengths precisely
- Apply correction factor when measuring (known mass = measured mass × (left arm / right arm))

**Off-center pivot:**
- Adjust the pivot position slightly until balanced
- This is the most critical adjustment

**Unequal pans:**
- If the right pan is slightly heavier, you'll need slightly more mass to balance
- Record the correction and apply it

### Part 5: Using the Balance

**Step 1: Pre-weighing checks.**

1. Place the balance on a level surface (use a spirit level if available)
2. Ensure the balance area is calm (no drafts or vibrations)
3. Allow time for temperature to stabilize (balance is sensitive to temperature changes)

**Step 2: Weigh an unknown sample.**

1. Place the sample on the left pan
2. Add standard masses to the right pan until the beam is level (use the pointer to judge)
3. Record the total mass on the right pan
4. This is the sample mass

**Step 3: Fine adjustments for precision.**

For measurements below 1 gram:
1. Use a sliding mass (a small rod that slides along the beam)
2. The sliding mass can be positioned at any point along the beam
3. Position determines the effective mass (a mass at twice the distance exerts twice the torque)
4. Example: A 1-gram mass at the far end equals a 0.5-gram load near the pivot

**Reading the scale:**
1. The sliding mass moves along a scale marked in tenths of a gram
2. Position on the scale directly shows the fractional gram value
3. Total mass = standard masses + scale reading

### Part 6: Maintenance

**Step 1: Keep the balance clean.**

1. Dust the beam and pans regularly (use a soft brush)
2. Protect from moisture and corrosive environments
3. Store in a dry box when not in use

**Step 2: Check the knife-edge.**

1. Periodically inspect for dullness or damage
2. A dull knife-edge increases friction and reduces accuracy
3. If damaged, carefully sharpen with a fine stone or replace

**Step 3: Protect from environmental factors.**

1. **Temperature:** A 10-degree change can affect measurements by 0.1%. Avoid temperature swings.
2. **Air currents:** Drafts push the pans. Use a wind guard around the balance.
3. **Vibrations:** Machinery vibrations affect measurements. Place the balance on a stable surface away from machinery.

## Verification

A properly functioning balance:

- **Is level:** The beam rests horizontal when unloaded
- **Is balanced:** The pointer returns to center when the same mass is on both pans
- **Is sensitive:** Adding 0.1 gram causes noticeable deflection
- **Is accurate:** Weighing a known mass gives the correct result
- **Is repeatable:** Weighing the same sample multiple times gives the same result (within 0.1 gram)

A poorly functioning balance:

- **Tilts to one side:** Indicates unequal arms or off-center pivot
- **Sticks:** Indicates friction in the knife-edge (dull or bent)
- **Oscillates excessively:** Takes a long time to settle (could indicate damping issues)
- **Gives inconsistent results:** Weighing the same sample gives different results (environmental factors or balance degradation)

## Common Mistakes

- **Touching the beam:** This introduces variability. Let the balance settle before reading.
- **Not accounting for pans:** The pans have weight. Always use the same pans or correct for their weight.
- **Temperature changes:** Measure in a stable temperature environment.
- **Uncalibrated masses:** If your standard weights are not accurate, all measurements are off.
- **Dull knife-edge:** The balance loses sensitivity and accuracy.
- **Overloading:** Exceeding the balance's capacity can bend the beam. Know the maximum load.
