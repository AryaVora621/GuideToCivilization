## Overview

A telescope allows humans to see celestial objects too faint or distant to see with the naked eye. Two main designs exist:

1. **Refractor:** Uses a large lens (objective) to gather light and a smaller lens (eyepiece) to magnify. Invented in the early 1600s.
2. **Reflector:** Uses a curved mirror to gather light and a lens to magnify. Simpler to build to high quality; pioneered by Newton.

A good telescope reveals: craters on the Moon, moons of Jupiter, rings of Saturn, thousands of stars invisible to the naked eye, and galaxies.

## Prerequisites

- Ability to grind optical lenses and mirrors (Vol 32, Ch 1)
- Understanding of basic astronomy (Vol 30)
- Mechanical skills (drilling, threading, assembly)
- Patience (building a telescope takes weeks)

## Materials & Tools

### For a Refractor Telescope

- **Objective lens:** Large diameter (50–100 mm), weak lens (long focal length, 800–1500 mm)
- **Eyepiece lens:** Small diameter (20–30 mm), strong lens (short focal length, 20–50 mm)
- **Tube:** Metal or wood tube long enough to hold both lenses (roughly the focal length of the objective)
- **Lens cells:** Holders for each lens
- **Focusing mechanism:** Screw to move the eyepiece to focus
- **Mount:** Tripod or stand to hold the tube steady
- **Diagonal mirror or prism (optional):** To direct light to the eyepiece at a comfortable angle

### For a Reflector Telescope

- **Primary mirror:** Large curved mirror (50–200 mm diameter), focal length 600–2000 mm
- **Secondary mirror:** Small flat mirror (to redirect light to the eyepiece)
- **Eyepiece lens:** Strong lens, same as above
- **Tube:** Metal or wood tube
- **Mirror cell:** Supports and adjusts the primary mirror
- **Secondary mirror holder:** Allows alignment
- **Focusing mechanism:** To move the eyepiece
- **Mount:** Tripod or stand

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Refractor Telescope (Lens-Based)

A refractor uses light-gathering and focusing achieved by two lenses.

**Step 1: Grind the objective lens.**

The objective is the most critical lens. It must be:
- Large diameter (50–100 mm; larger gathers more light)
- Weak curvature (long focal length, 800–1500 mm)
- Extremely precise grinding (aberrations degrade the image severely)

**Process:**
1. Grind a plano-convex lens (one flat side, one curved side) using the method from Vol 32, Ch 1
2. Focal length can be approximated: focal length ≈ radius of curvature / 2
3. For a focal length of 1000 mm, radius of curvature should be roughly 2000 mm (very gentle curve)
4. Polish extremely carefully to minimize aberrations

**Step 2: Obtain or grind the eyepiece lens.**

The eyepiece is smaller and simpler to make:
- Diameter: 20–30 mm
- Focal length: 20–50 mm (depending on desired magnification)
- A strong biconvex lens (both sides curved)

**Step 3: Build the tube.**

The tube holds both lenses separated by the correct distance.

**Tube design:**
1. The tube length should equal the focal length of the objective (approximately)
2. Example: Objective focal length 1000 mm → tube length 1000 mm
3. Diameter: Large enough to accommodate the objective lens plus mounting hardware (roughly 10 cm diameter)
4. Material: Metal (brass or steel) is better than wood; brass does not rust and can be machined

**Assembly:**
1. Create a cell (holder) for the objective lens at the front (open end) of the tube
2. Create a cell for the eyepiece at the rear (near your eye)
3. Both lenses must be aligned along the tube's axis (coaxial)

**Step 4: Create a focusing mechanism.**

The eyepiece distance from the objective must be adjustable (roughly equal to the focal length of the objective, plus the focal length of the eyepiece).

**Helical focusing:**
1. Thread the eyepiece cell with fine threads (1 mm pitch typical)
2. Thread a corresponding female mount into the tube
3. Turning the threaded eyepiece cell moves it in or out
4. Allow roughly 50 mm of focusing range

**Step 5: Mount the telescope.**

The telescope must be held steady; any vibration blurs the image.

**Simple altazimuth mount (moves up-down and left-right):**
1. Attach the tube to a tripod (wood or metal)
2. The tube can pivot up and down on a horizontal axis (altitude)
3. The tripod can rotate left and right (azimuth)
4. Use slow-motion screws (fine-pitch screws) to adjust position smoothly

**Step 6: Focus on a bright object.**

1. Point the telescope at a bright star or the Moon
2. Adjust the focusing mechanism (turn the eyepiece cell) until the image is sharp
3. The objective lens will form an image roughly at its focal length; the eyepiece magnifies this image

### Part 2: Reflector Telescope (Mirror-Based)

A reflector uses a curved mirror instead of a large lens.

**Step 1: Grind the primary mirror.**

The primary mirror is the most critical component. It must be:
- Large diameter (50–200 mm for good light-gathering)
- Parabolic or spherical shape (spherical is simpler to grind)
- Highly polished (to reflect as much light as possible)

**Mirror grinding (similar to lens grinding, Vol 32, Ch 1):**
1. Grind a glass disk to a curved shape (focal length similar to a refractor objective: 600–2000 mm)
2. For a focal length of 1000 mm and a mirror diameter of 100 mm:
   - Sagitta (depth of curve) = (100 mm)² / (8 × 1000 mm) = 0.125 mm
   - Very shallow curve; requires extremely careful grinding

3. Polish the surface with fine abrasives (polishing powder, rouge, or cerium oxide)
4. The mirror should be reflective (coat with silver or aluminum)

**Silvering the mirror (creating a reflective surface):**
1. Clean the mirror surface thoroughly
2. Apply a thin layer of silver using a chemical method (historical method: silver nitrate + reducer)
   - Modern alternative: Aluminum foil adhered with adhesive (less effective but workable)
   - Or: Use a polished metal surface (copper or aluminum) instead of glass
3. Allow 24 hours to cure/oxidize
4. The reflective surface should be on the back of the glass for simplicity (simpler than coating the front and then grinding)

**Step 2: Create a mirror cell.**

The primary mirror must be supported without distortion.

**Simple mirror cell:**
1. Three or more curved support points hold the mirror from behind
2. The supports should touch the mirror near the edge, not the center
3. Allow tiny adjustments (tilting screws) to align the mirror

**Step 3: Grind or prepare the secondary mirror.**

The secondary mirror is small and flat (or nearly flat).

**Purpose:** To redirect light from the primary mirror to the eyepiece at a convenient angle

**Construction:**
1. A small plane (flat) mirror, 25–50 mm diameter
2. Mount it on a spider (thin metal struts) in front of the primary mirror
3. Angle it at 45 degrees to the tube axis (so light from the primary redirects to the side)
4. Hold it with fine adjustment screws for alignment

**Step 4: Build the tube and mounting.**

Similar to the refractor:
1. Tube length: Roughly equal to the focal length of the primary mirror
2. The eyepiece is mounted at the side (after the secondary mirror redirects light)
3. Include a focusing mechanism (screw to move the eyepiece in or out)

**Step 5: Align the optics (collimation).**

Proper alignment is critical for image quality.

**Collimation procedure:**
1. Look down the tube (remove the eyepiece) during daylight (not at the sun)
2. You should see the primary mirror's reflection and the secondary mirror's reflection
3. The center of the primary mirror should appear centered in the secondary mirror reflection
4. Use adjustment screws on the secondary mirror to achieve this
5. Use adjustment screws on the primary mirror cell to align the primary if needed
6. Repeat until reflections are concentric

### Part 3: Comparing Refractors and Reflectors

| Feature | Refractor | Reflector |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| **Main optic** | Large lens | Large mirror |
| **Ease of grinding** | Difficult (lens) | Simpler (mirror) |
| **Tube length** | Long (focal length) | Shorter (can be folded) |
| **Maintenance** | Easy (sealed) | Harder (mirror can tarnish) |
| **Cost** | High (large lens expensive) | Lower (mirror easier) |
| **Light loss** | Small (reflection from lens surfaces) | Slightly more (secondary mirror blocks light) |
| **Aberrations** | Chromatic aberration common | No chromatic aberration |
| **Field of view** | Good | Acceptable |

### Part 4: Achieving Good Image Quality

**Step 1: Optimize mirror or lens quality.**

- For reflectors: The primary mirror is paramount. Small imperfections (scratches, uneven polish) severely degrade image quality. Polish to extreme smoothness.
- For refractors: The objective lens is the priority. Minimize chromatic aberration by using high-quality glass or multiple lenses.

**Step 2: Reduce optical aberrations.**

**Spherical aberration (in reflectors):**
- Mirrors ground to a spherical shape (simple to make) focus off-axis light slightly differently than on-axis light
- Results in slightly fuzzy images especially at the edge of the field
- Solution: Grind the mirror to a parabolic shape (more complex) or use a smaller mirror diameter (reduces the effect)

**Chromatic aberration (in refractors):**
- Different colors focus at different distances
- Results in rainbow fringing around bright objects
- Solution: Use a doublet objective (two lenses of different glass types) to correct color separation

**Coma and astigmatism:**
- Off-axis image distortion
- Solution: Use good collimation and limit the field of view (do not try to use the entire lens or mirror)

**Step 3: Maintain proper collimation.**

Even slight misalignment degrades image quality.
- Check collimation weekly (point at a star and observe if the image is perfectly centered and symmetrical)
- Adjust as needed

**Step 4: Use the telescope in optimal conditions.**

- Observe when the air is stable (calm nights, not immediately after sunset when the ground is cooling)
- Allow the telescope to reach ambient temperature (thermal expansion causes focus errors)
- Avoid observing through open windows or near heat sources

### Part 5: Using the Telescope for Observation

**Step 1: Locate an object.**

- Begin with the Moon (easy to find and bright)
- Progress to bright planets (Venus, Jupiter, Saturn)
- Then to dim stars and galaxies

**Step 2: Focus carefully.**

- With low magnification, focus on an easy object (Moon or Jupiter)
- Adjust the eyepiece slowly until the image is sharp
- Increase magnification (use different eyepieces if available) only after focus is perfect

**Step 3: Observe and adapt.**

- The human eye adapts to darkness. Allow 10–20 minutes of observation for your eyes to fully adjust.
- Your eye's sensitivity will improve, allowing you to see fainter objects
- Use averted vision (look slightly to the side) to see dim objects better (the edge of the retina is more sensitive)

**Step 4: Record observations.**

- Sketch what you see (especially for planets)
- Note the date, time, and atmospheric conditions
- Compare observations over time to track planetary motion or features

## Verification

A functional telescope shows:

- **Sharp image:** Stars appear as points, not blurry disks (unless magnification is too high)
- **Magnification:** Objects are clearly enlarged
- **Brightness:** Objects are bright enough to see detail
- **Stability:** The image does not vibrate when you release the focus knob
- **Aligned optics:** The image is centered and symmetric
- **Absence of aberrations:** No color fringing (refractor) or off-axis distortion

A poorly functioning telescope shows:

- **Blurry image:** Cannot focus sharply even with fine adjustments
- **Poor brightness:** Image is dim; hard to see detail
- **Instability:** Image drifts or vibrates after you touch the telescope
- **Misalignment:** Image is off-center or asymmetric
- **Chromatic aberration:** Rainbow colors around bright objects
- **Spherical aberration:** Image is slightly blurry except at the exact center

## Common Mistakes

- **Objective lens/mirror too weak:** A lens or mirror that does not curve enough will not focus light properly. The focal length must be appropriate for the telescope's intended magnification.
- **Tube too short:** If the tube is much shorter than the objective focal length, the eyepiece cannot be positioned correctly for focus. Build the tube the full focal length.
- **Poor collimation:** Misaligned optics degrade image quality more than almost anything else. Spend time aligning carefully.
- **Secondary mirror too large:** The secondary mirror blocks light. Keep it as small as possible while still capturing all the light from the primary.
- **Thermal expansion:** Using the telescope immediately after moving it from a warm room to a cold night causes focus errors (glass expands/contracts). Allow 30 minutes to acclimate.
- **Dirty optics:** Dust on the objective or mirror severely degrades the image. Keep optics covered when not in use.
- **Overmagnification:** Using too much magnification (eyepiece too strong) makes the image darker and harder to see. Use the lowest magnification that still shows desired detail.
- **Inadequate mirror quality:** A mirror ground to a sphere (simple) instead of a parabola (harder) will show spherical aberration. For reflectors, spend extra time grinding the primary mirror.
