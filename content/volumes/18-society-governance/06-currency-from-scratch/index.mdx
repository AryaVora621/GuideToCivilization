## Overview

Barter works for small communities: "I trade you 10 kg of grain for your goat." But as communities grow, barter breaks down. A baker wants shoes but the shoemaker wants grain; neither has what the other wants at that moment. Currency solves this by being a medium of exchange: everyone accepts it, so the baker can trade grain for currency, then currency for shoes.

This chapter covers how to create and manage currency without banks, central governments, or inflation spirals.

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Choosing a Currency Type

**Option A: Commodity-based currency**

A real good serves as currency. Examples:
- Grain (everyone needs it, spoils slowly, divisible)
- Metal (gold, silver, copper—durable, divisible, hard to counterfeit)
- Salt (historically valuable, durable, divisible)

Advantages:
- Has intrinsic value (grain can be eaten, metal can be worked)
- Hard to counterfeit (can't fake grain or metal)
- Stable value (tied to real demand)

Disadvantages:
- Heavy (moving large amounts is difficult)
- Spoilable (grain rots, metals corrode)
- Hard to divide (cutting a bar of metal is wasteful)

**Option B: Token-based currency**

A symbolic good represents value. Examples:
- Coins (stamped metal or clay discs)
- Tokens (marked pieces of wood, bone, or clay)
- Vouchers (written promises: "This slip represents 1 kg of grain stored in the central granary")

Advantages:
- Light (easy to carry)
- Divisible (coins can be made in many sizes)
- Standardized (all coins of the same size have the same value)

Disadvantages:
- Requires trust (tokens are only valuable if people believe they can trade them for real goods)
- Subject to counterfeiting (people can make fake coins)
- Requires enforcement (community must prevent counterfeits)

**Option C: Credit-based currency**

No physical currency. Instead, a ledger tracks who owes what. Examples:
- Ledger system: Central record keeps track of each person's balance ("+10 kg grain owed to Alice," "-5 kg grain owed by Bob")
- Tally sticks: Two parties split a stick; each keeps one half as proof of a debt

Advantages:
- No physical currency to lose or counterfeit
- Flexible (can represent any amount)
- Encourages record-keeping

Disadvantages:
- Requires literacy or trusted third party
- Less portable (balances are in a ledger, not in your pocket)
- Harder for a trader to verify balances on the fly

### Part 2: Creating Commodity-Based Currency

If grain is your currency unit.

**Step 1: Define the unit.**

1 unit of currency = 1 kg of grain (or whatever amount is tradeable).

Public rule: "All prices in our market are stated in kilograms of grain equivalent. 1 goat = 50 kg grain."

**Step 2: Create a standard storage system.**

Since grain is the currency, central storage is critical:
- Establish a central granary (see Vol 13 for storage design)
- Anyone with grain can deposit it for a receipt
- Receipt says: "Depositor: Alice. Amount: 100 kg. Issued: 2026-06-15. Valid for: 1 year."
- Receipts are traded instead of grain itself

**Step 3: Set the exchange rate.**

What else is currency worth?
- 1 kg grain = value of what?
- Example: 1 kg grain = 0.1 kg salt = 1 hour of skilled labor = 1 day of unskilled labor

Publish a table so everyone knows the exchange rate.

**Step 4: Manage debasement risk.**

Over time, pressure builds to dilute the currency:
- When the harvest is poor, people want to mint more currency than grain reserves
- This causes inflation: more currency chases same goods, prices rise
- Eventually currency becomes worthless

Prevention:
- Rule: "No more currency can be issued than backed by grain in storage"
- Audit the granary regularly (count grain, verify it matches issued currency)
- When currency is returned, destroy it (so it doesn't circulate again, inflating the money supply)

### Part 3: Creating Token-Based Currency (Coins)

**Step 1: Design the coin.**

Decide:
- Material (bronze, silver, copper, clay)
- Size and shape (round discs are standard)
- Weight (larger coins are worth more)
- Markings (stamp or mark the coin so people know it's official)

Example: A bronze coin weighing 10 grams, stamped with "Community of [Town], Year 2026, 1 Grain Unit"

**Step 2: Set coin denominations.**

Decide what values coins represent:
- Large coin: 10 kg grain equivalent
- Medium coin: 1 kg grain equivalent
- Small coin: 0.1 kg grain equivalent

This allows people to make change and handle transactions of different sizes.

**Step 3: Establish a mint.**

One place where coins are officially created:
- Only authorized people can mint coins
- All coins are stamped with an official mark
- Counterfeiting coins is a serious crime (see Vol 8, Ch 2 for punishment)

**Step 4: Back the coins (initially).**

To build trust, tie coins to real goods:
- For every 1 kg coin you mint, you must have 1 kg of grain in storage
- Anyone who doesn't trust the coin can exchange it at the mint for actual grain

After time, if the coin is trusted, you can relax this requirement (coins gain value from trust, not just backing).

**Step 5: Prevent counterfeiting.**

Counterfeits destroy currency value. Protection:
- Use hard-to-copy stamps (official marks on coins)
- Vary the design each year (so old coins with last year's mark are easily spotted as counterfeits)
- Punish counterfeiting severely (execution, mutilation, or slavery, depending on your culture)
- Regularly inspect coins in circulation (remove suspicious ones)

### Part 4: Creating Credit-Based Currency

**Step 1: Establish a ledger.**

A public record where each person's balance is written:
- Alice: +50 kg (she is owed grain)
- Bob: -30 kg (he owes grain)
- Carol: +10 kg

This ledger is kept by a trusted official (recorder, see Vol 8, Ch 5).

**Step 2: Make transactions via ledger.**

When Alice buys a goat from Bob (worth 50 kg equivalent):
- Recorder writes: "Alice owes 50 kg, Bob is owed 50 kg"
- Later, when Alice works for the community and earns 50 kg, her balance goes to 0
- Bob uses his +50 kg balance to trade for something else (say, tools from Carol)
- Recorder updates: Bob is owed 0, Carol owes 50 kg

**Step 3: Settle accounts periodically.**

At the end of each season or year:
- Those with positive balance can claim real goods (grain, tools, labor)
- Those with negative balance must pay off their debt (in goods or labor)
- Ledger is reset for the next period

**Step 4: Create tally sticks for portable credit.**

If ledger-based currency is too centralized, use tally sticks:
- Two parties agree on a debt: "I owe you 10 kg grain"
- They split a stick, each keeping one half
- When the debt is paid, the sticks are burned
- If disputed, the two halves should match (if counterfeited, halves won't align)

### Part 5: Managing Inflation and Stability

Inflation happens when the money supply grows faster than goods. Prices rise, and currency value falls. Prevention:

**Step 1: Maintain a fixed relationship between currency and goods.**

For commodity-based:
- Rule: "No more grain-backed currency can be issued than grain in storage"
- Audit annually

For token-based:
- Rule: "Coins minted this year cannot exceed goods produced last year"
- This keeps money supply roughly tied to economic growth

For credit-based:
- Rule: "Total outstanding credit cannot exceed annual surplus"
- This prevents over-lending

**Step 2: Retire worn or damaged currency.**

Old coins wear down and become worth less. Remove them from circulation:
- When people bring worn coins to the mint, melt them down
- Issue new coins in return (or credit their account)
- This controls the money supply without destroying value

**Step 3: Discourage hoarding.**

If people hold currency instead of trading, velocity drops and deflation can occur:
- Tax unused currency (e.g., 1% per year on held coins)
- Or create incentive to spend (e.g., currency issued this year is only valid for 3 years; after that, you must exchange it for new currency at a 5% loss)

**Step 4: Monitor prices.**

Track the price of common goods over time:
- If prices are rising (inflation), tighten the money supply (mint fewer coins, reduce credit)
- If prices are falling (deflation), loosen the money supply (mint more coins, increase credit)

### Part 6: Preventing Currency Collapse

**Step 1: Maintain trust.**

Currency is valuable only if people believe it will be accepted tomorrow. Maintain trust:
- Never debase the currency (don't reduce the amount of backing goods)
- Never counterfeit (leaders must follow the rules they set)
- Punish counterfeiters harshly (shows the community takes currency seriously)

**Step 2: Enforce acceptance.**

Make sure everyone uses the currency:
- Taxes must be paid in currency (see Vol 8, Ch 4)
- Markets accept currency (not barter alone)
- Government accepts currency for services

**Step 3: Back up the currency in crisis.**

If a crisis strikes (bad harvest, war, plague), people lose faith in currency. Prevent this:
- Maintain emergency reserves (extra grain, extra metal) for backing
- Be transparent: "We have 10,000 kg grain in reserve to back 10,000 coins"
- In crisis, be willing to redeem currency for goods to maintain trust

**Step 4: Create successor currencies carefully.**

If the old currency collapses, creating a new one is hard. Prevention:
- Guard the first currency jealously
- Only reform or change if absolutely necessary
- When forced to, create a new currency and allow a transition period where old and new are both accepted

## Verification

**Currency is working if:**
- People accept it in trade (willing to exchange goods for currency)
- Prices are stable (the same goat is worth 50 kg of grain equivalent, month after month)
- Counterfeits are rare (people trust their coins)
- Transactions are efficient (people trade currency instead of barter)

**Signs of failure:**
- People revert to barter (currency is not trusted)
- Prices are rising rapidly (inflation, loss of currency value)
- Counterfeits are common (people question authenticity)
- Currency is hoarded (people hold it but don't trade, creating shortages)

## Common Mistakes

- **Over-minting.** Creating more currency than backing goods causes inflation. Stick to a rule: no more currency than goods in reserve.
- **Trusting a disreputable minter.** If the person minting coins is known to counterfeit or debase, no one trusts the currency. Choose a minter with a reputation for honesty.
- **No enforcement of acceptance.** If the government doesn't require taxes in currency, people won't use it. Make government acceptance mandatory.
- **Making coins too small.** If the smallest coin is too valuable, no one can make small purchases. Create denominations people actually use.
- **No punishment for counterfeiting.** If counterfeiting is seen as a minor crime, fakers abound and currency becomes worthless. Punish severely.
- **Sudden currency changes.** If you suddenly declare the old currency worthless and issue new currency, people lose faith. Always allow a transition period.
- **No reserves.** If the granary is empty and currency is supposed to be grain-backed, people lose faith. Maintain visible reserves.
