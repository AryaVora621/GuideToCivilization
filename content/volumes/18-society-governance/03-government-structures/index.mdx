## Overview

Post-collapse communities need to make collective decisions: when to plant, how to defend against raiders, whether to admit refugees, how to distribute surplus. Without professional government, decisions happen in assemblies or councils. Your structure must be: inclusive (people accept the rules), transparent (no secret deals), and time-bounded (rotation prevents dictators).

This chapter covers three proven structures—assembly-based, council-based, and mixed systems—and how to run them without full-time politicians.

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Assembly-Based Government

Assembly-based governance means all adults (or all male adults, depending on your culture) gather to decide major issues. This works best for communities under 150–200 people.

**Step 1: Define who votes.**

Decision: Do all adults vote, or only property owners, or only men, or only those over a certain age? Your choice determines legitimacy. Most post-collapse communities use:
- All adults (15+), regardless of gender or property
- Heads of households (one per family)
- All free people (excluding slaves or servants, if your society has them)

Record the rule explicitly. Consistency matters.

**Step 2: Set a regular assembly schedule.**

Assemblies happen frequently enough to handle routine business but not so often that people ignore them. Common patterns:
- Weekly assemblies during growing season (May–September), monthly in winter
- Monthly assemblies year-round
- Assemblies called on demand for emergencies

**Step 3: Set quorum.**

Quorum is the minimum number who must attend for decisions to be binding. Low quorum (20% of eligible voters) allows decisions but risks excluding people. High quorum (70%+) ensures broad consent but risks gridlock when people don't attend.

Recommended: 50% of eligible voters for routine business, 70% for major decisions (war, law changes, banishment).

**Step 4: Define what decisions require assembly.**

Not all decisions go to assembly. Routine work (daily administration, minor disputes) goes to magistrates or councils. Assembly decides:
- Major laws or law changes
- War and peace
- Taxation or redistribution of surplus
- Admission of outsiders
- Banishment of community members
- Major infrastructure projects (canal, bridge, wall)

Minor decisions (schedule changes, individual disputes, repairs) are delegated.

**Step 5: Run an assembly.**

1. **Call the assembly:** Announce date, time, location. Ideally at least 3 days notice.
2. **State the agenda:** What issues are up for decision? Post the list so people know what to expect.
3. **Open discussion:** Anyone can speak (time limits prevent endless debate; recommend 5–10 minutes per person on each issue).
4. **Clarifying questions:** Keep focused on facts, not rhetoric.
5. **Vote:** Most common is simple majority (>50% of those present). Some communities use supermajority (2/3) for major decisions.
6. **Record the decision:** Write down what was decided, who voted which way (if decisions were close, record minority views). Keep the record public.

**Step 6: Enforce assembly decisions.**

Assembly decisions are law. If someone defies an assembly decision, they face court (see Vol 8, Ch 2).

### Part 2: Council-Based Government

Councils are smaller groups (5–13 people) who make decisions on behalf of the community. This works for communities of 150–500 people. Councils are more efficient than assemblies but require trust that councillors represent the people.

**Step 1: Choose council size.**

- 5 people: Small, fast decisions, but easily corrupted
- 7 people: Good balance (odd number avoids 4–4 ties)
- 9–13 people: Larger councils with representatives per sector (agriculture, defense, health, etc.)

**Step 2: Select councillors.**

Methods:
- **Lottery:** Draw names from all eligible voters (ensures random representation, hard to corrupt)
- **Election:** All eligible voters vote; top N vote-getters join the council (subjects to popularity bias)
- **Appointment by sector:** Agriculture council member chosen by farmers, defense by militia, etc. (ensures expertise, but can exclude minorities)
- **Rotation:** Each family or district takes a turn sending a representative (ensures inclusion, but timing matters)

Most robust: combination of lottery + rotation (e.g., yearly lottery from each district).

**Step 3: Set council term.**

How long does a councillor serve before rotation?
- 1 year: Fast rotation, many people gain experience, but no depth
- 2–3 years: Balance (person learns the role, then steps down before corruption sets in)

**Step 4: Define council authority.**

Does the council have free rein, or must they consult the assembly on major issues?

Options:
- **Strong council:** Council makes all decisions; assembly only votes if council requests
- **Weak council:** Council advises; major decisions require assembly vote
- **Mixed:** Council handles day-to-day; assembly handles law changes and major projects

Recommended: Council handles routine business; assembly votes on law changes, war, banishment.

**Step 5: Run council meetings.**

1. **Regular schedule:** Weekly, or every two weeks
2. **Quorum:** Usually 4/7 or 5/9 (majority required to make decisions)
3. **Voting:** Simple majority or consensus (if consensus is required, one person can block; fast but risks gridlock)
4. **Transparency:** Meetings are public, or at least decisions are announced publicly
5. **Record keeping:** Write down what was decided and who voted how

**Step 6: Prevent council corruption.**

- **Rotation:** No one serves more than 2–3 consecutive terms; then required to sit out one term before re-eligibility
- **Transparency:** All decisions public, all spending accounted for, anyone can audit the records
- **Recall:** If a councillor is widely seen as corrupt, assembly can hold a recall vote (50%+ eligible voters) to remove them before their term ends
- **Accountability:** After their term, councillors report to the assembly on what they did (similar to a trial; they can be censured or have property confiscated if found to have abused office)

### Part 3: Magistrate-Based Government

Magistrates are elected (or appointed) individuals with specific responsibilities: one handles markets, one handles disputes, one coordinates defense. This scales for communities of 200–500 people.

**Step 1: Define magistrate roles.**

Common magistrates:
- **Market master:** Enforces fair weights, prevents fraud, collects market taxes
- **Judge/arbitrator:** Hears civil and criminal disputes (see Vol 8, Ch 2)
- **Commander:** Organizes militia, defense, external relations
- **Recorder:** Maintains records, registries, archives
- **Overseer:** Manages commons, directs labor projects, handles waste

**Step 2: Select magistrates.**

- Elected by assembly (popular but subjects to bias)
- Appointed by council (efficient but less democratic)
- Lottery from qualified people (fair but requires prior training)
- Rotation (each family takes a turn)

**Step 3: Set powers and limits.**

Each magistrate can act alone on routine matters (settle minor disputes, fine petty cheaters) but must consult council or assembly on major decisions (execute someone, remove someone from commons, wage war).

**Step 4: Set term and rotation.**

1–2 years, then mandatory rotation. A person can be re-elected after sitting out one term.

**Step 5: Hold magistrates accountable.**

After their term, magistrates must account for all decisions and spending. Assembly can audit them and impose penalties (fine, confiscation, banishment) if they abused power.

### Part 4: Mixed Systems

Most successful communities blend assembly, council, and magistrates:
- **Assembly:** Votes on major laws, war, admission/banishment
- **Council:** Day-to-day decisions, budget, major projects
- **Magistrates:** Specific responsibilities with power to act alone on routine matters

**Step 1: Define the balance.**

Assembly decides policy; council implements; magistrates execute.

**Step 2: Size accordingly.**

- Community 50–150: Mostly assembly, small council (5 people), few magistrates (2–3)
- Community 150–400: Regular council (7 people), monthly assemblies, 4–6 magistrates
- Community 400+: Divided into districts, each with a council, reports to a higher council

**Step 3: Ensure rotation at all levels.**

If assembly members need to be selected, rotate them. If council rotates, ensure staggered rotation (don't replace everyone at once; continuity matters). If magistrates rotate, ensure the person taking over has trained on the role.

**Step 4: Prevent concentration of power.**

Rules that work:
- No one can hold two magistracies simultaneously
- Waiting periods: After serving, person must sit out one term before re-eligibility
- Transparency: All decisions and spending are public
- Transparency: Council meetings are open to observers (people can attend and listen, even if they can't vote)
- Accountability: After term, magistrate or councillor must submit to audit and questioning

## Verification

**Your government is working if:**
- Decisions are made consistently (not arbitrary)
- Participation is high (>50% attend assemblies or vote in elections)
- Power doesn't concentrate (no one person makes all decisions)
- Decisions are enforced (people follow assembly or council decisions)
- Complaints are rare (if people accept authority, it's legitimate)

**Signs of failure:**
- One person or family dominates (dictator, oligarchy)
- Decisions change based on who's in power (no rule of law)
- Participation is low (people don't trust the system)
- Factions fight instead of accepting decisions (civil conflict)
- Records are not kept (decisions are reinterpreted later)

## Common Mistakes

- **No rotation.** Councillors and magistrates stay in power too long and become dictators. Mandatory rotation every 1–2 years prevents this.
- **No transparency.** If decisions are made in secret, people lose trust. Open meetings and public records are essential.
- **No accountability.** If magistrates or councillors can't be questioned after their term, they abuse power without fear. Require an accounting session.
- **Quorum too low.** If 20% of people can make decisions binding on 100%, resentment builds. Aim for 50%+ for routine business, 70%+ for major decisions.
- **Too many assemblies.** If assemblies meet weekly on every tiny issue, participation drops and people get burnt out. Delegate routine decisions to councils.
- **No delegation.** If all decisions require assembly votes, the community can't move fast and people get frustrated. Use magistrates for day-to-day.
- **Unclear boundaries.** If it's not clear whether a decision is "routine" (council decides) or "major" (assembly votes), fights erupt. Define categories explicitly.
- **No written rules.** If government procedures are oral traditions, they drift and change. Write them down.
