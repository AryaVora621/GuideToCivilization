## Overview

Rule of law means everyone—powerful and weak—is subject to the same rules. Without it, might makes right and disputes become violence. Your settlement needs a system that is: predictable (same crime = same punishment), transparent (everyone knows the rules), and enforceable (violators face penalties).

This chapter covers criminal law (theft, assault, murder) and civil law (contracts, damage, debt). Both can be administered by community members without professional lawyers or judges.

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Establishing a Law Code

**Step 1: Define crimes.**

Core crimes (apply everywhere):
- **Murder:** Intentional killing. Penalty: death, or compensation to family
- **Assault:** Intentional wounding or violence. Penalty: compensation to victim, labor, or corporal punishment
- **Theft:** Taking another's property without permission. Penalty: return item + fine, or labor
- **Fraud:** Deception for gain. Penalty: compensation + fine
- **Rape/sexual assault:** Non-consensual sex. Penalty: severe (castration, banishment, or death in harsh traditions)
- **Breach of contract:** Failure to fulfill agreed obligation. Remedy: forced performance or compensation

**Step 2: Set penalties.**

Establish scales:
- **Minor crime (petty theft, insult):** Fine (small goods), labor (1–10 days)
- **Major crime (assault, significant theft):** Large fine, labor (weeks to months), or corporal punishment (whipping)
- **Extreme crime (murder, rape):** Death, permanent banishment, or slavery (in some systems)

**Step 3: Write the law code.**

Publish a written code that everyone can read:
- Crime + definition + penalty
- Exceptions (self-defense, accident vs. intent)
- Procedures (how disputes are tried)

Post it in the assembly or market. Read it aloud quarterly.

**Step 4: Establish proportionality.**

Ensure penalties fit crimes:
- Petty theft: Fine, not death
- Insult: Not punished severely (free speech matters)
- Accidental harm: Lesser penalty than intentional harm

### Part 2: Criminal Trial Process

**Step 1: Accusation.**

Someone accuses another of crime. They bring the charge to the magistrate or council.

**Step 2: Evidence gathering.**

Both accuser and accused present evidence:
- Witnesses (people who saw the act)
- Physical evidence (stolen item, weapon, wound)
- Motive (why did they do it?)
- Character (reputation, prior crimes)

**Step 3: Determine intent.**

Criminal liability requires intent in most systems. Distinctions:
- **Intentional:** Deliberately did the act. Highest penalty.
- **Reckless:** Knew the act was dangerous but did it anyway. Medium penalty.
- **Accidental/negligent:** Did not intend harm but was careless. Lower penalty or no penalty.
- **Self-defense:** Did the act to prevent immediate harm. No penalty if proportional.

Example: If A strikes B with a stick:
- Intentional assault → fine + labor
- Reckless (threw stick without looking, hit B by accident) → compensation to B, small fine
- Self-defense (B attacked first) → no penalty

**Step 4: Deliberation.**

Judge or judges (usually 3–7 community members) discuss evidence and render judgment.

**Step 5: Verdict and sentence.**

Announce publicly: guilty or not guilty. If guilty, state the penalty.

**Step 6: Enforcement.**

Carry out the sentence: collect the fine, assign labor, or execute (if capital crime).

### Part 3: Civil Disputes (Contract, Debt, Damage)

**Step 1: Types of civil disputes.**

- **Breach of contract:** "He promised to plow my field by harvest, but didn't"
- **Debt:** "I lent him 10 kg of grain; he refuses to repay"
- **Damage:** "His pig ate my crops; I want compensation"
- **Inheritance:** "She claims she is the rightful heir"

**Step 2: Burden of proof.**

In civil disputes, the person bringing the suit must prove their case. Standard: "preponderance of evidence" (more likely than not).

**Step 3: Civil trial process.**

1. **Plaintiff states claim:** "I lent him 5 kg of wheat on [date]. He promised to repay by [date]. He has not."
2. **Defendant responds:** "I don't owe him anything" or "The debt is paid" or "I will pay in [timeframe]"
3. **Evidence:** Both sides present witnesses, documents (written contracts, witnesses to lending), physical evidence
4. **Judge decides:** Based on evidence, which party is right
5. **Remedy:** Judge orders payment, return of property, or compensation

**Step 4: Enforcing civil judgments.**

If the losing party refuses to pay:
- Seize property equal to the debt
- Require labor (debtor works to repay)
- Public shaming (announce the debt and refusal)
- Banishment (if persistent refusal and severe debt)

### Part 4: Special Cases and Exceptions

**Step 1: Self-defense.**

If someone acts to prevent immediate harm, it is not a crime (with limits):
- Can use force proportional to threat
- Cannot escalate (if threatened with fist, cannot use weapon)
- Must retreat if possible (in some systems)

**Step 2: Insanity.**

If the accused is severely mentally ill (cannot distinguish right from wrong), they may not be criminally liable. They are confined but not punished. (This is rare and requires clear evidence.)

**Step 3: Necessity.**

If someone breaks a law to prevent greater harm (stealing food to prevent starvation), the penalty may be reduced or waived.

**Step 4: Family disputes.**

Crimes within families (parent beating child, wife leaving husband) are often treated differently. Most systems separate family matters from criminal law. Abuse is a crime; normal discipline is not.

### Part 5: Avoiding Corruption and Ensuring Justice

**Step 1: Rotate judges.**

Do not use the same judge forever. Rotate every few years to prevent corruption and bias.

**Step 2: Allow appeals.**

If the accused believes the judgment was unjust, allow one appeal to a higher authority (council, elder, or different judge).

**Step 3: Transparency.**

Announce all trials publicly. Let the community witness decisions. This prevents secret injustice.

**Step 4: Recusal.**

If a judge has a personal interest in the case (the accused is a relative, or the judge benefits from the outcome), they must withdraw.

**Step 5: Punishment must be visible.**

If someone is fined, the community knows it (fine is paid publicly). If someone is imprisoned or exiled, it is known. This deters others.

## Verification

**Rule of law is working if:**
- Crimes are prosecuted consistently (same crime = similar punishment)
- Decisions are predictable (people know roughly what penalty they face)
- Public accepts judgments (most people agree verdicts are fair)
- Crime rate is stable or declining (harsh punishment deters crime if applied consistently)

**Signs of failure:**
- Powerful people are never convicted (law is selective)
- Punishment varies wildly (similar crimes get different penalties)
- People appeal to violence instead of courts (courts lack authority)

## Common Mistakes

- **Ambiguous laws.** "Thou shalt not steal" is vague. Define theft, exceptions, and penalties.
- **Excessive punishment.** Death for petty theft alienates the community. Proportional penalties are more effective.
- **No enforcement.** Judgments without enforcement are ignored. Follow through on fines and labor.
- **Corrupt judges.** Judges who favor the powerful destroy trust. Rotate judges, allow appeals, and require transparency.
- **No written code.** Oral law is forgotten or reinterpreted. Write it down and post it.
- **Victim as judge.** If the person wronged is also the judge, bias is inevitable. Use neutral judges.
- **No appeal.** If there is no way to contest unjust judgments, people lose faith in courts.
