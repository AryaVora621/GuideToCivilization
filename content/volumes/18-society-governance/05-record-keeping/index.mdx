## Overview

Oral traditions fade within a generation. Disputes resurface. Laws are forgotten or reinterpreted. Without written records, each crisis forces the community to reinvent solutions already discovered decades earlier. Your settlement needs archives: a place where records are kept safe, accessible, and passed to the next generation.

This chapter covers how to organize records, what to record, how to preserve them, and how to train archivists.

- Writing materials (paper, parchment, ink, or clay tablets)
- Storage location (dry building, sealed containers)
- Organization method (filing system, catalog)
- Copying materials (for preserving records through duplication)
- Personnel (trained scribe or registrar)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: What to Record

Not everything needs recording. Too many records become unmanageable; too few and history is lost. Prioritize.

**Step 1: Legal and property records (highest priority).**

These form the basis of civilization and prevent endless re-litigation:
- Property deeds (ownership, boundaries, transfers)
- Wills and inheritance claims (who owns what after death)
- Marriage records (who is married to whom, and when)
- Loan and debt agreements (who owes what)
- Court decisions (who won disputes and why)
- Law code (what the rules are)

Without these, property disputes return every generation, inheritances are contested, and debts are forgotten.

**Step 2: Administrative records (important).**

Records needed to run government:
- Tax records (what was collected, from whom, when)
- Census or population records (how many people, what skills)
- Infrastructure records (what projects were built, when, cost)
- Budget records (what money was spent on)
- Government decisions (what the council or assembly decided, when)

Without these, tax collection becomes chaotic, planning is impossible, and past decisions are lost.

**Step 3: Historical and knowledge records (valuable but secondary).**

Records that preserve learning:
- Recipes and techniques (how to make things)
- Medical knowledge (how to treat illnesses)
- Natural knowledge (properties of plants, animals, seasons)
- Historical events (what happened in previous years)
- Genealogies (family trees)

These are less urgent than legal records but preserve knowledge that would otherwise be lost.

**Step 4: Religious or cultural records (community-dependent).**

Depending on your culture:
- Religious texts or prayers
- Stories and myths
- Poetry and literature
- Birth and death records

### Part 2: Setting Up a Record System

**Step 1: Designate a record keeper.**

One person is responsible for maintaining all records:
- A scribe (trained to write)
- A registrar (trained in organization)
- Someone with access to a secure location

This person should be:
- Literate (can read and write)
- Organized (can file and retrieve records)
- Trusted (won't falsify records for personal gain)
- Trained in procedures (knows what to record and how)

**Step 2: Choose a location for archives.**

The archive must be:
- Dry (moisture destroys paper and ink)
- Safe from fire (separate from living areas and workshops)
- Secure (locked or guarded, so people can't steal or alter records)
- Cool (paper lasts longer in cool temperatures)
- Accessible (people can visit to check records, but with supervision)

Options:
- Stone building (cellar or dedicated archive room)
- Waterproof storage in a secure room
- Sealed containers (for portable records)

**Step 3: Create a filing system.**

Records must be organized so they can be found:
- By type (property, legal, administrative, historical)
- By date (chronological within each type)
- By person or entity (whose record is it?)

Example structure:
```
Archive/
  Property/
    Deeds/
    Transfers/
    Boundaries/
  Legal/
    Court Decisions/
      Year 2025/
      Year 2026/
    Laws/
  Administrative/
    Taxes/
    Budgets/
    Census/
  Historical/
    Events/
    Recipes/
```

**Step 4: Create an index or catalog.**

So people can find records without reading every document:
- List of all property deeds (by location and owner)
- List of all laws (by topic)
- List of all major court cases (by date and names involved)
- Timeline of major events

The catalog itself should be a record, kept in duplicate, so it survives if the original is damaged.

### Part 3: Recording Documents

**Step 1: Standardize document format.**

All records should follow a consistent format so they can be found and understood:

For property deeds:
- Date recorded
- Names of parties (grantor and grantee)
- Property description (location, boundaries, size)
- Consideration (what was paid or exchanged)
- Signatures or marks of witnesses

For court records:
- Date of trial
- Names of disputants
- Charge or dispute
- Evidence presented
- Verdict and reasoning
- Penalties imposed

For laws:
- Title of law
- Date enacted
- Text of the law
- Penalties for violation
- Any amendments or repeals

**Step 2: Use durable materials.**

Paper fades; ink can be altered. Use materials that last:
- Parchment (treated animal skin) lasts 500+ years
- Paper (rag-based) lasts 100–300 years depending on quality
- Stone or clay tablets last 1000+ years but are harder to write on
- Ink should be iron-based (fades slower than carbon-based)

For important records, use parchment and iron ink.

**Step 3: Make copies of critical records.**

The original might be lost or damaged. Keep duplicate copies:
- For property deeds: Store one copy in the central archive, one with the property owner
- For laws: Multiple copies, in different locations
- For critical records: At least three copies, in different places

**Step 4: Include annotations and amendments.**

Records change over time:
- If a law is repealed, note the date and reason
- If property is transferred, record the new owner
- If a debt is paid off, mark the record "satisfied"

Create amendment records separately so the original is not erased; instead, note "amended on [date]" and attach the amendment.

### Part 4: Preserving Records

**Step 1: Protect from moisture.**

Moisture is the biggest threat to paper and parchment:
- Store in a dry room with good drainage
- Use sealed containers (ceramic jars, wooden boxes lined with cloth)
- Keep away from water sources (wells, streams)
- Place boxes on shelves or stands (not on damp floors)

**Step 2: Protect from pests.**

Insects and rodents eat paper:
- Store in sealed containers so animals can't access them
- Use herbs to deter insects (lavender, mint, cedar)
- Check regularly for damage

**Step 3: Protect from fire and theft.**

- Scatter copies in different locations (if one archive burns, copies survive)
- Keep in a secure room with restricted access
- For very important documents (law code, foundational deeds), scatter originals geographically

**Step 4: Periodically recopy old documents.**

Paper deteriorates:
- Every 50–100 years, recopy important records
- Use the recopying as an opportunity to check for errors and update information
- Keep both old and new copies

**Step 5: Create a backup archive.**

Appoint someone in a different location (another settlement or region) to maintain copies of critical records. If your settlement is destroyed, the knowledge survives.

### Part 5: Training and Succession

**Step 1: Recruit and train a successor.**

The record keeper will eventually age or die. Train someone to take over:
- Teach writing and reading to the successor
- Teach how to organize and file records
- Teach what counts as important information
- Have the successor assist for at least one year before taking over

**Step 2: Create a written procedures manual.**

Write down how the archive is organized and managed:
- What records are kept and where
- How to file new records
- How to search for existing records
- How to preserve documents
- What counts as authorized access (who is allowed to see records)

This manual is itself a critical record and should be copied multiple times.

**Step 3: Hold regular training sessions.**

Teach the community why records matter:
- How records prevented a property dispute from escalating to violence
- How old recipes saved people during a food shortage
- How past decisions informed current choices

When people see the value of records, they are more likely to maintain them.

### Part 6: Accessing Records

**Step 1: Set access rules.**

Who can read which records?
- Property deeds: Owner and potential buyers can view
- Court decisions: Public (everyone can read)
- Tax records: Council/magistrates only (but results are public)
- Personal information (genealogies, medical): Restricted to family or council

**Step 2: Allow public inspection (with limits).**

Transparent records build trust:
- Anyone can request to see their own property deed or court decision
- Anyone can read the law code
- Records are inspected in the archive (not removed, so originals aren't damaged)

**Step 3: Create certified copies.**

If someone needs a copy of a record (to prove ownership, to show a decision):
- The record keeper makes a copy
- Certifies it with a mark or seal (indicating it matches the original)
- Provides a copy (sometimes with a fee to offset copying costs)

## Verification

**Archive system is working if:**
- Records are retrievable (people can find documents quickly)
- Records are accurate (documents match agreements people remember)
- Records are preserved (old documents are still readable)
- Records are trusted (people use them to resolve disputes)

**Signs of failure:**
- Records are lost or unreadable (forgotten in storage, water damage, deterioration)
- Records are inaccessible (no one can find what they need)
- Records are falsified (people alter documents for personal gain)
- Disputes resurface (the same dispute is argued again 10 years later)

## Common Mistakes

- **No archive location.** If records are stored in someone's home, they are lost when that person dies or moves. Use a dedicated, public, secure location.
- **Disorganized records.** If documents are piled randomly, finding a specific deed takes hours. Create a filing system.
- **No copies.** If the only copy of a deed burns, ownership is disputed forever. Make copies.
- **Single record keeper.** If the only person who knows the system dies, the archive becomes useless. Train a successor.
- **Inaccessible records.** If the record keeper is the only one allowed to see records, disputes happen privately. Allow public inspection with reasonable restrictions.
- **Poor preservation.** If records are stored in a damp cellar, they mold and become illegible in decades. Use dry storage.
- **No index.** If there's no catalog, finding a record means reading every document. Create an index.
- **Altering original records.** If documents are erased or overwritten, their history is lost. Use amendment records instead.
- **No succession plan.** If the record keeper retires or dies without training a replacement, the archive system collapses. Train someone years in advance.
