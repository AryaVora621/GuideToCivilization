## Overview

Your settlement needs to build walls, maintain roads, support soldiers, feed administrators, and pay for community infrastructure. In the early years, you have no money and cannot simply print it. You must raise resources through taxation or labor obligation (corvée). The system you choose—and how fairly you implement it—will determine whether your settlement collectively prospers or fragments into factions fighting over the tax burden.

This chapter teaches you three proven taxation systems, how to set rates that are sustainable without starvation, and how to transition from one system to another as your settlement matures. Your goal is simple: collect enough to fund critical public works without crushing individual families or creating the perception that some people are treated unfairly.

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Calculate Your Tax Base and Public Needs (Month 1)

You cannot tax money you do not need or that people do not have.

**Assess public needs (annual costs):**

**Defense:** 
- How many soldiers do you need? (Assume 1 soldier per 20–50 people for early years; higher if under threat)
- Each soldier needs:
  - Food: 400g grain/day × 365 days = 146 kg per year
  - Weapon and armor: amortized over 5 years, roughly 20 kg grain-equivalent in value per year
  - Wage/goods: 40 kg grain-equivalent value per year
  - Total: ~200 kg grain-equivalent per soldier per year
- Example: 1,000-person settlement needs 20–50 soldiers = 4,000–10,000 kg grain per year for defense

**Administration:**
- Council members, judges, administrators: ~10 people
- Each needs food (80 kg/year) + materials = ~100 kg grain-equivalent per year
- Total: ~1,000 kg/year

**Infrastructure:**
- Road maintenance, bridge repair, building construction
- Rough estimate: 2,000–5,000 kg grain-equivalent value per year (depends on project scope)

**Public health & sanitation:**
- Latrines, water supplies, public baths
- Rough estimate: 500–1,500 kg grain-equivalent per year

**Total annual public budget:** 8,000–18,000 kg grain-equivalent per year (for a 1,000-person settlement)

**Example:** 1,000 people, 10,000 kg grain-equivalent needed. Per person, that is 10 kg per year, or ~27g per day per person (roughly 7% of a basic 400g daily ration).

**Assess your tax base:**
- Total farmland under your control: [hectares]
- Expected annual harvest: [kg per hectare] × [hectares] = [total kg]
- Surplus after feeding everyone: [total kg] − [consumption needs] = [surplus kg]
- Taxable surplus: [surplus kg] × 60% (leave 40% buffer for bad years and contingencies)

Example: 200 hectares, 4,000 kg/hectare, = 800,000 kg gross. Consumption for 1,000 people = 400g × 365 days × 1,000 = 146,000 kg. Surplus = 654,000 kg. Taxable surplus = 392,000 kg. You can tax up to 10,000 kg annually (2.5% of surplus) without hardship.

### Step 2: Choose a Taxation System (Week 2, with community input)

Three proven systems exist. Choose one to start, but plan to transition later.

**System A: Land Tax (Immediate, easiest to start)**

**How it works:**
- Every person who owns land pays a percentage of the annual harvest to the government
- Rate: 5–10% of the harvest (adjust based on your needs and capacity)
- Collection: once per year, at harvest, when food is most abundant
- Payment: in grain, animals, or labor (see conversion below)

**Who is taxed:**
- Landowners only (those with at least 1 hectare of productive land)
- Renters and landless laborers are exempt (they do not have the harvest to pay)

**Advantages:**
- Easy to assess: you count the harvest, calculate 10%, collect
- Scales naturally: more productive land pays more; poor land pays less
- Works with barter: people can pay in kind (grain, oil, animals) without money
- Seen as fair: rich landowners pay the most absolutely

**Disadvantages:**
- Exempts landless people entirely; they feel they do not contribute
- Seasonal: you only collect once per year; in months 2–11, you have no tax revenue
- Vulnerable to crop failure: in a bad year, people cannot pay; you must forgive debt or people starve

**Sample formula:**
```
Land Tax Rate: 10% of annual harvest

Assessment:
- Farm 1: 5 hectares, expected yield 20,000 kg = tax 2,000 kg
- Farm 2: 2 hectares, expected yield 8,000 kg = tax 800 kg
- Farm 3: 1 hectare, expected yield 4,000 kg = tax 400 kg
  (Total: 3,200 kg collected)

Payment options:
- In grain (2,000 kg grain = 2,000 kg tax for farm 1)
- In animals (10 sheep = 2,000 kg grain value = tax paid)
- In labor (100 days of labor at 20 kg grain value per day = 2,000 kg tax paid)

Collection: September–October (harvest season)
Exemptions: Poor families (harvest < 2,000 kg) are exempt
```

**System B: Labor Obligation (Corvée, common in medieval societies)**

**How it works:**
- Every able-bodied adult contributes a set number of days per year to public works
- Rate: 10–20 days per year (roughly 2–4 weeks)
- Work: roads, fortifications, public buildings, infrastructure maintenance
- Exemptions: pregnant women, nursing mothers, the sick, elderly (over 70)

**Who is taxed:**
- Everyone aged 16–60 who is not engaged in critical essential work (not a full-time soldier, healer, or blacksmith)
- If you are one of the essential 10–15 people who cannot be spared, you pay a grain tax instead (see System C)

**Advantages:**
- Does not require money: people pay with their labor
- Builds community: everyone works together on shared projects
- Flexible: can be collected in bursts (everyone helps build walls in spring) or spread throughout the year
- Fair: literally equal contribution for equal-capacity people

**Disadvantages:**
- Disruptive: pulls people from their farms in critical seasons
- Vulnerable to shirking: people can drag, work slowly, or do poor-quality work
- Harder to assess work quality: is a day of work actually productive?
- Exemptions create resentment: if you are exempt, you pay nothing while others work

**Sample formula:**
```
Corvée Rate: 12 days per year per person

Mechanism:
- Divide the year into 12 work-weeks (3-day weeks spaced across the year)
- Every person does 1 work-week per year
- Work assignments: roads (weeks 1–3), fortifications (weeks 4–9), public buildings (weeks 10–12)

Exemptions:
- Soldiers (full-time) — instead pay 500 kg grain-equivalent tax
- Healers — instead pay 300 kg grain-equivalent tax
- Blacksmiths/essential craftspeople — instead pay 300 kg grain-equivalent tax
- Pregnant women, nursing mothers — exempt entirely
- Sick or injured — exempt until recovery

Verification:
- Every person checks in at the work site each morning with a witness
- Work supervisor signs off at end of day (20 tally marks = 20 days completed)
- Ledger tracks who completed their corvée and who still owes

Non-compliance:
- Failure to work: must pay 50 kg grain-equivalent per missed day to hire replacement labor
```

**System C: Income/Labor Tax (Barter economy, medium-term)**

**How it works:**
- Every person or household pays a percentage of the value of goods they produce or trade
- Rate: 5–10% of annual production value
- Collection: as transactions occur (difficult at first; easier once you have merchants and markets)
- Payment: in highest-value item (grain, animals, crafted goods, labor)

**Who is taxed:**
- Everyone: farmers, craftspeople, healers, traders

**Advantages:**
- Fair across occupations: a blacksmith and a farmer both pay the same percentage of their output
- Scales to economic reality: grows as people produce more
- Applies to everyone: no exemptions based on land or labor

**Disadvantages:**
- Hard to assess: how do you know the true value of a blacksmith's work or a healer's services?
- Requires record-keeping and honesty: if people lie about production, you collect nothing
- Requires money or standardized barter: you need a way to measure "10% of value"
- Slow to implement: only works well once your economy has stabilized and you have markets

**Sample formula:**
```
Income Tax Rate: 7% of annual production value

Assessment by profession:
- Farmer: 10 hectares, 40,000 kg harvest = 2,800 kg tax
- Blacksmith: produces 20 tools per year, each worth 100 kg grain = 2,000 kg production = 140 kg tax
- Healer: treats 365 patients/year, each pays 2 kg = 730 kg production = 51 kg tax
- Trader: buys for 10,000 kg, sells for 12,000 kg = 2,000 kg profit = 140 kg tax
  (Total: 3,131 kg collected)

Collection: Annually, verified by traders and witnesses
Exemptions: Those earning less than 1,000 kg grain-equivalent per year are exempt
```

### Step 3: Implement Tax Collection (Month 2+)

Pick one system. Announce it publicly. Do not change systems every month.

**Announcement process:**
- Council drafts the tax code (2–3 pages: what is taxed, rates, exemptions, consequences)
- Council posts it publicly and reads it aloud
- Community discussion: answer questions, explain why the rate is set at that level
- Community vote: must pass with 60%+ approval
- Once approved, do not raise rates for at least 2 years without a new vote

**Assessment (System A: Land Tax):**
1. In month 1 (early spring): create a registry of all landholdings and expected yields
2. Interview each landowner: "How many hectares? What crops? What is your realistic yield per hectare?"
3. Cross-check with neighbors and market prices
4. Write it down: "Farm X: 5 hectares wheat, 4 hectares barley, expected total 22,000 kg, tax owed: 2,200 kg"
5. Tax assessment is posted publicly so everyone can verify it is fair

**Collection (System A: Land Tax):**
1. At harvest (September–October): send a tax collector + witness to each farm
2. Measure the harvest (using containers, scales, or agreed estimates)
3. Calculate 10% (or your rate)
4. Collector removes the tax share into sealed containers marked with farm name
5. Record: "Farm X, collected 2,200 kg, witness: Maria, date: 2026-09-15"
6. Ledger is posted publicly within one week; anyone can verify their farm's entry

**Collection (System B: Corvée):**
1. Post work schedules in month 1 (spring): when each person/family is due to work
2. Each week, a work supervisor signs in people and marks attendance
3. At end of day, they get a tally mark in the ledger (visible, public)
4. After 12 marks, they are done for the year
5. Ledger is posted monthly; anyone can count their marks and verify they are on track

**Collection (System C: Income Tax):**
1. This requires a market or regular trading; only implement in year 2+
2. For now, use System A or B

### Step 4: Set Up the Tax Ledger (Ongoing)

**What to record:**
- Person/farm name
- Obligation (e.g., "10% of harvest" or "12 corvée days")
- Amount owed (calculated at assessment)
- Amount paid (collected)
- Date paid
- Form of payment (grain, animals, labor hours, etc.)
- Witness signatures (at least 2)
- Exemptions applied (if any)
- Notes (e.g., "crop failure, exempted by council vote")

**Posting schedule:**
- Monthly: post current month's collections (who paid, who still owes)
- Quarterly: post summary (total collected vs. budget, shortfalls)
- Annually: post full year's ledger so everyone can see where the money went

**Example ledger entry:**
```
Date: 2026-06-16
Farm: Maria Gonzalez, 5 hectares
Expected yield: 20,000 kg
Tax rate: 10%
Amount owed: 2,000 kg
Amount paid: 2,000 kg (grain, September harvest)
Witness 1: Juan Martinez
Witness 2: Anna Larson
Status: PAID
Note: None
```

### Step 5: Handle Non-Compliance Fairly (Ongoing)

Some people will not pay. Have a graduated response.

**Land Tax non-compliance:**
1. **First reminder (month after deadline):** "Your tax is overdue. The deadline is [date]. Please bring your payment by [new date, 2 weeks later]."
2. **Second notice (2 weeks before new deadline):** "Your tax is seriously overdue. If you do not pay by [date], we will impose consequences. If you cannot pay because of hardship, you can appeal to the council."
3. **Consequence (if payment is not received by deadline):**
   - Option A: Fine (pay 1.5× the original tax next year)
   - Option B: Forced labor (30 days of corvée to pay off the debt)
   - Option C: Lien (government takes a portion of next year's harvest until debt is paid)
   - Do not immediately starve people; give them a path to recover

**Corvée non-compliance:**
1. **First notice:** "You owe 3 days of work. The schedule is [dates]. Show up or you will owe 50 kg grain equivalent fine."
2. **Second notice:** "You did not show up. You now owe 50 kg grain or 3 additional work days. Pay within 2 weeks or face [consequence]."
3. **Consequence (if unpaid):** 
   - Confiscate items of equivalent value (tools, animals, goods)
   - Or require 10 additional work days at reduced meal allowance
   - Or forbid participation in markets/trading until debt is paid

**Exemptions and appeals:**
- If someone claims they cannot pay because of genuine hardship, they appeal to the council
- Council reviews their ledger: did they pay last year? Is this a new hardship?
- Council can grant partial exemption (pay 5% instead of 10%) or extend deadline
- Decision is recorded and posted: "Maria's tax reduced to 5% due to farm fire, approved by council, 2026-06-16"

### Step 6: Create a Budget and Show People Where the Money Goes (Quarterly)

Transparency prevents resentment.

**Post quarterly:**
```
TAX COLLECTION & EXPENDITURE REPORT — Q2 2026

COLLECTED (Jan–Mar):
- Land tax: 8,500 kg
- Corvée labor value (20 days at 25 kg/day): 500 kg equivalent
- Total: 9,000 kg

SPENT:
- Soldiers' food & wages: 3,000 kg
- Road maintenance: 1,200 kg
- Administrator salaries: 600 kg
- Public latrines & sanitation: 800 kg
- Granary maintenance & storage: 400 kg
- Reserve (bad year fund): 3,000 kg

BALANCE: 0 kg (fully spent)

RESERVE FUND BALANCE: 12,000 kg (good for 1.3 years at current rate)

Next quarter priority: Begin fortification work (expected cost: 2,000 kg)
```

Post this publicly. Invite questions. This prevents people from assuming taxes are being stolen or wasted.

## Verification

**Success looks like:**
- After month 1: tax code is written, posted, and approved by community
- After month 3: first tax collection happens; 80%+ of people pay on time
- After month 6: ledger is posted publicly; disputes are handled fairly via appeals
- After year 1: 90%+ of population accepts the tax system as fair (even if they dislike taxes)

**Failure looks like:**
- Tax rate is secret or unexplained; people suspect corruption
- The same person collects taxes and uses them privately; no ledger exists
- Non-compliance is punished harshly without appeal options
- The tax rate is raised repeatedly without community vote
- No one knows where tax money goes

## Common Mistakes

1. **"We can just decide to tax whenever we need money"** — Surprise taxes cause resentment and non-compliance. Set a rate, announce it, and keep it stable for at least 2 years. Then re-vote if you need to raise it.

2. **"Let's tax the poor more; they are more numerous"** — This causes immediate starvation and rebellion. Tax capacity (ability to pay without hardship). This usually means taxing landowners, not the landless.

3. **"We will let the leader keep some tax money as a salary"** — Make the leader's salary explicit in the budget. Do not let them pocket tax revenues. This is the fastest path to authoritarianism.

4. **"Non-payment should result in banishment/execution"** — This is counterproductive. Punish with fines or labor. Give people a path to pay their debt. A person forced into debt slavery will revolt.

5. **"We don't need a ledger if we remember who paid"** — You will forget. Disputes will arise. Keep a written ledger. Post it.

6. **"Let's use System C (income tax) immediately"** — You need markets and record-keeping for this to work. Start with System A (land tax) or System B (corvée) in the first years. Transition to System C in years 3–5.

7. **"If the harvest fails, we should still collect the tax"** — You will starve people. Instead: reduce the rate by 50% in a bad year, or waive collection entirely, and make up the shortfall next year or via reserve fund. This is why you build reserves.

8. **"We should tax merchants/outsiders more heavily"** — This discourages trade and makes you look unfair to outsiders. Use the same rate for everyone.
