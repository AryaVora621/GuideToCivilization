## Overview

Property rights are the legal foundation of resource distribution. Without clarity on who owns what, every dispute becomes violence. Your settlement must decide: are forests commons (everyone can hunt), or private (owner controls access)? Are pastures shared, or divided into family plots?

This chapter covers two proven systems: commons management (sustainable harvest from shared resources) and private property (individual ownership with transfer rights). Both work. Ambiguity kills.

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Commons (Shared Resources)

**Step 1: Define the commons.**

Commons are resources managed collectively: forests, pastures, rivers, hunting grounds. Rules govern access and harvest limits.

**Step 2: Set harvest rules.**

- **Pasture commons:** Each family gets X animals; herds are co-managed
- **Forest commons:** Each family can cut Y cords of firewood per year; hunting is unrestricted
- **River commons:** Fishing rights on certain dates; water draw is metered by time/quantity

**Step 3: Enforce limits.**

Assign monitors to track usage. Penalties for exceeding limits:
- First violation: Warning, fine (small goods or labor)
- Second violation: Large fine or labor obligation
- Repeated violation: Exclusion from commons

**Step 4: Rotate management.**

Annually or every few years, rotate who manages the commons (collects fines, resolves disputes). This prevents corruption and distributes the burden.

**Step 5: Adjust rules by season.**

Pasture capacity varies by season (better in spring, worse in winter). Adjust animal limits accordingly.

### Part 2: Private Property

**Step 1: Divide land.**

Allocate plots to families or individuals. Methods:
- Equal division (each household gets X hectares)
- Lottery (draw lot locations)
- Purchase (settlers buy plots)
- Inheritance (families receive ancestral land)

**Step 2: Mark boundaries.**

Stone piles, ditches, or posts mark property lines. Every 10–20 meters place a visible marker. Record boundary locations in writing.

**Step 3: Create ownership records.**

Write a deed for each plot:
- Owner name and date acquired
- Boundaries (directions and distances, or description: "the parcel south of the stream, north of the road")
- Area (in standard units, see Vol 25, Ch 4)
- Any encumbrances (right-of-way for neighbors, easements for water)

**Step 4: Register transfers.**

When land changes hands:
- Write a new deed
- Record the transfer in the registry (who sold, who bought, date)
- Both parties sign or mark the deed
- Register the change within [time period — recommend 30 days]

**Step 5: Resolve boundary disputes.**

If two neighbors disagree on a boundary:
1. Both parties and three neutral witnesses walk the boundary
2. Compare to original deeds
3. If deeds are unclear, measure from known reference points (stream, road, landmark)
4. Both parties agree on the corrected boundary (in writing)
5. If they cannot agree, use arbitration (see Vol 8, Ch 2)

### Part 3: Mixed Systems (Commons + Private)

Most successful systems combine both:
- **Private:** Housing plots, agricultural fields, orchards
- **Commons:** Forests, pastures, rivers

**Step 1: Allocate private plots.**

Families own their house lot (0.1–0.5 hectares) and farming land (1–5 hectares) outright.

**Step 2: Maintain commons for specific uses.**

- Forest commons: Firewood, hunting, foraging (regulated to prevent depletion)
- Pasture commons: Grazing rights for cattle/sheep
- River commons: Fishing and water rights

**Step 3: Set sustainable harvest levels.**

For forest commons, calculate sustainable yield:
- Inventory: How much timber, game, firewood is available?
- Growth rate: How much grows/reproduces per year?
- Sustainable harvest: No more than growth rate

Example: A forest contains 10,000 cords of firewood and grows 500 cords/year. Maximum harvest: 500 cords/year.

**Step 4: Monitor and adjust.**

Every 3–5 years, re-inventory. If resources are declining, reduce harvest. If abundant, increase cautiously.

### Part 4: Recording and Dispute Resolution

**Step 1: Create a land registry.**

A central record of:
- All properties (private and commons)
- Owners (names, families)
- Boundaries (descriptions or map references)
- Transfers (history of ownership changes)
- Disputes (any unresolved boundary or ownership questions)

**Step 2: Maintain the registry.**

- One person is registrar (trained, trustworthy)
- All land transactions recorded within [time period]
- Registry open for public inspection (anyone can verify ownership)
- Copies maintained (in case original is lost)

**Step 3: Resolve ownership disputes.**

If two people claim the same land:
1. Both present evidence (original deed, purchase records, occupation)
2. Arbiters (usually three elders or community council) hear both sides
3. Decision is based on: earliest clear deed, longest continuous occupation, community recognition
4. Loser can appeal to higher authority (if one exists) or accept the decision

**Step 4: Resolve access disputes (commons).**

If someone exceeds commons limits:
1. Monitor reports violation with evidence (number of trees cut, animals grazed)
2. Violator responds (claimed he was within limits, or needed exception due to hardship)
3. Council decides: fine, labor, or reduced future allocation

## Verification

**Commons are working if:**
- Resources are stable (not being depleted)
- Disputes are rare (everyone understands rules)
- Harvest limits are observed (monitored via spot checks)

**Private property is working if:**
- Boundaries are clear (few disputes)
- Owners feel secure (willing to invest in improvements)
- Transfers are smooth (buyers and sellers trust the registry)

**Mixed system is working if:**
- Private plots support families
- Commons provide supplementary resources (fuel, game, pasture)
- Total system is sustainable (resources renew faster than consumption)

## Common Mistakes

- **Ambiguous boundaries.** "The land south of the stream" is vague if the stream moves. Use fixed markers (rocks, posts, cardinal directions).
- **No written records.** Oral tradition fails after generations. Write all deeds and registry entries.
- **Excessive commons.** If commons are too generous (unlimited access, no harvest limits), resources deplete in years.
- **Insufficient commons.** If families have only private plots and no access to forests or pasture, they become impoverished if crops fail.
- **Unequal division.** If some families receive much larger plots than others, resentment and conflict follow. Aim for equity within constraints.
- **Lazy registry.** If transfers are not recorded promptly, disputes multiply. Enforce registration deadlines.
- **No enforcement.** Rules without penalties are ignored. Monitor commons, fine violators, remove repeat offenders from access.
