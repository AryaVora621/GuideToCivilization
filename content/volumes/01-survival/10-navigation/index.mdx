## Overview

The difference between being lost and being displaced is navigation. When collapse severs your connection to maps and GPS, you have four tools: the sun's predictable path, the stars' fixed positions, the terrain's shape, and the natural signs around you.

This chapter teaches dead reckoning (moving from one known point to another), terrain association (matching the land to memory or simple sketches), and celestial orientation (finding direction from heavenly bodies). Combined, these methods will keep you moving toward a goal or finding your way home.

The key skill is not performing perfect navigation—it is noticing when you are drifting and correcting course.

## Prerequisites

- Ability to read basic terrain (ridges, valleys, water flow). See Vol 2, Ch 2 (Terrain Navigation).
- Knowledge of where you are trying to go (even rough direction is enough to start).
- Clear sky at least partially (for sun and stars; terrain navigation works in any weather).

## Materials & Tools

**For sun-based navigation:**
- Your body (cast a shadow; place a stick upright for a longer shadow)
- Optional: a watch with hands (if available)

**For star-based navigation:**
- Eyes and clear sky (minimum visibility of 10 stars)
- Optional: a simple star chart or memory of key stars (Polaris in Northern Hemisphere, Southern Cross in Southern Hemisphere)

**For terrain navigation:**
- High ground or elevated viewpoint
- Memory or rough sketch of terrain features (ridges, peaks, valleys, water)
- Ability to see 5+ km ahead (clear day helps)

**For distance estimation:**
- Landmarks and map scale (if you have it)
- Pacing: count double-steps (one pace = two footsteps; this is used in military distance estimation)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Sun-Based Navigation

The sun rises in the east and sets in the west everywhere on Earth. At noon (when the sun is highest), it points due south in the Northern Hemisphere and due north in the Southern Hemisphere.

#### Step 1: Find East and West

Stand in an open area. Face the direction the sun is rising (before noon) or setting (after noon). If you face the sunrise, east is in front of you and west is behind you. If you face the sunset, west is in front and east is behind.

This method has a margin of error of ±10 degrees because the sun rises slightly north of due east in summer and slightly south in winter. But 10 degrees is acceptable for survival navigation.

#### Step 2: Find North and South Using a Stick Shadow (Northern Hemisphere)

Place a stick upright in the ground. Wait 15 minutes. The shadow will move—it always moves east to west. The end of the shadow points in the direction the sun has moved from. This is west. The base of the stick is the east direction.

To find north and south, stand with east on your left and west on your right. North is in front, south is behind. This method is accurate to within ±5 degrees.

In the Southern Hemisphere, the shadow motion is reversed: shadows move east to west still, but north is behind you when you stand with east on your left.

#### Step 3: Use a Watch to Find Direction (Northern Hemisphere)

If you have an analog watch:
1. Point the hour hand (the short hand) at the sun.
2. Imagine a line halfway between the hour hand and the 12 o'clock mark.
3. This line points south.

The sun appears to move 15 degrees per hour (360 degrees in 24 hours). The hour hand moves 30 degrees per hour. By pointing the hour hand at the sun, you are using the hour hand as a sun indicator. The angle between it and 12 is half the sun's motion from noon.

Accuracy: ±10 degrees.

In the Southern Hemisphere, the line points north instead.

#### Step 4: Correct for Latitude

The sun's path changes with latitude:
- At the equator, the sun passes directly overhead twice per year. At noon, it can point north or south depending on the season.
- At higher latitudes (above 40 degrees), the sun never reaches the overhead position. At 60+ degrees north, summer sun barely sets, and winter sun barely rises.

If you know your approximate latitude, adjust your sun navigation:
- Above 45 degrees latitude in summer: the sun is always north of due east when rising, and north of due west when setting.
- Below the equator: reverse "north" and "south" in all instructions.

For survival purposes, if you are between 20–50 degrees in either hemisphere, sun navigation is reliable within ±10 degrees.

### Star-Based Navigation (Reliable, Accurate)

Stars are fixed in their positions in the sky. They rotate around the celestial pole (the point in the sky directly above the Earth's north or south pole). Learn two anchor stars and you can navigate by night regardless of season.

#### Step 1: Find the North Star (Polaris) — Northern Hemisphere

Look for the constellation Ursa Major (the Big Dipper or Plough). It is a pattern of seven bright stars arranged in a ladle shape. The two stars on the opposite side of the "handle" point upward toward Polaris, the North Star. Polaris sits almost directly above true north.

To find Polaris:
1. Locate the Big Dipper.
2. Find the two outer stars of the "bowl" (the squared part of the ladle).
3. Draw an imaginary line through these two stars upward.
4. Polaris is approximately five times the distance between these two stars, along that line.

Polaris is not the brightest star, but it is distinctive: it is surrounded by much dimmer stars and sits alone at the top of the celestial dome.

#### Step 2: Find the Southern Cross — Southern Hemisphere

The Southern Cross (Crux) is a small constellation visible only south of the equator. It has five stars; the four brightest form a cross.

To find due south:
1. Locate the Southern Cross.
2. Extend an imaginary line along the long axis of the cross downward.
3. Where this line meets the horizon is due south.

The Southern Cross rotates around the South Celestial Pole, similar to how Polaris sits over the north in the Northern Hemisphere. The pointers of the cross (two bright nearby stars) also indicate south if the cross is obscured.

#### Step 3: Use Polaris to Find Your Latitude

The angle of Polaris above the horizon equals your latitude in degrees. Stand facing north and measure the angle of Polaris from the horizon using your hand:
- Your fist at arm's length is about 10 degrees.
- Your hand spread (thumb to pinky) is about 15 degrees.
- A closed fist plus a thumb is about 15 degrees.

If Polaris is 40 fists high, you are at 40 degrees north latitude. This is useful for staying at the same latitude while traveling east-west (following the same climate zone).

### Terrain-Based Navigation

Terrain is the most reliable navigation tool if you can match the land to your memory or a map.

#### Step 1: Find High Ground

Climb the highest hill or mountain in sight. From there, you can see 5–25 km depending on elevation and clarity. Match the ridges, valleys, and peaks you see to your knowledge of the region.

#### Step 2: Use Ridge Lines and Valleys

In mountainous terrain, ridges run north-south or east-west. Water flows downhill into valleys. If you need to travel north and you see a north-running ridge, follow it. If you need to travel downhill toward a river, follow any valley downslope.

Water always flows downhill. In the Northern Hemisphere, streams tend to erode deeper on the right bank (due to Earth's rotation), leaving a shallower left bank. Valleys carved by water point downhill.

#### Step 3: Match Terrain to Memory

If you know roughly where you are and where you are going, sketch or memorize key landmarks:
- "Home is beyond three ridges to the northeast."
- "The river flows from mountains in the west toward the delta in the east."
- "I follow the valley to the flat land, then I look for the distinctive twin peaks."

Every 5–10 km, stop at high ground, reorient, and verify you are on track.

#### Step 4: Estimate Distance by Pacing

Count every double-step (right foot forward = one pace). A typical double-step is 1.5 meters. So 1,000 paces = 1.5 km.

Adjust for terrain: climbing reduces speed; descending increases it. Rough terrain reduces speed by 20–40%.

Practice pacing on known distances (such as your home to a landmark) to calibrate your stride.

### Dead Reckoning: Combining Direction and Distance

Dead reckoning is the art of moving from one known position to another by:
1. Setting a bearing (a direction).
2. Traveling a known distance.
3. Correcting for drift.

#### Step 1: Establish Your Starting Point

You must know (or guess) where you are. This can be "I am near the river" or "I am on the south side of the ridge I climbed yesterday."

#### Step 2: Set a Bearing

Using sun, stars, or terrain, determine the direction you need to travel. Pick a distant landmark (a distinctive tree, peak, or hilltop) in that direction. This is your intermediate waypoint.

#### Step 3: Travel to the Waypoint and Correct

Walk toward the waypoint. Every 1–2 km, stop, reorient using the sun or stars, and check if the landmark you are heading for is still in the same relative direction. If you have drifted, adjust course.

Drift happens because:
- Your footsteps are not perfectly straight.
- Terrain forces you to zigzag around obstacles.
- You unconsciously favor one side (most people veer slightly left).

Expect to drift 10–20 degrees over a few hours.

#### Step 4: Reach the Waypoint and Repeat

Once you reach the waypoint, stop on high ground, pick the next landmark, and repeat. This way, errors compound slowly over long distances but are caught at waypoints.

### Nighttime Navigation When You Cannot See Stars

If clouds obscure the sky:
1. Use terrain navigation (feel the slope, follow water, use the wind).
2. Wind often has a consistent direction—the prevailing wind in most regions shifts only slightly over hours.
3. Listen to water sounds (they indicate downhill direction).
4. Feel the ground (uphill is usually harder under your feet).
5. Wait for stars if possible. Cloud breaks often reveal stars for 10–30 minutes.

If none of these work, stop and shelter. Nighttime navigation in complete darkness without terrain cues risks walking off cliffs or into water.

## Verification

**Sun navigation:**
- You have correctly identified east and west if the sun is where you expect it.
- Your north-south bearing is correct if terrain features you remember are where you expect them.

**Star navigation:**
- Polaris is in the correct position if it is surrounded by much dimmer stars and sits alone at the top of the sky (when you look north).
- Your latitude calculation is correct if the angle matches known latitudes in the region.

**Terrain navigation:**
- You have matched the terrain correctly if you can see the same peaks or ridges from multiple positions and they are in consistent positions relative to each other.

**Dead reckoning:**
- You are on course if you reach waypoints in the expected timeframe (within 10–20% error is acceptable).
- You have drifted too far if terrain does not match memory or a map.

## Common Mistakes

**Confusing magnetic north and true north**: Polaris points to true north. The sun also points to true geographic directions. Magnetic compasses point to the magnetic pole, which is 10–20 degrees offset from true north in many regions. Without a compass, you navigate by true directions (sky and sun), which is actually preferred because magnetic poles shift.

**Using the sun as a constant pointer**: The sun moves. It is highest and most southerly at noon (in the Northern Hemisphere). Before noon, it points east; after noon, it points west. Remember: sunrise is always east, sunset is always west.

**Walking at night without confirming stars**: One wrong star can lead you 10–20 degrees off course. If you are uncertain, wait for a clearer sky or use terrain and wind direction instead.

**Ignoring drift**: Walking straight in wilderness is almost impossible. Expect to drift ±10 degrees per hour. Stop frequently to correct.

**Traveling without waypoints**: Picking a waypoint every 5 km gives you a backup orientation point. Traveling 20 km in a straight line without stopping means errors can accumulate to 200 meters or more.

**Underestimating distance**: Rough terrain is slower than it looks. A ridge that appears 2 km away may take 1.5 hours to reach due to loose rock and steep sections. Always add 30% to estimated time.

**Navigating by memory alone after collapse**: If infrastructure has collapsed, landmarks may have changed (burned buildings, downed trees). Combine memory with sun and terrain to verify. If terrain does not match memory, stop and reassess.
