## Overview

Food scarcity is the greatest threat to survival after water and shelter. A reliable way to preserve today's catch for next month's hunger is the difference between thriving and starving. Preservation extends the season of abundance into the season of scarcity.

This chapter covers five core preservation methods suited to post-collapse conditions: drying, salt-curing, fermenting, smoking, and cool storage. Each method works by stopping the biological processes that cause spoilage—microbial growth, oxidation, and enzymatic decay.

## Prerequisites

- Knowledge of fire-making (Vol 1, Ch 2) for smoking and drying
- Ability to hunt, fish, or forage successfully (Vol 1, Chs 5–6)
- Understanding of basic chemistry: salt, acid, fermentation (see Vol 5: Chemistry for depth)
- Access to common materials: salt, vinegar, smoke, cold storage locations

## Materials & Tools

### Universal (all methods)
- Sharp knife for cutting and processing
- Containers: clay jars, wooden barrels, glass bottles, or sealed boxes
- Salt (critical for salt-curing and fermentation; see Vol 9: Natural Resources for salt harvesting)
- Cloth or netting for wrapping and hanging
- Labels and a system to track what is stored and when

### Specific to method
**Drying:**
- Heat source: sun, fire, or low-temperature oven
- Racks or frames: wood slats, woven branches, or cloth
- Protection from insects: mesh cloth or elevated location

**Salt-curing:**
- Large quantity of salt (15–25% by weight of the food)
- Barrels or boxes lined with salt

**Fermenting:**
- Salt (5–20% by weight of the food)
- Containers that can be sealed but allow gas to escape
- Weight to keep food submerged (rock, ceramic weight, or wooden board)

**Smoking:**
- Smoke source: hardwood fire (see Vol 1, Ch 2 for fire building)
- Smoke chamber: outdoor structure, cave, or enclosed space
- Meat hooks or racks

**Cool storage:**
- Underground cellar or cave with stable temperature (ideally 32–50°F)
- Bins or shelves
- Root vegetables, squashes, or other hardy items

## Part 1: Drying (Longest Shelf Life, 6–12 months)

Drying removes water, which microbes need to grow. Dried foods are lightweight, shelf-stable, and highly concentrated in nutrients.

### Method 1: Sun Drying

**Best for:** Fish, meat, fruit, herbs. Requires consistent warm, dry weather.

**Step 1: Prepare the food.**
- Meat: Slice thin (1/4 inch to 1/2 inch), following the grain. Lean meat dries faster than fatty meat.
- Fish: Gut and split lengthwise, or cut into thick fillets.
- Fruit: Slice or halve, remove pits.
- Herbs: Hang whole or spread on cloth.

**Step 2: Arrange for drying.**
Spread food on clean racks or cloth in a location with full sun and good air circulation. Space pieces so air can flow around them. Elevate racks off the ground to protect from insects and ground moisture.

**Step 3: Monitor daily.**
Turn meat over once daily. Check for mold, insect damage, or spoilage. In humid climates, cover with mesh cloth at night to prevent moisture reabsorption.

**Step 4: Judge doneness.**
Meat is done when it bends but does not snap. It should feel dry, not moist. Fruit is done when leathery and no moisture comes out when squeezed. This takes 3–14 days depending on thickness and humidity.

**Step 5: Store.**
Allow fully dried food to cool completely. Pack in airtight containers in a cool, dark place. Check occasionally for signs of spoilage (mold, off-smells, insects).

### Method 2: Fire Drying

**Best for:** Fish, meat, in any climate.

**Step 1: Build a drying frame.**
Create a rectangular frame 3–4 feet tall from sticks. Cover the top two-thirds with racks (wooden slats or woven branches). Leave the bottom open.

**Step 2: Build a slow fire below.**
Create a small fire beneath the frame. Use hardwood and green wood to generate smoke without excessive heat. The goal is smoke, not heat—the temperature should be warm but not hot (around 100–120°F if you could measure it).

**Step 3: Hang or rack the food.**
Drape meat or fish over racks or suspend from string. Space pieces to allow smoke circulation.

**Step 4: Maintain the fire.**
Keep smoke continuous for 12–24 hours for thin pieces, 24–48 hours for thick pieces. Add wood as needed. Monitor for spoilage.

**Step 5: Judge doneness.**
Meat should be hard, dark, and brittle. A piece should snap easily when bent. This indicates all moisture has been removed.

**Storage:** Dried meat (called "jerky" in some traditions) lasts 6–12 months in a cool, dry place.

## Part 2: Salt-Curing (3–12 months shelf life)

Salt draws water out of food and creates an environment where harmful bacteria cannot grow. This method is fast and reliable but requires significant salt.

### Step 1: Prepare the food.

- **Fish:** Gut and clean. Leave the skin on. Cut into 2–3 inch thick pieces.
- **Meat:** Use a single large cut (a brisket, shoulder, or fish whole if small). Remove excess fat (salt doesn't preserve fat well; it goes rancid).
- **Vegetables:** Peel and cut into uniform size.

### Step 2: Dry-salt or wet-brine.

**Option A: Dry-salt (dry cure)**

1. Layer salt at the bottom of a barrel or crock (1 inch thick).
2. Place a layer of meat or fish (2–3 inches thick).
3. Cover completely with salt (1/2–1 inch thick).
4. Repeat layers until the container is full.
5. Top with 1 inch of salt.
6. Cover loosely. Do not seal—liquids need to escape.

A brine will form as salt draws moisture from the food. The meat sits in its own brine.

**Option B: Wet-brine (brine cure)**

1. Dissolve salt in water in a ratio of 1 part salt to 5 parts water (about 1 cup salt per gallon of water).
2. Place meat or fish in a container.
3. Pour brine to cover completely.
4. Weight the food down with a rock or wooden board so it stays submerged.
5. Cover loosely.

### Step 3: Store and monitor.

Place in a cool location (ideally below 50°F). Check daily for the first week:
- If white mold appears on the surface, skim it off (it is harmless; this is salt crystallization mixed with mold).
- If liquid smells sour or the meat looks slimy, it has spoiled. Discard it.

After the first week, check weekly.

### Step 4: Use.

Before eating, remove the meat and soak in fresh water for 2–4 hours to reduce saltiness. Then cook. Salt-cured food lasts 3–12 months depending on the salt concentration and storage temperature.

## Part 3: Fermentation (Long shelf life, varies by item)

Fermentation uses salt and beneficial bacteria (Lactobacillus) to preserve food while increasing nutrient bioavailability and creating complex flavors.

### Fermented vegetables (sauerkraut, kimchi, pickled vegetables)

**Step 1: Prepare vegetables.**
Chop cabbage, turnips, carrots, or other hard vegetables into bite-sized pieces. Remove any damaged or diseased portions.

**Step 2: Mix with salt.**
Combine vegetables with salt in a ratio of 2–5% salt by weight. For 2 pounds of vegetables, use 1–2 ounces of salt. Mix thoroughly and let sit for 15 minutes.

The salt draws water from the vegetables, creating a brine.

**Step 3: Pack into containers.**
Press vegetables into a clean jar or crock, packing them down firmly. The brine should rise above the vegetables and cover them completely. If not enough brine forms, dissolve more salt in water and pour it over to submerge.

**Step 4: Keep submerged.**
Place a weight (a rock, wooden board, or ceramic weight) on top so the vegetables stay under the brine. Mold grows on surfaces exposed to air.

**Step 5: Allow fermentation.**
Cover loosely (cloth or loose lid, not airtight—gas needs to escape). Leave at room temperature (60–75°F is ideal). Fermentation takes 1–4 weeks depending on temperature and desired sourness. Taste periodically after one week.

**Step 6: Store.**
Once fermented to your liking, seal and store in a cool place. Fermented vegetables last 6–12 months or longer.

### Fermented fish and meat

Fish and meat fermentation is more complex and requires careful attention to prevent harmful bacteria.

**Method: Fish sauce (garum)**

1. Layer small fish (like anchovies) with salt in a ratio of 4:1 salt to fish.
2. Press down firmly in a sealed container.
3. Leave in a warm place (65–80°F) for 1–3 months. The fish will liquefy.
4. Strain the liquid. This is fish sauce—a pungent, nutrient-dense condiment.

This method is high-risk; improper salting or temperature can result in harmful pathogenic bacteria. Use only if you understand the risks.

## Part 4: Smoking (2–6 months shelf life)

Smoking combines drying with antimicrobial compounds from smoke, creating preserved food with a distinctive flavor.

### Step 1: Prepare the smokehouse.

Build or use an existing structure (a barrel, cave, or wooden shed) with a small opening at the bottom for smoke entry and ventilation holes at the top. The smoke should move horizontally through the food, not rise straight up.

### Step 2: Prepare the food.

- **Fish:** Gut, split lengthwise, and lightly salt. Let salt penetrate for 4–12 hours.
- **Meat:** Cut into uniform 2–3 inch thick pieces and lightly salt.
- **Sausages:** Make from ground meat mixed with salt and spices (if available). Stuff into casings (animal intestines or plant fiber tubes).

### Step 3: Hang food in the smokehouse.

Suspend meat or fish on hooks or racks. Space so smoke reaches all surfaces.

### Step 4: Generate smoke.

Build a small, low fire using hardwood (oak, hickory, or similar). Green wood produces more smoke. Avoid softwood (pine) which produces resinous, bitter smoke. Keep the fire small and cool (avoid heat above 100°F). Smoke should be thin and blue, not thick and white.

### Step 5: Smoke continuously.

Maintain smoke for 12–48 hours depending on thickness of meat and desired flavor. Thick pieces require longer smoking. Check that meat is not cooking (it should not be hot).

### Step 6: Cool and store.

Remove from smoke and let cool completely. Store in a cool, dry place. Smoked meat lasts 2–6 months.

## Part 5: Cool Storage (1–8 months, depending on item)

Some foods preserve naturally in cool, dark, dry conditions. This method requires no processing but depends on temperature control.

### Suitable foods

- **Root vegetables:** Potatoes, turnips, carrots, beets, onions, garlic (last 4–8 months)
- **Squashes:** Winter squash, pumpkins (last 2–4 months)
- **Apples and hard fruits:** Last 2–4 months
- **Nuts and seeds:** Last 6–12 months (keep dry)
- **Grains and legumes:** Last 1+ years if kept dry and pest-free

### Storage conditions

- **Temperature:** 32–50°F is ideal. Warmer = faster spoilage. Freezing stops spoilage but reduces some nutrient quality.
- **Humidity:** 70–90% prevents shriveling but risks mold. Balance is critical.
- **Light:** Complete darkness. Light causes sprouting and nutrient loss.
- **Ventilation:** Good air circulation prevents mold. Ensure airflow around stored items.
- **Pest control:** Keep stored food elevated and protected from rodents and insects.

### Method

1. Choose unblemished items. Remove any that show signs of damage or disease.
2. Layer in bins or on shelves with straw or wood shavings between items to prevent contact.
3. Keep separate bins for different items (potatoes in one bin, squashes in another) so spoilage doesn't spread.
4. Check monthly and remove any items showing mold, rot, or sprouting.

## Verification

Your preservation is successful if:

1. **No spoilage:** After storage, food shows no mold, slimy growth, foul odor, or discoloration.
2. **Proper texture:** Dried food is hard and dry. Cured food is firm. Fermented food is crisp and tangy. Stored vegetables are unblemished.
3. **Taste:** Flavors are preserved or improved (fermented items develop complex flavors). No rancid, sour, or off-tastes.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Insufficient salt:** Curing requires 15–25% salt by weight. Less salt = spoilage.
2. **Incomplete submersion:** In fermentation and brine curing, exposed food grows mold and spoils.
3. **High temperature in storage:** Warm storage speeds spoilage exponentially. Even 10°F warmer cuts shelf life in half.
4. **Not drying completely:** Partially dried food grows mold. Dry completely or it will rot.
5. **Improper smoke:** Too hot and you cook the food instead of smoking it. Meat should never be hot during smoking.
6. **Mixing old and new:** Once stored food begins to spoil, it can contaminate nearby items. Monitor and remove spoiled items immediately.
