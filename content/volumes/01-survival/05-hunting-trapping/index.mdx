## Overview

Protein acquisition determines your population growth after collapse. The quickest path is passive trapping—setting snares and deadfalls that work while you tend shelter and crops. This chapter covers five trap types, placement strategy, and field processing. Active hunting (bow, spear) is secondary to trapping because a trap kills while you work. A well-placed deadfall can feed two people for three days; a single hunt can fail entirely. After you have snares working reliably, then develop hunting as a backup.

Trapping is not random. Animals follow paths, follow water, follow food. Learn to read sign (see Vol 2, Ch 1), then place traps in those corridors. Success comes from patience and observation, not luck.

## Materials & Tools

### Deadfall Trap (Requires: logs, trigger stick, bait)
- Two logs, 10–15 cm diameter, 1.2–1.5 meters long
- One trigger stick, 15–20 cm long, 5 cm diameter
- Rock or additional log for weight (must be able to crush skull or spine in one impact)
- Cordage or natural fiber to set trigger (jute, bark strips, sinew)
- Bait: offal from recent kill, rotten meat, or seasonal plant material

### Snare Loop (Requires: cordage, trigger, bait)
- Snare wire or twisted fiber cordage, 3–6 mm diameter, 1–2 meters long
- Trigger stake or toggle stick, 20 cm long
- Tree or brush to anchor
- Bait (if using bait snare) or evidence of fresh animal path for blind snare

### Pit Trap (Requires: digging, sharpened stakes, covering material)
- Pit 1.5–2.0 meters deep, dug along animal trail
- 6–12 sharpened stakes, 2–3 cm diameter, 30–50 cm above pit floor (angled inward)
- Covering material: branches, grass, dirt — covering must hold weight of small deer
- Bait scent or bait hung above pit (optional, dangerous if trap operator misjudges)

### Bow and Arrow (Requires: wood, cordage, feathers, sharpened point)
- Bow: ash, elm, or yew wood, 1.2–1.5 meters, 40–60 lbf draw weight minimum
- Arrow shaft: 65 cm long, 5–8 mm diameter, straight wood or cane
- Arrowhead: flint, bone, or sharpened wood (see Vol 1, Ch 9; Vol 2, Ch 4)
- Bowstring: twisted plant fiber, sinew, or gut
- Quiver or bundle: 20+ arrows minimum for hunt

### Field Dressing Kit
- Sharp blade (see Rope & Knots or Primitive Tools chapters)
- Containers for offal and organs (can be animal bladder or leather pouch)
- Fire for singeing hide (remove guard hairs)
- Cloth or leaves for wrapping

**Scavengeable items:** Large logs (from fallen trees), stones, sharp obsidian or flint, plant fiber.
**Must-produce items:** Cordage (twisted plant fiber, bark, or processed sinew), bow and arrows.

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Choose Your Target

Animal weight vs. effort to kill and process:

- **Rabbit/Hare** (1.5–2.5 kg): Minimal effort, too small for active hunting. Worth 500–800 kcal dressed. Snares and deadfalls only.
- **Squirrel/Groundhog** (0.5–3 kg): Snares, deadfalls. Easy processing, 800–1200 kcal.
- **Deer** (30–80 kg): Highest return per kill (30,000–60,000 kcal). Requires pit trap, careful stalking, or group drive. Process takes 4–6 hours.
- **Pig/Boar** (20–100 kg): 20,000–50,000 kcal. Dangerous when wounded. Pit trap or spear. Takes 5–7 hours to process.
- **Waterfowl** (1–3 kg): 600–1400 kcal. Snares around water, or shot with bow. Quick processing.

**Rule of thumb:** Start with rabbits and groundhogs. Master snares on them first. Then graduate to deer when you understand animal movement patterns.

### Step 2: Scout the Location

Walk the terrain for 2–3 days before setting traps. Look for:

- **Animal trails** (scat, prints, browse line on vegetation)
- **Feeding areas** (stripped bark, overturned soil, visible cropping)
- **Water crossings** (compressed earth, mud, animal tracks converging)
- **Escape routes** (if cornering animals, know where they bolt to)

Mark the path with inconspicuous cuts in bark or stones (not obvious to humans, visible to you on repeat visits). Never place a trap in a trail you cannot return to safely and repeatedly.

### Step 3: Construct a Deadfall Trap

**Location:** Place deadfall at the entrance to a thicket, burrow, or along a narrow animal trail.

**Step 3a: Position the logs.**
Lay one log perpendicular to the animal path (blocking it). Position the second log parallel above it, supported by the trigger stick. The gap between logs should be just large enough for the target animal to crawl under—typically 10–15 cm for rabbit-sized prey, 20–30 cm for groundhog.

**Step 3b: Install the trigger.**
Insert the trigger stick under the upper log so it supports the weight. The trigger should:
- Lean against a bait post or hanging bait (rope or stick tied to bait)
- Be loose enough that a struggling animal will dislodge it
- Be solid enough that wind or rain won't trigger it (use a weight stone on the trigger if needed)

**Step 3c: Prepare bait.**
If trapping herbivores (rabbit, groundhog), use a piece of fresh vegetable or apple. If trapping carnivores (fox, marten), use fresh meat offal (liver, kidneys). Hang bait from a stick or rope 5 cm in front of the trigger. Make bait too fragrant to ignore and impossible to reach without pulling the trigger.

**Step 3d: Disguise the setup.**
Place dried grass, leaves, and soil around the trap. Do not disturb the path itself—animals are sensitive to changes. Approach the trap from downwind when checking it.

**Trigger sensitivity:** A properly set deadfall will kill on impact (crushing cervical spine or skull). If the animal is only trapped and thrashing, the trigger failed. Redesign: move the trigger closer to the bait, increase the weight stone, or lower the upper log slightly.

### Step 4: Construct a Snare Loop

**Location:** Along a trail at the height of the animal's head (rabbit: 5–10 cm above ground; groundhog: 8–12 cm).

**Step 4a: Anchor the snare.**
Drive a stake deeply into the ground (at least 30 cm) at one edge of the trail, or tie the snare cord around a low tree branch. The anchor must be immovable.

**Step 4b: Form the loop.**
Create a loop 8–10 cm diameter for rabbit, 12–15 cm for groundhog. The loop should hang vertically and slightly forward into the path. Use a running knot (barrel knot or figure-eight loop) so the loop tightens when the animal thrashes.

**Step 4c: Set the toggle stick (optional).**
If using a toggle stick (a 15 cm stick tied to the snare), the animal will catch the snare on its neck, pull the toggle, and the toggle will catch on brush or rocks, trapping the animal in place. This prevents the animal from fleeing with the snare or tangling around trees.

**Step 4d: Camouflage.**
Cover the snare cord with dirt or grass. Leave the loop opening clear and natural-looking.

**Snare diameter rule:** The loop should fit tightly over the animal's neck when the animal's head is at the level of the loop. Too large and the snare won't tighten quickly; too small and it won't catch.

### Step 5: Construct a Pit Trap

**Location:** Along a heavily used trail, preferably in a narrow corridor (between brush, rocks, or trees) where the animal cannot step around.

**Step 5a: Dig the pit.**
Dig 1.5–2.0 meters deep. Wider is easier (1.0–1.5 meters wide) so the animal cannot escape on the way down. Sides should be slightly undercut (not vertical) so escape is impossible.

**Step 5b: Prepare stakes.**
Cut 6–12 pointed stakes from strong wood (hawthorn, ash, hazel). Points should be 5–10 cm long and very sharp. Sharpen in fire to harden the point (hold stick 15 cm from flame for 10–15 seconds, rotating). Do not char through the wood (char reduces strength). Angle the stakes inward at 30–45 degrees so their points cross in the center of the pit.

**Step 5c: Install covering.**
Lay poles or branches across the top of the pit. Cover with grass and 5–10 cm of soil to hide the pit. The covering must hold the weight of the target animal without breaking (test with your own weight if safe). For deer-sized animals, use thick poles with multiple layers.

**Step 5d: Mark the location.**
Drive a small stick or pile three rocks at a clear distance from the pit (3–5 meters downwind) so you know the exact location if you approach in darkness. Never forget where your pit is—stepping into your own trap is a realistic risk.

**Warning:** Pit traps are effective but dangerous to the operator. They require daylight or excellent memory to avoid. Only use in areas you can revisit safely, and only in controlled territory where unexpected visitors won't fall in.

### Step 6: Active Hunting with a Bow

**Step 6a: Practice your draw.**
Before hunting, practice shooting 20 arrows daily for at least one month. You need to:
- Draw the bow smoothly and repeatably (aim for 2–3 second draw time)
- Release without flinching (your fingers should relax, not snap)
- Group arrows within 20 cm at 20 meters
- Know your effective kill range (usually 15–25 meters for a 50 lbf bow)

**Step 6b: Scout upwind.**
Move slowly and quietly, always approaching animals from downwind. Stop every 5–10 meters to listen and observe. Most animals smell you before they see you. If you encounter an animal, freeze and wait. Many animals have poor vision in stationary objects.

**Step 6c: Aim for vital organs.**
The kill zones are:
- **Heart/lungs:** Arrow enters behind the front leg, 10–15 cm up from the belly. This is the largest target and most reliable kill. The animal may run 20–50 meters before falling.
- **Spine:** Harder to hit, but kills instantly if successful.

Never aim for the head—arrow penetration is unreliable and the animal often escapes wounded.

**Step 6d: After the shot, wait.**
Do not immediately pursue. Wait 30 minutes before tracking. The animal will circle and bed down to rest; pursuing immediately will drive it further. Follow the blood trail at ground level.

### Step 7: Field Dressing

**Step 7a: Position the animal.**
Lay the animal on its back, hind legs pointing away from you. For large game, you may need rope to position it.

**Step 7b: Open the cavity.**
Cut from the sternum (breastbone) down to the pelvis, cutting carefully to avoid puncturing the stomach or intestines. Cut 2–3 cm deep (only through skin and muscle). 

**Step 7c: Remove the organs.**
Reach in and pull the stomach, intestines, and liver out. Keep the liver—it is an organ meat, rich in vitamins. The liver should be dark and firm; if it is pale or mushy, discard it (disease). Work quickly; exposed organs spoil in warm weather. In cold weather (below 10°C), you can leave the cavity open for 24 hours without spoilage.

**Step 7d: Cool the carcass.**
Wipe the cavity clean with grass or cloth. If weather is warm (above 15°C), skin the animal and butcher it at the kill site, or drag the unskinned carcass to a shaded location near water. Do not transport in a sealed bundle—air circulation prevents spoilage.

**Step 7e: Butcher into sections.**
Working with a knife, separate the hind legs, front legs, and backstrap (loin). For a deer, this yields 4 large sections, each 3–8 kg. Remove the meat from the bones. Keep the bones for bone tools (Vol 1, Ch 9) or for bone stock (simmer for 12+ hours to extract collagen and nutrients).

**Processing time:** 
- Rabbit: 15–20 minutes
- Groundhog: 30–40 minutes
- Deer: 3–4 hours (if done alone)

**Meat yield (percentage of live weight):**
- Rabbit: 70%
- Groundhog: 65%
- Deer: 55–65%

### Step 8: Preserve the Meat

After processing, meat must be:
- **Dried** (see Vol 1, Ch 4: Food Preservation)
- **Smoked** (see Vol 3, Ch 12: Food Science & Fermentation)
- **Salted** (see Vol 3, Ch 12)
- **Stored in cold** (winter only, or in a root cellar)

In warm weather, meat spoils in 4–6 hours without preservation. Process immediately after killing.

## Verification

A successful trap or hunt should result in:

**Deadfall:** Dead or fatally injured animal trapped under the logs, with minimal suffering. The animal may struggle briefly, but should stop within 1–2 minutes if spine or skull is crushed.

**Snare:** Dead or strangled animal, suspended or tangled in snare, with no signs of injury except around the neck. If the animal escaped the snare, your loop was too large or trigger was too loose.

**Pit:** Dead animal at the pit floor, impaled on stakes or with injuries from the fall. Inspect carefully—a wounded but mobile animal may require a second spear or arrow to finish.

**Bow hunt:** Dead animal found within 50 meters of the shot location, with an arrow lodged in the chest cavity. If the animal is alive, approach carefully from behind and use a spear or second arrow to finish it.

If you find a wounded animal that escaped, this is failure. It means:
- Deadfall: trigger was too sensitive or bait placement was poor
- Snare: loop was too loose or animal had enough strength to break cordage
- Pit: stakes were not sharp enough or properly angled
- Bow: arrow was too light, target was too far away, or aim was poor

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Scent contamination.**
Humans have strong odor. If you approach a trap or scout location without accounting for wind, animals will detect you and avoid the area for days. Always approach upwind. Wash hands in water or soil before handling traps. Do not talk or smoke near the trap location.

**Mistake 2 — Trigger too sensitive.**
A deadfall or snare triggered by wind, rain, or small animals wastes bait and teaches animals to avoid the trap. A proper trigger should require deliberate contact and sustained pressure (at least 2 seconds). Test your trigger by pushing gently—it should not release until you push with moderate force.

**Mistake 3 — Poor bait placement.**
Bait too close to the trigger will kill the animal before it reaches the trap mechanism. Bait too far away and the animal won't investigate. The animal should have to fully commit to reaching the trigger before eating bait. For deadfalls, hang bait 5 cm forward of the trigger stick. For snares, bait should be 10–15 cm from the loop.

**Mistake 4 — Checking traps too often.**
Every time you visit, you leave human scent and disturb the area. Check traps daily if possible, but not more than once every two days. Mark your approach route so you do not wander and compress the trail.

**Mistake 5 — Setting traps in declining animal populations.**
If you see sign of an animal once and never again, that animal has moved through or is rare in that area. Do not trap areas with no regular animal traffic. Spend 2–3 days observing before investing labor in trap construction.

**Mistake 6 — Bow hunting without sufficient practice.**
Most novice bow hunters miss. A miss is a wounded animal and wasted effort. Spend one month practicing before attempting a hunt. The first kill should be at under 20 meters distance on a calm day.

**Mistake 7 — Leaving meat in the field.**
Insects, scavengers, and spoilage bacteria will destroy meat in hours if left unprocessed. Always process immediately after the kill, even if processing continues into darkness. Use fire or torch light if needed.

**Mistake 8 — Not understanding body temperature timing.**
Warm-blooded animals with high body temperature (37–40°C) spoil faster than you might expect. In hot weather (above 20°C), meat has only 4 hours before spoilage begins. If you kill an animal and cannot process it immediately, hang it in shade and open the cavity to cool the core. If you kill late in the day, process by firelight and dry the meat overnight.
