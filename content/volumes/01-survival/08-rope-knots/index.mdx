## Overview

A rope and knowledge of knots is a fundamental survival tool. Strong, reliable knots hold your shelter against wind, secure your tools, create hunting traps, and can save your life in an emergency. A single knot tied incorrectly can collapse your shelter or lose your food. This chapter teaches ten essential knots ranked by importance and difficulty.

The knots in this chapter are selected for survival conditions: they work with wet, worn, or improvised cordage; they hold under load; they can be tied and untied quickly; and they do not require special tools.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of rope properties (see Vol 5: Materials)
- Ability to recognize when a knot is secure
- Practice time (these knots require physical practice, not just reading)
- Access to cordage (natural fiber, paracord, or improvised rope)

## Materials & Tools

### Essential
- Rope or cordage (1/4 to 1/2 inch diameter is ideal; paracord, sisal, hemp, or plant-fiber rope all work)
- Practice materials: different rope types, wet rope, old rope, improvised cordage from bark or vine
- Reference: drawings or memory of knot sequences

### Optional
- Carabiner (for safety, in climbing contexts; not required for survival)
- Fuse or tape (to seal rope ends and prevent unraveling)

## Part 1: Understanding Rope

Before tying knots, understand rope structure and behavior.

### Rope parts

A knot consists of:
- **Standing end:** The long part of the rope that bears weight
- **Working end:** The short part of the rope used to tie the knot
- **Loop (bight):** A U-shaped bend in the rope
- **Turn (wrap):** When the rope wraps around itself or an object
- **Standing line:** The rope segment not involved in the knot

### Rope properties

- **Strength:** Dry rope is stronger than wet rope. New rope is stronger than old rope. Thicker rope is stronger than thin rope.
- **Grip:** Rough rope (hemp, sisal) grips better than smooth rope (nylon). Wet rope can slip.
- **Stretch:** Natural rope stretches slightly. Synthetic rope may stretch more.
- **Failure modes:** Rope can break under shock load, slip at the knot, or fail if the knot is tied incorrectly.

### How knots fail

- **Slipping:** The knot unties because the rope slides through the knot. This happens with smooth rope or too few wraps.
- **Capsizing:** The knot inverts under load and becomes a weaker knot. This happens when the knot is not dressed correctly.
- **Breaking:** The rope breaks at the knot. This happens under shock load or if the rope is damaged.

To avoid failure:
- Tie the knot correctly (right number of wraps, correct sequence)
- Dress the knot (tighten it so all rope segments lie flat and parallel)
- Test the knot before trusting it with weight
- Inspect the rope before use (no frays, cuts, or weak spots)

## Part 2: Ten Essential Knots

### Knot 1: Overhand Knot (Basis for many knots)

**Use:** Prevents rope from unraveling. Used as the basis for other knots.

**Difficulty:** Trivial

**How to tie:**

1. Make a loop with the working end of the rope.
2. Thread the working end through the loop.
3. Pull tight.

**When to use:** Any time you need a simple stopper knot. Always practice this first.

### Knot 2: Square Knot (Joins two ropes of equal thickness)

**Use:** Joins two ropes together. Reliable under moderate load.

**Difficulty:** Easy

**How to tie:**

1. Hold one rope in each hand.
2. Cross the right rope over the left rope.
3. Wrap the right rope around the left rope and pull through.
4. Now cross the left rope over the right rope (this is the reverse of step 2).
5. Wrap the left rope around the right rope and pull through.
6. Pull both rope ends to tighten.

**Remember:** "Right over left, left over right." The two sequences must be reversed or the knot will slip.

**When to use:** Joining cordage to lash shelter components. Not suitable for high-impact loads (use the Fisherman's Knot instead).

### Knot 3: Bowline (Creates a permanent loop)

**Use:** Creates a loop that will not slip or tighten under load. One of the most reliable knots.

**Difficulty:** Moderate

**How to tie:**

1. Make a small loop in the working end (the "rabbit hole").
2. Thread the working end up through the loop from below.
3. Wrap the working end around the standing line.
4. Thread the working end back down through the loop.
5. Pull tight.

**Mnemonic:** "The rabbit comes out of the hole, around the tree, and back down the hole."

**When to use:** Creating a fixed anchor point. Tying yourself to a rope. Any situation where you need a loop that will not slip.

### Knot 4: Clove Hitch (Wraps around a post or beam)

**Use:** Secures a rope to a post, beam, or branch. Fast to tie and untie.

**Difficulty:** Easy

**How to tie:**

1. Wrap the rope around the post (one complete wrap).
2. Cross the working end over the standing line.
3. Wrap the rope around the post again (a second wrap).
4. Tuck the working end under the second wrap.
5. Pull tight.

**When to use:** Lashing beams together. Securing a rope to a fixed object. Tying off a rope when you need quick adjustment.

### Knot 5: Trucker's Hitch (Creates a movable pulley)

**Use:** Provides mechanical advantage (2:1) to tighten a load or move something heavy.

**Difficulty:** Moderate

**How to tie:**

1. Tie a Bowline in the middle of the rope (this creates your pulley).
2. Thread the working end around a fixed point (a post or anchor).
3. Thread the working end back through the Bowline loop.
4. Pull the working end to tension. The Bowline acts as a movable pulley.
5. Tie off the working end with a Clove Hitch around the standing line.

**When to use:** Tightening a shelter's ridgeline. Securing a heavy load. Providing mechanical advantage when manual strength alone is insufficient.

### Knot 6: Fisherman's Knot (Joins two ropes securely)

**Use:** Joins two ropes of different thicknesses or materials. More reliable than the Square Knot under stress.

**Difficulty:** Moderate

**How to tie:**

1. Lay the two ropes parallel, overlapping.
2. Tie an Overhand Knot with the first rope around the second rope (the second rope acts as the standing line).
3. Tie an Overhand Knot with the second rope around the first rope.
4. Pull both ropes to tighten. The two knots should jam against each other.

**When to use:** Joining cordage of different thicknesses. Any permanent rope join under high load.

### Knot 7: Taut-Line Hitch (Slides but locks under load)

**Use:** Creates a loop that can slide along a rope but locks when under load. Ideal for adjustable anchor points.

**Difficulty:** Moderate

**How to tie:**

1. Make two wraps around the standing line with the working end, starting near the standing line.
2. Make a third wrap around the standing line, but pass the working end through the loop created by the third wrap (this creates the lock).
3. Pull the standing line and working end to tighten.

**When to use:** Adjusting shelter guy-lines. Creating a loop that can slide until tensioned, then locks in place.

### Knot 8: Timber Hitch (Secures loads or logs)

**Use:** Grips a load (log, bundle, pole) without slipping. Can be quickly tied and untied.

**Difficulty:** Easy

**How to tie:**

1. Wrap the working end around the load (one wrap).
2. Wrap the working end around the standing line in a figure-eight pattern.
3. Pull tight. The wraps should grip the load.

**When to use:** Securing a log to carry. Bundling firewood. Any situation where you need to grip a cylindrical load.

### Knot 9: Sheet Bend (Joins ropes of different thicknesses)

**Use:** Joins a thin rope to a thick rope. Faster than the Fisherman's Knot but less reliable under shock load.

**Difficulty:** Easy

**How to tie:**

1. Form a loop with the thick rope (the standing end).
2. Thread the thin rope through the loop from below.
3. Wrap the thin rope around both parts of the thick rope loop.
4. Tuck the working end back under the thin rope.
5. Pull tight.

**When to use:** Quick joins of different rope thicknesses. Less critical applications where shock load is not expected.

### Knot 10: Reef Knot (Joins two ropes, alternative to Square Knot)

**Use:** Alternative to the Square Knot. Slightly more secure under certain conditions.

**Difficulty:** Easy

**How to tie:**

1. Lay the two ropes parallel.
2. Make an Overhand Knot on the right side.
3. Make an Overhand Knot on the left side.
4. Pull tight.

**When to use:** Joining cordage when you need redundancy. Some people find this more reliable than the Square Knot.

## Part 3: Advanced Techniques

### Dressing a knot

"Dressing" means tightening and aligning the knot so all parts are parallel and tight.

1. After tying, pull on both the standing line and working end.
2. Inspect the knot: all wraps should be parallel, no crossing or twisting.
3. If the knot has twisted or capsized, untie and retie.

A properly dressed knot looks neat and symmetric.

### Sealing rope ends

Frayed rope ends can come untied. Seal them by:

1. **Fusing:** If the rope is synthetic (nylon, polypropylene), carefully melt the end over a small flame. The melted plastic seals.
2. **Tying an Overhand Knot:** Tie a knot at the end to prevent unraveling.
3. **Wrapping:** Wrap the end tightly with thread, tape, or cloth.

### Testing a knot

Before trusting a knot with significant load:

1. Pull the knot sharply and suddenly (shock test).
2. Pull steadily and hard (sustained load test).
3. Inspect for slipping or capsizing.
4. If the knot holds, it is likely safe.

## Verification

A secure knot should:

1. **Not slip** under sustained load.
2. **Not capsize** (change shape under load).
3. **Be dressable** (tighten into a neat, symmetric shape).
4. **Hold** when the rope is wet, worn, or under shock load.

Test each knot multiple times before relying on it in survival situations.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Insufficient wraps:** Most failures result from too few wraps around the load or anchoring point. Add more wraps if unsure.
2. **Not dressing the knot:** A loose, twisted knot can slip. Always tighten and align the knot after tying.
3. **Using the wrong knot:** Each knot is optimized for a specific purpose. Using the wrong knot (e.g., a Square Knot for a permanent load) can fail.
4. **Trusting a knot without testing:** Always test a new knot under load before relying on it.
5. **Not securing the working end:** After tying, the working end can slip out. Always tie an Overhand Knot at the very end.
6. **Using slippery rope:** Smooth synthetic rope (polypropylene) can slip. Use rougher rope (hemp, sisal) when possible.
7. **Over-tightening:** Some knots (Fisherman's, Taut-Line Hitch) rely on wraps to lock. Over-tightening can prevent the locking mechanism from working.
