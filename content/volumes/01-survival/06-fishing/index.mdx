## Overview

Fish are the fastest renewable protein source in post-collapse survival. A single trap set overnight can feed a family for a day. Hand-line fishing catches fish in minutes. Unlike hunting, which requires luck and athleticism, fishing rewards patience and position.

This chapter covers four methods for different water conditions: hand-line for flowing water and lakes, weirs for streams, traps for deep pools, and spears for shallow water. Choose by what water you have access to.

## Materials & Tools

### Hand-Line Fishing

- **Hooks:** Bone (carved from leg bone), wood (split willow with barb), or salvaged steel
- **Line:** Plant fiber cordage (see Vol 3, Ch 8), sinew, or twisted plant fiber — must be at least 20 kg breaking strength
- **Rod (optional):** Flexible sapling, 2–3 meters long (makes casting easier, not required)
- **Bait:** Insects (grubs, flies, ants), worms, small fish, or scraps of meat

### Weir (Fish Trap)

- **Sticks:** Flexible willow or alder, 1–2 cm diameter, 1–2 meters long (20–30 pieces)
- **Binding cord:** Plant fiber (see Vol 3, Ch 8), 2–3 mm diameter
- **Funnel entrance material:** Smaller sticks or woven plant material
- **Rocks or weights:** To anchor the weir to the stream bed
- **Trap box (optional):** Woven basket or wooden box at the weir's exit

### Net Fishing

- **Net material:** Plant fiber (flax, hemp), animal fiber (hair), or salvaged net
- **Frame:** Flexible wood, ash or willow, 1–2 meters diameter
- **Weights:** Rocks or lead (salvaged)
- **Floats (optional):** Bark bundles, sealed gourds, or cork

### Spear Fishing

- **Shaft:** Ash or spruce sapling, 2–3 meters long, 3 cm diameter
- **Prongs:** Bone or sharpened wood, 4–6 prongs for larger fish
- **Binding material:** Sinew or cordage
- **Light source (night fishing):** Torch or fire basket for attracting fish

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Method 1: Hand-Line Fishing

**Step 1: Locate fish.**
Look for deep pools in streams, eddies behind boulders, undercut banks (where banks overhang the water), and the deeper sections of lakes. Fish hold in these places to conserve energy. Shadows indicate depth. Fish in moving water face upstream.

**Step 2: Make a bone hook.**
Split a bone lengthwise. Carve a hook shape from the inside edge — the curve should close to a point to snag the fish's mouth. Alternatively, tie two small bones perpendicular (90° angle) with cordage: one vertical, one horizontal. The horizontal piece becomes the barb.

**Step 3: Attach hook to line.**
Use a clinch knot (see Vol 3, Ch 8) to attach the hook securely. Test the knot by pulling hard — it should not slip.

**Step 4: Attach bait.**
Thread the hook through a grub, grasshopper, or small worm — or tie on a bait fish by the tail with light cordage through its gill covers.

**Step 5: Present the bait.**
In a stream: cast upstream of where you see or expect fish. Let the bait drift naturally downstream. In a lake: cast and retrieve with slow, jerking motions to mimic an injured fish. Keep tension on the line so you feel a bite.

**Step 6: Hook the fish.**
When you feel a tug, jerk the rod (or line) upward sharply to drive the hook home. Pull steadily without jerking — jerking breaks the line or hook.

**Step 7: Land the fish.**
Reel it in hand-over-hand if using a rod, or pull slowly by hand. Keep the line tight. If the fish is large (over 2 kg), lead it to shore slowly; a large fish will tire quickly if you do not let it tire.

### Method 2: Building a Weir

**Step 1: Scout the stream.**
Find a narrow section 1–3 meters across with steady flow. The water should be 0.5–1.5 meters deep on at least one side. Avoid locations above waterfalls (fish cannot swim up).

**Step 2: Drive stakes.**
Hammer wooden stakes (2–3 cm diameter saplings sharpened at the base) into the stream bed in a V or funnel shape: the opening faces upstream, the point faces downstream. Stakes should be 30 cm apart, driven 30–50 cm deep into the bed. The V should span the full width of the stream.

**Step 3: Weave the weir wall.**
Lash flexible sticks (willow or alder) between the stakes with cordage. Weave them tightly — there should be no gaps larger than 1 cm. As fish swim downstream, they cannot escape around the sides.

**Step 4: Build the funnel entrance.**
At the upstream end of the V, create a funnel leading into a narrow trap area. Arrange sticks so fish can easily swim in but cannot find their way back out. The funnel mouth should be 30–50 cm wide; the exit should be 10–15 cm wide.

**Step 5: Create a collection box.**
At the downstream end of the funnel, place a woven basket or construct a wooden box (see Vol 17, Ch 2) with an opening where the funnel exits. Alternatively, pound the streambed into a shallow depression to collect trapped fish.

**Step 6: Anchor with rocks.**
Place heavy rocks on both upstream and downstream sides of the weir to prevent the current from sweeping it away.

**Step 7: Harvest daily.**
Check the trap every morning and evening. Remove fish and reset if necessary. The weir remains functional for weeks if not damaged.

### Method 3: Hand Net or Casting Net

**Step 1: Construct a hand net frame.**
Bend a flexible branch into a circle 1–2 meters in diameter (depends on water depth). Lash the circle closed with cordage.

**Step 2: Attach the net bag.**
Weave or knit plant fiber netting (see Vol 16, Ch 5) to create a bag 1–2 meters deep. Attach the netting to the circular frame with sinew or cordage — secure points every 10 cm around the frame.

**Step 3: Attach handles.**
Lash two handles (branches or cordage) at opposite points on the frame to make the net easy to swing.

**Step 4: Net fish in shallow water.**
Wade to knee depth in clear water where you can see fish (usually morning or evening). Position the net in front of a fish, then sweep it in a quick scooping motion to trap the fish inside. Twist the handles to close the net mouth.

**Step 5: For deeper water, use a cast net instead.**
A cast net requires more skill: throw it like a frisbee so it spreads in a circle, sinks, and traps fish underneath. Weight the edges so it sinks fast. This method takes practice; the hand net is more reliable for beginners.

### Method 4: Spear Fishing

**Step 1: Make or sharpen the spear head.**
Carve or split a wooden shaft into 4–6 prongs (for large fish, 2–3 prongs; for small fish, 4–6). Sharpen each prong to a point and barb (carve a small notch backward on each prong so a fish cannot slide off). Hardwood (oak, ash) holds a point longer than soft wood.

Alternatively, lash sharpened bones to the shaft with sinew, spacing them 5–10 cm apart.

**Step 2: Lash the head to the shaft.**
Use strong cordage or sinew (see Vol 3, Ch 8). Wrap the binding around the shaft 5 cm below the prongs, then around the prongs themselves, creating a tight bundle. Test by striking hard against a rock — the head should not loosen.

**Step 3: Balance the spear.**
The spear should be easy to hold and throw. Test by holding it at the midpoint — it should feel balanced, not tip-heavy.

**Step 4: Find fish in shallow water.**
Spear fishing works in water less than 1 meter deep. Wade slowly upstream and look for fish in clear water. Morning light at a low angle makes spotting easier.

**Step 5: Strike quickly and decisively.**
Approach the fish from behind (fish do not see well directly behind). Once you are within striking distance (1–2 meters), thrust the spear downward and slightly forward in a single quick motion. Aim for the body, not the head.

**Step 6: Pin the fish.**
Once you have struck, lean on the spear to pin the fish to the bottom. Do not lift the spear head — keep it pressed down until the fish stops moving.

**Step 7: Retrieve the fish.**
Lift the spear and fish together. If the fish is large, hold it firmly while you dispatch it with a blow to the head.

## Verification

**Hand-line fishing worked if:** You felt a bite, hooked the fish, and landed it. A single hand-line in a good spot catches 1–3 fish per hour.

**Weir worked if:** Fish entered the funnel and were trapped overnight. Weirs typically catch 2–5 fish per night in productive streams.

**Net worked if:** The net spread properly and you scooped fish without tearing the net. A hand net catches 0–3 fish per hour depending on density.

**Spear worked if:** You struck the fish and it remained on the prongs until you reached shore.

**Signs of failure:**
- Hook breaking: bone hooks are fragile; steel is better
- Fish escaping after hook: the hook did not set, or the line broke
- Weir damaged overnight: the current was too strong; reinforce with more rocks
- Net tearing: the net material was too weak; use thicker plant fiber

## Common Mistakes

- **Fishing in the wrong spots.** Fish concentrate in deep water, behind obstacles, and in shade. Moving water above and below obstacles is best.
- **Using bait fish head-first.** The hook should go through the small of the back or through the gill covers, not the head. Head-first bait spins in the water.
- **Weir too loose.** If water flows through gaps, fish escape. Weave tightly and check daily for damage.
- **Approaching fish from upstream.** Fish face upstream; they see you coming. Approach from behind or the side.
- **Spearing at night without light.** Fish see the spear's shadow in moonlight. Use a torch to blind and attract them instead.
- **Not harvesting the weir often.** Fish die in traps and rot, fouling the trap. Check daily and reset.
- **Fishing during warm afternoons.** Fish are less active in heat. Fish at dawn, dusk, or night.
- **Using poor cordage.** Fishing line must not break. Test it to at least 20 kg before trusting it.
