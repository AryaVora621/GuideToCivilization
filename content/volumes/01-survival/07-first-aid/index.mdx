## Overview

First aid is the bridge between catastrophe and survival. In the hours after collapse, before you can establish shelter or fire, injuries will happen: cuts from sharp objects, burns from cooking attempts, fractures from falls, wounds from tools. Without proper care, infection sets in within 24–48 hours. Infection becomes sepsis. Sepsis becomes death.

This chapter covers the four most common immediate threats: bleeding control, wound cleaning, burns, and fractures. All methods use materials you can improvise from almost anything. The goal is not perfect medicine—it is preventing an injury from becoming fatal.

## Prerequisites

None. This chapter is standalone. Read it before you need it.

## Materials & Tools

**For stopping bleeding:**
- Clean cloth, bark, or cloth scraps (anything absorbent)
- Optional but valuable: plant fibers from plantain, yarrow, or nettles (act as coagulants)
- Tourniquet if needed: vine, cord, or cloth strip

**For cleaning wounds:**
- Boiled or purified water (see Vol 3, Ch 1 — Water Purification)
- Clean cloth or cloth strips for washing
- Ash, honey, or rendered animal fat for wound dressing
- Optional: willow bark tea (mild antiseptic)

**For burns:**
- Clean water
- Fat or honey for sealing
- Clean cloth wrapping

**For fractures:**
- Straight branches or sturdy plant stems (splinting material)
- Cloth strips or cordage for binding
- Padding material: leaves, cloth, animal hide

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Stop the Bleeding

Apply direct pressure with the cleanest cloth available—hold it firmly on the wound for a minimum of 3 minutes without lifting. If bleeding is heavy, maintain pressure for 5–10 minutes. The pressure slows blood flow and allows clotting to begin.

If blood soaks through the cloth, **do not remove it**. Add another layer on top and continue pressure. Removing the cloth disrupts clots and restarts bleeding.

For severe arterial bleeding (bright red, pulsing): Apply pressure, then tie the cloth in place with a tourniquet above the wound (between the wound and the heart) if it is on a limb. A tourniquet is a cloth or vine twisted tight with a stick. This is a last resort only—prolonged tourniquet use (over 2 hours) risks losing the limb. Release pressure every 15 minutes for 30 seconds to check if bleeding has slowed.

### Step 2: Elevate the Wound

If the injury is on a limb, raise it above heart level. This reduces pressure and blood flow to the wound, slowing bleeding. Combine elevation with direct pressure for best results.

### Step 3: Clean the Wound

Once bleeding is controlled, gently clean the wound with boiled water. Use a cloth to wipe away blood, dirt, and debris. Be gentle—too much agitation reopens clots.

For deep wounds, flush them: Pour clean water slowly into the wound to wash out embedded debris. Do this twice if you have water to spare.

If boiled water is not available, use rainwater or spring water that you have allowed to settle. Infection is worse than temporary swelling, so if the water is even slightly questionable, boil it first.

### Step 4: Apply Wound Dressing

Once clean and dry, cover the wound with a dressing:

**Honey**: If available, is one of the best wound treatments. It is antiseptic, absorbs moisture, and promotes healing. Apply a thin layer directly to the wound, then cover with clean cloth. Change the dressing every 2–3 days or if it becomes wet or dirty. Honey prevents infection for up to one week per application.

**Fat or animal lard**: Rendered animal fat seals the wound from air and debris. Apply a thin layer and cover with cloth. Less effective than honey but more likely to be available. Change every 3 days.

**Ash from clean wood fire**: Sift ash to remove charcoal. Fine ash can be dusted over a damp wound to help it dry and can be mixed with water into a paste. Ash has weak antiseptic properties but risks irritation in deep wounds. Use only on minor cuts.

**Willow bark tea**: Brew willow bark in boiled water for 5–10 minutes. The liquid contains salicin, a mild pain reliever and antiseptic. Soak cloth in the cooled tea and apply as a wet dressing, changing it when it dries.

### Step 5: Bind and Monitor

Cover the wound with clean cloth and bind it gently but firmly to prevent dirt from entering. The binding should not cut off circulation—you should be able to slip a finger under it.

Check the wound twice daily. Signs of infection to watch for:
- Warmth or heat radiating from the area
- Swelling that worsens after day 2
- Pus (whitish or yellowish discharge—this is infection fighting, but increasing pus means you are losing)
- Red streaks extending from the wound upward (indicates infection spreading through the lymph system)
- Fever, chills, or body aches

If infection begins, increase water and salt intake (to support immune response), apply warm compresses to draw pus, and change dressings more frequently. See Vol 4, Ch 3 (Germ Theory in Practice) if infection worsens.

## Burns

### Classification

**First-degree burns** (red skin, painful, no blistering): Surface skin only. These heal in 3–5 days without treatment but are very painful.

**Second-degree burns** (red or blotchy, blistering, painful): Reach into the dermis. Risk of infection is high. Need careful treatment. Heal in 2–3 weeks if kept clean.

**Third-degree burns** (white or charred, often less painful because nerves are destroyed): Full-thickness destruction. Beyond first aid—see Vol 4, Ch 2 (Wound Care) for surgical options.

### Treatment

**Immediate care**: Cool the burn with water for 5–10 minutes. This stops the heat damage from spreading deeper into tissue. Use room-temperature or cool water, not ice (ice causes additional damage).

**First-degree**: After cooling, apply fat or honey. Wrap loosely in clean cloth. Pain will persist for days. Do not puncture blisters that form (they protect the skin beneath). Cover with cloth and leave them until they naturally reabsorb.

**Second-degree**: After cooling, do not puncture blisters. If a blister breaks, clean the area gently and treat as an open wound. Apply honey or fat to prevent infection. Change dressing daily. Watch closely for signs of infection.

Pain relief: Willow bark tea or other pain-relieving plants (see Vol 4, Ch 9 — Herbal Pharmacopoeia) will help. Elevation of the burned area reduces swelling.

## Fractures and Dislocations

### Identifying a Fracture

**Likely fracture if you see:**
- Deformity (the limb looks bent or wrong shape)
- Severe swelling within minutes
- Inability to move the limb without extreme pain
- Bone protruding through skin (compound fracture — see Vol 4, Ch 2)

**Possible fracture if you see:**
- Pain that worsens with weight-bearing
- Tenderness at one specific point when you press gently
- Swelling and bruising

### Splinting

A splint stabilizes the injury and prevents movement that could cause further damage. For most fractures, immobilization for 4–6 weeks allows healing.

**Materials**: A straight stick or branch thinner than your thumb, padded with cloth, leaves, or animal hide. Length should be from above the joint above the injury to below the joint below the injury (e.g., for a forearm fracture, splint from elbow to hand).

**Steps**:
1. Gently position the limb as straight as possible. If you are in extreme pain, leave it as is—do not force it.
2. Pad the splint with cloth or leaves to prevent pressure sores.
3. Position the padded splint alongside or around the injury.
4. Bind the splint in place with cloth strips or cordage, tying knots above and below the injury. The binding should be snug but not cutting off circulation.
5. Check circulation after 15 minutes: can you feel a pulse in the limb beyond the splint? Can you move fingers or toes? If circulation is cut off, loosen the binding.
6. Elevate the limb if possible to reduce swelling.

### Dislocations

A dislocation is when a bone slips out of its joint (usually shoulder, knee, finger, or ankle). The limb is often visibly deformed and extremely painful.

**Reduction** (putting it back) is best done quickly, while swelling is still minimal. The first hour is critical.

**Shoulder dislocation**: Have the person lie on their back. Gently, very slowly, move the dislocated arm up and back in a rotating motion. The goal is to let the ball of the shoulder slowly rotate back into the socket. This may take 5–10 minutes. Stop if pain becomes unbearable. Ice or cold water can help numb the pain slightly. Once reduced, strap the arm to the body with cloth and keep it immobile for 3 weeks.

**Knee dislocation**: This is more serious and less likely to self-reduce. Attempt gentle traction (pulling the lower leg away from the body while slightly rotating) but do not force it. Immobilize with a splint and elevate. Transport to someone with medical training if possible.

**Ankle dislocation**: Often occurs with fracture. Apply gentle traction to the foot while rotating slightly. Ice the area immediately if available. Splint and elevate. Do not bear weight on the ankle for 6 weeks.

## Verification

**For bleeding control**: The bleeding has stopped within 5–10 minutes and does not restart when you gently lift the cloth after 30 minutes.

**For wound cleaning**: The wound is visibly free of dirt and debris. It is damp but not actively bleeding. There is no foul smell.

**For burns**: First-degree burns begin to lose redness within 24 hours. Blisters form and hold fluid without leaking. Second-degree burns form clean blisters with clear or slightly yellow fluid inside. Any sign of pus or dark fluid is infection.

**For fractures**: The limb is immobile. Moving the splint does not cause pain or movement of the injury site. Swelling is stable or decreasing by day 2.

## Common Mistakes

**Removing a cloth too early**: Bleeding has not truly stopped if you lift the cloth after 1–2 minutes. Wait the full 3–5 minutes. Impatience causes blood loss.

**Touching the wound with dirty hands**: Infection is worse than temporary swelling. If your hands are not clean, boil water and use cloth to apply treatments instead.

**Ignoring early infection signs**: Warmth and swelling on day 1 are normal. But if they worsen on days 2–3, you have an infection. Act immediately. Change dressings to honey twice daily, keep the area elevated, and increase fluid intake.

**Over-tightening a tourniquet or splint**: Numbness, coldness, or pale color below the binding means circulation is cut off. You have perhaps 2–4 hours before tissue damage becomes permanent. Loosen immediately.

**Using dirty cloth for direct pressure**: If no clean cloth exists, use the cleanest scrap you have. A dirty cloth is still better than no pressure, but prevention is better: keep cloth scraps clean and dry in a dedicated location.

**Puncturing blisters from burns**: Blisters are nature's bandage. Puncturing them causes infection. Let them reabsorb naturally. Only drain a blister if it ruptures on its own, and then treat the open area as a burn wound.

**Splitting a broken bone that may be dislocated**: If the limb is severely bent or deformed, do not try to straighten it first. Splint it as is. Forcing a dislocated joint straight can cause nerve damage.
