## Overview

Fire is the original technology. It provides warmth, cooks food that would otherwise make you sick, boils water, signals for help, works metal, and makes the night survivable. Starting a fire without modern tools is a learnable skill, not luck.

This chapter covers four methods ranked by difficulty and equipment required.

## Prerequisites

None. Learn this before you need it.

## Materials & Tools

**For friction fire (bow drill) — no tools needed:**
- Fireboard: dry, soft, non-resinous wood (willow, cottonwood, cedar, basswood, mullein stalk)
- Spindle: same or similar dry wood, straight, 30–40 cm long
- Bow: curved stick, 50–70 cm long
- Bowstring: cordage (shoelace, paracord, plant fiber — see Vol 1, Ch 8)
- Handhold: hardwood (to grip the top of the spindle)
- Tinder bundle: dry grass, bark fibers, cattail fluff, shredded dry leaves
- Ember catcher: flat piece of bark or leaf

**For flint-and-steel:**
- Flint, chert, or quartzite (hard rock that produces sparks)
- High-carbon steel (old file, knife spine, or carbon steel striker)
- Char cloth or dried fungus (Amadou) to catch spark

**For lens fire:**
- Magnifying glass, reading glasses, or clear water in a clear plastic bag or bottle bottom
- Bright direct sun (will not work in shade or overcast)
- Dry tinder

**For prepared fire:**
- Lighter, matches, or ferrocerium rod (carry one always)
- Tinder and kindling

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Method 1: Bow Drill (Most reliable friction method)

**Step 1: Prepare the fireboard.**
Carve a shallow depression into the fireboard near one edge. Carve a notch from the edge to the center of the depression — a V-shaped notch about 1/8 of the hole's diameter. This notch collects the hot dust (the ember).

**Step 2: Prepare the spindle.**
The spindle should fit the depression loosely, be straight, and have a blunt point. Too sharp = no friction. Too flat = stalls.

**Step 3: Set up.**
Place the ember catcher (bark or leaf) under the notch to catch the dust. Place one foot on the fireboard near the notch to hold it still. Loop the bowstring once around the spindle — it should not slip but also not be so tight the bow cannot move.

**Step 4: Start drilling.**
Hold the handhold firmly on top of the spindle. Move the bow back and forth steadily with long, even strokes. Press down with moderate pressure on the handhold. The goal is heat from sustained friction — not speed. Too fast with light pressure produces nothing. Moderate speed with firm pressure produces a coal in 30–60 seconds of continuous motion.

**Step 5: Produce smoke, then a coal.**
Dark, thick smoke from the notch indicates you are close. Keep going until smoke continues for 2–3 seconds after you stop — this means a coal has formed in the dust.

**Step 6: Transfer the coal to your tinder bundle.**
Tap the coal from the ember catcher into the center of your tinder bundle. Fold the bundle loosely around it. Blow gently and steadily at the coal — not short puffs. Watch for the bundle to glow and begin to smoke more. Increase breath pressure slowly until flame erupts.

**Step 7: Build the fire.**
Transfer the flaming bundle to your fire lay (see below). Add small dry sticks first, then larger ones.

### Method 2: Flint and Steel

**Step 1: Prepare char cloth.**
Char cloth catches sparks best. Cut small pieces of cotton cloth, place in a metal tin with a small hole, and hold in fire until smoke stops. The cloth inside chars to black without burning.

**Step 2: Strike the spark.**
Hold the flint in one hand, char cloth on top of the flint. Strike the steel downward across the flint edge in a quick, glancing blow. Sparks land on the char cloth.

**Step 3: Transfer to tinder.**
A caught spark on char cloth glows red. Fold the char cloth into your tinder bundle and blow as in Method 1.

### Fire Lays

**The teepee:** Best for starting. Stack small sticks in a cone over your tinder. Air rises through center, igniting all sides.

**The log cabin:** Sustainable, burns longer. Lay two logs parallel, two logs perpendicular on top, repeat. Place tinder in the center.

**The trench fire:** Wind protection. Dig a shallow trench aligned with the wind. Fire in the trench; wind feeds it from one end.

## Verification

- You have produced a sustained flame from your tinder bundle
- The fire can be built up to a sustainable size

## Common Mistakes

- **Wet wood.** Friction fire requires bone-dry wood. Test by bending: dry wood snaps, damp wood bends.
- **Pressure without stroke length.** Short strokes with high pressure stall the spindle. Long, steady strokes with firm pressure.
- **Blowing too hard on the coal.** Blowing too aggressively scatters the coal. Gentle, steady breath first.
- **Wrong wood combination.** Resinous softwoods (pine) clog the notch. Use soft, dry, non-resinous woods.
- **Too-large tinder.** The tinder bundle should be loosely packed dry grass or bark fiber, not sticks.
- **Not making the notch.** Without the notch, dust has nowhere to collect and cool before igniting.
