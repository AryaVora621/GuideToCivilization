## Overview

Shelter is your first line of defense against the elements. Exposure kills faster than hunger or thirst. A person can survive three weeks without food, three days without water, but only three hours without adequate shelter in extreme conditions. The right shelter keeps you warm, dry, and safe while requiring minimal materials and time.

This chapter covers five shelter types: emergency debris shelter (fastest), lean-to, A-frame, wickiup, and semi-subterranean. Each has specific conditions where it excels.

## Prerequisites

You should know how to identify a good site (sheltered from wind, elevated for drainage, near water but not in a flood zone), recognize weather patterns, and have practiced with rope and knots (Vol 1, Ch 8). Fire-making (Vol 1, Ch 2) is essential for warming any shelter in cold climates.

## Materials & Tools

### Universal (all shelter types)
- Large branches or saplings (6–12 inches diameter, 8–15 feet long)
- Smaller branches for framework (1–3 inches diameter)
- Cordage (rope, vines, bark strips; see Vol 1, Ch 8)
- Insulation materials: leaves, pine needles, moss, straw, bark sheets
- Cutting tools: knife, axe, or saw
- Water source nearby (for drinking and fire safety)

### Specific to shelter type
- **Debris shelter:** None beyond materials above
- **Lean-to:** Open front (no additional materials)
- **A-frame:** None beyond materials above
- **Wickiup:** Large supple branches or saplings 15+ feet long, bark
- **Semi-subterranean:** Digging tools (shovel, fire-hardened stick, antler)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Type 1: Debris Shelter (Emergency, 30–60 minutes)

The fastest shelter. A ridge-pole supported between two trees or forked sticks, covered with branches and then insulating debris.

**Step 1: Find or create a ridge.**
Look for two trees 6–10 feet apart at roughly the same height. Fallen logs also work. If you must create a ridge using forked branches, drive two forked sticks into the ground 8–10 feet apart. The fork should be 3–4 feet high.

**Step 2: Lay the ridge-pole.**
Find a straight branch 8–12 inches in diameter and 12–15 feet long. Rest it in the forks. Secure it with rope or cordage if the fork is not deep enough to grip it.

**Step 3: Create ribs.**
Lean branches (2–4 inches diameter, 8–12 feet long) against the ridge-pole at a 45-degree angle, starting at the ground and working upward. Space them 1–2 feet apart. Tie them to the ridge-pole if wind is likely.

**Step 4: Close the ends.**
Weave smaller branches horizontally across the ribs at both ends to close the shelter. Leave a small opening (2–3 feet) at one end for entry.

**Step 5: Insulate heavily.**
Pile leaves, pine needles, moss, bark, and straw over the entire structure — thicker than you think necessary. A 2–3 foot layer of loose debris provides excellent insulation. Fill gaps between ribs to stop wind from entering. Pack insulation under you as well (not just overhead).

**Step 6: Create a low door.**
At the entry, hang bark or a branch to block wind while allowing you to crawl in and out. The entrance should be as small as possible to retain heat.

**Duration in shelter:** A debris shelter can protect you adequately for 1–3 nights. The insulation may compress over time; monitor and add more if needed.

### Type 2: Lean-To (Versatile, 1–2 hours)

Faster than an A-frame but more durable than a debris shelter. Good for mild climates and where you want easy access to a central fire.

**Step 1: Find or create a backing.**
A large fallen log, rock face, or ridge-pole works as the back. If you must build one, create it the same way as the debris shelter ridge (two forked sticks and a horizontal pole, 4–5 feet high).

**Step 2: Build the ribs.**
Lean branches (3–6 inches diameter, 10–12 feet long) against the backing at a 45-degree angle, spaced 1–2 feet apart. The lower end should be 8–10 feet from the backing.

**Step 3: Add a strong roof layer.**
Lay smaller branches (2–3 inches diameter) horizontally across the ribs, parallel to the backing. Space them 6–12 inches apart.

**Step 4: Thatch or cover.**
Cover the roof with bark sheets, large leaves, or woven branches. Overlap them like shingles so water runs down, not through. Bank insulating material along the sides and back to block wind.

**Step 5: Build a fire reflector (optional).**
Place a wall of stacked branches or stones in front of the lean-to, parallel to the opening. Fire heat reflects into the shelter, warming you without filling it with smoke.

**Duration:** A well-built lean-to lasts weeks to months if the roof doesn't rot or collapse under heavy snow.

### Type 3: A-Frame (Durable, 2–4 hours)

The classic survival shelter. Enclosed and strong, with good insulation.

**Step 1: Create the ridge.**
Lay two branches of equal length (10–15 feet long, 4–6 inches diameter) forming an A. Tie them together at the peak with cordage. The base of the A should be 6–8 feet apart.

**Step 2: Secure the base.**
Drive the base ends firmly into the ground or wedge them against logs to prevent them spreading. Ensure the structure is stable before proceeding.

**Step 3: Add crossbeams.**
Tie horizontal branches (1–2 inches diameter) to both sides of the A-frame at 1–2 foot intervals, running perpendicular to the ridge. These ribs distribute weight and provide attachment points for covering.

**Step 4: Add end ribs.**
Create two smaller A-frames at the ends (front and back). Tie them to the main ridge. Leave one end partially open (3–4 feet tall and 3–4 feet wide) for entry; close the other end.

**Step 5: Cover the roof.**
Layer bark sheets or woven branches, overlapping like shingles. Ensure water runs downward on both sides.

**Step 6: Insulate.**
Pack leaves, moss, and pine needles under and over the roof (inside and outside). Fill all gaps. Create an insulated floor by laying branches and covering them with a thick layer of leaves.

**Step 7: Create a door.**
Weave branches or hang bark as a door to cover the opening. Leave it removable so you can exit quickly if needed.

**Duration:** An A-frame can last months to years if properly maintained. Inspect and reinforce the roof before each rainy season.

### Type 4: Wickiup (Rapid, 1–3 hours)

A cone-shaped frame lashed with branches and insulation. Strong in wind and space-efficient.

**Step 1: Gather long supple branches.**
Collect 8–12 branches or saplings 15–20 feet long, 1–2 inches in diameter. Willow, birch, or aspen work well if they bend without snapping.

**Step 2: Create the base circle.**
Lay a rope or vine in a circle 8–12 feet in diameter. This marks your floor plan.

**Step 3: Lash the poles.**
Bind all branches together at the top with cordage, forming a cone. Stand them upright within the circle, with the bottom ends on or just inside the marked line. Ensure they are evenly spaced. The cone peak should be 12–15 feet high.

**Step 4: Add a framework.**
Weave horizontal branches through the poles at 2–3 foot intervals, creating a lattice. This prevents the long poles from shifting.

**Step 5: Thatch and insulate.**
Cover the frame with bark sheets, large leaves, or woven branches, overlapping downward. Leave a small opening at the apex for smoke ventilation if you plan to have a fire inside. Pack heavy insulation (leaves, moss, bark) around the base, outside and inside.

**Step 6: Create a door.**
Leave a 3–4 foot gap at the base for entry and close it with bark or woven branches.

**Duration:** A wickiup lasts weeks to months. The cone shape sheds water and resists wind well.

### Type 5: Semi-Subterranean Shelter (Durable, 4–8 hours)

The warmest option, requiring more labor but excellent insulation from earth.

**Step 1: Select and excavate the site.**
Find a slight slope or bank where you can dig into earth 2–3 feet deep. Avoid flood zones, waterlogged soil, and areas near root systems. Excavate a rectangular pit 6–8 feet long, 4–6 feet wide, and 3–4 feet deep. Slope the floor slightly toward the entrance for drainage.

**Step 2: Build the frame.**
Drive strong posts (3–4 inches diameter) into the ground at the four corners and midpoints of the long sides. These support the roof.

**Step 3: Create the roof.**
Lay strong beams (5–6 inches diameter) across the posts, running the long way. Cover these with a layer of smaller branches and then bark sheets.

**Step 4: Add insulation and waterproofing.**
Pile soil, leaves, and moss on top of the roof to a depth of 1–2 feet. This insulates from cold and keeps water out. Slope the ground away from the shelter so water runs off.

**Step 5: Create ventilation and entrance.**
Dig a sloped tunnel entrance 3–4 feet long and 3 feet high leading into the shelter. This traps cold air, reducing drafts. At the far end of the shelter, create a small opening for smoke ventilation if you have a fire.

**Step 6: Insulate the floor.**
Lay branches on the floor and cover with a deep layer of leaves and moss for comfort and warmth.

**Duration:** A semi-subterranean shelter lasts years if the roof is properly maintained. Water is the main threat—monitor drainage after heavy rain.

## Verification

A good shelter meets these criteria:

1. **Weathertight:** No rain leaks through the roof. Water runs off, not into the structure.
2. **Windproof:** Air cannot blow through gaps or under walls. Entrances are small and protected.
3. **Insulated:** Thick insulation on all sides (at least 1 foot in cold climates). You feel significantly warmer inside than outside.
4. **Accessible:** You can enter and exit without collapsing the structure or crawling more than 10 feet.
5. **Sized appropriately:** Large enough to lie down in and stretch, but not so large that body heat cannot warm it.
6. **Stable:** The structure does not sway, shift, or groan in moderate wind. Lashings are tight.
7. **Safe:** No loose branches overhead that could fall on you. No risk of collapse.

Test your shelter before nightfall by spending an hour inside. Make repairs while light remains.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Building too large:** A shelter twice as big as needed will be twice as hard to keep warm. Build small.
2. **Skimping on insulation:** Most people underestimate how much insulation is needed. Add more than seems necessary, especially overhead and under the floor.
3. **Poor siting:** Building near dead trees, in a wind funnel, or in a flood zone guarantees regret. Scout the area carefully.
4. **Forgetting drainage:** Build on a slight slope with water running away, not toward your shelter.
5. **Leaving gaps:** Wind and cold find every gap. Fill them completely.
6. **Using green wood:** Freshly cut wood is heavy and slow to lash. Use deadfall where possible.
7. **Inadequate entry protection:** A large open door wastes heat. Make the entrance small and sealable.
8. **Building in darkness:** Start shelter building in daylight. Never wait until dusk.
