## Overview

Shelter is your first line of defense against the elements. Exposure kills faster than hunger or thirst. A person can survive three weeks without food, three days without water, but only three hours without adequate shelter in extreme conditions. The right shelter keeps you warm, dry, and safe while requiring minimal materials and time.

This chapter covers five shelter types: emergency debris shelter (fastest), lean-to, A-frame, wickiup, and semi-subterranean. Each has specific conditions where it excels.

## Prerequisites

You should know how to identify a good site (sheltered from wind, elevated for drainage, near water but not in a flood zone), recognize weather patterns, and have practiced with rope and knots (Vol 1, Ch 8). Fire-making (Vol 1, Ch 2) is essential for warming any shelter in cold climates.

## Materials & Tools

### Universal (all shelter types)
- Large branches or saplings (6–12 inches diameter, 8–15 feet long)
- Smaller branches for framework (1–3 inches diameter)
- Cordage (rope, vines, bark strips; see Vol 1, Ch 8)
- Insulation materials: leaves, pine needles, moss, straw, bark sheets
- Cutting tools: knife, axe, or saw
- Water source nearby (for drinking and fire safety)

### Specific to shelter type
- **Debris shelter:** None beyond materials above
- **Lean-to:** Open front (no additional materials)
- **A-frame:** None beyond materials above
- **Wickiup:** Large supple branches or saplings 15+ feet long, bark
- **Semi-subterranean:** Digging tools (shovel, fire-hardened stick, antler)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Type 1: Debris Shelter (Emergency, 30–60 minutes)

The fastest shelter. A ridge-pole supported between two trees or forked sticks, covered with branches and then insulating debris. Effective in nearly any environment where fallen wood and debris are abundant.

**Step 1: Find or create a ridge.**
Look for two trees 6–10 feet apart at roughly the same height. Fallen logs also work. If you must create a ridge using forked branches, drive two forked sticks into the ground 8–10 feet apart. The fork should be 3–4 feet high. Angle the forked sticks slightly inward (toward each other) so the ridge-pole naturally settles into the fork and resists sliding. In soft ground, drive the forks deeper (1–2 feet) for stability.

**Step 2: Lay the ridge-pole.**
Find a straight branch 8–12 inches in diameter and 12–15 feet long. Rest it in the forks. Secure it with rope or cordage if the fork is not deep enough to grip it. If your ridge-pole bends noticeably under its own weight, add a center support: drive a third forked stick under the midpoint of the ridge.

**Step 3: Create ribs.**
Lean branches (2–4 inches diameter, 8–12 feet long) against the ridge-pole at a 45-degree angle, starting at the ground and working upward. Space them 1–2 feet apart. Tie them to the ridge-pole if wind is likely. Begin stacking branches in a radial pattern: position your heaviest branches first (the lowest ones), as they bear the most weight.

**Step 4: Close the ends.**
Weave smaller branches horizontally across the ribs at both ends to close the shelter. Leave a small opening (2–3 feet) at one end for entry. At the closed end, build an angled wall to shed water: lean branches at a steeper angle (60 degrees or more) so rain runs off rather than pooling.

**Step 5: Insulate heavily.**
Pile leaves, pine needles, moss, bark, and straw over the entire structure — thicker than you think necessary. A 2–3 foot layer of loose debris provides excellent insulation. Fill gaps between ribs to stop wind from entering. Pack insulation under you as well (not just overhead). In wet climates, place a layer of bark or woven branches under the leaf layer to prevent water from wicking up through the leaves.

**Step 6: Create a low door.**
At the entry, hang bark or a branch to block wind while allowing you to crawl in and out. The entrance should be as small as possible to retain heat. If you have spare cordage, create a door flap that stays open during the day (for light and visibility) but closes at night. Consider creating a second small opening on the opposite side for emergency exit if the main entrance becomes blocked.

**Step 7: Line the floor.**
Before settling in, lay a bed of dry leaves, pine needles, or straw inside the shelter. This prevents you from lying directly on cold, damp ground. A 12-inch layer of loose insulation under you is often more important than insulation overhead.

**Duration and maintenance:** A debris shelter can protect you adequately for 1–3 nights. The insulation may compress over time; monitor and add more if needed. If you're sheltering in the same debris shelter for more than 2 nights, completely rebuild or significantly reinforce it on day 2—the original structure will have degraded.

**Regional variations:** In deserts with minimal vegetation, use sand instead of leaves for insulation, or build a darker shelter to absorb heat during the day and radiate it at night. In forests with abundant coniferous trees, pine needles are ideal for both insulation and repelling insects. In arctic regions with limited wood, use turf (sod) and snow blocks instead of branches.

### Type 2: Lean-To (Versatile, 1–2 hours)

Faster than an A-frame but more durable than a debris shelter. Good for mild climates and where you want easy access to a central fire. Also excellent for creating shade in hot, dry climates where retention of heat is undesirable.

**Step 1: Find or create a backing.**
A large fallen log, rock face, or ridge-pole works as the back. If you must build one, create it the same way as the debris shelter ridge (two forked sticks and a horizontal pole, 4–5 feet high). The backing should run perpendicular to prevailing winds—survey the area first to identify wind direction by observing blown leaves, bent grasses, or tree damage.

**Step 2: Build the ribs.**
Lean branches (3–6 inches diameter, 10–12 feet long) against the backing at a 45-degree angle, spaced 1–2 feet apart. The lower end should be 8–10 feet from the backing. In areas with heavy snow, use a steeper angle (50–60 degrees) to shed snow more effectively. Secure the lower ends of the ribs by laying heavy logs on top of them or by tying them to ground stakes.

**Step 3: Add a strong roof layer.**
Lay smaller branches (2–3 inches diameter) horizontally across the ribs, parallel to the backing. Space them 6–12 inches apart. This creates a lattice that distributes weight evenly. Ensure each horizontal branch overlaps at least two ribs.

**Step 4: Thatch or cover.**
Cover the roof with bark sheets, large leaves, or woven branches. Overlap them like shingles so water runs down, not through. Start at the lower edge and work upward. Each layer should cover the joints of the layer below. Bank insulating material along the sides and back to block wind. For longevity, add a second layer of bark or branches over the thatch before piling insulation.

**Step 5: Build a fire reflector (optional).**
Place a wall of stacked branches or stones in front of the lean-to, parallel to the opening. Fire heat reflects into the shelter, warming you without filling it with smoke. Lean flat branches behind the fire reflector to direct heat further back into the lean-to. This can increase interior warmth by 30–50 degrees Fahrenheit.

**Step 6: Side insulation (optional but important).**
Add partial walls on the sides by laying horizontal branches and covering them with bark and insulation material. Leave one side partially open for light and visibility. This creates a more enclosed, warmer shelter while maintaining the open front advantage.

**Duration:** A well-built lean-to lasts weeks to months if the roof doesn't rot or collapse under heavy snow. Inspect the roof after heavy rain or snow; tighten lashings and add additional thatch as needed.

### Type 3: A-Frame (Durable, 2–4 hours)

The classic survival shelter. Enclosed and strong, with good insulation. Excellent all-weather protection and easily scaled to accommodate groups.

**Step 1: Create the ridge.**
Lay two branches of equal length (10–15 feet long, 4–6 inches diameter) forming an A. Tie them together at the peak with cordage. Use a square lashing (see Vol 1, Ch 8) for maximum strength. The base of the A should be 6–8 feet apart. For extreme wind, use branches 6–8 inches in diameter instead.

**Step 2: Secure the base.**
Drive the base ends firmly into the ground or wedge them against logs to prevent them spreading. Dig a small trench and position the base branches into it for added stability. Ensure the structure is stable before proceeding. Have someone push on the frame from all sides to test stability.

**Step 3: Add crossbeams.**
Tie horizontal branches (1–2 inches diameter) to both sides of the A-frame at 1–2 foot intervals, running perpendicular to the ridge. These ribs distribute weight and provide attachment points for covering. Use half-hitches rather than full lashings for faster installation. The spacing should be uniform so the covering material sits evenly.

**Step 4: Add end ribs.**
Create two smaller A-frames at the ends (front and back). Tie them to the main ridge using the same rope techniques. Leave one end partially open (3–4 feet tall and 3–4 feet wide) for entry; close the other end completely. The closed end should face the prevailing wind to reduce drafts.

**Step 5: Cover the roof.**
Layer bark sheets or woven branches, overlapping like shingles. Ensure water runs downward on both sides. If using bark, seek out large sheets from dead birch, cedar, or other suitable trees. Woven branches should be dense enough that light cannot pass through. Overlap each layer by at least 6 inches.

**Step 6: Insulate exterior and interior.**
Pack leaves, moss, and pine needles under and over the roof (inside and outside). Fill all gaps. In cold climates, create a double roof: build the A-frame, add insulation and bark, then add another layer of branches on top before the final covering. This creates an air gap that provides additional insulation.

**Step 7: Create an insulated floor.**
Lay branches (2–3 inches diameter) perpendicular to the ridge line, spaced 12 inches apart. Cover these with smaller branches or bark, then add a thick layer (12–18 inches) of leaves, moss, or straw. This provides thermal insulation from the cold ground. In extremely cold climates, raise the floor 1–2 feet off the ground by building a sturdy wooden platform.

**Step 8: Create a door.**
Weave branches or hang bark as a door to cover the opening. Leave it removable so you can exit quickly if needed. Make the door slightly larger than the opening so it overlaps and blocks wind. Consider creating a second inner door to trap warm air.

**Step 9: Add interior improvements (optional).**
Inside the A-frame, construct a simple sleeping platform 12–18 inches off the insulated floor. This keeps you further away from ground cold. Create small niches or shelves by driving branches into the frame to store water, food, and tools off the floor.

**Duration:** An A-frame can last months to years if properly maintained. Inspect the roof monthly for water leaks or damage. Reinforce the roof structure before each rainy season. Replace rotting insulation when it becomes compacted or damp.

### Type 4: Wickiup (Rapid, 1–3 hours)

A cone-shaped frame lashed with branches and insulation. Strong in wind and space-efficient. Widely used by indigenous peoples across North America, the wickiup is highly adaptable to regional materials.

**Step 1: Gather long supple branches.**
Collect 8–12 branches or saplings 15–20 feet long, 1–2 inches in diameter. Willow, birch, aspen, or juniper work well if they bend without snapping. Test branches for flexibility before committing. If suitable saplings are scarce, use 2–3 shorter branches lashed together end-to-end to create longer poles.

**Step 2: Create the base circle.**
Lay a rope or vine in a circle 8–12 feet in diameter. This marks your floor plan. The larger the circle, the more interior headroom but the longer the poles must be. An 8-foot circle with 15-foot poles gives adequate headroom (about 7 feet at peak).

**Step 3: Lash the poles.**
Bind all branches together at the top with cordage, forming a cone. Stand them upright within the circle, with the bottom ends on or just inside the marked line. Ensure they are evenly spaced. The cone peak should be 12–15 feet high. Have one person hold the poles while another ties the apex lashing. Use a locking knot (see Vol 1, Ch 8) to prevent the lashing from slipping as weight settles.

**Step 4: Add a framework.**
Weave horizontal branches through the poles at 2–3 foot intervals, creating a lattice. Start at the base and work upward. These branches should be more rigid (3–4 inches diameter) than the main poles. This prevents the long poles from shifting or spreading under load. Lash the intersection points with light cordage or vine.

**Step 5: Thatch and insulate.**
Cover the frame with bark sheets, large leaves, or woven branches, overlapping downward like roof shingles. Start at the base and work upward so water runs downward. Leave a small opening (1–2 feet diameter) at the apex for smoke ventilation if you plan to have a fire inside. Pack heavy insulation (leaves, moss, bark) around the base, outside and inside. Create a 12–18 inch layer of insulation at the base both inside and outside the wickiup.

**Step 6: Create a removable door.**
Leave a 3–4 foot gap at the base for entry and close it with bark or woven branches. Make the door removable or hinged so you can adjust ventilation and light. In winter, add an inner door or door flap to trap heat. The wickiup works best with a central fire that you can control and tend.

**Step 7: Interior setup.**
If using fire inside, keep the fire small and centered under the smoke hole. Arrange sleeping areas around the perimeter so you're elevated on a bed of insulation, away from the cold ground. Hang cordage from the apex to create storage areas for supplies.

**Duration:** A wickiup lasts weeks to months. The cone shape sheds water and resists wind well. Inspect the lashing at the apex regularly—this is the stress point. Replace insulation when it becomes compressed or damp.

**Regional note:** In the Southwest (desert wickiups), reduce the amount of insulation and leave more ventilation to manage heat. In the Pacific Northwest, use large bark sheets and create additional weather-proofing layers. Arctic peoples adapted the wickiup design by using skins instead of bark and adding snow blocks for additional insulation.

### Type 5: Semi-Subterranean Shelter (Durable, 4–8 hours)

The warmest option, requiring more labor but excellent insulation from earth. Also called a dugout or sod house, this structure provides stable temperatures year-round and maximum protection from extreme weather.

**Step 1: Select and excavate the site.**
Find a slight slope or bank where you can dig into earth 2–3 feet deep. Avoid flood zones, waterlogged soil, and areas near active root systems. Test soil drainage by digging a 12-inch hole and pouring water into it; if water doesn't drain within 30 minutes, the location is unsuitable. Excavate a rectangular pit 6–8 feet long, 4–6 feet wide, and 3–4 feet deep. Slope the floor slightly (1–2 inches per 5 feet) toward the entrance for drainage. Compact the floor with stones or logs to prevent settling.

**Step 2: Assess structural needs.**
For shallow excavations (2–3 feet), the surrounding earth provides lateral support. For deeper excavations (4+ feet), you need stronger support. Drive strong posts (4–6 inches diameter) into the ground at the four corners and midpoints of the long sides. Use green wood (freshly cut) for posts—it's stronger than deadfall.

**Step 3: Create the roof structure.**
Lay strong beams (5–6 inches diameter) across the posts, running the long way (parallel to the length of the shelter). Space beams 3–4 feet apart. If wood is scarce, lay the beams closer together and use smaller supports. Cover these beams with a layer of smaller branches (2–3 inches diameter) running perpendicular to the beams. This creates a grid that distributes weight. Finally, add a layer of bark sheets or bark-wrapped branches.

**Step 4: Add waterproofing layer.**
Before piling earth on top, create a waterproof barrier. Use large bark sheets layered like shingles, overlapping downward. Alternatively, use clay mixed with straw if available. This barrier prevents water from seeping through into the shelter.

**Step 5: Add insulation and weatherproofing.**
Pile soil, leaves, and moss on top of the roof to a depth of 1–2 feet. This insulates from cold and keeps water out. Layer different materials: 6 inches of leaves and moss, then 6 inches of soil, then another 6-inch layer of insulation. Slope the ground away from the shelter on all sides so water runs off. Create a shallow drainage ditch around the shelter perimeter if water tends to collect.

**Step 6: Create ventilation and entrance.**
Dig a sloped tunnel entrance 3–4 feet long and 3 feet high leading into the main chamber. This entrance acts as a cold trap, reducing drafts and preventing wind from entering the main shelter. The tunnel should slope downward as it approaches the main chamber. At the far end of the shelter (opposite the entrance), create a small opening (6–8 inches diameter) for smoke ventilation. This opening can be covered with a bark flap that you adjust to control airflow.

**Step 7: Insulate the floor.**
Lay branches (2–3 inches diameter) on the floor, spaced 12 inches apart. Cover these with smaller branches or straw, then add a deep layer (12–18 inches) of leaves, moss, and insulation. This creates a comfortable sleeping area elevated off the cold ground. In extremely cold climates, add a second layer of insulation after the branches.

**Step 8: Interior improvements.**
Create sleeping ledges or platforms on one or both sides of the shelter by raising the insulation layer. This keeps you 12–18 inches off the floor. Construct small niches or shelves for storing food, water, and supplies. If the shelter is large enough, designate a separate area near the entrance as a cold trap and food storage.

**Step 9: Structural reinforcement.**
After several weeks, inspect the roof for settlement cracks or areas where the earth has collapsed. Add additional support posts or beams if needed. The earth will compress; monitor and reinforce seasonally.

**Duration:** A semi-subterranean shelter lasts years if the roof is properly maintained. Water is the main threat—monitor drainage after heavy rain. Inspect the entrance tunnel for erosion. Re-seal the waterproof layer every 1–2 years if water seeping appears.

**Alternative designs:** In areas with permafrost, dig only partially into the earth (12–18 inches) to avoid thawing issues. In areas with hard rock, use shallow cuts and build the majority of the shelter above ground with stone walls. In areas with snow, build the shelter at ground level and allow snow to accumulate around it for insulation.

## Verification

A good shelter meets these criteria:

1. **Weathertight:** No rain leaks through the roof. Pour water on the roof and verify it runs off the exterior; none should drip inside. Check that seams and overlaps are tight.
2. **Windproof:** Air cannot blow through gaps or under walls. Light a small piece of bark or grass and hold it near the entrance and walls. The smoke should flow toward the shelter (inward), not away from it. Entrances are small and protected with double doors or baffles if possible.
3. **Insulated:** Thick insulation on all sides (at least 1 foot in cold climates). You should feel significantly warmer inside than outside. Measure temperature difference with your hand: place it on the outside wall, then the inside. The difference should be at least 10–15 degrees Fahrenheit.
4. **Accessible:** You can enter and exit without collapsing the structure or crawling more than 15 feet. Practice exiting quickly in daylight. If you need to dig or tunnel to enter, ensure the entrance doesn't collapse when you exit.
5. **Sized appropriately:** Large enough to lie down in and stretch fully, but not so large that your body heat cannot warm it. As a rule, if you can fit 2–4 people comfortably, the shelter is properly sized for 1 person.
6. **Stable:** The structure does not sway, shift, or groan in moderate wind. Push hard on the walls and roof from multiple directions. Lashings should be tight with no slack. If something creaks or moves, reinforce it immediately.
7. **Safe:** No loose branches overhead that could fall on you. Check the roof from inside and outside. No risk of collapse from settling or weather. If you use fire inside, ensure ventilation is adequate—light a twig and watch the smoke to verify it exits through the intended opening.
8. **Protected from animals:** If the entrance is ground-level, close it with tight-fitting bark or branches so small animals cannot enter. In areas with large predators, ensure the shelter is elevated or locked at night.

**Formal test:** Before occupying your shelter for the night, spend at least 2–3 hours inside during daylight. Take notes on temperature, drafts, water leaks, and accessibility. Make repairs while light remains. If the temperature inside is more than 5 degrees Fahrenheit cooler than your normal body temperature (around 93 degrees inside when it's 30 degrees outside), the insulation is insufficient.

**Cold stress test:** In cold weather, spend 4–6 hours in the shelter including the evening. Monitor your core temperature: if you start shivering uncontrollably or feel drowsy, the shelter is not providing adequate warmth and needs additional insulation.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Building too large:** A shelter twice as big as needed will be twice as hard to keep warm. Build small—the interior volume should be just large enough to lie down and sit up. Every extra cubic foot means more air to heat. Exception: if you're sheltering multiple people, ensure adequate space but avoid cathedral ceilings.

2. **Skimping on insulation:** Most people underestimate how much insulation is needed. Add more than seems necessary, especially overhead and under the floor. Insulation is more important than structural integrity. A poorly insulated shelter will kill you in cold weather; a poorly built but well-insulated shelter might save you. The general rule: if you can see daylight through the insulation from outside, add more.

3. **Poor siting:** Building near dead trees, in a wind funnel, or in a flood zone guarantees regret. Scout the area carefully before investing time. Dead trees (snags) can drop branches or fall entirely in wind. Wind funnels occur where mountains or hills channel wind into narrow valleys. Flood zones are anywhere water has previously pooled during storms. Check for signs: eroded banks, dead vegetation above the current ground level, water-worn rocks.

4. **Forgetting drainage:** Build on a slight slope with water running away, not toward your shelter. If standing water collects around your shelter, you will eventually wake up in a mud puddle. Create a small ditch around the shelter perimeter if the natural slope is inadequate. Slope the shelter entrance slightly outward so water exits rather than pooling.

5. **Leaving gaps:** Wind and cold find every gap. Fill them completely with insulation, bark, or mud. Mud chinking between logs is highly effective—mix mud with straw for better adhesion. Small gaps around branches can be sealed with clay, mud, or even leaves packed tightly.

6. **Using green wood for structural elements:** Freshly cut wood is heavy, difficult to lash tightly, and prone to splitting and warping. Use deadfall where possible. Exception: green wood for lashing points may actually grip better than aged wood. The rule is: green wood for framing is slower; deadfall is faster.

7. **Inadequate entry protection:** A large open door wastes heat. Make the entrance small and sealable. The entrance should be just large enough to crawl through (2–3 feet high and 2 feet wide). Use a two-stage entrance (an outer door and an inner door with space between) to trap warm air. In extreme cold, create a wind-break on the windward side of the entrance.

8. **Building in darkness:** Start shelter building in daylight. Never wait until dusk. You need light to properly secure lashings, assess the structure, test for gaps, and make repairs. If you start late in the day, you may end up sleeping in an incomplete shelter. Aim to complete 90% of the shelter by 2 hours before sunset.

9. **Ignoring local animals:** Ensure your shelter is secure against local wildlife. In areas with bears, build the shelter elevated or create a strong barrier. In areas with wolves or large predators, ensure walls are solid and the entrance can be locked. Small rodents can damage insulation and food stores; ensure gaps are filled.

10. **No ventilation plan:** If using fire inside (A-frame, wickiup), ensure adequate ventilation. Improperly vented fires cause carbon monoxide poisoning, which is often mistaken for drowsiness or cold. A person poisoned by CO will often not seek fresh air—they'll simply fall asleep. Always open a ventilation hole before lighting an interior fire, and ensure it remains open throughout the night.

11. **Building without testing:** Some people build a shelter and then only sleep in it when they're exhausted. Test your shelter in daylight. Spend an hour inside before night. Identify problems while you can still fix them. If it's not working, rebuild rather than sleep poorly and lose the next day to exhaustion.

12. **Overreliance on single shelter type:** Different conditions call for different shelters. Learn all five types. In one region, debris shelters might be fastest; in another, lean-tos. Master the variety so you can adapt to what materials are locally available.
