## Overview

Flint knapping is one skill. Making tools from bone and wood is another, faster path to functional equipment. A single animal bone becomes multiple tools: a harpoon point, fish hooks, needles, and scrapers. A piece of hard wood becomes a digging stick, handle, or club. Soft stone yields grinding tools and scrapers.

This chapter covers tools that do not require advanced stone-working, complementing the flint techniques in Vol 2, Ch 4. Focus on what you can make in an hour without prior experience.

## Materials & Tools

### Bone Tools

- **Source material:** Long bones (femur, tibia, humerus) from deer, cattle, or birds
- **Bone-working tools:** Rough stone, metal file (if salvaged), or another bone to break and shape
- **Shaping tools:** Sandstone or volcanic rock for grinding and finishing
- **Finishing:** Leather strap for polishing

### Wood Tools

- **Hardwood:** Oak, ash, or elm for handles and digging sticks (harder = more durable)
- **Softwood:** Pine or spruce for containers and quick shapes
- **Shaping tools:** Stone scraper, rough rock for hewing
- **Fire:** For hardening wooden points (optional but effective)

### Stone Tools

- **Grinding stones:** Granite, basalt (volcanic rock), or quartzite
- **Hand axes:** Soft stone (soapstone, basalt) or hard stone (flint, chert)
- **Scrapers:** Obsidian if available; granite or flint otherwise
- **Sharpening stone:** Fine-grit volcanic rock or sandstone

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Method 1: Bone Needles and Hooks

**Step 1: Select a bone.**
Long bones from small animals (rabbits) or the smaller bones from larger animals work best. The bone should be straight and at least 5 cm long.

**Step 2: Rough out the shape.**
Use a sharp stone to strike the bone along its length, splitting it or creating a blank. If the bone has natural length, carve a notch at one end for the eye (needle) or a barb (hook).

**Step 3: Carve the hook shape (for fish hooks).**
Using a pointed bone or sharp stone, carve a small hook at the tip. The hook should be subtle — just enough to catch a fish's mouth. Carve a barb pointing backward on the inside of the curve.

**Step 4: Drill the eye (for needles).**
For sewing needles, drill a small hole near one end. Use a sharp bone point and rotate it steadily while pushing into the bone. This takes patience — a minute or two per needle.

**Step 5: Sharpen and smooth.**
Use sandstone or a fine-grit volcanic rock to sharpen the point and smooth the surface. A smooth needle slides through hide; a rough one catches and tears.

**Step 6: Finish.**
Polish with a leather strap to remove any burrs. The finished needle should be smooth enough to slide easily through hide without snagging.

### Method 2: Bone Harpoon Points and Fish Spears

**Step 1: Select a long, straight bone.**
The bone should be 10–15 cm long and at least 1 cm in diameter. Split along the length to create a flat surface.

**Step 2: Carve barbs.**
Using a sharp stone, carve backward-pointing barbs on both sides of the tip. The barbs should be subtle — 2–3 mm deep — and angle backward so a fish cannot slide off.

**Step 3: Sharpen the tip.**
Grind the tip to a point on fine sandstone. The point should pierce hide and scale easily.

**Step 4: Lashing holes (optional).**
If you plan to attach the point to a wooden shaft, carve small notches or drill shallow holes on the sides of the base. This allows for secure lashing with sinew (see Vol 3, Ch 8).

**Step 5: Harden in fire (optional but effective).**
Heat the bone point carefully in fire until it is dark brown (not black — black means burnt and brittle). This hardens the surface and makes it less likely to splinter.

### Method 3: Digging Sticks and Wooden Levers

**Step 1: Select wood.**
Choose hardwood (oak, ash, elm) for tools that need to last. The stick should be straight, 1–2 meters long, and 3–5 cm in diameter at the base.

**Step 2: Shape a point (for digging sticks).**
Split the bottom end into a wedge shape. Use a stone scraper or rough stone to create a V-shaped point that can penetrate soil. The point should be 10–15 cm long.

**Step 3: Harden the point in fire.**
Hold the wooden point in fire for 1–2 minutes until it chars slightly (turns dark brown, not black). The charring hardens the surface and makes it less likely to split when struck.

**Step 4: For handles and levers, create a grip.**
If the stick needs a handle, carve a narrower section in the middle (2–3 cm diameter) using a stone scraper. This allows a firm grip without the tool rolling.

**Step 5: Sand smooth.**
Use fine sandstone to remove splinters and make the handle smooth. A rough handle causes blisters and reduces control.

### Method 4: Wooden Containers and Scoops

**Step 1: Select a softwood log.**
Pine or spruce logs 15–30 cm in diameter and 20–40 cm long work well. Avoid wood that has splits or large knots.

**Step 2: Split in half.**
Using a wedge and hammer (or by carefully striking with a heavy stone), split the log lengthwise.

**Step 3: Hollow out the interior.**
Using a pointed stick or bone, scrape out the interior. Alternatively, burn out the interior by placing hot coals inside and letting them smolder. Scrape out ash and char as the wood burns.

**Step 4: Shape the rim.**
Smooth the rim with a stone scraper. The edge should be smooth enough not to splinter.

**Step 5: Sand and finish.**
Use fine sandstone to smooth the interior and exterior. A smooth container holds water better and lasts longer.

### Method 5: Stone Grinding Tools

**Step 1: Select stones.**
You need two: a mortar (lower, concave stone) and a pestle (handheld stone, rounded or oval). Granite and basalt work well. The mortar should be at least 15 cm across and 5–10 cm deep.

**Step 2: Create a bowl.**
Use a hard stone (flint or another granite piece) to strike the mortar repeatedly. Strike around the center in a spiral pattern, gradually deepening the depression. This takes 30–60 minutes of steady striking.

**Step 3: Test smoothness.**
Rub the interior with your hand. Rough spots catch and hurt seeds during grinding. Continue striking and smoothing until the interior is relatively smooth.

**Step 4: Select the pestle.**
Choose a river-worn stone (already rounded) or shape one from granite. The pestle should fit comfortably in your hand and be 7–10 cm in diameter.

**Step 5: Test.**
Grind some seeds or nuts. The tool should crush food without the pestle getting stuck.

### Method 6: Stone Hand Axes and Scrapers

**Step 1: Select stone.**
For soft volcanic rock (soapstone, basalt), use a stone hammer. For harder rock (granite), use another hard stone. Flint works but requires more skill (see Vol 2, Ch 4).

**Step 2: Strike the edge (for hand axes).**
Hold the stone in one hand, use another stone to strike glancing blows along one edge. Each strike removes a chip. The goal is to create a sharp edge running 5–10 cm along the stone.

**Step 3: Thin the body (optional).**
Strike the center to thin the stone slightly. A thinner hand axe is easier to control.

**Step 4: For scrapers, create a right angle.**
Strike one face hard enough to remove a large flake. The resulting edge will have two intersecting surfaces at roughly 90°. This is the scraper blade.

**Step 5: Sharpen further if needed.**
Small strikes at a steep angle (almost perpendicular to the blade) make the edge sharper.

**Step 6: Smooth the grip.**
If the hand axe will be held (not hafted), round the opposite end of the striking edge by striking more carefully.

## Verification

**Needles work if:** The eye does not crack the bone, and the needle slides smoothly through hide without snagging.

**Hooks work if:** The barbs are present and subtle, the point is sharp, and a fish cannot easily slide off once caught.

**Harpoon points work if:** The barbs are symmetric and point backward, the point is sharp, and the point does not splinter when struck firmly.

**Digging sticks work if:** The point penetrates soil easily and the wood does not crack when struck.

**Grinding tools work if:** The mortar holds a clear depression and seeds crush under steady grinding without the pestle sticking.

**Hand axes work if:** The edge is sharp enough to scrape bone, and the stone does not shatter when struck.

**Signs of failure:**
- Bone splinters when carved: the bone is too fresh or too old; try another specimen
- Wooden point splits in fire: the wood was too dry or the heat was too intense; start over with shorter heating
- Grinding stone cracks while striking: use a softer stone or strike more gently
- Scrapers become dull quickly: they are made from soft stone; harder stone lasts longer

## Common Mistakes

- **Using wet bone.** Fresh bone is difficult to carve. Let bone dry for a few days first.
- **Carving hooks too large.** Small, subtle barbs work better than large hooks. A fish hook should catch the corner of the mouth, not impale.
- **Fire-hardening too long.** Bone turns black and becomes brittle if overheated. Dark brown is the target.
- **Not smoothing needles.** A rough needle shreds hide and makes sewing slower. Sand thoroughly.
- **Digging stick tip too blunt.** A rounded tip does not penetrate soil; carve a clear V-point.
- **Grinding mortars too shallow.** Seeds scatter from a shallow mortar. Strike for a full minute to create a proper bowl.
- **Using the wrong stone for axes.** Flint creates sharp edges (see Vol 2, Ch 4) but is harder to knap. Soft stone like basalt is faster for beginners.
- **Not testing tools before relying on them.** A needle that breaks mid-sewing is useless. Test each tool before depending on it.
