## Overview

Thermionic emission is the release of electrons from a heated metal surface. It is one of the most important discoveries in the history of electronics.

When you heat metal hot enough (approximately 1000–2000°C), electrons at the surface gain enough energy to break free and escape into the surrounding space. If those electrons are captured and directed by electric fields, they can be used to detect radio signals, amplify weak electrical signals, and ultimately transmit information wirelessly.

This chapter explains:

1. **The physics of thermionic emission:** Why heated metal releases electrons
2. **Materials that emit:** Which metals work best for thermionic cathodes
3. **Practical cathode design:** How to construct efficient thermionic emitters
4. **Applications:** How thermionic emission enables vacuum tubes and early radio

Understanding thermionic emission is the gateway to vacuum tube technology and everything that follows.

## Part 1: The Physics of Thermionic Emission

### Why Electrons Escape from Hot Metal

**Electrons in cold metal:**

In a metal at room temperature, electrons are bound to atoms by electrical attraction. Electrons have kinetic energy (they vibrate and move randomly), but this energy is typically 0.025 eV (electron-volts) at room temperature.

To escape the metal, an electron must overcome the **work function** — the minimum energy barrier at the metal surface. Work function is typically 2–5 eV depending on the metal.

**Electrons in hot metal:**

When metal is heated, electrons vibrate faster. At 1500°C, average electron kinetic energy increases to approximately 0.12 eV. This is still far below the work function (2–5 eV), yet some electrons escape.

Why? **Statistical distribution of energy.** Not all electrons have the same energy. Some have much more energy than the average. In hot metal, a small fraction of electrons have enough energy to escape.

The higher the temperature, the larger the fraction of electrons with sufficient energy.

### The Richardson-Dushman Equation

The thermionic emission current can be calculated using the Richardson-Dushman equation:

```
J = A × T² × e^(-φ/kT)
```

Where:
- **J** = emission current density (amps per cm²)
- **A** = Richardson constant (approximately 60 A/cm²/K²)
- **T** = absolute temperature in Kelvin
- **φ** = work function in eV
- **k** = Boltzmann constant (8.6 × 10⁻⁵ eV/K)
- **e** = exponential function

**Key insight:** Emission current increases exponentially with temperature. A small increase in temperature causes a large increase in electron emission.

**Examples:**

- At 1000 K: J ≈ 0.001 A/cm² (tiny current)
- At 1500 K: J ≈ 1 A/cm² (practical for vacuum tubes)
- At 2500 K: J ≈ 100 A/cm² (very strong emission)

### Work Function and Material Selection

Different metals have different work functions (energy barrier to escape):

| Metal | Work Function (eV) | Melting Point (°C) |
|-------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Tungsten | 4.5 | 3422 |
| Molybdenum | 4.6 | 2623 |
| Nickel | 5.6 | 1455 |
| Platinum | 5.7 | 1768 |
| Thoriated tungsten | 2.6–2.8 | ~3400 |
| Oxide-coated cathode | 1.0–1.5 | &lt;1200 |

**For practical vacuum tubes:**

- **Tungsten:** High work function (4.5 eV), very high melting point (3422°C). Requires heating to 2300–2600°C for useful emission. Durable and long-lasting.

- **Thoriated tungsten:** Tungsten alloy with approximately 1–2% thorium. Lower work function (2.6–2.8 eV), requires heating to only 1800–2000°C. More efficient than pure tungsten.

- **Oxide-coated cathode:** Metal coated with metal oxides (barium oxide, strontium oxide). Very low work function (1.0–1.5 eV), operates at only 900–1100°C. Efficient and long-lasting, but sensitive to activation procedure. Most common in modern tubes.

### Emission Current and Space Charge

When electrons are emitted, they create a cloud of negative charge around the filament. This charge repels other electrons from escaping—the **space charge effect**.

In a vacuum with no collecting electrode, the space charge limits emission to a relatively small current. To collect more electrons, apply a positive voltage to an electrode (plate) near the filament. This attracts the electrons and pulls them away from the filament region, allowing more emission.

## Part 2: Thermionic Cathodes in Vacuum Tubes

### Direct Heating (Filament Cathode)

The filament itself is heated and emits electrons.

**Advantages:**
- Simple (just one element)
- Direct control of emission by adjusting filament current

**Disadvantages:**
- Filament current flows through the electron beam, creating complications
- High filament current required (2–10 amps typical)
- Filament is fragile and has limited lifetime

**Construction:**

1. Draw tungsten or thoriated tungsten wire into a thin filament (0.5–2 mm diameter)
2. Form into a tight coil to maximize surface area in limited space
3. Support with ceramic or metal supports inside the vacuum tube
4. Pass heating current through the filament (2–15 amps, 2–10 volts)

**Operation:**

- Filament glows bright red (900–1200°C) to yellow (1200–1600°C) to nearly white (1600–2600°C)
- Color indicates temperature (red = ~900°C, yellow = ~1200°C, white = ~2600°C)
- At proper operating temperature, useful electron emission occurs (1–100 mA depending on filament size and design)

### Indirect Heating (Heater and Cathode Separate)

A heater element warms a separate cathode electrode.

**Advantages:**
- Cathode is isolated from filament current
- Allows use of oxide-coated cathodes
- More efficient (oxide coatings have lower work function)
- Longer cathode lifetime

**Disadvantages:**
- More complex (two heating elements instead of one)
- Slight thermal lag between heater and cathode

**Construction:**

1. Heater: Tungsten wire (0.5–1 mm), heated by 6–10 amps at 6–10 volts
2. Cathode: Metal cylinder (nickel or molybdenum) surrounding the heater
3. Space between heater and cathode: 1–2 mm (allows heat transfer via radiation and conduction)
4. Cathode coated with oxide mixture (barium oxide, strontium oxide, calcium oxide)

**Operation:**

- Heater reaches approximately 1100°C
- Heat radiates to surrounding cathode
- Cathode reaches approximately 900–1100°C
- Oxide coating emits electrons efficiently at lower temperature than bare metal
- Electron emission: 10–500 mA depending on cathode size and coating

### Thermionic Emitter Design Considerations

**Surface area:** More surface area = more electron emission. Designs use coils, spirals, or multi-strand configurations to maximize area while fitting in limited space.

**Temperature uniformity:** Electrons are emitted preferentially from hottest regions. Uniform heating ensures even emission distribution. Heating patterns (heater coil spacing) affect this.

**Thermal management:** Heat is generated internally but must be radiated to surroundings. Black body radiation improves heat transfer.

**Emission lifetime:** Electron bombardment and chemical reactions degrade cathodes over time. Typical lifetime: 100–10,000 hours depending on design and operating conditions.

## Part 3: Practical Thermionic Emission in Vacuum

### Creating a Vacuum Environment

Electrons are emitted, but what happens to them depends on the surrounding environment.

**In air:**

Emitted electrons collide with air molecules almost immediately (within nanometers) and are scattered. No useful electron beam forms.

**In vacuum:**

Electrons travel in straight lines until they hit an electrode. This allows them to be collected and used.

**Vacuum requirement:**

Practical vacuum tubes need pressure below 0.001 torr (approximately 10⁻³ millimeters of mercury). At this pressure, the mean free path of electrons (average distance before collision) is approximately 1 meter—long enough for useful electron trajectories in a small tube.

**Achieving vacuum:**

1. Use a mechanical pump to reduce pressure to approximately 0.1 torr
2. Use a diffusion pump or turbomolecular pump for ultra-high vacuum
3. Use getters (special materials) to absorb residual gases
4. Seal the tube after pumping down

### Observing Thermionic Emission

**Simple experiment:**

1. Create a glass tube with two electrodes: a heated filament (cathode) and a metal plate (anode)
2. Evacuate to approximately 0.001 torr
3. Heat filament to approximately 1500°C (dull red color)
4. Apply positive voltage (100–200 volts) to plate relative to filament
5. Observe current flow in external circuit (ammeter shows 10–50 mA)

The current is the electron beam traveling from hot filament to cold plate.

**Properties of the emission:**

- **Current proportional to temperature:** Higher filament temperature → higher current
- **Current proportional to voltage:** Higher plate voltage → higher current (up to saturation)
- **Saturation effect:** At high voltage, all available electrons are collected; further voltage increase doesn't increase current
- **Visible glow:** Electrons striking the anode create light (visible glow or X-rays)

### Emission Control and Regulation

**Temperature control:**

Varying the filament heating current controls the electron emission rate.

- 0–2 amps: Minimal emission (filament cool, dark)
- 2–5 amps: Practical emission (filament glowing dull red, 100–500 mA emission current)
- 5–10 amps: Strong emission (filament bright, up to several amps emission)
- Above 10 amps: Very strong emission, but filament degrades rapidly

**Voltage control:**

Varying the plate voltage (anode) from 0 to several hundred volts controls the collected electron current.

- 0 volts: Few electrons collected (some thermal electrons have enough energy anyway)
- 50 volts: Moderate electron collection
- 100–200 volts: Strong electron collection (approaching saturation)
- Above 200 volts: Saturation (all available electrons collected)

## Part 4: Applications to Vacuum Tubes

### The Basic Vacuum Diode

The simplest vacuum tube contains:

- **Cathode (heated filament or oxide-coated element):** Emits electrons
- **Anode (plate):** Collects electrons

**Operation:**

1. Filament is heated (2–10 amps)
2. Electrons are emitted from filament
3. Positive voltage is applied to plate (50–300 volts depending on tube design)
4. Electrons are accelerated toward plate and collected
5. Electron current flows in external circuit

**Electron trajectory:**

Electrons travel in straight lines from cathode to anode (in absence of deflecting fields). With a positive anode, all electrons are attracted and collected.

### Control of Electron Current

In the basic diode, emission current is controlled by:

1. **Temperature:** Filament current adjusts emission rate
2. **Anode voltage:** Voltage determines collection efficiency

Later vacuum tubes add additional electrodes (**grid**) to control current with small voltages instead of large temperature changes. This enables amplification and switching.

### Practical Efficiency

Thermionic cathodes are thermally inefficient:

- Most heat goes into heating the metal
- Only a small fraction becomes electron energy

Efficiency = (useful electron current × average electron energy) / (heating power)

Typical efficiency: 1–5% (meaning 95–99% of heat is wasted).

Despite low efficiency, thermionic emission was revolutionary because it enabled the first electronic devices.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Thermionic Emission from Tungsten

**Scenario:** Design a cathode for a vacuum tube using tungsten filament.

**Parameters:**

- Target emission current: 100 mA (0.1 amps)
- Filament length: 20 cm
- Filament diameter: 0.5 mm
- Work function of tungsten: 4.5 eV

**Calculation:**

Using Richardson-Dushman equation:

J = A × T² × e^(-φ/kT)

For 100 mA from a 20 cm length, 0.5 mm diameter filament:
- Surface area = π × 0.05 cm × 20 cm ≈ 3.1 cm²
- Required J = 100 mA / 3.1 cm² ≈ 32 mA/cm²

Using the equation and work function 4.5 eV:

At T = 2000 K (approximately 1727°C):
J ≈ 60 × (2000)² × e^(-4.5/(8.6×10⁻⁵×2000))
J ≈ 60 × 4×10⁶ × e^(-26)
J ≈ very small (not enough)

At T = 2400 K (approximately 2127°C):
J ≈ 60 × (2400)² × e^(-4.5/(8.6×10⁻⁵×2400))
J ≈ 60 × 5.76×10⁶ × e^(-21.8)
J ≈ 32 mA/cm² (achieves target)

**Conclusion:** Heat tungsten filament to approximately 2400 K (2127°C, yellow-white glow) to achieve 100 mA emission. This requires approximately 5–8 amps of filament current at 2–4 volts.

### Example 2: Oxide-Coated Cathode Comparison

**Scenario:** Compare tungsten vs. oxide-coated cathode for same emission current.

**Tungsten filament:**
- Work function: 4.5 eV
- Required temperature: 2400 K (2127°C)
- Filament power: approximately 10–20 watts

**Oxide-coated cathode:**
- Work function: 1.5 eV (much lower)
- Required temperature for same emission: 1000 K (727°C)
- Filament power: approximately 2–4 watts
- Additional heating for cathode: approximately 1–2 watts
- **Total power: approximately 3–6 watts (2–3 times more efficient)**

**Advantages of oxide-coated:**
- Lower operating temperature (longer lifetime)
- Lower power consumption
- Can use weaker heater
- More stable emission

**Disadvantages:**
- Oxide coating can degrade if exposed to air (requires careful manufacturing)
- Slightly longer warm-up time

3. You understand work function and know which metals work best
4. You can design and heat a thermionic cathode (filament or oxide-coated)

If you can create a thermionic cathode, heat it to observable emission, and collect electrons in a vacuum, you understand thermionic emission.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Operating filament too cool.** Thermionic emission is exponential with temperature. A filament at 1500°C produces very little emission. Need 2000–2600°C for practical vacuum tubes. (Visual guide: dull red = too cool; yellow-white = about right.)

2. **Attempting thermionic emission in air.** Electrons collide with air molecules immediately. A vacuum (under 0.001 torr) is essential.

3. **Forgetting vacuum maintenance.** Leaks allow air back in; emission disappears. Periodic checking with a vacuum gauge is essential. (A UV lamp shined on getter material shows if vacuum is lost: shiny getter = good vacuum; dull/white getter = poor vacuum.)

4. **Overheating the filament.** Filaments have maximum temperature ratings (typically 2600°C for tungsten). Exceeding causes rapid degradation. Observe filament color and limit to yellow or yellow-white.

5. **Using wrong filament material.** Nickel or iron filaments don't work well for thermionic emission (too high work function). Use tungsten, thoriated tungsten, or oxide-coated cathodes.

6. **Expecting useful current at low temperature.** Thermionic emission follows exponential function. At 1000°C, emission is negligible. Expect significant emission only above 1500–2000°C.

7. **Inadequate plate voltage.** Without sufficient voltage on the anode (typically 50–300 volts), most electrons don't reach it. Space charge effect limits emission.

8. **Creating arcs or shorts.** High voltage with insufficient vacuum can cause arcing. Maintain good vacuum to prevent this.

9. **Not accounting for warm-up time.** Filaments take 10–30 seconds to reach operating temperature. Don't expect instant current.

10. **Assuming thermionic emission is efficient.** Most energy goes into heating, not into useful electron current. Efficiency is typically 1–5%.
