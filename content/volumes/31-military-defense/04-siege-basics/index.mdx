## Overview

A siege is a prolonged assault in which an attacker surrounds your settlement and prevents supplies from entering. Unlike a battle, a siege is a test of endurance, not strength.

A siege succeeds when:
- Your food or water runs out.
- Disease breaks out (from poor sanitation or crowding).
- Morale collapses (people panic or mutiny).
- Your fortifications cannot be repaired as they are damaged.

A siege fails (the attacker is forced to leave) when:
- Your settlement receives reinforcements.
- You break out successfully.
- The attacker runs out of supplies.
- The attacker's army deteriorates (disease, desertion, starvation).

This chapter teaches how to endure a siege. Assume that you cannot count on reinforcements or relief. Assume you are alone.

## Prerequisites

Understand fortification and defense (Vol 31, Ch 1-3). Understand negotiation and authority (Vol 8, Ch 2).

## Part 1: Preparation for Siege

Preventing a siege is easier than surviving one. Preparation begins before an attacker arrives.

### Water Storage and Access

Water is your most critical resource. A settlement can survive 3 weeks without food; it cannot survive 3 days without water.

**Ensure water access cannot be cut:**
- **Well inside the fortifications:** If your settlement has a well within the walls, the attacker cannot cut water supply by occupying external sources.
- **Spring source inside:** If the water source is inside the walls, it is protected.
- **Multiple water sources:** Have at least 2 sources (well + cistern, or 2 wells). If one is damaged or contaminated, you have another.

**Cistern construction** (if you don't have a well):
- A cistern is a large tank for storing rainwater or spring water.
- Build one inside your fortifications.
- Size: Estimate 100 liters per person per day (drinking, cooking, animals, sanitation). For 200 people over 4 months, you need 2.4 million liters—unrealistic.
- More realistic: Rainwater cisterns (use the roof area of buildings to collect water during rains).
- Cistern size: Even a 10,000-liter cistern extends water supply by a few weeks.

**Well-digging (if building capacity exists):**
- A shallow well (5–10 meters deep) is easier to dig than a deep well.
- Once dug, it is not dependent on weather (unlike cisterns) or flowing water (unlike rivers).
- Takes 2–4 weeks of labor by a team of 5–10 people to dig a functional well.

**Water purification during siege:**
- Boiling kills pathogens (use fire).
- Settling in containers (let sediment fall out over a day) improves clarity.
- Cloth straining removes large particles.
- Avoid contaminated sources (dead animals, latrine seepage).

### Food Storage

**Baseline:** 500–750 grams of grain per person per day. Over 6 months, 200 people need 54–81 tons of grain.

Most post-collapse settlements cannot store this much. Plan for a shorter siege (2–4 months).

**Storage priorities** (in order):
1. **Grain:** Longest shelf life (years if kept dry). Takes up the most space.
2. **Legumes (beans, lentils):** Protein, long shelf life, compact.
3. **Salt, sugar, oil:** Long shelf life; not filling but improve nutrition.
4. **Preserved meat (dried, salted):** High calorie, space-efficient.
5. **Root crops (potatoes, turnips):** If you have root cellars (cool, dark storage), these last 3–6 months.

**Storage location:** Build a fortified, dry room for grain storage. It must be:
- Inside the fortifications (so it is not captured if outer walls fall).
- Secure (locked to prevent theft or panic raids).
- Protected from moisture (cover with cloth, keep off ground).
- Elevated on the settlement (easier to defend a single location).

**Grain preservation:**
- Keep grain in sealed containers (clay jars with covers, wooden barrels).
- Stored in a cool, dark location extends shelf life (lower temperature slows spoilage).
- Check for rodents and insects regularly.

### Animal Resources

Livestock cannot survive a long siege without fodder. Plan to slaughter animals for meat before the siege begins.

**Slaughter schedule** (if siege is likely):
1. Keep only breeding animals (a few breeding cows, goats, chickens).
2. Slaughter draft animals (horses, oxen) for meat—preserve by salting or smoking.
3. Slaughter excess animals (extra goats, excess chickens) for immediate meat supply.

**Fodder for surviving animals:**
- If the siege lasts months, you need to feed surviving animals.
- Stored hay (2–5 tons for surviving animals) extends their utility.
- Without fodder, slaughter them rather than watch them starve.

### Weapons and Tools

A siege means fortifications will be damaged and require constant repair. Ensure supplies:
- **Replacement tools:** Axes, shovels, hammers (for repairs).
- **Materials:** Wood for repairs, stone or clay for wall repairs.
- **Weapons:** Extra arrows, spearheads, bow strings.
- **Repair materials:** Rope, cloth, leather (for patching and binding).

### Medical Supplies

Siege means wounds, illness, and disease. Prepare:
- **Herbs and medicines:** Preparations for pain, fever, infection (Vol 31, Ch 9).
- **Bandages and cloth:** Clean cloth for wound dressing.
- **Tools:** Needle for sutures, knife for cleaning wounds.
- **Isolation area:** A building where sick people can be kept separate to prevent spread of disease.

### Morale Supplies

A long siege is psychologically exhausting. Prepare:
- **Alcohol (if culturally acceptable):** Small amounts for rationing (morale boost, pain relief).
- **Tobacco or other mild stimulants** (if available).
- **Games, music, stories:** Entertainment for the long wait.

## Part 2: When the Siege Begins

The first days of a siege are critical. Decisions made now determine whether the settlement survives.

### Immediate Actions

**1. Secure the perimeter:** Ensure all gates are closed, walls are manned, no one leaves or enters without authorization.

**2. Count supplies:** Gather all food, water, and materials. Create an accurate inventory. Know exactly how long supplies last.

**3. Calculate daily rations:** Divide total food by people by estimated siege duration. If supplies last 120 days, each person gets less than 500g of grain per day (uncomfortable but sustainable).

**4. Shelter non-combatants:** Move children, elderly, and non-combatants to the strongest part of the settlement (inner fortifications). They consume food and water but do not defend.

**5. Prepare the army:** Assign defenders to permanent posts. Expect them to rotate through duties (sentry duty, repairs, rest) for weeks or months.

**6. Assess morale:** Are people panicking? Confident? Angry? Address immediate concerns (fear of starvation, fear of being abandoned).

### Establishing Routine

A siege is a marathon, not a sprint. Establish a daily routine that:
- **Prevents boredom:** Defenders need tasks (repairs, training, patrols).
- **Preserves morale:** Regular meals, time for socializing, religious ceremonies (if applicable).
- **Maintains fitness:** Guards should exercise; disease often spreads due to inactivity.
- **Rations supplies:** Strict accountability for food, water, and fuel use.

**Sample day routine:**
- **6 AM:** Wake, breakfast (thin porridge, water).
- **7 AM – 12 PM:** Work shift (fortification repairs, training, maintenance).
- **12 PM:** Lunch (small portion of bread and dried meat).
- **1 PM – 5 PM:** Second work shift or guard duty.
- **5 PM:** Dinner (thin stew, bread).
- **6 PM – 9 PM:** Free time (games, music, socializing).
- **9 PM:** Bedtime.

Repeat for weeks or months.

## Part 3: Managing the Siege

### Rationing Food and Water

**Water rationing:**
- **Minimum:** 1–2 liters per person per day (survival level).
- **Comfortable:** 3–4 liters per person per day (normal life).
- If water is running low, drop to 1 liter per person and warn that standards will be strict.

**Food rationing:**
- **Standard ration:** 500 grams of grain per person per day (about 1200 calories, inadequate for heavy labor but sustainable).
- **Reduced ration:** 250 grams per day (about 600 calories, uncomfortable but sustainable for 3–6 months).
- **Starvation ration:** 150 grams per day (about 360 calories, creates weakness and disease, not recommended beyond 2 weeks).

**Supplementation:**
- Hunt animals inside the settlement (if possible) for meat.
- Forage for plants (dandelions, nettles, etc.) if allowed outside walls temporarily.
- Breed rabbits or chickens inside the settlement for meat and eggs.

**Ration enforcement:**
- All people eat the same ration (no privileged access to food).
- Punishment for stealing food must be severe (exile, loss of food privileges).
- Children and nursing mothers may be given extra rations (prevents malnutrition-related deaths).

### Repair and Maintenance

Attackers will damage your fortifications. Plan for constant repairs.

**Repair duty rotation:**
- Assign 20–30% of defenders to repair duty each day (not defending, but fixing walls, gates, structures).
- Rotate people so defenders do not get exhausted.
- Have all necessary materials (wood, stone, clay) stockpiled near damage points.

**Prioritize repairs:**
1. **Water system:** If the well is damaged, fix it immediately.
2. **Gates:** If gates are broken, repair them urgently.
3. **Walls:** Patch holes large enough for people to climb through.
4. **Morale:** Fix damaged buildings that people live in (shelter deterioration destroys morale).

### Disease and Sanitation

Siege creates crowding, which spreads disease. This can kill more people than the actual fighting.

**Prevention:**
- **Latrines:** Maintain latrines outside the heavily populated areas. If outside the walls, dig them far from the water source.
- **Bathing:** Even in siege, people should bathe or wash regularly (reduces lice, skin infections).
- **Waste disposal:** Remove dead animals, rotting food, and garbage regularly. Bury them far from water sources.
- **Isolation:** Any person showing signs of illness (cough, fever, rash) should be moved away from the main population.

**Management of illness:**
- Establish an isolation area with caretakers.
- Limit contact between sick and healthy people.
- Provide healers and medical supplies (Vol 4: Medicine).
- Accept that some people will die (disease is not always preventable).

### Morale Management

A long siege breaks morale. People lose hope, question leadership, and may threaten mutiny.

**Maintaining morale:**
- **Communication:** Leaders should speak to people regularly, explain the situation, and answer questions honestly.
- **Small victories:** Celebrate successful defense, repelled attacks, or good weather. Small wins matter psychologically.
- **Fairness:** Ensure rationing and duty assignments are perceived as fair. Favoritism destroys unity.
- **Meaning:** Help people understand why they are fighting (self-defense, protecting families, preserving their way of life).
- **Ritual:** Religious ceremonies, music, or storytelling provide psychological comfort.

**Warning signs of morale collapse:**
- Increased complaints or arguments.
- Desertion (people trying to leave).
- Refusal to follow orders.
- Self-harm or suicide.

If morale is collapsing, consider what will restore it (better food, a successful sortie, negotiation with the attacker) or whether surrender is necessary.

## Part 4: Sallies and Sorties

A sortie is an attack outside the walls by a small force during a siege. It is risky but can break a deadlock.

### When to Attempt a Sortie

**Attempt a sortie when:**
- The attacker's army is weakened (disease, low morale, weakly positioned).
- You have a specific, achievable objective (destroy siege equipment, kill the attacker's supply line, recover resources).
- You have defenders to spare (10% of your army is enough; do not risk more).

**Do NOT attempt a sortie when:**
- The attacker is strongly positioned outside the walls.
- You have no clear objective (random fighting wastes lives).
- Defenders are exhausted or demoralized.

### Planning a Sortie

**The plan:**
1. **Timing:** Choose a moment when the attacker is least alert (dawn, during a ceremony, poor weather).
2. **Force:** Use your fastest, best-armed defenders (50–100 people).
3. **Objective:** Clear target (destroy siege towers, raid supply depot, kill key commanders).
4. **Retreat:** Plan the retreat before you go—do not fight to the death.

**Execution:**
1. Open the gate and charge quickly to overwhelm the initial defenders.
2. Strike the objective hard and fast.
3. Retreat to the gate immediately (do not stay to fight for territory).
4. Seal the gate behind you.

**Cost-benefit:** A successful sortie that kills 20% of the attacker's army at the cost of 5% of your defenders is worthwhile.

### Psychological Impact

A successful sortie can:
- Restore settler morale (we can defeat them).
- Damage attacker morale (we are not helpless).
- Create the impression that you are stronger than you are.

A failed sortie can:
- Devastate settler morale.
- Embolden the attacker (they are stronger than we thought).
- Cost defenders you cannot replace.

## Part 5: Negotiation During Siege

Negotiation is a valid option. Sometimes surrender on good terms is better than fighting to the last person.

### Negotiation Objectives

**From the settlement's perspective:**
- Safe passage out (if surrender is necessary, negotiate safe evacuation).
- Hostages released (if your people are captured, negotiate their return).
- Terms of surrender (taxation, vassalage, ransom—anything short of massacre or slavery).

### Negotiation Protocol

**1. Initiate contact:** A trusted representative under a flag of truce approaches the attacker's commander.

**2. State the proposal:** "We wish to discuss terms of surrender" or "We propose a negotiated settlement."

**3. Listen:** Hear the attacker's demands. Are they reasonable?

**4. Respond:** Offer counter-proposals (taxation instead of massacre, ransom instead of slavery).

**5. Agree or break off:** If terms are acceptable, agree and execute (opening gates, paying ransom, accepting governance). If terms are unacceptable, break off and prepare for continued siege.

### When to Surrender

**Consider surrender when:**
- Food or water will run out in days.
- Disease has killed 20%+ of your population.
- Fortifications are too damaged to defend.
- Morale is collapsing and mutiny is imminent.
- The attacker offers reasonable terms.

**Do NOT surrender when:**
- You still have supplies and defensive ability.
- The attacker demands massacre or slavery.
- You believe reinforcements are coming soon.
- You can negotiate from strength (repair a wall, achieve a military victory, receive external support).

## Part 6: Breaking a Siege

If surrender is unacceptable, you must break the siege by force.

### Breakout Attack

A breakout is a full-force assault to break through the siege line and escape.

**Preconditions:**
- You have enough defenders and supplies for one major fight.
- You have identified a weak point in the siege (thin line, poor terrain, distracted forces).
- You have a clear destination (a nearby friendly settlement, fortified position, or defensible terrain).

**Planning:**
1. **Concentration:** Gather all mobile defenders (mounted if possible, otherwise trained fighters).
2. **Objective:** Target the weakest point in the siege line.
3. **Speed:** Attack at dawn or dusk when visibility is poor, moving fast to break through before attackers regroup.
4. **Secondary objectives:** Have two assault groups; the second reinforces the first or creates a diversion.

**Execution:**
1. Defenders assault the weak point at maximum force.
2. Once a gap is opened, the non-combatants (children, elderly) move through quickly.
3. The second assault group covers the escape.
4. Retreat toward the destination, do not stop to fight.

**Cost:** A breakout costs 20–40% of your fighting force (expect heavy casualties).

**Outcome:** If successful, your settlement escapes. If unsuccessful, defenders retreat behind walls and the siege continues.

## Verification

Your siege readiness is sound if:
1. **Water access:** A well or cistern inside walls that cannot be cut off.
2. **Food storage:** 3+ months of grain for the entire population.
3. **Repair supplies:** Wood, stone, clay, and tools for fortification repairs.
4. **Medical supplies:** Herbs, bandages, and basic healing materials.
5. **Routine plan:** A daily routine that preserves morale and prevents disease.
6. **Ration system:** Clear rules for food and water rationing.
7. **Negotiation authority:** A leader authorized to negotiate surrender terms.

## Common Mistakes

**Inadequate water supply.** People will not last weeks without water. Every settlement should have a well inside the fortifications.

**Unsustainable rationing.** Cutting rations to 150 grams per day causes weakness, disease, and collapse. Maintain at least 300+ grams per day or people starve.

**Ignoring disease.** Crowding and poor sanitation kill more people than attacks. Maintain latrines and isolation areas.

**Reckless sorties.** A sortie that costs 30% of your defenders to kill 10% of attackers is a losing trade.

**Surrendering too early.** Siege lasts weeks or months. Do not surrender because of hunger in week 1 if you have food for week 8.

**Breakout without planning.** A disorganized escape often ends in massacre. Plan escape route and destination before attempting breakout.
