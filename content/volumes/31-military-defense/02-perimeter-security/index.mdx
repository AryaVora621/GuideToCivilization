## Overview

A well-fortified settlement is useless if your defenders are exhausted. Perimeter security is the daily work of watching your walls, gates, and boundaries. It involves:

1. **Guard rotations** that keep lookouts alert without exhausting community labor.
2. **Alarm systems** that communicate threats quickly.
3. **Checkpoints** that prevent unauthorized entry.
4. **Patrols** that detect threats before they reach walls.

This chapter teaches you how to organize these systems. The focus is on sustainability—a security system that works for years without consuming all your community's labor or driving defenders to despair.

## Prerequisites

Understand your fortification layout (Vol 31, Ch 1). Understand your community structure and labor capacity (Vol 8, Ch 3).

## Part 1: Guard Rotation System

### The 3-Watch System

The most sustainable guard system divides the day and night into thirds. Each watch is 8 hours long.

**Watch periods** (assuming sunrise at 6 AM, sunset at 6 PM):
- **Day watch 1** (6 AM – 2 PM): Morning and early afternoon. Busiest time (traders, workers come and go).
- **Day watch 2** (2 PM – 10 PM): Late afternoon and evening. Moderately busy. Shift includes twilight (hardest to see).
- **Night watch** (10 PM – 6 AM): Dark hours. Quietest time, hardest on defenders (darkness + fatigue).

**Guards per watch:**
- **Day watch:** 2–4 guards (one per major gate or wall section, plus one floating).
- **Night watch:** 2–3 guards (fewer threats, but fatigue is the enemy).
- **Total guard force required:** 8–11 people for a basic system.

### Rotation Schedule (4-Week Cycle)

Create a schedule that rotates guards through all three watches over time. A 4-week cycle is sustainable; longer cycles cause people to forget their rotation; shorter cycles are too disruptive.

**Example for a 9-person guard force:**
- Week 1: Guards A, B, C on Day Watch 1; D, E, F on Day Watch 2; G, H, I on Night Watch.
- Week 2: Rotate everyone forward one watch (A, B, C move to Day Watch 2; D, E, F to Night Watch; G, H, I to Day Watch 1).
- Week 3: Rotate again.
- Week 4: Rotate again.
- Repeat after 4 weeks.

**Result:** Each guard works Day 1, Day 2, and Night roughly equally over the cycle. After 4 weeks, they return to their starting assignment (if rotation works properly).

### Rest Between Watches

**Critical:** Ensure guards have at least 16 hours off-duty between watches. A person who stands watch from 6 AM – 2 PM should not return to work until 6 AM the next day (16 hours of rest).

This prevents sleep deprivation and poor decision-making.

### Guard Responsibilities During Watch

**During the watch:**
- Walk the entire perimeter once per hour (or twice per 8-hour shift).
- Observe and report: strangers, unusual activity, damaged fortifications, signs of animal threats.
- Operate gates: check people entering, record travelers.
- Respond to alarms (see Part 2 below).
- Keep weapons and tools accessible (spear, bow, water, food).

**Log or report at end of watch:**
- Any unusual activity.
- Damage to walls, gates, or structures.
- Number of people who entered/exited.
- Weather, visibility, animal signs.

Keep a simple log (even if just marks on a stick for each day). This creates a record.

### Command Structure

Designate one **Watch Captain** per watch (or one for all watches). The Watch Captain:
- Assigns guards to positions each day.
- Authorizes entry/exit (or escalates decisions to the settlement leader).
- Coordinates with the Night Watch Captain if a threat is detected.
- Reports to the settlement leader at the end of their watch.

Choose Watch Captains who are:
- Calm under pressure.
- Trusted by other guards.
- Capable of making quick decisions.
- Not too proud to ask for help.

### Scaling for Larger Settlements

If your settlement grows to 200+ people, expand the system:
- Add more guards per watch (4–6 instead of 2–3).
- Add sub-positions: Gate Guard, Tower Guard, Patrol Guard.
- Create a day-time peace officer or constable who watches the settlement interior (not just the perimeter).
- Keep Watch Captains but add a **Guard Chief** who oversees all watches.

## Part 2: Alarm Systems

Alarm systems communicate a threat to the entire settlement in seconds. Without alarms, only guards near a threat know it is happening.

### Simple Alarm: Bells

**Setup:**
- Mount a large bell (or metal object that rings) on the wall or a central tower.
- Guards with access to a rope or hammer.
- Agreed-upon ring patterns for different threats.

**Ring patterns:**
- **One ring, pause, one ring:** An unknown group approaching. Prepare but do not panic.
- **Three fast rings:** Armed threat. Take defensive positions.
- **Continuous rapid ringing:** Active attack. Sound the general alarm.

**Advantages:** Audible across entire settlement. Works in dark. Requires no special equipment (any metal and rope works).

**Disadvantages:** Easily confused; people might not remember patterns; deaf people cannot hear it.

### Better Alarm: Signal Flags

**Setup:**
- Flags of different colors at the highest point of your settlement (top of tower, high building).
- Designated meaning for each color.
- Line-of-sight visibility to the entire settlement.

**Example:**
- **No flag:** All clear.
- **Blue flag:** Stranger approaching, check credentials.
- **Red flag:** Armed threat.
- **Red + Blue together:** Attack underway.

**Advantages:** Clear, unambiguous, visible from distance, accessible to deaf people.

**Disadvantages:** Requires clear line of sight; useless in fog or darkness; slow (takes time to hoist flag).

### Hybrid System (Best for Most Settlements)

Use both bells and flags:
- **Flag for status:** Hoist flag once a threat is identified (Blue for stranger, Red for armed).
- **Bell for urgency:** Ring once guards observe the threat (confirms it is real).
- Combination (Blue flag + one ring = strange group approaching and confirmed; Red flag + rapid rings = attack).

### Alarm Response Protocol

Define what each person should do when the alarm sounds.

**Upon hearing the alarm:**
1. **Non-combatants (children, elderly, sick):** Go to a designated safe shelter (often inside a strong building or secondary fortification).
2. **Workers and support:** Move to secondary roles (carry water, bandages, weapons to fighting positions).
3. **Defenders:** Move to defensive positions assigned by the Watch Captain.
4. **Settlement leader:** Assemble to make decisions (escalate, negotiate, fight).

Write this protocol down or teach it verbally until everyone knows it. Practice monthly (do a "fire drill" without actual threat).

## Part 3: Watch Towers and Lookout Positions

### Tower Placement for Observation

Towers are better lookout posts than walls. From a high vantage, a guard can see farther and have better defensive angles.

**Key requirements:**
- **Height advantage:** At least 3–4 meters tall so a lookout can see over the wall and terrain.
- **Multiple sight lines:** Tower should provide views in several directions (not just one).
- **Protection:** Tower should provide cover for the lookout (not completely open to fire).

### Stationing Guards in Towers

**Best practice:**
- One guard per tower during the day (many approaches, need visibility).
- One shared guard in main tower at night (fewer threats; one good lookout covers most perimeter).
- Rotate guards between towers every 2 hours (prevents boredom and keeps the mind sharp).

### Lookout Posts (Lighter Alternative)

If building towers is too labor-intensive, create simple lookout posts:
- A wooden platform 4–6 meters high, built into a corner of the wall.
- Railing for safety; small roof for weather protection.
- Stairs or ladder for access.
- Much faster to build than a tower; serves 70% of the same purpose.

## Part 4: Checkpoints and Entry Control

### Gate Checkpoint

Every person entering or leaving the settlement should be observed and recorded. This is boring, but essential.

**At each gate:**
- **Position:** A designated area just inside the gate where people stop.
- **Guard:** One or two people manning the checkpoint, able to challenge entrants.
- **Record:** Simple mark or note (name, group size, purpose, time).
- **Authority:** The gate guard can permit entry, deny entry, or escalate to the Watch Captain.

**Checkpoint decisions:**
- **Known community member:** Wave through.
- **Unknown individual or small group:** Ask purpose, check for weapons, permit or deny based on Watch Captain's instructions.
- **Large armed group:** Deny entry and sound alarm.

### Checkpoint Rules (Suggested)

These should be set by your settlement's leadership, but here are common rules:
- **No weapons past the gate** (or only designated types: spears, not bows and arrows).
- **One enter, one exits** (prevents people from sneaking in while gate is open).
- **Traders must submit goods for inspection** (weapons, poisons, explosives).
- **Any stranger arriving at night must wait until morning** (cannot observe properly in dark).

## Part 5: Patrols

Patrols extend your security beyond the walls. They detect threats before they reach your fortification.

### Patrol Frequency and Range

**Minimum:** One patrol per day, covering all approaches to your settlement.

**Better:** Two patrols per day (morning and evening), before and after peak activity hours.

**Range:** 2–5 kilometers from the settlement, depending on terrain and threats.

### Patrol Composition

- **Small patrols:** 2–3 people (fast, observant, can scout ahead).
- **Large patrols:** 5–10 people (can defend themselves against small threats, intimidate).

**Best for post-collapse:** Small patrols (2–3 people) who know the terrain well. They can cover distance faster and detect tracks, smoke, or movement better than a large group.

### What Patrols Look For

- **Fresh tracks:** Human or large animal (predators, unusual herds).
- **Smoke or fire:** Distant settlements, camps, or cook fires.
- **Marked trees or signs:** Messages left by travelers, territorial marks by raiders.
- **Dead animals:** Signs of traps, poison, or hunting by others.
- **Disturbed ground:** Digging (camps, graves, buried supplies).

### Patrol Log

Each patrol should report findings to the Watch Captain or settlement leader:
- Area covered.
- Anything unusual observed.
- Recommendations (e.g., "Large animal tracks near the north water source").

## Part 6: Night Watch Special Considerations

Night watching is hard. Humans are diurnal; staying alert in darkness is exhausting.

### Mitigating Fatigue

**1. Shorter watches:** Consider 6-hour watches at night instead of 8 hours (trades more guard labor for better alertness).

**2. Lighting:** If you have lanterns or torches, position them so the Watch Captain can see the gates and walls, but guards are slightly in shadow (making them harder to see from outside).

**3. Activity:** Have night guards do patrols or work rather than standing still. Movement keeps the mind engaged.

**4. Buddy system:** Pair guards so they can talk quietly, keeping each other awake.

**5. Rotation:** Do not assign the same person to night watch more than 2 nights in a row.

### Heightened Alert at Vulnerable Hours

**Twilight and dawn** (roughly 30 minutes before and after sunrise/sunset) are when visibility is worst and attacks are common. Increase alert level during these hours:
- Have all available guards on the wall.
- Close gates temporarily if possible.
- Have someone report to the Watch Captain every 5 minutes.

## Part 7: Community Participation

Security is everyone's responsibility. Do not leave it to only the guard force.

### Training

- Teach all adults and older children basic alarm recognition (what each bell ring or flag means).
- Drill monthly (practice response to alarms).
- Teach people the location of safe shelters and how to move to them quickly.

### Reporting

- Any community member who sees something unusual should report it to a guard or the Watch Captain.
- Create a culture where this is normal, not tattling.

### Secondary Defense

- Even non-combatants can help (carry water to defenders, move wounded, carry messages).
- Build a system where community members know their secondary role in case of threat.

## Verification

Your perimeter security system is effective if:
1. **Guard rotation is predictable** (people know when they are on watch).
2. **No guard works more than 1 in 3 days** (prevents burnout).
3. **Rest between watches is 16+ hours** (prevents sleep deprivation).
4. **Alarm system is clear and practiced** (everyone knows what each alarm means).
5. **Checkpoint is always staffed** (no one enters without observation).
6. **Patrols cover all approaches** (at least once per day).
7. **Community drills happen monthly** (people know what to do).

## Common Mistakes

**Overworking the guard force.** Exhausted guards miss threats. Rotate regularly and ensure adequate rest.

**Unclear alarm signals.** If people do not know what the bell means, the alarm is useless. Practice drills monthly.

**No written system.** A security system that exists only in the Watch Captain's head breaks when that person is sick or away. Write down the rotation schedule, alarm signals, and checkpoint rules.

**Neglecting patrols.** Raiders count on you not knowing they are coming. Patrols are your early warning system.

**Treating security as punishment.** If people view guard duty as a punishment assignment, they will resent it and do it poorly. Rotate fairly and treat guards with respect.

**Falling asleep on watch.** This is a universal problem. Acknowledge it; build systems to prevent it (buddy watch, movement, rotation).
