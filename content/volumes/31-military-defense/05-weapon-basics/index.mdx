## Overview

Post-collapse, weapons are tools for defense, not symbols of power. This chapter teaches the simplest weapons that are most useful for defending a settlement:

1. **Spears:** Reach, simplicity, defensive advantage.
2. **Bows:** Range, accuracy, sustained fire.
3. **Shields:** Protection, formation strength.
4. **Crossbows:** Power, ease of use, damage.

This chapter does NOT cover:
- Cannons or firearms (Vol 37: Electrical Engineering transition).
- Cavalry tactics or mounted warfare.
- Naval warfare (Vol 36: Seafaring).
- Assassination or ambush weapons.

The focus is on weapons useful for defending walls, gates, and chokepoints.

## Prerequisites

Understand fortification design (Vol 31, Ch 1). Understand mechanical principles (Vol 33: Mechanical Engineering, if available).

## Part 1: Spears

A spear is the simplest weapon: a sharp point on a long stick. It is useful for every defender.

### Why Spears?

**Advantages:**
- **Easy to make:** A pointed stone or metal tip tied to a wooden shaft. Minimum training to produce.
- **Multipurpose:** Used for hunting, fishing, construction, and defense.
- **Reach:** 2–3 meters of reach keeps attackers away from you.
- **Formation weapon:** Ranks of spear-holders create a nearly impenetrable wall (shield wall + spears).
- **Damage:** A properly thrust spear can penetrate most armor and kill quickly.

**Disadvantages:**
- **Requires strength:** Thrusting a spear against resistant opponent takes muscle.
- **Slow to recover:** After thrusting, you need a moment to pull the spear back.
- **Vulnerable to projectiles:** Spear-holders are exposed to arrows.

### Spear Construction

**Shaft:**
- **Material:** Ash, oak, or other hard wood. Avoid soft woods (pine) which split easily.
- **Length:** 2.5–3.5 meters. Longer is not better (harder to control); shorter loses reach advantage.
- **Thickness:** 3–5 cm diameter (thick enough to resist breaking when you thrust hard; thin enough to not be too heavy).
- **Weight:** Total spear should be 1.5–2.5 kg (light enough for repeated thrusting; heavy enough to do damage).

**Point:**
- **Material:** Sharpened stone (flint, obsidian) or forged metal (iron, steel). Stone is easier to make but dulls quickly; metal lasts longer.
- **Shape:** Leaf-shaped or triangular. Narrow point penetrates armor; wider blade does more damage.
- **Attachment:** Tied tightly to the shaft with sinew or twine, or slotted into the shaft and wedged with wood.

**Ferrule (butt end):**
- **Purpose:** Prevents the shaft from splitting when you plant it on the ground or use the butt to block.
- **Material:** Iron band or wrapped leather.
- **Optional:** A small spike on the butt can stab attackers approaching from behind.

### Spear Techniques

**Thrusting grip:**
- Hold the shaft at roughly 1/3 of its length from the butt (so the point is well in front).
- Point the shaft forward, slightly tilted down.
- Thrust by pushing the spear forward from your shoulders, using your whole body (not just arms).

**Overhand thrust:**
- Hold the spear high, point down.
- Thrust downward into an opponent (effective against shields, into limbs).
- Uses gravity to add power.

**Low thrust:**
- Hold the spear waist-high, point forward.
- Thrust at the opponent's torso or legs.
- Defensive and safe—you are protected by the spear's reach.

**Parry and block:**
- Use the shaft to block incoming attacks (swords, axes).
- Requires quick wrist movements; takes training.

### Spear Formations

**Phalanx (shield wall + spears):**
- Defenders stand shoulder-to-shoulder with shields overlapping.
- Each defender holds a spear over the shield, point forward.
- Result: An nearly unbreakable wall—attackers cannot get past the spears without going through the shields first.

**Pike square:**
- Larger formation, 10+ meters per side.
- Defenders arranged in ranks: front rank attacks with spears; rear ranks hold spears up/forward to stop cavalry or reinforce.
- Very effective against cavalry (horse will not run into a wall of spear points).

**Loose spear line:**
- Defenders spread out along a wall or fortification.
- Each defender has a spear, uses the wall for cover.
- Effective in static defense; less useful in mobile combat.

## Part 2: Shields

A shield protects against arrows, blows, and projectiles. It also creates psychological confidence (you feel safer behind a shield).

### Shield Types

**Round shield:**
- **Diameter:** 60–80 cm.
- **Weight:** 2–4 kg.
- **Construction:** Wooden frame with leather cover; iron boss (central raised area) for reinforcement.
- **Best for:** Single-handed use; formation defense (shield wall).

**Heater shield:**
- **Shape:** Heart-shaped or triangular.
- **Diameter:** 70–90 cm.
- **Weight:** 3–5 kg.
- **Best for:** Foot soldiers; protects legs and torso effectively.

**Kite shield:**
- **Shape:** Long and pointed at the bottom.
- **Diameter:** 80–100 cm.
- **Weight:** 4–6 kg.
- **Best for:** Mounted warriors or tall shields (covers more body area).

**Tower shield:**
- **Size:** 1.5+ meters tall, 0.7 meters wide.
- **Weight:** 8–12 kg.
- **Best for:** Fixed positions (gates, walls). Too heavy for mobile combat.

### Shield Construction

**Frame:**
- **Material:** Ash, oak, or other hard wood.
- **Thickness:** 2–3 cm.
- **Construction:** Wooden planks glued and nailed together, or a curved frame.

**Boss (central reinforcement):**
- **Material:** Metal (iron preferred).
- **Purpose:** Protects your hand from impacts and adds structural strength.
- **Shape:** Dome or cone, 20–30 cm diameter, raised 10–15 cm from the shield face.

**Cover:**
- **Material:** Leather, cloth, or bark.
- **Purpose:** Protects the wood from rain and damage.
- **Attachment:** Glued and nailed around the edges.

**Handle:**
- **Position:** Behind the boss (center of the shield).
- **Material:** Leather-wrapped wood or rope.
- **Grip:** Two-handed grip uses both hands; one-handed grip uses your dominant hand and leaves the other hand free for weapons.

### Shield Techniques

**Overhead protection:** Hold the shield high to protect against arrows or overhead strikes.

**Waist protection:** Hold the shield low to protect the legs.

**Full coverage:** Angle the shield slightly forward to cover most of your torso and head.

**Shield wall:** Stand next to another shield-bearer so your shields overlap slightly. This creates a continuous wall that is hard to break.

## Part 3: Bows

A bow is a projectile weapon. It is useful for defenders on walls (high ground, clear sight lines to attackers).

### Why Bows?

**Advantages:**
- **Range:** 40–100 meters, depending on type and skill.
- **Sustained fire:** One archer can fire 8–12 arrows per minute (high rate of fire).
- **Accuracy:** With training, archers hit targets 30–50 meters away consistently.
- **Armor penetration:** Heavy arrows can penetrate leather and lightly-armored opponents.
- **Psychological impact:** Rain of arrows breaks morale and forces attackers to take cover.

**Disadvantages:**
- **Training required:** Effective archery takes weeks of practice.
- **Ammunition dependent:** You need arrows; when they run out, you are done.
- **Weather sensitive:** High wind and rain reduce accuracy.
- **Close combat weak:** Once an attacker is inside your defense, you cannot use a bow effectively.

### Bow Types

**Longbow:**
- **Draw weight:** 45–90 kg (requires significant strength).
- **Range:** 100+ meters.
- **Accuracy:** Good at range.
- **Skill required:** High (takes months to develop.
- **Advantage:** Very long range, high power.
- **Disadvantage:** Requires dedicated training.

**Shortbow:**
- **Draw weight:** 20–40 kg (accessible to most people).
- **Range:** 50–80 meters.
- **Accuracy:** Good at 30–50 meters.
- **Skill required:** Moderate (takes weeks to develop).
- **Advantage:** Quick to learn, versatile.
- **Disadvantage:** Shorter range, less penetrating power.

**Composite bow:**
- **Materials:** Bone, sinew, and wood laminated together.
- **Draw weight:** 40–80 kg.
- **Range:** 80–120 meters.
- **Advantage:** High power and range.
- **Disadvantage:** Complex to manufacture; requires resources.

**Recommendation for post-collapse:** Shortbow. Easy to learn, materials are accessible (wood), and effective for defensive archery.

### Bow Construction (Shortbow)

**Stave (the bow shaft):**
- **Material:** Ash, yew, or oak. Avoid soft woods.
- **Length:** 1.2–1.5 meters.
- **Thickness:** Taper from thick at the center to thin at the tips.
- **Curve:** The bow curves when drawn (not straight).
- **Construction:** A single piece of wood, carved to shape (curved limbs, grip section).

**Bowstring:**
- **Material:** Plant fiber (flax, hemp), animal sinew, or leather strips twisted together.
- **Strength:** Must be strong enough to pull back the bow without breaking.
- **Length:** Shorter than the bow; creates the curve.

**Nocking point:**
- **Purpose:** Where the arrow fits on the bowstring.
- **Material:** A small knot or wrapped section of the string.
- **Position:** Roughly center of the string.

### Arrow Construction

**Shaft:**
- **Material:** Birch, ash, or straight wood.
- **Length:** 60–70 cm (roughly from your shoulder to your fingertips).
- **Thickness:** 5–7 mm diameter.
- **Straightness:** Critical; a bent arrow flies badly.

**Fletching (feathers):**
- **Material:** Goose, duck, or other bird feathers.
- **Quantity:** 3–4 feathers per arrow.
- **Purpose:** Stabilizes the arrow in flight.
- **Attachment:** Tied or glued to the shaft near the nock.

**Arrowhead (point):**
- **Material:** Sharpened stone (flint, obsidian) or forged metal (iron).
- **Shapes:**
  - **Blunt:** Kills small animals without tearing the hide; used for hunting.
  - **Broadhead:** Wide blade; maximum bleeding and damage (war arrow).
  - **Bodkin:** Narrow, hard point; penetrates armor (armor-piercing arrow).

**Nock (the back of the arrow):**
- **Purpose:** Fits onto the bowstring.
- **Material:** Split wood fitted onto the arrow shaft and wrapped with leather.
- **Notch:** A small notch cut into the nock to fit on the bowstring.

### Bow Techniques

**Stance:**
- Stand with feet shoulder-width apart, perpendicular to the target (sideways).
- Keep your body relaxed, arms slightly bent.

**Drawing:**
- Nock the arrow on the bowstring.
- Pull the bowstring back smoothly using your back muscles (not just arms).
- Draw to your cheek (consistent positioning for accuracy).
- Aim at the target.

**Release:**
- Let the bowstring go smoothly; do not "punch" it.
- The bowstring should pass the side of your face without striking your cheek.
- Follow through (keep your arm extended after release).

**Rate of fire:**
- Trained archers: 8–12 arrows per minute.
- Untrained archers: 2–4 arrows per minute.

### Massed Archery

Archery is most effective when multiple archers fire together.

**Technique:**
- Archers spread across the wall or fortification (so they do not interfere with each other).
- On command, all archers nock arrows.
- On second command, all archers draw and release simultaneously.
- Result: Rain of arrows that forces attackers to take cover or flee.

**Effectiveness:**
- Against unarmored attackers: Devastating (many casualties).
- Against armored attackers: Still effective but fewer casualties (arrows don't penetrate heavy armor).
- Against moving/running attackers: Hard to hit (moving targets are difficult).

## Part 4: Crossbows

A crossbow is a more powerful but slower bow. It requires less training than a longbow but more training than a shortbow.

### Why Crossbows?

**Advantages:**
- **Power:** Penetrates armor better than bows.
- **Easy to use:** Requires less training than bows (stronger people can pull a crossbow trigger; bows require practiced muscle memory).
- **Faster training:** A person can become reasonably proficient in weeks.
- **Mechanical advantage:** The mechanism multiplies your strength.

**Disadvantages:**
- **Slow rate of fire:** 2–4 bolts per minute (slower than bows).
- **Complex to manufacture:** More parts than a bow.
- **Heavy:** Heavier than bows, more tiring to carry.
- **Harder to carry ammunition:** Bolts are shorter and heavier than arrows.

### Crossbow Construction

**Prod (the bow part):**
- **Material:** Laminated wood, bone, and sinew (composite construction is stronger).
- **Draw weight:** 40–80 kg.
- **Shape:** Thinner and shorter than a bow.

**Stock (the wooden frame):**
- **Material:** Hardwood (ash, oak).
- **Length:** 50–70 cm.
- **Shape:** Roughly like a rifle stock (curved to fit against your shoulder).

**Trigger mechanism:**
- **Purpose:** Holds the bowstring back; releasing the trigger fires the crossbow.
- **Materials:** Wood and metal (iron or bone).
- **Types:**
  - **Simple lever:** A lever that holds the string; pulling it releases.
  - **Sophisticated trigger:** A mechanical system that allows for fine control.

**Crossbow loading (cocking):**
- **Cocking lever:** A device to help you pull back the very stiff bowstring. Without it, you cannot load a heavy crossbow.
- **Foot claw:** A hook at the end of the stock that you place your foot through to help pull back the string (for stronger springs).

### Bolt Construction

**Bolt (the projectile):**
- **Shaft:** 30–40 cm, shorter and heavier than arrows.
- **Fletching:** Plastic or wooden vanes (not feathers; more rigid).
- **Head:** Broadhead, bodkin, or barbed head (same types as arrows, but heavier).

### Crossbow Techniques

**Stance:**
- Stand with the crossbow stock against your shoulder.
- Keep both hands on the crossbow (unlike bows, which use both hands and arms in different ways).

**Aiming:**
- Some crossbows have sights (notch or open sight); others require point-and-aim.
- Aim at the target and pull the trigger.

**Safety:**
- Never point a loaded crossbow at anything you do not intend to shoot.
- Keep your fingers away from the string (it moves very fast when released).

## Part 5: Maintenance and Crafting

Weapons degrade with use. Plan for replacement and repair.

### Bow Maintenance

- **Check the stave:** Look for cracks or warping. A warped bow will not shoot straight.
- **Replace the string:** Bowstrings wear out and eventually break. Keep spare string material.
- **Straighten arrows:** Bent arrows are less accurate. Soak them in water and straighten them carefully.

### Arrow and Bolt Supply

- **Production rate:** One skilled fletcher can make 50–100 arrows per day (cutting shafts, attaching fletching, making points).
- **Loss rate:** In combat, expect to lose 10–30% of arrows (they break, are lost, are captured).
- **Storage:** Store arrows in quivers (arrow bags) or bundles. Keep them dry (moisture causes wood to warp).

### Weapon Manufacturing

**Post-collapse priorities:**
1. **Year 1:** Shortbows from local wood, arrows with stone or bone points.
2. **Year 2–3:** If you have metalworking (Vol 33: Mechanical Engineering), make iron arrowheads and spear points.
3. **Year 3+:** Crossbows, if you have the mechanical knowledge and materials.

## Verification

Your weapon capability is sound if:
1. **Spears:** You can make 1–2 spears per day per craftsperson.
2. **Shields:** You have shields for at least half your defenders.
3. **Bows:** You have shortbows for 20–30% of defenders.
4. **Arrows:** You have 1000+ arrows stockpiled (50–100 per archer).
5. **Crossbows:** If available, they supplement bows (not replace them).
6. **Training:** Defenders practice archery weekly; expectation is 50%+ hit rate at 30 meters.
7. **Crafting:** You have people trained to make and repair weapons.

## Common Mistakes

**Expecting untrained people to use bows effectively.** Archery requires weeks of training. Do not expect accuracy from untrained archers.

**Storing arrows improperly.** Wet arrows warp and are useless. Store in dry place, tips protected.

**Giving up archery because arrows are lost in battle.** You will lose arrows. Plan for attrition and manufacture replacements.

**Overcomplicating weapons.** Simple spears and shortbows are better than no weapons. Do not wait for "perfect" weapons; use what you have.

**Assuming one weapon type is enough.** Different weapons have different roles. Use spears for formation defense, bows for ranged fire, crossbows for armor penetration.
