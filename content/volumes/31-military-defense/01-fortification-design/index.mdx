## Overview

A fortified settlement is not about invulnerability—no wall is impenetrable. Rather, fortifications make organized attacks expensive enough that they are not worth attempting. A poorly-designed wall invites siege; a well-sited wall makes an attacker think twice, then move on to easier targets.

Fortification design answers three questions:
1. **Where** should the fortification be located? (Terrain advantage, water access, escape routes)
2. **What** should the barrier be? (Wall height and thickness, ditch dimensions, tower spacing)
3. **How** should defenses integrate? (Lines of fire, fallback positions, internal strongpoints)

This chapter teaches you to site and size defensive structures. The focus is on defenses that can be built and maintained by a post-collapse community with hand tools and basic engineering.

## Prerequisites

Understand your community's labor budget (Vol 6, Ch 3). Understand terrain, water flow, and geology (Vol 13). Know how to do basic surveying (Vol 24, if available).

## Part 1: Site Selection

The best defense is choosing terrain that works for you.

### Location Priorities (in order)

**1. High ground.** Defensible terrain forces attackers uphill. A 10-meter elevation advantage is worth 100 meters of wall. Occupying a hill, ridge, or plateau makes you harder to surround and harder to assault.

**2. Water barrier.** Rivers, streams, or wet ground slow attackers and provide a moat without excavation. A settlement on a peninsula, island, or inside a river bend has three sides protected for free.

**3. Narrow approaches.** Choose a location where attackers are forced into a small area to assault. A narrow river valley, forest edge, or pass naturally constrains attackers and lets your defenders concentrate forces.

**4. Escape route.** Always have a way out. Multiple valleys, forests, or water routes allow your people to evacuate if a siege is unbreakable. A fortification with no escape is a trap.

**5. Resources inside the perimeter.** Include water sources, arable land for siege-duration food, timber, and stone. A fortification 50 meters from its only water supply will fail to a siege that cuts access.

### Bad Locations (Avoid These)

- **Completely flat terrain with no water.** Every meter of wall must do the work of terrain advantage. Unsustainable.
- **Surrounded by high ground.** Attackers placed above you can bombard into the settlement. Unusable.
- **Single approach.** No escape, no fallback. Easy to encircle.
- **No water inside or nearby.** Siege will break the settlement in days.

### Assessment Protocol

1. **Walk the perimeter.** Spend half a day walking the likely boundary of your settlement. Where are the natural choke points? Where can attackers hide? Where are approaches easiest?
2. **Plot elevation.** If possible, sketch a rough topographic profile. High ground to the north? Water to the east?
3. **Check water availability.** Is there a spring, stream, or well inside? Can enemies cut it off?
4. **Estimate the wall.** Based on terrain, where do you actually need a wall? Where does terrain do the work for you?

Most communities find that only 30-40% of their perimeter needs a constructed wall; the rest is protected by terrain, water, or natural obstacles.

## Part 2: Wall Design

### Wall Dimensions

A wall must be thick enough to resist assault and tall enough to prevent climbing. Too thin, it fails under pressure. Too tall, it costs more labor than it prevents.

**Minimum dimensions:**
- **Height:** 3 meters (about one story). Shorter than this, attackers can use ladders or hand-over-hand climbing. Taller than 5 meters is rarely necessary and very expensive.
- **Thickness at base:** 1.5 meters for stone, 2 meters for rammed earth or adobe. A wall that is too thin will collapse when an attacker uses a battering ram against the base.
- **Taper:** Wall should be thicker at the base, thinner at the top. Reduce thickness by 0.5 meters every 2 meters of height.

**Example dimensions** (3-meter wall):
- Base thickness: 1.5 meters
- Top thickness: 1.0 meter
- Height: 3.0 meters

**Why these dimensions:**
- 3 meters prevents easy climbing without being impossibly expensive to build.
- 1.5 meters at the base is thick enough to resist battering ram and cannon-adjacent weaponry without requiring stone-cutting tools (raw stacked stone or rammed earth can achieve this).
- Taper is important: it reduces weight high on the wall (reducing collapse risk) and allows defenders to drop objects downward from the top without obstruction.

### Wall Crest Design

The top of the wall should protect defenders while allowing them to shoot downward.

**Parapet design** (the wall-top fortification):
- **Merlons:** Stone or wooden posts 1.0–1.5 meters tall, spaced 2 meters apart (enough to protect a standing defender's head).
- **Crenels:** Gaps between merlons, 1.0 meter wide, allow defenders to lean out and shoot or drop objects.
- **Parapet height above wall top:** 0.5–1.0 meter. Taller than this, defenders must climb to see and shoot; shorter than this, arrows can arc over.

**Alternative (if stone is scarce):** Wooden palisade. Posts 0.3 meters in diameter, spaced 0.5 meters apart, tied together with crossbeams. Less durable than stone but faster to build and repairable.

### Wall Material

**Rammed earth or adobe:**
- Fastest to build (weeks to months for a long wall).
- Requires formwork and labor for pounding.
- Durability: 50–100 years without maintenance. Cracks form in dry climates; repair with fresh mud.
- Cost: Minimal (earth is free on-site; timber for formwork is reusable).
- Best for: Communities with plenty of labor, dry climates, no access to stone quarries.

**Stone:**
- Longest-lasting (centuries).
- Requires quarrying or gathering fieldstone.
- If fitted (shaped, mortared): Requires skill and time.
- If dry-stacked (raw stones piled with no mortar): Faster but less stable; needs thicker walls.
- Cost: High (labor for quarrying, shaping, mortaring).
- Best for: Communities with stone resources and time for construction.

**Fired brick:**
- Durable (centuries) and strong.
- Requires kiln capacity (Vol 15, Ch 4-6).
- Post-collapse, brick is likely not available immediately.
- Cost: Very high (each brick requires fuel).
- Best for: Long-term (year 5+) reconstruction.

**Recommendation for immediate post-collapse:** Rammed earth or dry-stacked stone are the fastest defensible walls. Plan for a permanent stone or brick wall later.

## Part 3: Ditch Design

A ditch slows attackers, makes battering rams harder to use, and can be a moat if you want to fill it with water.

### Ditch Dimensions

- **Width:** 4–6 meters at the top, 2–3 meters at the bottom (sloped sides).
- **Depth:** 2–3 meters. Deeper than this, you are excavating a moat (more labor). Shallower, attackers can easily climb out.
- **Slope:** 45-degree angle on the inner slope (toward the wall) to prevent attackers from walking straight out; 30-degree angle on the outer slope to prevent collapse.

### Ditch Purposes

**Without water:** A dry ditch is a barrier and a trap. Attackers who jump in have difficulty climbing out under fire. The excavated soil is used to raise terrain inside your wall (the "berm"), adding effective height.

**With water (moat):** If water is available and your site has poor drainage, fill the ditch to create a moat. A 2-meter-wide moat is very difficult to cross under fire. However:
- Water requires maintenance (algae, silt, disease vectors like mosquitoes).
- If your settlement is sieged, the moat becomes a sanitation problem.
- A moat only works if you can keep it filled during a siege; otherwise, attackers drain it or ford it.

**Recommendation:** Dry ditches are simpler and lower-maintenance. Add water only if your site naturally accumulates water and you have resources to maintain it.

## Part 4: Tower Placement

Towers serve three purposes:
1. **Fire support:** Defenders in towers can shoot along the wall to prevent attackers from using the base.
2. **Strongpoints:** Towers can be held as last-resort positions if the main wall is breached.
3. **Morale:** A tall tower is visible for miles and communicates "this settlement is prepared."

### Tower Dimensions

- **Diameter or width:** 4–6 meters (large enough for 4–6 defenders and supplies).
- **Height:** Same as wall or slightly taller (3–4 meters) so defenders in the tower can shoot over the wall.
- **Wall thickness:** 1.0–1.5 meters (same as wall thickness or thicker; towers take a beating).
- **Interior space:** Minimize walls inside; a tower should be mostly hollow so defenders are not trapped in separated rooms.

### Tower Spacing

- **Interval:** Towers should be placed so that a defender in one tower can shoot at attackers trying to climb the wall between towers. This is roughly 40–50 meters (the accurate range of a bow or spear).
- **At corners:** Place towers at all corners of the fortification. Corners are vulnerable to being climbed or ignored; a tower at each corner ensures full coverage.
- **At gates:** Place towers flanking any gate (one on each side). This allows defenders to protect the gate from attackers who breach it.

### Round vs. Square Towers

- **Round towers:** Better at resisting battering rams (force is distributed) and arrow fire (no corners to hide behind). Slightly harder to build (curved stonework).
- **Square towers:** Easier to build (rectangular formwork). Slightly more vulnerable to corner damage. Corners provide places for defenders to shelter while shooting (one defender shoots to the left, another to the right).

**Recommendation:** For post-collapse construction, square towers are acceptable and faster to build.

## Part 5: Gate Design

Gates are your settlement's vulnerability. Attackers will focus on gates because they are openings in the wall.

### Gate Basics

- **Placement:** Do not place gates at the lowest point of your perimeter (low ground is harder to defend). Place gates on terrain where defenders can shoot down at attackers.
- **Width:** 2–3 meters for normal traffic (wagons, livestock). Narrower gates are harder to defend; wider gates are easier to breach.
- **Depth:** The gate passage should be at least 4 meters long (enough for one wagon length), creating a bottleneck where attackers are concentrated.

### Gate Types

**Simple gate (palisade):**
- Heavy wooden door(s) reinforced with crossbeams or iron bands.
- Can be locked with a bar or wooden lock.
- Takes 1–2 minutes to open (intentional delay to allow defenders to prepare).
- Burnable; attackers can set it alight. Use water buckets or a ditch with water nearby.

**Double gate (gatehouse):**
- Two gates separated by 4–6 meters.
- Attackers who breach the first gate are trapped between two walls while defenders shoot from towers.
- More defensible but takes more labor to build and maintain.

**Portcullis (if metalworking is available):**
- A heavy wooden or iron grating that slides down to block the gate opening.
- Takes 10–15 seconds to lower.
- Must be built into the gate passage architecture (expensive).
- Recommended only after your settlement is stable enough to have a blacksmith.

**Recommendation for immediate post-collapse:** Simple gate with reinforced wooden doors, flanked by towers. Plan for a double gate or portcullis once resources allow.

## Part 6: Defensive Angles and Lines of Fire

Fortifications fail when attackers can climb the wall without being shot at. To prevent this, design the wall so that every point is covered by defensive fire.

### Flanking Fire

Every section of wall should be under fire from at least one tower or bastion. If an attacker can climb the wall in a "dead zone" where defenders cannot shoot, your fortification has failed.

**Check by drawing lines of sight:**
1. Imagine a defender in each tower with a bow (range ~40 meters, assuming clear sight lines).
2. Can that defender shoot at any point on the wall between towers?
3. If there is a dead zone (a section of wall not covered), move the tower or add another one.

### Salient and Flanking

A **salient** is a corner or bastion that sticks out. Salientsallow defenders to shoot along the wall.

A **flank** is the side face of a bastion or tower. A well-designed flank is 45–60 degrees to the wall (not perpendicular), so defenders can shoot along the wall while staying protected by the wall's thickness.

### Gunpowder Considerations

If your settlement has or will develop cannon or firearms:
- Walls should be lower and thicker (to resist cannon fire).
- Bastions should be angular (sharp angles to deflect cannonballs), not round.
- More on this in Vol 37 (Electrical Engineering transition) or advanced fortification texts.

For the immediate post-collapse period (no firearms), focus on height and archer coverage.

## Part 7: Building in Phases

You cannot build a complete fortification in one year. Plan in phases.

### Phase 1 (Months 1–6): Emergency wall
- Palisade (wooden posts and crossbeams) on the most exposed side.
- Simple gates.
- Goal: Prevent casual raiders.
- Labor: 20–50 people, part-time.

### Phase 2 (Months 6–18): Main wall
- Rammed earth or dry-stacked stone wall on all sides.
- Ditch where terrain permits.
- Simple towers at corners and gates.
- Goal: Defend against organized attack.
- Labor: 50–100 people, most of their time.

### Phase 3 (Years 2–5): Hardening
- Upgrade wooden palisade to stone.
- Add interior strongpoints.
- Expand towers for storage and housing (dual-purpose).
- Install double gates.
- Goal: Sustainable defense and comfortable living.
- Labor: Smaller teams, ongoing.

## Verification

Your fortification plan is sound if:
1. **Terrain advantage:** High ground, water barrier, or narrow approaches provide 50% of defense.
2. **Wall dimensions:** Height 3 meters, base thickness 1.5 meters, tapered to 1.0 meter at top.
3. **Ditch:** 4–6 meters wide, 2–3 meters deep (if excavating).
4. **Tower spacing:** No dead zones; every point on wall is under fire from a tower.
5. **Gate protection:** Gates flanked by towers, 4+ meters of passage depth.
6. **Phased construction:** Year 1 is palisade, Year 2+ is permanent wall.
7. **Escape route:** Path out of settlement if besieged.

Build a small-scale model or draw a map with lines of sight from each tower. If you see a section of wall where no tower can shoot, fix it before you build.

## Common Mistakes

**Ignoring terrain.** A wall alone will not protect you. Terrain is half the defense.

**Over-building early.** Spending a year on a perfect stone wall while your community starves is a failure of priorities. Start with fast palisade, upgrade later.

**Walls too thin.** A wall under 1.0 meter thick at the base will fail under battering ram pressure. Invest in thickness.

**Dead zones.** Building a wall that towers cannot defend is useless. Always check lines of sight before excavating.

**Gate in the wrong place.** Placing a gate in low ground or on a wide, flat approach makes it easy to breach. Gates should be on defended terrain.

**No escape route.** A fortification without an escape route is a death trap. Always ensure your people can evacuate.
