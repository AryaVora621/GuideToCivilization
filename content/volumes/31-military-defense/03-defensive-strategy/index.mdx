## Overview

Defensive strategy is how you use your fortifications, terrain, and people to resist an attack. It is not about winning a battle—it is about making an attack too expensive or difficult to be worth attempting.

The core principle: **Make attacking you more costly than leaving.** If an attacker calculates that taking your settlement will cost them more people, time, or resources than they gain, they move on.

This chapter teaches:
1. **Defense in depth:** Multiple barriers so an attacker cannot break through with one assault.
2. **Chokepoints:** Terrain features that force attackers into small areas.
3. **Fallback positions:** Positions you retreat to if outer defenses fail.
4. **Psychological defense:** How to seem stronger than you are.

## Prerequisites

Know your fortification layout (Vol 31, Ch 1) and your security system (Vol 31, Ch 2). Understand the ethics of defending your settlement (Vol 6, Ch 1).

## Part 1: Defense in Depth

Defense in depth means having multiple lines of defense, not just one wall. If the first line fails, you fall back to the second. If that fails, you retreat to a third.

### Three Lines of Defense

**Outer line:** Your perimeter wall, ditch, and gates. Designed to slow attackers and force them into predictable positions.

**Middle line:** Inner fortifications, terrain features, or secondary walls that protect key resources (water, grain storage, shelters). This is where you retreat if the outer wall is breached.

**Inner line:** A strong building, tower, or final strongpoint where your people make a last stand. This is where the most important people (children, elderly, healers) can shelter if all else fails.

### Spacing Between Lines

- **Outer to middle:** 50–200 meters, depending on terrain. Far enough that attackers cannot assault both lines simultaneously; close enough that defenders can move between them in 2–5 minutes.
- **Middle to inner:** 50–100 meters. Very close; defenders retreat here in minutes.

### How Depth Works

If an attacker breaks your outer wall:
1. Defenders in the outer wall retreat to the middle line.
2. They use the middle fortifications to force the attacker to regroup.
3. If the attacker succeeds again, they retreat to the inner line.

Each time the attacker breaks through, they lose people, time, and momentum. By the third or fourth break, they may not have enough strength to take the inner line.

**Cost to attacker:** Breaching three lines might cost 3–5x as many people as breaching one line.

### Middle Line Examples

**Terrain-based:**
- A ridge or hill inside your settlement that forces attackers uphill again.
- A river or stream that attackers must cross.
- Dense forest where riders and larger formations are useless.

**Structure-based:**
- A secondary palisade or stone wall around the settlement center.
- The settlement's largest buildings arranged as a defensive perimeter.
- A tower or watchtower that dominates the interior.

**Resource-based:**
- Your water supply and grain stores are in the most defensible area.
- Shelters for non-combatants are on higher ground.
- Defensive supplies (arrows, spears, rocks) are staged at the middle line.

### Inner Line

The inner line is your last-stand position. It should be:
- **Very strong:** Multiple walls, small entry points, defendable by a small force.
- **Supplied:** Water, food, and weapons for weeks.
- **Populated:** All non-combatants (children, elderly, healers) shelter here once fighting starts.

Examples:
- A strong stone tower with supplies stored inside.
- The largest building in your settlement, fortified with an inner wall.
- A natural strongpoint (hill, rock outcropping) with fortifications built on it.

**When to retreat to the inner line:** Only when the middle line is about to be breached. Retreating too early wastes the middle line's defensive value.

## Part 2: Chokepoints and Forcing Engagement

A chokepoint is a terrain feature or constructed barrier that forces attackers to:
1. Narrow their formation (so they cannot flank you).
2. Bunch together (so they suffer greater casualties in a small area).
3. Slow down (so defenders have time to react).

### Natural Chokepoints

**Terrain:**
- **Narrow valley:** Attackers cannot spread out; they must go single-file or in small columns. Defenders positioned on the ridges above rain arrows or rocks down.
- **River with few fords:** Attackers must cross at specific points where defenders are waiting.
- **Forest:** Thick vegetation forces small-group formation; mounted cavalry and large formations are useless.
- **Hill:** Attackers must come uphill; defenders have gravity advantage.

### Constructed Chokepoints

**Gates:**
- Force attackers through narrow openings (gates are chokepoints by design).
- Wider gates are easier to breach; narrower gates are harder to pass through.
- A 2-meter-wide gate forces attackers into single-file passage, concentrating casualties.

**Walls and ditches:**
- Walls force attackers to climb or find a way around; ditches slow them.
- Combinations create multiple chokepoints (ditch, then wall, then inner wall).

**Barriers inside the settlement:**
- Partial walls or fences that force attackers to navigate a specific path.
- This path is where your defenders wait.

### Channeling Strategy

Use chokepoints to channel an attacker into a location where you have numerical or positional advantage.

**Example:**
1. Outer wall has one main gate and two secondary gates.
2. Secondary gates are partially collapsed or difficult to open (appearing weaker).
3. Attackers naturally gravitate toward the easier path.
4. You station your best defenders at the secondary gates, which become chokepoints.
5. Attackers bunch up, suffer heavy casualties, and retreat.

## Part 3: Fallback Positions and Retreat

Retreat is not a failure; it is part of a good strategy.

### When to Retreat

**Retreat when:**
- Your current position is about to be breached.
- You have taken 30–50% casualties (you can no longer defend effectively).
- An attacker is flanking your position (you are about to be surrounded).
- You are outnumbered 3:1 or worse (you cannot win; stop trying).

**Do NOT retreat when:**
- You have barely engaged the enemy (give the position time to work).
- You have a significant advantage (why throw it away?).
- You are retreating to a position that is no longer defensible (sometimes standing and fighting is better).

### Coordinating a Retreat

A disorganized retreat becomes a rout (people flee in panic, abandonment of the settlement). A coordinated retreat is a controlled, orderly movement to the next defensive line.

**Retreat procedure:**
1. **Signal:** The commanding defender signals a retreat (bell, horn, shout).
2. **Phases:** Defenders retreat in phases:
   - Half retreat first (while the other half covers).
   - Once the first group reaches the middle line, the second half retreats.
3. **Covering fire:** Defenders covering the retreat use a higher rate of fire (arrows, spears) to suppress the attacker's pursuit.
4. **Seal the position:** Once everyone is through, seal the middle line (close gates, rebuild barriers quickly).

### Training for Retreat

Most people will not retreat willingly (it feels like cowardice). You must train them:
- **Practice drills:** Simulate retreating from the outer wall to the middle line. Make it routine.
- **Explain the strategy:** People are more willing to retreat if they understand it is part of the plan, not a sign of failure.
- **Trust in the next position:** Defenders will fight hard only if they believe the next line is defensible. Show them the middle line, explain its strength.

## Part 4: Positioning and Strength Ratios

### Defending Requires Fewer People than Attacking

This is a fundamental principle. A defender behind a wall is worth 3–5 attackers in the open.

**Why:**
- Defenders have cover (walls, trenches, buildings).
- Attackers must cross open ground under fire.
- Defenders can choose when and where to fight.
- Attackers must commit to a single approach.

**Strength ratios:**
- **Parity (1:1):** Defenders win easily.
- **Outnumbered 2:1:** Defenders still have significant advantage.
- **Outnumbered 3:1:** Defenders are at disadvantage but can still hold with good positioning.
- **Outnumbered 4:1 or worse:** Defenders cannot hold a single line; must use depth and fallback.

### Positioning Principles

**High ground:** Defenders on high ground have a 2:1 advantage over attackers below. Occupy hills, ridges, and raised terrain.

**Cover:** Defenders behind walls, in trenches, or in buildings have a 2:1 advantage over attackers in the open. Build fortifications.

**Surprise:** Defending from an expected position is less valuable than defending from a position the attacker doesn't expect. Keep some defenders hidden until the attack is underway.

**Flexibility:** Do not lock all your defenders into one position. Keep a reserve (20–30% of defenders) to respond to where the attacker breaks through.

### Distributing Defenders

**Example settlement: 200 people, 50 capable combatants.**

- **Outer wall:** 20 defenders (spread across 500 meters of wall and gates).
- **Middle line:** 15 defenders (positioned at key chokepoints).
- **Inner line:** 10 defenders (final strongpoint).
- **Reserve:** 5 defenders (respond to breaches, reinforce weak points).

**Under attack:**
- Outer wall is breached.
- Outer defenders retreat, or are overwhelmed.
- Reserve joins the outer defenders; they make a stand at a chokepoint (narrower section of wall).
- If that chokepoint is breached, retreat to the middle line.

## Part 5: The Siege Scenario

If an attacker is large enough or well-supplied enough, they may choose to besiege rather than assault. A siege is a test of endurance.

### Siege Dynamics

A siege works like this:
1. Attacker surrounds your settlement and prevents supplies from entering.
2. You stop attacking and defend passively.
3. Over weeks or months, your food and supplies run out.
4. You must either surrender, break out, or starve.

### How to Survive a Siege

**Preparation (before siege):**
- Stock 6–12 months of food inside the settlement (or have a way to grow food inside).
- Ensure water supply cannot be cut off (well inside, not river outside).
- Build fortifications strong enough that attackers cannot breach them quickly.

**During siege:**
- Do not waste people in sorties (attacks outside the walls) unless you have a specific, achievable objective.
- Defend only the inner line if supplies are critical (defending a large perimeter wastes food).
- Maintain morale (see Part 6 below).
- Look for opportunities to break out if the siege is not improving.

**Breaking a siege:**
- **Reinforcement:** An army from outside attacks the besiegers. (Hope for this, but do not count on it.)
- **Breakout:** Your people fight their way out of the settlement, abandoning it.
- **Negotiation:** Agree to terms with the attacker (surrender, ransom, vassalage).

## Part 6: Psychological Defense

Some of the strongest defenses are psychological.

### Appearance of Strength

- **Visible fortifications:** A strong wall visible from a distance discourages attack.
- **Disciplined defenders:** Attackers judge your strength by how well-organized your defense is. Defenders standing in neat lines, responding to commands, are intimidating.
- **Banners and symbols:** Flags, paint, or insignia on the wall signal unity and strength.
- **Noise:** Drums, horns, and organized chanting make your force sound larger than it is.

### Demoralization of Attackers

- **Casualties:** Every casualty the attacker takes is a person not fighting. Make sure defenders kill the maximum number of attackers per exchange.
- **Bogging down:** A siege that takes weeks is demoralizing for attackers (they have no shelter, no supply line, no glory in a slow war). Defend passively; let them get bored.
- **Visible confidence:** If your defenders seem calm and confident, attackers will assume you are stronger than you are.

### Information and Rumor

- **Spread false information:** Let word get out that you have reinforcements coming, supplies for a year, or allies nearby.
- **Exaggerate your strength:** If you have 50 defenders, call them 100. Rumors travel.
- **Minimize attacker victories:** If they breach an outer wall, claim you planned the retreat, not that you were forced back.

These are not honorable tactics, but they are effective.

## Part 7: Training and Drills

Defensive strategy only works if your people know what to do during an attack.

### Monthly Drills

- **Gate defense:** Defenders practice defending a specific gate against simulated attack.
- **Retreat and reposition:** Defenders practice retreating from the outer wall to the middle line while maintaining formation.
- **Communication:** Defenders practice signaling (horns, drums, flags) so communication works under stress.
- **Casualty response:** Practice removing wounded from the line and replacing them quickly.

### Winter Planning

Use the winter (least likely time for attacks) to:
- Repair fortifications.
- Train new defenders.
- Refine your defensive plan based on lessons from previous year.
- Stockpile supplies and weapons.

## Verification

Your defensive strategy is sound if:
1. **Multiple lines exist:** Outer, middle, and inner defensive positions are clear.
2. **Chokepoints are used:** Terrain or constructed barriers force attackers into predictable positions.
3. **Fallback is planned:** Defenders know where to retreat if a line is breached.
4. **Strength ratio is understood:** You know how many attackers you can defeat (roughly 1:1 to 3:1 depending on fortifications).
5. **Reserves exist:** You have defenders not assigned to any line, ready to respond to breaches.
6. **Drills happen monthly:** Defenders practice defending, retreating, and communicating.
7. **Supplies are stockpiled:** Food, water, weapons for a siege lasting weeks are stored.

## Common Mistakes

**Linear defense (one line only).** A single wall is not enough. Multiple lines prevent a breakthrough from ending the siege.

**Defending everywhere equally.** Spread too thin, you are weak everywhere. Concentrate defenders at chokepoints and expect to lose the areas between them.

**Retreating too early.** If you retreat before a position is actually breached, you waste its defensive value and demoralize your people.

**Over-confident defending.** Thinking you can defeat any attacker by standing and fighting is how fortifications fall. Sometimes yielding ground (retreating) saves the settlement.

**Poor communication.** Defenders who do not know when to retreat or where to go create chaos. Train and drill constantly.

**Neglecting supplies.** A well-fortified settlement starves if it has not stockpiled food. Fortifications only work if you can endure a siege.
