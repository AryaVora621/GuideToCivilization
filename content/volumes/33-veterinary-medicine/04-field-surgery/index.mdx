## Overview

Field surgery is surgical intervention on animals in a farm setting (not a veterinary clinic). Post-collapse, many surgical procedures will fall to you.

This chapter covers:
1. **Castration:** Removing testicles (reduces aggression, improves meat quality).
2. **Dehorning:** Removing horns (prevents injury, improves safety).
3. **Wound care:** Treating injuries and surgical complications.

These are not gentle procedures, but they improve animal welfare and prevent worse problems.

## Prerequisites

Understand surgical principles (Vol 4, Ch 7). Know animal anatomy (Vol 11, Ch 1-3).

## Part 1: Principles of Field Surgery

### Anesthesia

Ideal post-collapse: Local anesthesia (numbs the area). Not available: Use restraint and speed.

**Local anesthetics (if available):**
- Procaine, lidocaine: Injected near the surgical area.
- Numbs the area for 30-60 minutes.
- Allows painless surgery.

**Without anesthesia:**
- Restraint (immobilization of the animal).
- Speed (do the procedure quickly).
- Acceptance that the animal will feel pain.
- Some cultures perform these procedures without anesthesia as routine.

**Pain management:**
- Post-surgery: Anti-inflammatory herbs or medications if available (willow bark, meadowsweet).
- Rest and isolation (prevent fighting, which increases pain).

### Asepsis (Cleanliness)

**Before surgery:**
- Wash hands thoroughly.
- Clean the surgical area with antiseptic (iodine, bleach 1:10, or soap and water).
- Use clean instruments (sterilized in boiling water for 20 minutes, or cleaned with alcohol if available).

**During surgery:**
- Minimize touching the wound with bare hands.
- Use clean cloth/gloves.
- Keep the area clean.

**After surgery:**
- Apply clean dressing.
- Watch for infection (swelling, heat, discharge, fever).

### Hemorrhage Control

Bleeding (hemorrhage) is a primary risk in surgery.

**Prevention:**
- Work carefully; avoid cutting major blood vessels.
- Know where major vessels are (so you avoid them).

**Control:**
- Pressure: Apply firm pressure with clean cloth for 5-10 minutes.
- Tourniquet: Tie cloth tightly above the wound to cut off blood flow (use only if pressure fails; loosen every 30 minutes to prevent tissue death).
- Cautery: Sear the wound with a hot iron (cauterizes vessel; prevents bleeding but causes pain/scarring).
- Ligation: Tie off the bleeding vessel with thread (requires skill).

**Excessive bleeding (uncontrolled):**
- If bleeding does not stop after 15 minutes of pressure, the animal is in shock risk.
- Stop the surgery if not essential.
- Provide fluids (oral if animal can swallow; IV if you have the skill).

## Part 2: Castration

Castration removes testicles (castrates males). Benefits:
- Reduced aggression (major: less fighting, safer handling).
- Better meat quality (less "gamey" flavor, improved palatability).
- Prevents unwanted breeding (enables mixed-gender housing).
- Animals can be housed together safely without breeding conflicts.

### Timing

**Best age:**
- Cattle: 2-4 months (painful but recovers quickly; younger animals have smaller testicles, easier extraction).
- Pigs: 1-4 weeks (very young = quick recovery; pain response brief).
- Goats/sheep: 1-3 months (similar to cattle; earlier = faster healing).
- Younger = easier recovery; older = more difficult, higher bleeding risk, longer healing.

**General rule:** Perform castration when the animal is young enough for rapid recovery but old enough to be healthy and strong.

### Methods

**Surgical castration (cutting):**
1. Restrain animal securely (cannot move during procedure).
2. Wash scrotum thoroughly with antiseptic (iodine or diluted bleach).
3. Make small incision (1-2 cm) over each testicle (cut through skin and tissue layer).
4. Extract testicle through incision (pull with gentle pressure; it will come out with traction).
5. Cut off the spermatic cord (the stalk connecting testicle to body).
   - Ligate (tie off) with thread/twine if available, then cut (most controlled).
   - Or, cut and cauterize (sear with hot iron to seal blood vessels).
   - Or, cut and let blood clot naturally (acceptable if careful; observe for bleeding).
6. Repeat on other side.
7. Do not close the incision; leave open to drain (prevents abscess formation).

**Elastrator (rubber band method):**
- Place rubber band very tightly around the scrotum above both testicles.
- Testicles die from lack of blood and fall off (7-14 days).
- Less painful than cutting; no surgery required.
- Downside: Requires elastrator tool (not improvised); takes days to complete; animal still experiences discomfort for 24-48 hours.
- Note: If rubber band slips off, bleeding can be severe.

**Burdizzo (crushing method):**
- Clamp that crushes the spermatic cord (doesn't cut; blocks blood flow).
- Testicles die slowly; are reabsorbed over weeks.
- No bleeding; low infection risk.
- Requires specialized Burdizzo tool (expensive, unlikely post-collapse).

### Care After Castration

- **Infection watch:** Check daily for swelling, discharge, fever (signs of infection).
- **Bleeding:** Some oozing is normal (first 12-24 hours). If bleeding continues heavily after 24 hours, apply pressure or cautery.
- **Healing:** Incision heals in 2-3 weeks; full tissue healing takes 4-6 weeks.
- **Activity:** Allow rest for 3-5 days; then gradual return to normal grazing/exercise.
- **Antibiotics:** If infection appears (swelling, discharge, fever), antibiotics if available (see Vol 4 for infection treatment).
- **Pain management:** Herbal pain relief (willow bark) post-procedure if available.

## Part 3: Dehorning

Dehorning removes horns. Benefits:
- Prevents animal injuries (to self and others; horned animals will gore each other, handlers, and themselves on fencing).
- Safer for handlers (reduces risk of puncture/goring).
- Reduces space needed (horned animals require more space to avoid hitting others with horns).
- Improves transport (easier to move dehorned animals without injuring them).

**Caution:** Horns are painful to remove (lots of nerve endings and blood vessels). Do when animal is young (if possible); older animals have larger horns and more blood loss.

### Timing

**Best age:**
- Cattle: 2-8 weeks (horns are just button-sized, still emerging; scoop method is easy).
- Goats/sheep: 2-12 weeks (similar timing to cattle; disbudding works well).
- Older animals: More painful (larger horn base); higher bleeding risk (larger blood vessels); healing slower.

**Rule:** Dehorn as early as possible when buds are small.

### Methods

**Surgical dehorning (scoop method):**
1. Restrain animal (immobilize head completely; cannot move).
2. Wash horn base and surrounding area with antiseptic.
3. Use a specialized scoop tool or sharp knife to cut around the horn base (go deep enough to get the germinal tissue that regrows the horn).
4. Remove the horn; expect bleeding (may be heavy).
5. Control bleeding:
   - Apply firm pressure with clean cloth for 10-15 minutes.
   - Or, cauterize (sear with hot iron to seal blood vessels; create eschar).
   - Or, apply styptic (if available; powder that promotes clotting).
6. Apply antiseptic to the wound and horn base.
7. Wound heals in 2-4 weeks.

**Disbudding (cautery, for young animals only):**
- Use a heated iron (special disbudding iron; like a branding iron) on the horn bud (before horn grows out).
- Heat kills the horn-growing tissue (germinal layer).
- No bleeding (tissue is cauterized); pain is brief (intense but brief; heat nerve endings and animal is done).
- Requires special disbudding iron (unlikely post-collapse).
- Must be done at 1-8 weeks (before horn erupts through skin).

**Paste/caustic method (for very young animals):**
- Apply caustic paste to horn bud (destroys horn tissue chemically).
- Destroys germinal tissue; prevents horn growth.
- Very young animals tolerate this better (less pain than surgery).
- Requires specialized caustic paste (not available post-collapse easily; making caustic paste is dangerous).

### Care After Dehorning

- **Infection watch:** Horn base is prone to infection (bacteria enter through the open wound). Check daily for discharge, swelling, foul smell.
- **Bleeding:** Some oozing is normal (first 24 hours). If bleeding continues heavily after 24 hours, apply pressure again or cauterize.
- **Healing:** Takes 4-6 weeks for full tissue healing.
- **Antibiotics:** If infection appears (swelling, discharge, fever, foul smell), antibiotics if available.
- **Fly prevention:** Flies are attracted to open wounds. Keep flies away if possible (cover wound or use fly-repellent).
- **Activity:** Allow 2-3 days rest; then normal activity.

## Part 4: Wound Care

### Laceration (Cut)

**Assessment:**
- Is the cut deep (beyond skin to muscle or deeper)?
- Are edges clean or ragged?
- How much bleeding (steady ooze vs. spray)?
- Is there dirt or debris?
- How long ago was the cut (fresh vs. hours old)?

**Treatment:**
1. **Stop bleeding:** Apply firm pressure with clean cloth for 5-15 minutes. Do not look or prod the wound during this time.
2. **Clean:** Flush with water (hot water is better if available). Remove dirt, debris, dead tissue with clean cloth.
3. **Assessment:** After cleaning, look at the wound.
   - Clean edges coming together easily: suture or leave open (both work).
   - Ragged/torn edges: leave open; suturing may increase infection risk.
   - Deep wound (beyond skin): suturing can trap infection if you do not have clean technique; consider leaving open.
4. **Closure:** If you have sterile thread and needle, and edges are clean and close together, suture. Otherwise, leave open.
5. **Dressing:** Apply antiseptic (iodine, diluted bleach, or honey) and cover with clean cloth. Change dressing daily.

**Healing timeline:**
- Superficial cuts: 1-2 weeks.
- Deep cuts: 3-4 weeks.

### Puncture Wound

**Risk:** Sealed wound = high infection risk (bacteria trapped inside; infection builds pressure and destroys tissue).

**Treatment:**
1. Flush with water (try to open the wound if possible; do NOT seal puncture wounds).
2. Remove any foreign material (dirt, wood splinter, metal shard). Use tweezers if available; water pressure if not.
3. Do NOT close with stitches (infection will build pressure and rupture or spread to deeper tissues).
4. Leave open for drainage.
5. Apply antiseptic (iodine or honey are good; avoid heavy bandaging).
6. Watch closely for infection (fever, swelling, discharge, lameness, reluctance to use area).
7. If infection appears (swelling after 2-3 days, discharge, fever), drain the wound:
   - Flush with water.
   - Apply antiseptic.
   - Leave open; do NOT close.

### Abrasion (Scrape)

**Assessment:** Skin is scraped; underlying tissue is not cut.

**Treatment:**
1. Clean thoroughly (use running water to remove dirt and embedded debris).
2. Apply antiseptic (iodine, honey, or herbal).
3. Allow to dry or apply light gauze to protect.
4. Change dressing every 2-3 days.
5. Watch for infection (unlikely unless contamination was severe).

**Healing timeline:** 1-2 weeks.

### Abscess (Enclosed Infection)

**Signs:** Swelling (localized bulge), heat (warm to touch), fluctuance (fluid movement under skin when you press), pain.

**Treatment:**
1. Drain: Lance (cut) the abscess with a clean knife to open it. Cut at the top of the abscess (where it's most fluid-filled).
2. Empty: Squeeze out pus thoroughly (you want all pus out; incomplete drainage will refill).
3. Flush: Rinse the cavity with water or antiseptic solution.
4. Leave open: Do not close; allow continued drainage.
5. Clean daily: Redress with antiseptic; squeeze out any new pus.
6. Healing: Takes 1-2 weeks.

**Caution:** Do not lance if you cannot identify the abscess clearly (is it truly pus or is it swelling?). Lancing deep internal pus can be dangerous (may hit organs or major vessels). When in doubt, apply warmth and wait a day or two to see if it comes to a head.

### Surgical Site Infection

**Prevention:**
- Aseptic technique (clean hands, clean tools, clean area before surgery).
- Avoid contamination during healing (keep wound clean; change dressings).
- Do not allow animal to lick or chew the wound.

**Signs of infection:** Swelling, heat, discharge (especially pus or foul smell), fever, animal unwilling to use area or put weight on limb.

**Treatment:**
- Open the incision if it's sealed (drain pus).
- Flush with water or antiseptic.
- Dress daily with antiseptic; leave open for continued drainage.
- Antibiotics if available (see Vol 11, Ch 1 for dosing).
- Monitor closely; if infection spreads (fever, systemic illness), consider culling the animal to prevent suffering.

## Part 5: Surgical Complications and Management

### Hemorrhage (Excessive Bleeding)

**Risk:** Major blood loss during or after surgery can lead to shock and death.

**Prevention:**
- Know where major blood vessels are (avoid them during surgery).
- Use ligature (tie off) large vessels before cutting.
- Work carefully; do not nick blood vessels accidentally.

**Management:**
- Pressure: Apply firm, continuous pressure for 10-15 minutes. Do not look or remove cloth during this time.
- Tourniquet: If bleeding does not stop, tie cloth tightly above the wound (between wound and body). This cuts off all blood flow. Loosen every 30 minutes to prevent tissue death.
- Cautery: Sear bleeding vessel with hot iron (creates eschar; seals vessel).
- Ligation: If you can see the bleeding vessel, tie it off with thread.

**When to stop surgery:** If bleeding is uncontrolled after 15-20 minutes of pressure, consider stopping the procedure if it is not essential. The animal is in shock risk.

### Shock

**Signs:** Rapid heartbeat, rapid breathing, pale mucous membranes, cold extremities, lethargy, collapse.

**Cause:** Major blood loss, severe pain, or severe stress.

**Treatment:**
- Lay animal down (reduce metabolic demand).
- Warmth (blankets if available).
- Fluids: Give oral fluids (water with salt and sugar) if animal can swallow. IV fluids if you have the skill.
- Rest: Minimize further stress.
- Antibiotics: If available (infection can trigger shock).

**Prevention:** Anesthesia and pain control reduce shock risk. Fast surgery = less shock.

### Seroma (Fluid Collection)

**Signs:** Swelling under surgical incision (soft, fluid-filled), appears 2-5 days after surgery.

**Cause:** Lymph fluid accumulates in the surgical space.

**Treatment:**
- Usually resolves on its own (fluid is reabsorbed).
- If large and causing problems, can lance and drain (but risk of infection).
- Apply warm compresses (may encourage reabsorption).
- Leave the surgical site open if possible (allows drainage).

**Caution:** Seroma is not infected (it's clear fluid, not pus). Do not treat as an abscess.

### Dehiscence (Incision Opens)

**Signs:** Sutures break or incision opens; wound splits.

**Cause:** Animal movement (licking, scratching, excessive activity), weak sutures, or infection underneath.

**Treatment:**
- Re-suture if possible (if freshly opened and infection-free).
- Or leave open and let heal slowly (called secondary healing; takes longer).
- Keep clean; prevent infection.
- Restrict animal movement (rest, isolation).

## Part 6: Special Surgical Procedures

### Teat Surgery (Goats/Sheep Dairy)

**Problem:** Blocked or damaged teat (prevents milking).

**Assessment:** Can you feel a blockage? Is the teat injured?

**Treatment (if blocked):**
- Try massaging the teat (may dislodge blockage).
- Try milking gently (may force blockage out).
- If that fails, you may need to insert a small tube to bypass the blockage (very delicate; requires care).
- Surgery: Removing a blocked teat is last resort (dam loses milking capacity).

### Hernia Repair

**Hernia:** Organ protrudes through a hole in the body wall.

**Types:**
- Umbilical (at navel): Common in young animals; often closes on its own.
- Ventral (belly): Can be serious if large.

**Treatment:**
- Small hernias often close on their own (no treatment needed).
- Large hernias: Surgical closure (requires expertise; consider culling if you cannot repair).

## Part 7: Anesthesia and Pain Management

### Without Anesthesia

Post-collapse, anesthesia will not be available. Procedures must be done with restraint.

**Restraint methods:**
- Physical (holding the animal down with ropes, people).
- Pressure (applying pressure to nerves, which blocks some sensation).
- Combinations of the above.

**Speed:** Work quickly. Pain is briefer if surgery is fast.

### Herbal Pain Management

**Before surgery (mild sedation):**
- Valerian root (if available).
- Chamomile infusion.

**After surgery (pain relief):**
- Willow bark (contains salicin, like aspirin).
- Meadowsweet.
- Ginger root.

**Effectiveness:** Less than pharmaceutical pain relievers, but some relief.

## Verification

Your field surgery capability is adequate if:
1. **Restraint:** You can safely immobilize animals for procedures.
2. **Asepsis:** You understand cleanliness and contamination prevention.
3. **Hemorrhage control:** You can stop bleeding using pressure, cautery, or tourniquet.
4. **Castration:** You can perform castration safely (minimal complications).
5. **Dehorning:** You can remove horns or disbuds.
6. **Wound care:** You can treat lacerations, punctures, and abscesses.
7. **Infection monitoring:** You watch for post-surgical infections and treat them.

## Common Mistakes

**Rushing the procedure.** You increase mistakes and complications.

**Not controlling bleeding.** Hemorrhage is life-threatening. Practice pressure and cautery.

**Leaving wounds closed.** Sealed wounds get infected. Leave open for drainage.

**Not monitoring post-surgery.** Infection is the main post-surgical risk. Check daily.

**Operating on unhealthy animals.** Surgery on sick animals is very dangerous. Ensure animal is healthy first.

**Not restraining properly.** A loose animal is a dangerous animal (and inaccurate surgery). Restrain completely.

**Using dirty instruments.** Infection ruins everything. Keep instruments clean.
