## Overview

Parasites live in or on animals, consuming their blood and nutrients. Parasite-infested animals are weak, grow poorly, and are prone to secondary infections.

This chapter covers:
1. **Internal parasites:** Worms (roundworms, tapeworms, flukes).
2. **External parasites:** Lice, mites, fleas, flies.
3. **Prevention:** Rotation, sanitation, nutrition.
4. **Treatment:** Herbal remedies, isolation, culling.

Post-collapse, parasite prevention will consume more time than any other animal health task.

## Prerequisites

Understand animal health (Vol 11, Ch 1-3). Know crop rotation (Vol 10, Ch 5).

## Part 1: Internal Parasites (Worms)

### Types of Worms

**Roundworms (Nematodes):**
- Live in stomach and intestines.
- Consume blood and nutrients.
- Most common parasite in livestock post-collapse.

**Tapeworms (Cestodes):**
- Segment into the body; absorb nutrients.
- Less common than roundworms.
- More difficult to treat.

**Flukes (Trematodes):**
- Live in bile ducts and liver.
- Require aquatic snail as intermediate host (affect cattle grazing wet pasture).
- Cause anemia, weakness, death if severe.

### Signs of Internal Parasites

**Heavy parasite load:**
- Poor growth (thin animal, ribs visible).
- Rough, dull coat.
- Anemia (pale gums, pale mucous membranes).
- Diarrhea (loose stool, may be bloody).
- Lethargy (animal is weak, depressed).
- Pot-belly appearance (bloated abdomen).
- Failure to gain weight despite eating.

**Risk factors:**
- Young animals more susceptible than adults.
- Poor nutrition increases susceptibility.
- Wet pasture (facilitates transmission).
- High stocking density (more parasites on pasture).
- Failure to rotate pasture.

### Lifecycle of Roundworms (Most Common)

Understanding the lifecycle helps you prevent infection.

1. **Adult worms in intestines:** Lay eggs in feces.
2. **Eggs on pasture:** Survive in soil for weeks/months.
3. **Larvae develop:** In damp conditions on pasture (7-21 days).
4. **Infection:** Animal grazes, ingests infective larvae.
5. **Larvae mature:** In animal's intestines (2-4 weeks).
6. **Cycle repeats:** Adults lay eggs; shed in feces.

**Key point:** Pasture is contaminated only if infected animals graze it. Clean pasture (no infected animals in weeks) has minimal parasite risk.

### Prevention of Internal Parasites

**Pasture rotation (most effective):**
- Move animals to clean pasture every 1-3 weeks (cattle, goats, sheep).
  - Cattle: Every 2-3 weeks (heavier grazing pressure; pasture recovery slower).
  - Goats/sheep: Every 1-2 weeks (lighter animals; graze more efficiently; parasite transmission faster).
- Leave old pasture ungrazed for 4-6 weeks minimum (kills larvae in soil; depends on temperature and moisture).
  - Warmer, wetter climates: 4 weeks.
  - Cool or dry climates: 6-8 weeks.
- Cycle back to first pasture after rest period.

**Practical rotation example (5-paddock system):**
1. Week 1: Animals on paddock 1.
2. Week 2: Animals move to paddock 2; paddock 1 rests.
3. Week 3: Animals move to paddock 3; paddocks 1-2 rest.
4. Week 4: Animals move to paddock 4; paddocks 1-3 rest.
5. Week 5: Animals move to paddock 5; paddocks 1-4 rest.
6. Week 6: Animals return to paddock 1 (now clean; larvae have died).
7. Repeat.

**Rules:**
- Cattle to cattle (do not rotate with different species on same pasture; different parasites).
- Sheep/goats separate from cattle (parasite transmission varies by species).
- Poultry can follow other animals (eat larvae and break cycle; beneficial).
- Do not mix susceptible and resistant species (loses benefit; parasites adapt).

**Sanitation:**
- Remove manure from housing daily (breaks lifecycle; prevents buildup).
- Compost manure for 4+ weeks at high temperature (kills parasite eggs and larvae).
- Do not allow water sources to become contaminated with feces (separate water from manure areas).
- Keep animals off wet, muddy ground (reduces parasite transmission; wet conditions favor larval survival).
- Clean feeders and water troughs weekly (prevent fecal contamination).

**Nutrition (critical factor):**
- Well-fed animals resist parasites better (immune system is stronger).
- Malnourished animals become heavily infected quickly.
- Adequate protein is critical for immune response (10-14% protein for young animals; 8-10% for adults).
- Mineral deficiencies (copper, zinc, selenium) increase susceptibility.
- Supplementation post-collapse: Feed legumes (beans, clover) for protein; mineral supplementation if available.

**Avoid stress:**
- Stressed animals (transport, weather, hunger) have increased susceptibility.
- Minimize handling and disturbance.
- Provide adequate shelter from heat, cold, and rain.
- Ensure water is always available (dehydration increases parasite transmission risk).

### Treatment of Internal Parasites

**Herbal treatments (limited effectiveness, but available post-collapse):**
- **Garlic:** Feed raw garlic (1-2 cloves per day). Mild effect; some animals refuse it.
- **Wormwood (Artemisia absinthium):** Dried herb or infusion. Traditional anthelmintic. Effectiveness variable.
- **Diatomaceous earth (food-grade):** Mix into feed (10-15% of ration). Works by mechanical action (cuts parasites). Limited effectiveness.
- **Pumpkin seeds:** High in cucurbitacin; traditionally used. Mild effect.

**Effectiveness:** Herbal treatments are 0-30% effective at best. They slow parasite reproduction but do not cure heavy infections.

**Pharmaceutical anthelmintics (if available post-collapse):**
- Benzimidazoles (e.g., fenbendazole): Highly effective. If you have access, use sparingly.
- Macrocyclic lactones (e.g., ivermectin): Highly effective; also controls external parasites.

**Dosing:** Requires knowledge of animal weight and drug type. (See Vol 11, Ch 1 for antibiotic dosing principles; similar approach.)

**When to treat:**
- **Heavy infection (clinical signs present):** Treat immediately. Signs: severe diarrhea, visible anemia (pale gums), weight loss despite eating, lethargy, visible worms in stool.
- **Moderate infection:** Treat if animal is young or breeding. Losing weight = losing productivity and reproductive capacity.
- **Light infection:** Manage through rotation and nutrition; avoid treating if parasite load is manageable (animal maintains weight, no clinical signs).

**Treatment priority (if you have limited anthelmintic drugs):**
1. **Breeding females:** Before breeding season (improves conception rate, milk production, colostrum quality).
2. **Young animals:** Calves, piglets, lambs in spring (parasite pressure is highest; young animals cannot compete with parasite burden).
3. **Lactating females:** Currently producing milk (parasites compete for nutrients; reduces milk yield).
4. **Sick/weak animals:** Heavy infections causing clinical signs.

**Strategic treatment approach:**
- Do NOT treat all animals (saves drugs; prevents resistance).
- Treat the top 20-30% most affected animals.
- Leave lightly infected animals untreated (allows natural immune response; keeps parasite population diverse).

**Anthelmintic rotation (if drugs are available):**
- Do not use the same drug repeatedly (parasites develop resistance).
- Rotate between different drug classes (e.g., benzimidazole one year, macrocyclic lactone next).
- Record which drugs you use and when (prevents accidental repeat use).

### Monitoring for Parasites

**Visual signs:** Check regularly for:
- Dull coat.
- Visible ribs (poor condition).
- Pale gums (anemia).
- Diarrhea.
- Lethargy.

**Fecal exam (if possible):** Collect fresh manure sample; look for worm eggs under microscope (if available). High egg count = heavy infection.

**Action threshold:** If multiple animals show signs, or one animal is severely affected, take action (rotate pasture, treat).

## Part 2: External Parasites

### Types of External Parasites

**Lice:**
- Live on skin/hair; feed on blood and skin.
- Cause intense itching.
- Spread through direct contact.

**Mange mites:**
- Burrow into skin; cause scabies-like disease.
- Intense itching, hair loss, thick skin.
- Contagious; spread rapidly.

**Fleas:**
- Bite and feed on blood.
- Cause itching.
- Transmit tapeworms.

**Flies:**
- Biting flies (e.g., horn flies): Feed on blood, cause irritation and anemia.
- Screw-worms: Lay eggs in open wounds; larvae burrow into tissue (cause serious infection).

**Sheep ked (tick-like parasite):**
- Burrows under wool; causes intense itching.
- Wool loss, poor fleece quality.
- Contagious among sheep.

**Ticks:**
- Feed on blood.
- Transmit diseases (less common post-collapse if tick-borne diseases are not in your region).
- Cause anemia if heavy infestation.

### Signs of External Parasites

**General signs:**
- Itching (animal scratches constantly).
- Hair loss (bald patches).
- Rough, scurfy coat.
- Restlessness (cannot settle or sleep).
- Secondary skin infections (from scratching).
- Anemia (if parasite burden is high).

**Specific signs:**
- **Lice:** Visible nits (eggs) on hair; small moving insects.
- **Mange:** Thickened, crusty skin (especially neck, shoulders, legs).
- **Fleas:** Tiny jumping insects; flea dirt (dark specks) in coat.
- **Sheep ked:** Moving bumps under wool.

### Prevention of External Parasites

**Nutrition:**
- Well-nourished animals have healthier skin and stronger immune response.
- Deficiencies (zinc, vitamin A) increase susceptibility.

**Sanitation:**
- Keep housing clean (remove manure, bedding regularly).
- Reduce overcrowding (parasites spread faster in dense populations).
- Wash hands between handling animals (prevent transmission).

**Isolation:**
- Quarantine new animals for 2-4 weeks before mixing (detect parasites early).
- Isolate infested animals immediately (prevent spread).

**Pasture rotation:**
- Helps somewhat (parasites are shed in feces; rotating removes exposure source).
- Less effective than for internal parasites.

**Wool care (sheep):**
- Shearing once yearly (spring) removes sheep ked habitat.
- Regular shearing reduces parasite load in wool-bearing breeds.

### Treatment of External Parasites

**Herbal treatments:**
- **Sulfur dust:** Mix with lard or oil; rub into coat. Effective against mites and lice.
- **Neem oil:** Spray or rub onto affected areas. Repels parasites; may kill some (limited effectiveness).
- **Diatomaceous earth:** Dust onto coat. Kills some parasites by mechanical action.
- **Ash:** Rub into coat (traditional remedy). Limited effectiveness; animals dislike it.
- **Garlic:** Feed internally or apply topically (mix with oil). Limited effectiveness.

**Effectiveness:** Herbal treatments are 10-40% effective. Manual removal (combing) is often necessary.

**Pharmaceutical treatments (if available):**
- **Dips:** Bathe animal in insecticide solution (e.g., pyrethrin-based).
- **Topical treatments:** Ivermectin (pour-on), permethrin (spray).
- **Oral treatments:** Ivermectin (treats both internal and external parasites).

**Practical post-collapse approach:**
- Sulfur: Most likely available (mined/produced post-collapse).
- Manual removal: Comb, scrub with brush.
- Culling: Heavily infested animals that do not respond to treatment.

**Application:**
1. Isolate affected animal(s).
2. Apply sulfur or herbal treatment to affected areas.
3. Repeat every 3-5 days for 2-3 weeks (must catch new parasites as they emerge from eggs).
4. If no improvement after 3 weeks, cull the animal.

### Managing Fly-Borne Parasites

**Horn flies (biting flies):**
- Cause blood loss and irritation.
- Prevention: Pasture rotation, fly repellent (citronella, garlic).
- Treatment: Manual removal (swat flies), topical insecticide if available.

**Screw-worms (fly larvae in wounds):**
- Serious threat; larvae destroy tissue.
- Prevention: Keep wounds clean and covered; treat any open wound immediately.
- Treatment: Remove larvae manually (tweezers); clean wound thoroughly; apply insecticide if available.
- Caution: Screw-worm is serious; if widespread in your region, isolated animals will die. Emphasize wound prevention.

## Part 3: Integrated Parasite Management

### Strategic Approach

**Emphasis on prevention:** Parasite control post-collapse will depend on prevention, not treatment.

**Multi-pronged strategy:**
1. **Rotation:** Move animals frequently (1-3 weeks on clean pasture).
2. **Sanitation:** Remove manure; keep housing clean.
3. **Nutrition:** Feed animals well (adequate protein, minerals, vitamins).
4. **Isolation:** Quarantine new animals; isolate infected animals.
5. **Selective treatment:** Treat only when necessary (breeding animals, young animals, heavy infections).
6. **Culling:** Remove animals that do not improve with treatment or that have chronic, heavy infections.

### Timeline for Parasite Control

**Spring (March-May):**
- Highest parasite transmission (warm, wet conditions).
- Animals grazing on contaminated winter pasture.
- Monitor closely; treat breeding females before season.

**Summer (June-August):**
- Continued transmission; rotation is critical.
- External parasites (flies, lice) peak in hot weather.
- Monitor daily; act quickly on signs.

**Fall (September-October):**
- Transmission decreases (drier, cooler).
- Secondary treatment round (treat animals before winter).
- Prepare for winter confinement (housing can trap parasites if not cleaned).

**Winter (November-February):**
- Lowest transmission (cold, animals indoors).
- Daily manure removal is critical (prevents buildup).
- Monitor for lice and mites (indoor spread).

### Monitoring and Decision Tree

**Daily observation:**
- Look for signs: coat condition, appetite, energy.

**Weekly inspection:**
- Check 5-10% of herd (rotate which animals you check).
- Look for pale gums (anemia), dull coat, visible ribs, diarrhea.

**Action decision:**
- If 1-2 animals show signs: Isolate; rotate pasture; treat with herbal/antibiotic if severe.
- If 20%+ of herd affected: Pasture rotation is failing; move entire herd now; check for other problems (nutrition, water, disease).

## Verification

Your parasite control is adequate if:
1. **Prevention focus:** You emphasize rotation and sanitation over treatment.
2. **Rotation schedule:** Pasture is rotated every 1-3 weeks (animals have clean grazing).
3. **Sanitation:** Housing is cleaned daily; manure is removed.
4. **Monitoring:** You check animals regularly for signs of parasites.
5. **Treatment access:** You have herbal remedies available (garlic, sulfur, wormwood).
6. **Isolation:** You can isolate animals quickly (separate housing or pasture).
7. **Decision-making:** You know when to treat, when to cull, and when to accept light parasitism.

## Common Mistakes

**Over-reliance on treatment.** Herbal and pharmaceutical treatments work poorly post-collapse. Prevention is everything.

**Not rotating pasture.** Continuous grazing leads to heavy parasite load within weeks.

**Not isolating sick animals.** Parasites spread rapidly among grouped animals.

**Inadequate nutrition.** Malnourished animals cannot fight parasites.

**Ignoring early signs.** A single thin animal today is a epidemic tomorrow.

**Treating all animals.** Selective treatment preserves susceptible parasites (some without drug exposure = slower resistance).

**Not culling heavily infested animals.** A parasite reservoir that does not improve drains resources and infects others. Cull.

**Keeping animals on wet pasture.** Wet ground allows parasite eggs and larvae to survive; move animals to dry pasture.
