## Overview

Goats and sheep are smaller than cattle but produce meat, milk, fiber, and hides. They are hardy, prolific breeders, and resilient in harsh conditions.

**Goats:**
- Excellent browsers (eat brush, weeds, tree leaves).
- Produce milk (1-3 liters per day).
- Meat (15-25 kg per animal).
- Fiber (Angora, Cashmere breeds).
- Prolific breeders (multiple kids per year).

**Sheep:**
- Excellent grazers (eat grass efficiently).
- Produce wool (fleece per year).
- Meat (20-50 kg per animal).
- Milk (0.5-1.5 liters per day).
- Gestation and growth rates similar to goats.

This chapter teaches you to keep both species healthy and productive.

## Part 1: Goat Anatomy and Health

### Body Systems

**Digestive system:**
- Ruminant (four-chambered stomach like cattle).
- Excellent at extracting nutrition from poor forage.
- Can digest tough, fibrous plants others cannot.
- Rumination normal (cud-chewing visible).

**Reproductive system:**
- Does (females) breed seasonally (fall to winter in temperate climates).
- Gestation: 150 days (~5 months).
- Average kids per litter: 2-3 (twins common; triplets possible).
- Does can have 2-3 litters per 2 years if managed intensively.

**Udder (dairy breeds):**
- Two teats (one per side).
- Milk production 1-3 liters per day (varies by breed).

### Normal Signs of Goat Health

- **Eyes:** Bright, alert, responsive.
- **Nose:** Clean, slightly moist.
- **Coat:** Shiny, lying flat. No bald spots or lesions.
- **Body condition:** Ribs slightly visible (not protruding). Spine not sharp.
- **Appetite:** Eats readily, rumination visible.
- **Manure:** Formed balls (goats produce pellets like sheep).
- **Behavior:** Alert, playful, active.

### Common Goat Diseases

**Caprine arthritis-encephalitis (CAE):**
- **Cause:** Viral infection (retrovirus).
- **Signs:** Joint swelling, stiffness, arthritis. In kids: neurological signs (tremors, paralysis).
- **Prevention:** Avoid colostrum from infected does. Test before breeding.
- **Treatment:** No cure. Manage arthritis (pain relief, soft bedding).

**Caseous lymphadenitis (CL):**
- **Cause:** Bacterial (Corynebacterium pseudotuberculosis).
- **Signs:** Swollen, infected lymph nodes (abscess). Drain pus (thick, yellowish).
- **Prevention:** Isolate infected animals. Disinfect equipment. Test before breeding.
- **Treatment:** Drain abscesses. Antibiotics if available. Cull if widespread.

**Mastitis:**
- **Cause:** Bacterial infection of udder.
- **Signs:** Hot, hard, swollen udder. Clotty, discolored milk. Fever. Pain.
- **Prevention:** Clean udder before milking. Dry teats with clean cloth.
- **Treatment:** Antibiotics if available. Frequent milking. Warm compresses.

**Parasites:**
- **Internal:** Worms (much more problematic in goats than cattle).
- **Signs:** Poor growth, rough coat, anemia (pale gums), diarrhea.
- **Prevention:** Rotate pasture every 1-2 weeks (critical). Avoid wet ground.
- **Treatment:** Herbal (garlic, wormwood) or anthelmintics.

**Pneumonia:**
- **Cause:** Bacterial, often after stress/cold.
- **Signs:** Cough, nasal discharge, fever, lethargy.
- **Prevention:** Good ventilation, avoid drafts, minimize stress.
- **Treatment:** Antibiotics if available. Supportive care (warm shelter).

**Metabolic diseases (ketosis, hypocalcemia):**
- **Cause:** Imbalance in metabolism (energy or calcium).
- **Signs:** Weakness, loss of appetite, neurological signs, tremors.
- **Risk:** Pregnant or lactating does with inadequate nutrition.
- **Prevention:** Adequate feed and minerals.
- **Treatment:** Energy/calcium supplementation (drenching with glucose or calcium solution if available).

### Goat Nutrition

**Requirements:**
- Maintenance: 2-4% protein (depending on body condition).
- Pregnancy: 10-12% protein.
- Lactation: 12-14% protein.
- Minerals: Calcium, phosphorus (avoid excess potassium, which prevents calcium absorption).

**Good feeds:**
- Hay (mixed grass/legume).
- Grains (barley, oats, corn).
- Legumes (alfalfa hay, clover).
- Brush and tree leaves (goats prefer browsing over grazing).

## Part 2: Sheep Anatomy and Health

### Body Systems

**Digestive system:**
- Ruminant (four-chambered stomach).
- Excellent at converting grass to meat and wool.
- Less capable than goats of eating coarse forage.

**Reproductive system:**
- Ewes (females) breed seasonally (fall to early spring).
- Gestation: 147 days (~5 months).
- Average lambs per litter: 1-2 (twins common, triplets rare).
- Breeds can have 1-3 litters per 2 years.

**Wool production:**
- Shearing once per year (spring).
- Fleece weight: 2-6 kg per animal per year (depends on breed).

### Normal Signs of Sheep Health

- **Eyes:** Bright, alert.
- **Nose:** Clean, slightly moist.
- **Wool/coat:** Dense, no bare patches. Wool should be uniform color.
- **Body condition:** Ribs slightly visible.
- **Appetite:** Eats readily, rumination visible.
- **Manure:** Formed pellets (like goats).
- **Behavior:** Alert, active. Moves normally.

### Common Sheep Diseases

**Foot rot:**
- **Cause:** Bacterial infection of foot (between hooves).
- **Signs:** Severe lameness, swollen foot, foul smell, discharge.
- **Prevention:** Keep feet dry. Trim hooves regularly (every 6-8 weeks).
- **Treatment:** Soak foot in footbath, remove infected tissue, antibiotics, dry housing.

**Pneumonia:**
- **Cause:** Bacterial (often Mannheimia or Pasteurella).
- **Signs:** Cough, nasal discharge, fever, lethargy, rapid breathing.
- **Prevention:** Good ventilation, avoid stress.
- **Treatment:** Antibiotics if available.

**Internal parasites (worms):**
- **Cause:** Gastrointestinal nematodes (very common).
- **Signs:** Poor growth, rough coat, anemia, diarrhea, weight loss, fatigue.
- **Prevention:** Rotate pasture frequently (every 2-3 weeks). Avoid wet areas.
- **Treatment:** Herbal (garlic, wormwood) or anthelmintics.

**External parasites:**
- **Lice, mites:** Cause itching, wool loss, poor condition.
- **Sheep ked (tick):** Burrows under wool, causes intense itching.
- **Prevention:** Good nutrition strengthens resistance. Regular shearing (removes ked habitat).
- **Treatment:** Dips or topical treatments (if available). Herbal: sulfur powder.

**Bloat:**
- **Cause:** Gas accumulation in rumen.
- **Signs:** Abdominal distension (left side bloated). Difficulty breathing.
- **Risk:** Sudden access to lush legumes.
- **Prevention:** Introduce legumes gradually.
- **Treatment:** Pass tube to release gas. Stand uphill. Gentle pressure on left side.

**Pregnancy toxemia (ketosis):**
- **Cause:** Metabolic imbalance in late pregnancy/early lactation.
- **Signs:** Weakness, loss of appetite, neurological signs (stumbling, paralysis).
- **Risk:** Underfed pregnant ewes. Overweight ewes.
- **Prevention:** Adequate nutrition, especially in last 6 weeks of pregnancy.
- **Treatment:** Energy supplementation (glucose drenches, grain).

### Sheep Nutrition

**Requirements:**
- Maintenance: 8-10% protein.
- Pregnancy: 12-14% protein.
- Lactation: 14-16% protein.
- Minerals: Copper, zinc, selenium (deficiencies common in some regions).

**Good feeds:**
- Grass pasture (primary feed).
- Legume hay (alfalfa).
- Grains (barley, oats).

## Part 3: Comparison of Goats and Sheep

| Characteristic | Goats | Sheep |
|---|---|---|
| **Browsing** | Excellent (prefer brush/leaves) | Poor (grass-focused) |
| **Fencing** | High (escape artists) | Lower (stay contained) |
| **Milk production** | 1-3 L/day | 0.5-1.5 L/day |
| **Meat yield** | 15-25 kg | 20-50 kg |
| **Fiber** | Angora/Cashmere (optional) | Wool (standard) |
| **Parasite resistance** | Moderate | Low (parasite-prone) |
| **Breeding frequency** | 2-3 litters/2 years | 1-2 litters/year |
| **Feed selectivity** | Very selective (picky) | Less selective |
| **Predator vulnerability** | Moderate | High (need protection) |
| **Noise level** | High (vocal) | Low (quiet) |

## Part 4: Reproductive Management

### Breeding Season

**Goats (temperate climate):**
- Does come into heat: August-February (fall/winter).
- 21-day cycle (if not pregnant).
- Heat signs: Tail wagging, vocalization, vulva swelling.

**Sheep (temperate climate):**
- Ewes come into heat: September-April (fall/winter).
- 17-day cycle.
- Heat signs: Tail wagging, restlessness, seeking rams.

### Pregnancy and Birth

**Gestation length:**
- Goats: 150 days.
- Sheep: 147 days.

**Signs of impending birth:**
- Udder fills 1-2 days before birth.
- Vulva swells.
- Dam becomes restless.

**Assistance if needed:**
- Most births are unassisted.
- If labor is prolonged (2+ hours), check for complications.
- Present limbs should come out first (head between forelegs).
- If presentation is wrong (breach, head back), assistance may be needed (reposition fetus if possible; may require expert help).

### Nursing and Weaning

**Colostrum (first milk):**
- Critical: Contains antibodies and immunoglobulins.
- Kids/lambs must nurse within 2-4 hours of birth.

**Weaning age:**
- Goats: 8-12 weeks.
- Sheep: 8-16 weeks.
- Depends on dam's milk production and available feed.

## Part 5: Hoof and Wool Care

### Hoof Trimming

**Frequency:** Every 6-8 weeks.

**Tools needed:** Hoof knife or shears.

**Procedure:**
1. Pick out dirt/debris from hoof.
2. Trim sole (bottom) to remove excess growth.
3. Trim walls (sides) to shape hoof.
4. Trim heel to proper angle.
5. Check for signs of disease (smell, discharge).

**Proper hoof shape:**
- Sole: Slightly concave (not flat).
- Wall: Vertical (not splayed).
- Heel: 45-degree angle to ground.

### Wool Handling (Sheep)

**Shearing:**
- Once per year (spring, before summer heat).
- Fleece weight: 2-6 kg per animal.
- Clean fleece (low vegetable matter) is more valuable.

**Fleece care:**
- Keep sheep out of mud/dirt before shearing (cleaner fleece).
- Store fleece in cool, dry place.
- Sort fleece by color and quality.
- Process for wool (see Vol 16: Textile & Fiber Arts).

## Verification

Your goat and sheep health management is adequate if:
1. **Health signs:** You can identify healthy goats and sheep.
2. **Housing:** Shelter is well-ventilated, dry, predator-proof (especially sheep).
3. **Pasture management:** You rotate pasture frequently (parasite control).
4. **Reproduction:** You understand breeding season and manage breeding intentionally.
5. **Nutrition:** Animals are fed appropriate diet for their age/purpose.
6. **Disease recognition:** You spot illness early (lameness, respiratory signs, poor condition).
7. **Hoof care:** You trim hooves regularly (every 6-8 weeks).
8. **Parasite monitoring:** You watch for parasite signs and treat/rotate accordingly.

## Common Mistakes

**Overstocking.** Too many animals degrade pasture and increase parasites.

**Poor parasite management.** Not rotating pasture allows parasites to build up; animals become sick.

**Inadequate minerals.** Copper, zinc, selenium deficiencies are common. Supplement if needed.

**Ignoring foot disease.** Foot rot is painful and spreads quickly. Trim hooves regularly and treat any signs immediately.

**Mixing goats and sheep.** Different grazing patterns; parasites can cross-infect. Keep separate if possible.

**Not isolating sick animals.** Contagious diseases spread rapidly. Isolate immediately.

**Inadequate shelter.** Sheep especially are vulnerable to cold and wet. Shelter is essential.
