## Overview

Pigs and poultry (chickens, ducks, geese, turkeys) are smaller livestock than cattle, but they multiply protein production and convert scraps into eggs and meat.

Pigs:
- Convert grain and scraps into meat rapidly.
- Produce multiple piglets per breeding (5-10 per litter).
- Are hardy and disease-resistant compared to chickens.

Poultry:
- Convert grain efficiently into eggs (most important).
- Eggs provide protein, fat, and micronutrients year-round.
- Chickens mature to meat size in 8-12 weeks.
- Are vulnerable to disease (flocks can collapse rapidly).

This chapter teaches you to keep both healthy and productive.

## Part 1: Pig Anatomy and Health

### Body Systems

**Digestive system:**
- Monogastric (single-chambered stomach, like humans).
- Short digestive tract; food moves quickly.
- Can digest grains, vegetables, meat scraps.
- Naturally omnivorous.

**Reproductive system:**
- Sows (females) come into heat every 21 days (if not pregnant).
- Gestation: ~114 days (3 months, 3 weeks, 3 days).
- Average litter: 8-10 piglets (can be 4-15).
- Boars (males) can breed year-round.

**Respiratory system:**
- Prone to pneumonia (similar to cattle).
- Normal breathing: 8-20 breaths per minute.

### Normal Signs of Pig Health

- **Eyes:** Bright, alert.
- **Nose:** Clean, slightly moist.
- **Skin:** Pink (if not pigmented), no sores or lesions.
- **Coat:** Smooth, shiny.
- **Appetite:** Eats readily, eagerly.
- **Manure:** Formed, brown-colored.
- **Behavior:** Alert, active (rooting, exploring).
- **Udder (lactating sows):** Symmetrical, firm, no discharge except milk.

### Common Pig Diseases

**Swine fever (if present in your region):**
- **Cause:** Viral infection.
- **Signs:** High fever (40-42°C), lethargy, purple discoloration of skin, jaundice.
- **Prevention:** Isolate sick animals. Cull infected animals (no treatment).
- **Note:** Post-collapse, if disease appears, entire herds may need culling to prevent spread.

**Erysipelothrix infection (if in your region):**
- **Cause:** Bacterial infection (Erysipelothrix rhusiopathiae).
- **Signs:** High fever, pink/red skin lesions (diamond-shaped), lethargy, lameness.
- **Treatment:** Antibiotics if available (penicillin).

**Parasites:**
- **Internal:** Roundworms, hookworms.
- **Signs:** Poor growth, rough coat, diarrhea, anemia.
- **Prevention:** Rotate housing/pasture. Keep dry.
- **Treatment:** Herbal (garlic, wormwood) or anthelmintics.

**Pneumonia:**
- **Cause:** Bacterial infection (often after stress).
- **Signs:** Cough, nasal discharge, fever, lethargy.
- **Prevention:** Good ventilation, avoid overcrowding, minimize stress.
- **Treatment:** Antibiotics if available; supportive care.

**Foot and leg problems:**
- **Cause:** Injury, infection, mineral deficiency, overweight.
- **Signs:** Lameness, swelling, reluctance to move.
- **Treatment:** Isolate, reduce weight, antibiotics if infected.

**Abscesses and skin infections:**
- **Cause:** Wounds, fighting, parasites.
- **Signs:** Swelling, heat, discharge.
- **Treatment:** Drain pus (lance and squeeze), clean, antibiotics, isolation from other pigs.

### Pig Nutrition and Health

**Requirements per day (for a 100 kg growing pig):**
- Energy: 8-12 Mcal.
- Protein: 12-16% of diet.
- Minerals: Calcium, phosphorus, iron, zinc.

**Sources:**
- Grains: Corn, barley, wheat, oats (energy).
- Legumes: Beans, peas (protein).
- Scraps: Kitchen waste (fill stomach, some nutrients).
- Roots: Turnips, potatoes (energy if cooked; raw potato is toxic).

**Signs of malnutrition:**
- Slow growth.
- Rough coat.
- Leg weakness (mineral deficiency).
- Susceptibility to disease.

## Part 2: Poultry (Chicken) Anatomy and Health

### Body Systems

**Digestive system:**
- Crop (storage pouch in throat).
- Gizzard (muscular stomach that grinds food).
- Small intestines where nutrient absorption occurs.
- Very efficient digestion; food passes through in 12-24 hours.

**Respiratory system:**
- Unique air sac system (9 sacs); lungs are less flexible than mammals.
- Susceptible to respiratory infections (air sacs easily infected).
- Normal breathing: 10-20 breaths per minute.

**Reproductive system (females):**
- Single ovary; produces one egg at a time.
- Egg formation takes ~25 hours.
- Hens can lay 200-300 eggs per year.
- Do NOT need a rooster to lay eggs (unfertilized eggs).

### Normal Signs of Chicken Health

- **Eyes:** Bright, alert, responsive.
- **Comb and wattles:** Red and full (pale indicates anemia or illness).
- **Feathers:** Smooth, shiny, lying flat.
- **Posture:** Upright, active.
- **Droppings:** Formed, light brown or greenish.
- **Appetite:** Pecks readily, drinks water normally.
- **Behavior:** Active foraging, vocalizing (clucking, crowing).

### Common Poultry Diseases

**Coccidiosis (parasitic):**
- **Cause:** Coccidia protozoa (live in intestines).
- **Signs:** Diarrhea (often bloody), lethargy, ruffled feathers, poor growth.
- **Risk:** Warm, wet conditions. Overcrowding.
- **Prevention:** Keep litter dry. Rotate pasture. Avoid overcrowding.
- **Treatment:** Herbal (garlic, thyme) or pharmaceutical coccidiostats (if available).

**Marek's disease (viral):**
- **Cause:** Herpesvirus (highly contagious).
- **Signs:** Paralysis (legs, wings, neck), blindness, tumors.
- **Prevention:** Avoid infected birds. Cull sick birds.
- **Treatment:** No treatment. Cull infected birds.

**Newcastle disease (viral):**
- **Cause:** Paramyxovirus.
- **Signs:** Neurological (twisted neck, paralysis), respiratory (gasping), greenish diarrhea.
- **Prevention:** Isolate flock. Avoid contact with wild birds.
- **Treatment:** No specific treatment. Support infected birds; cull if severe.

**Fowl cholera (bacterial):**
- **Cause:** Pasteurella multocida bacteria.
- **Signs:** Sudden death (often no warning), fever, diarrhea, lameness.
- **Prevention:** Good sanitation, avoid stress.
- **Treatment:** Antibiotics if available (but spread is fast).

**Parasites:**
- **External:** Mites, lice, fleas.
- **Internal:** Roundworms, tapeworms.
- **Signs:** Scratching, ruffled feathers, poor growth, diarrhea.
- **Prevention:** Keep bedding clean and dry. Rotate pasture.
- **Treatment:** Herbal (diatomaceous earth, sulfur) or pharmaceutical parasite treatments.

**Respiratory infections:**
- **Cause:** Viral or bacterial.
- **Signs:** Sneezing, nasal discharge, coughing, wheezing.
- **Prevention:** Good ventilation, avoid drafts.
- **Treatment:** Supportive (warm, dry housing). Antibiotics if bacterial.

### Egg Production and Health

**Factors affecting laying:**
- **Light:** 14+ hours of light per day (need light to trigger laying).
- **Temperature:** 15-24°C ideal (too hot or too cold reduces laying).
- **Nutrition:** Calcium and phosphorus essential for strong eggshells.
- **Stress:** Fear, loud noise, overcrowding suppress laying.

**Signs of poor laying:**
- Soft-shelled or no-shell eggs (calcium deficiency).
- Internal laying (egg breaks inside; fatal to hen).
- Reduced egg production seasonally (winter) or with age.

**Egg-bound hen:**
- **Cause:** Egg stuck inside; often due to weak muscles or large egg.
- **Signs:** Hen strains, sits around, no eggs produced.
- **Treatment:**
  - Warm bath (relax muscles).
  - Gently massage abdomen (encourage egg release).
  - Lubricate vent with oil (ease passage).
  - If unsuccessful after 24 hours, cull (internal rupture will kill her).

## Part 3: Housing and Sanitation

### Pig Housing

**Space requirements:**
- Growing pigs: 0.5-1 m² per pig.
- Breeding sows: 2-3 m² per pig.
- Boars: 3-4 m² (separate housing).

**Features:**
- **Flooring:** Slotted (manure falls through) or solid with bedding.
- **Wallows:** Mud/water for thermoregulation (pigs overheat easily and sunburn).
- **Shelter:** Roof and walls to protect from weather.
- **Ventilation:** Must prevent ammonia buildup (irritates respiratory system).

**Sanitation:**
- Remove manure daily (prevents parasite buildup).
- Clean water continuously (pigs soil water quickly).
- Quarantine new pigs (avoid introducing disease).

### Poultry Housing

**Space requirements:**
- Inside coop: 0.3-0.5 m² per bird.
- Outside run: 1-2 m² per bird (more if possible).

**Features:**
- **Nesting boxes:** 1 box per 3 birds (for laying).
- **Roosting bars:** Birds sleep on bars at night.
- **Ventilation:** Remove ammonia and moisture.
- **Predator-proof:** Secure against rats, foxes, hawks.

**Sanitation:**
- Remove droppings daily (very high ammonia/disease concentration).
- Clean nesting boxes weekly.
- Dust baths (help birds control parasites; provide area with dust or sand).
- Quarantine sick birds immediately.

## Part 4: Preventing Disease Through Management

### Isolation (Quarantine)

**New animals:**
- Keep separated for 2-4 weeks.
- Observe for signs of disease.
- If healthy, introduce gradually to main herd/flock.

**Sick animals:**
- Immediate isolation (prevents spread).
- Separate water and feeding equipment.
- Handle sick animals last (reduce contamination to healthy animals).

### Flock/Herd Rotation

**Pasture rotation:**
- Move animals to clean pasture every 1-2 weeks (for poultry).
- Every 3-4 weeks (for pigs).
- Allows parasites to die off in old pasture.

**Housing rotation:**
- After moving animals out, clean and disinfect housing.
- Leave empty for 2+ weeks if possible (reduces disease).

### Biosecurity

- Keep wild birds and animals out of poultry housing.
- Do not mix species unnecessarily (diseases can cross).
- Wash hands between handling different animals.
- Do not share water sources between species (if possible).

## Part 5: Slaughter and Processing

### When to Slaughter

**Pigs:**
- Market weight: 100-120 kg (takes 5-6 months).
- Butcher weight: 80-130 kg (farmer preference).
- Larger animals take longer to grow but yield more meat.

**Chickens (meat breeds):**
- Broiler chickens: 8-12 weeks (2-3 kg).
- Spent hens: 12-24 months (at end of laying life).

### Humane Slaughter

- Minimize stress before slaughter (minimize chasing, yelling).
- Render animal unconscious instantly (before cutting).
- Cut major blood vessels (allow rapid bleeding).
- Process immediately after death (muscle quality).

**For pigs:**
- Knock unconscious (sledgehammer blow to forehead, or captive bolt if available).
- Immediately cut throat/bleed out.

**For poultry:**
- Cut jugular vein (quick loss of consciousness).
- Or, mechanical neck dislocation (instant death).

## Verification

Your pig and poultry health management is adequate if:
1. **Normal signs:** You can identify healthy pigs and chickens.
2. **Housing:** Shelter is clean, well-ventilated, predator-proof.
3. **Nutrition:** Animals are fed appropriate diet for their age/purpose.
4. **Disease recognition:** You spot illness early (fever, lethargy, respiratory signs).
5. **Parasite control:** You rotate housing/pasture; monitor for parasites.
6. **Sanitation:** Housing is cleaned regularly; manure removed daily.
7. **Quarantine:** New animals are isolated before joining herd/flock.
8. **Slaughter:** You can humanely end animal life and process the meat.

## Common Mistakes

**Overcrowding.** Too many animals in too little space spreads disease rapidly.

**Poor sanitation.** Dirty housing is a disease incubator. Clean daily.

**Ignoring early signs.** A chicken with ruffled feathers might have coccidiosis. Treat early.

**Mixing sick and healthy animals.** Sick animals should be isolated immediately.

**Inadequate nutrition.** Malnourished animals do not grow, do not reproduce, and are disease-prone.

**Not rotating housing.** Parasites build up. Rotation breaks the cycle.

**Slaughtering wrong animals.** Do not keep your best breeding stock; slaughter less-productive animals.
