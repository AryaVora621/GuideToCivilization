## Overview

Cattle are essential post-collapse animals: they plow fields, provide milk, generate meat, and produce manure. A single ox can replace 10+ person-days of manual labor. A dairy cow provides milk for cheese and butter year-round.

But cattle get sick. Diseases, injuries, parasites, and malnutrition can kill them. This chapter teaches you to:
1. **Recognize disease signs** early (before animals die).
2. **Treat common illnesses** (infection, parasites, wounds).
3. **Manage draft animals** safely (preventing injury).
4. **Know when to call an expert** (when you're beyond your skill).

Post-collapse, animal health is your agricultural insurance policy.

Understand anatomy and body systems (Vol 4, Ch 1). Know how to use cattle (Vol 10, Ch 8).

## Part 1: Cattle Anatomy and Normal Health

### Body Systems Overview

**Digestive system:**
- Ruminant: Four-chambered stomach (rumen, reticulum, omasum, abomasum).
- Cattle regurgitate and re-chew food (rumination, "chewing cud").
- Fermentation in the rumen allows cattle to digest tough plant material.

**Respiratory system:**
- Two lungs. Cattle breathe through nose and mouth.
- Normal breathing: 10-30 breaths per minute (at rest).
- Signs of respiratory disease: coughing, heavy breathing, nasal discharge.

**Cardiovascular system:**
- Heart pumps blood through body.
- Normal pulse: 40-80 beats per minute (at rest).
- High pulse indicates fever, pain, or stress.

**Reproductive system:**
- Female (cow) has uterus and ovaries; breeds annually.
- Male (bull) has testicles; produces sperm continuously.
- Gestation: ~280 days (9 months).

**Nervous system:**
- Brain, spinal cord, nerves.
- Controls movement, sensation, reflexes.
- Signs of nervous system disease: paralysis, tremors, loss of coordination.

### Normal Signs of Health

A healthy cow:
- **Eyes:** Bright, alert, responsive. No discharge or cloudiness.
- **Nose:** Clean, slightly moist. No nasal discharge.
- **Mouth:** Pink gums (not pale). Saliva present.
- **Coat:** Smooth, shiny. Hairs lie flat.
- **Body condition:** Ribs slightly visible (not protruding). No excessive fat.
- **Appetite:** Eats readily. Rumination normal (cud-chewing visible).
- **Manure:** Formed but not hard. Normal color (brown-green).
- **Urine:** Clear, yellow to amber.
- **Behavior:** Alert, responsive. Normal movement.
- **Udder (if dairy):** Firm, symmetrical. No discharge except during milking.

### Taking Vital Signs

**Temperature:**
- Normal: 38-38.5°C (100.5-101°F).
- Method: Insert thermometer in rectum for 1-2 minutes.
- High temperature (&gt;39°C) indicates fever/infection.

**Pulse:**
- Normal: 40-80 beats per minute.
- Location: Feel pulse at jaw (where artery crosses jaw bone) or on inner thigh.
- High pulse (&gt;100) indicates fever, pain, or shock.

**Respiration:**
- Normal: 10-30 breaths per minute.
- Count chest movements or feel air at nostrils.
- Rapid breathing (&gt;40) indicates pain, fever, or respiratory disease.

**Rumen movement:**
- Normal: 1-3 contractions per minute (visible on left side).
- You can feel rumen movement by placing hand on left flank.
- No rumen movement indicates digestive shutdown (serious).

## Part 2: Common Cattle Diseases

### Digestive Diseases

**Bloat (rumen distension):**
- **Cause:** Gas accumulates in rumen (cannot be released).
- **Signs:** Extreme abdominal distension (left side swollen like a balloon). Labored breathing. Distress.
- **Risk:** Lush green legume pasture (clover, alfalfa). Sudden diet changes.
- **Treatment:**
  - Emergency: Pass a tube down the esophagus into the rumen to release gas. Requires practice.
  - Or: Stand animal uphill (helps gas escape). Apply gentle pressure to left side.
  - Prevent: Do not allow sudden access to lush legumes. Introduce slowly over 2-3 weeks.

**Scours (diarrhea):**
- **Cause:** Bacterial infection, dietary upset, parasites.
- **Signs:** Watery diarrhea. Dehydration (sunken eyes, dry mucous membranes).
- **Risk:** Most common in calves. Also in adults with sudden diet change.
- **Treatment:**
  - Fluid replacement (oral or IV if severe). Give electrolyte solution (water + salt + sugar).
  - Reduce grain; increase hay.
  - Probiotics (if available) restore beneficial gut bacteria.
  - Antibiotics if bacterial (see below).

**Hardware disease (traumatic reticuloperitonitis):**
- **Cause:** Sharp metal object (nail, wire) swallowed. Lodges in reticulum, punctures internal organs.
- **Signs:** Sudden decrease in milk production. Stiff gait. Reluctance to move. Fever.
- **Prevention:** Remove metal from pasture and feed areas.
- **Treatment:** There is no good treatment post-collapse. A magnet (if you have one) can be administered orally to attract the metal, but often the damage is already done. Animal may need to be culled.

### Respiratory Diseases

**Pneumonia:**
- **Cause:** Bacterial infection (often secondary to stress, cold, or crowding).
- **Signs:** Cough, nasal discharge, fever, lethargy, labored breathing.
- **Risk:** Cold, wet weather. Overcrowding. Poor ventilation.
- **Treatment:**
  - Isolate sick animal.
  - Antibiotics if available (see infection treatment below).
  - Supportive: warm shelter, good ventilation, rest.

**Shipping fever:**
- **Cause:** Stress from transport or weather change triggers bacterial pneumonia.
- **Signs:** Fever, cough, nasal discharge, depression.
- **Prevention:** Minimize stress. Avoid transport in extreme weather.
- **Treatment:** Same as pneumonia.

### Infectious Diseases

**Foot rot:**
- **Cause:** Bacterial infection of the foot, particularly the interdigital space (between toes).
- **Signs:** Lameness (favoring one leg). Foot swelling. Foul smell. Discharge.
- **Risk:** Wet, muddy conditions. Poor pasture sanitation.
- **Treatment:**
  - Trim infected tissue away (remove dead/infected material).
  - Soak foot in footbath (water + salt or mild bleach, 1:10 dilution).
  - Apply antibiotic powder or ointment.
  - Keep foot dry (move to dry pasture).

**Mastitis (udder infection):**
- **Cause:** Bacterial infection of mammary gland.
- **Signs:** Hot, hard, swollen udder. Fever. Painful. Discolored milk (yellow, clotty, bloody).
- **Risk:** Poor milking hygiene. Cracked teats. Injury to teat.
- **Treatment:**
  - Antibiotics (if available).
  - Frequent milking (emptying reduces pressure, relieves pain).
  - Warm compresses to udder.
  - Prevention: Clean udder before milking. Dry teats with clean cloth.

### Parasites

**Internal parasites (worms):**
- **Types:** Roundworms, tapeworms, flukes in digestive tract.
- **Signs:** Poor growth, rough coat, diarrhea, anemia (pale mucous membranes).
- **Risk:** Wet pasture (flukes). High stocking density.
- **Prevention:** Rotate pasture every 2-3 weeks (breaks parasite cycle). Keep pasture dry.
- **Treatment:**
  - Herbal: Garlic, wormwood, diatomaceous earth (if available).
  - Pharmaceutical: Anthelmintic drugs (if available post-collapse).

**External parasites (lice, mange):**
- **Types:** Lice, mange mites, flies.
- **Signs:** Itching, hair loss, rough coat, skin irritation.
- **Risk:** Poor nutrition, stress, overcrowding.
- **Prevention:** Good nutrition, clean shelter, good sanitation.
- **Treatment:**
  - Herbal: Neem oil, sulfur dust.
  - Wash with soap and water (mechanical removal).

## Part 3: Treating Infections and Wounds

### Antibiotics (If Available)

Antibiotics are invaluable but may not be available post-collapse. Use sparingly.

**Common antibiotics for cattle:**
- **Penicillin:** Broad-spectrum, effective against many bacteria.
- **Tetracycline:** Broad-spectrum, effective against respiratory infections.
- **Sulfonamides:** Effective against some bacterial infections.

**Administration:**
- **Intravenous (IV):** Direct into bloodstream. Fast but requires skill.
- **Intramuscular (IM):** Into muscle. Easier than IV. Takes 30 minutes to hours to work.
- **Oral:** By mouth. Works but less effective (some is digested).

**Dosing:** Dose depends on antibiotic type and animal weight. A 500 kg cow needs 5-10x the dose of a 100 kg human. Follow instructions carefully.

**Duration:** Continue for 5-10 days (or as instructed). Stopping early allows bacteria to survive and become resistant.

### Wound Treatment

**Step 1: Clean**
- Remove dirt, debris, dead tissue.
- Wash with water (hot if possible).
- For deep wounds, flush with saline (salt water).

**Step 2: Disinfect**
- Use antiseptic (iodine, bleach 1:10 dilution, hydrogen peroxide).
- Herbal: Honey (antimicrobial), plantain leaves, yarrow.

**Step 3: Close (if appropriate)**
- Small wounds: Leave open to drain. Suture only if edges are clean and come together easily.
- Large wounds: If you can suture, do so (close with sterile thread and needle). Otherwise, leave open with frequent cleaning.

**Step 4: Prevent Infection**
- Apply antibiotic ointment or herbal dressing.
- Change dressing daily until healed.
- Watch for signs of infection (swelling, discharge, fever).

### When to Cull an Animal

Sometimes an animal is too sick to save. Culling (slaughtering) is the humane choice.

**Reasons to cull:**
- Fracture of leg (cannot heal; animal in pain).
- Hardware disease with internal bleeding (incurable).
- Severe infection with poor prognosis (sepsis, gangrene).
- Severe malnutrition or starvation (cannot recover).
- Uncontrollable suffering.

**Process:**
- Make the decision early (do not let animal suffer for days).
- Slaughter humanely (see Vol 10, Ch 8 for processing).
- Butcher for meat if carcass is healthy (otherwise bury deeply).

## Part 4: Working Draft Animals

### Harnessing and Safety

**Harness components:**
- **Yoke or collar:** Sits across neck/shoulders. Distributes load.
- **Lines:** Rope or leather reins for steering and stopping.
- **Bridle (optional):** Helps with steering.

**Fitting:**
- Collar should fit snugly but not restrict breathing.
- Yoke should distribute weight evenly (too tight on one side causes sores).
- Lines should have good contact (not so loose they drag).

**Safety:**
- Never stand behind an animal (they may kick).
- Speak softly and move slowly (avoid startling).
- Keep animals calm (a frightened animal is dangerous).

### Commands and Training

**Basic commands:**
- **"Gee" (or "haw"):** Turn right.
- **"Haw" (or "gee"):** Turn left.
- **"Whoa":** Stop immediately.
- **"Back":** Reverse.

**Training:**
- Start with young animals (calves). Begin at 18-24 months.
- Use gentle, consistent commands.
- Reward with food (praise, grain).
- Start with light load (empty cart). Gradually increase.
- Takes 2-4 weeks of daily training for basic commands.

### Preventing Injury

**Overwork:**
- Do not work animals on hot days (heat exhaustion).
- Limit work to 6-8 hours per day (animal gets tired, makes mistakes).
- Give rest days (work 6 days, rest 1).
- Provide water and shade during work.

**Lameness:**
- Check feet regularly (especially after working in rocky ground).
- Remove rocks from hooves.
- Trim overgrown hooves every 8 weeks.
- Keep hooves dry (wet hooves soften and crack).

**Harness sores:**
- Check skin under collar/yoke daily.
- Keep harness clean and dry.
- If sore appears, stop work immediately and treat (clean, dry, apply ointment).

## Part 5: Nutrition and Health Management

### Nutritional Requirements

**Energy (calories):**
- Maintenance: 8-10 Mcal per day for 500 kg cow (doing light work).
- Work: Add 2-5 Mcal per day for moderate work (plowing).
- Dairy: Add 2-3 Mcal per day per liter of milk produced.

**Protein:**
- Maintenance: 6-8% of diet.
- Growing animals: 10-14%.
- Dairy cows: 12-16%.

**Key minerals:**
- Calcium and phosphorus (bone growth, milk production).
- Magnesium (nerve function, milk production).
- Copper, selenium, zinc (immune function).
- Iodine (metabolic function, reproductive health).

### Signs of Malnutrition

- **Thin body condition:** Ribs very visible, backbone sharp.
- **Poor coat:** Rough, dull, patchy hair loss.
- **Lethargy:** Low energy, reluctance to move.
- **Poor reproductive function:** Delayed breeding, reduced milk production.
- **Susceptibility to disease:** Frequent infections (indicates weak immune system).

**Treatment:** Increase feed quality and quantity. Add supplements if available (grain, minerals, vitamins).

## Verification

Your cattle health management is adequate if:
1. **Normal signs:** You can identify a healthy cow.
2. **Vital signs:** You can take temperature, pulse, respiration.
3. **Disease recognition:** You can spot fever, infection, lameness, digestive upset early.
4. **Wound care:** You can clean and dress wounds.
5. **Parasite control:** You rotate pasture and monitor for parasites.
6. **Draft work:** You can harness safely and give basic commands.
7. **Nutrition:** You understand caloric needs for maintenance and work.
8. **Culling decision:** You know when to humanely end suffering.

## Common Mistakes

**Ignoring early signs.** A cow with a slight cough may have early pneumonia. Treat early; waiting for symptoms to worsen is dangerous.

**Overworking animals.** A tired animal makes mistakes, breaks equipment, or collapses. Rest is essential.

**Poor foot care.** Hooves that are not trimmed regularly cause lameness and lead to infection.

**Inadequate nutrition.** Malnourished animals are weak, do not reproduce, and are disease-prone.

**Leaving wounds open.** Dirty wounds get infected. Clean, close if possible, and dress daily.

**Not isolating sick animals.** Contagious diseases spread quickly. Isolate immediately.
