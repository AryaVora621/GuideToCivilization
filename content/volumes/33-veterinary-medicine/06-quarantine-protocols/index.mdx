## Overview

A single sick animal can infect your entire herd. Quarantine (isolation) is your most powerful disease-prevention tool post-collapse.

This chapter covers:
1. **New animals:** Isolating purchased/rescued animals before mixing.
2. **Sick animals:** Isolating infected animals to prevent spread.
3. **Biosecurity:** Preventing contamination between animal groups.
4. **Monitoring:** Detecting disease during quarantine.

Quarantine is inconvenient. But one epidemic will cost more than months of isolation effort.

## Prerequisites

Understand disease signs (Vol 11, Ch 1-5). Know sanitation principles (Vol 5, Ch 6).

## Part 1: Quarantine for New Animals

### Why Quarantine New Animals

**Risks of new animals:**
- May carry diseases unknown to your operation.
- May carry parasites adapted to your region.
- May have stress-induced illness (transport, new environment).
- May introduce pathogens that spread rapidly to your naive herd.

**Cost of not quarantining:**
- One infected animal can infect 50+ animals in weeks.
- Entire herd sickness = loss of productivity, death.
- Recovery takes months or years.

**Cost of quarantine:**
- 2-4 weeks of isolation (feed, housing).
- Close monitoring (1-2 hours daily).
- If animal is sick, treat or cull before it enters main herd.

Quarantine cost is tiny compared to epidemic cost.

### Quarantine Duration

**Standard quarantine:** 2-4 weeks (depending on disease risk).

**Rationale:**
- Most infectious diseases show signs within 7-14 days.
- Some diseases have longer incubation periods (up to 3-4 weeks).
- 4 weeks covers ~95% of diseases.

**Shorter quarantine (1-2 weeks) acceptable if:**
- Animal comes from nearby known source (same farm/breeder).
- Animal shows no signs and you trust the source.
- Space is extremely limited.

**Longer quarantine (4-6 weeks) necessary if:**
- Unknown source (purchased from unknown seller).
- Animal has been exposed to sick animals.
- Disease is known to be circulating in your region.

### Quarantine Housing

**Location:**
- Separate from main herd (visual separation is not enough; need physical barrier).
- Downwind from main herd (prevent airborne transmission).
- 30+ meters from main grazing areas (prevent parasite transmission).

**Facilities:**
- Shelter (protection from weather).
- Water source (separate from main herd water, if possible).
- Feeding area (separate from main herd).
- Space for movement (animal should not be confined; movement aids health).

**Post-collapse practicality:**
- Small herd: Spare barn, shed, or pen 30+ meters away.
- Large herd: Dedicated quarantine pasture (rotated).
- Minimal: Tie animal separately (less ideal; monitor closely).

### Daily Quarantine Monitoring

**Morning and evening checklist:**

1. **Temperature** (if thermometer available):
   - Normal: 38-38.5°C cattle; 38.5-39.5°C small ruminants.
   - Elevated (&gt;39°C): Fever; indicates infection or inflammation.
   - Note: Outdoor temperature affects body temperature; hot day = slightly elevated; cold day = slightly lower.

2. **Appetite:**
   - Eats willingly? (healthy animal eats immediately).
   - Eats some but not all? (may indicate illness; investigate further).
   - Refuses food? (serious sign; requires immediate attention).

3. **Manure:**
   - Formed (normal, healthy consistency).
   - Loose (mild GI upset).
   - Diarrheal (severe; sign of GI disease, parasites, or toxins).
   - Note: Sudden diet change (new pasture, new feed) can cause loose stool temporarily.

4. **Nose and eyes:**
   - Clear (normal).
   - Discharge (clear = minor; yellow/greenish = bacterial infection).
   - Redness/swelling around eyes (sign of infection or stress).
   - Crusting (sign of prior or ongoing infection).

5. **Breathing:**
   - Normal rate (10-30 breaths/min at rest).
   - Labored (increased effort; sign of respiratory disease or pain).
   - Cough (sign of respiratory infection; record frequency).

6. **Movement and gait:**
   - Walks normally (no lameness).
   - Favors one limb (sign of foot disease, arthritis, or injury).
   - Reluctant to move (sign of severe pain, systemic illness).

7. **Udder (if dairy animal):**
   - Normal size and texture.
   - Swollen or hot (mastitis or other infection).
   - Discharge (milk should be normal; pus indicates infection).

8. **Coat and skin:**
   - Shiny, smooth, hair lies flat (healthy).
   - Dull, rough, hair stands up (illness, stress, or poor nutrition).
   - Lesions, wounds, or parasites (visible on skin).
   - Scabbing or crust (sign of infection or parasite infestation).

9. **Behavior:**
   - Alert, responsive (normal).
   - Quiet, depressed, withdrawn (sign of systemic illness).
   - Agitated, anxious (sign of pain or stress).
   - Vocalizing excessively (sign of distress).

**Record observations:**
- Keep a written log (helps detect trends over days/weeks).
- Note date, time, observations, any abnormalities.
- Example entry: "6/16 AM: T=38.2, eating well, normal manure, clear discharge from nose (new arrival day). 6/16 PM: T=38.5, still eating, one episode loose stool, minor nasal discharge."
- If abnormality noted, follow up next observation to see if it resolves or worsens.

**Key signs to watch for (red flags):**
- **Fever** (high temperature; elevated pulse/respiration): Indicates infection; requires monitoring and potential treatment.
- **Diarrhea** (loose or watery stool): Sign of GI disease, parasites, or toxins; monitor for dehydration.
- **Respiratory signs** (cough, nasal discharge, labored breathing): Sign of respiratory infection; very contagious; isolate further.
- **Lameness** (inability to bear weight on limb): Sign of foot disease, systemic illness, or injury; investigate immediately.
- **Lethargy** (weakness, depression, unwillingness to move): Serious sign; indicates systemic illness.
- **Swelling** (lymph nodes, joints, udder): Sign of infection or injury.
- **Discharge** (nasal, eye, genital, or wound): Color/consistency indicates infection status (clear = normal; pus = bacterial infection).

### Decision at End of Quarantine

**If animal appears healthy:**
- Gradually introduce to main herd over 3-5 days (prevent stress).
- Start by grazing adjacent to main herd (visual contact only).
- Then mix fully (watch for signs of rejection; most animals integrate quickly).

**If animal shows signs of illness:**
- Continue quarantine (extend 1-2 weeks longer; recheck).
- Treat if treatable (see Vol 11, Ch 1-3 for disease-specific treatment).
- Cull if not treatable or if disease is serious (see Vol 11, Ch 1 culling criteria).

**If animal becomes severely ill during quarantine:**
- Isolate further (separate pen if with other quarantine animals).
- Treat aggressively (use available drugs/herbal).
- Cull if suffering is likely (do not delay; humane slaughter is merciful).

## Part 2: Quarantine for Sick Animals

### Immediate Isolation

**When to isolate:**
- Any animal showing signs of contagious disease (respiratory, diarrhea, fever).
- Any animal with unknown illness (assume contagious until proven otherwise).

**Isolation location:**
- Separate shelter 30+ meters upwind from main herd.
- Downwind animals cannot catch disease from upwind airflow.
- 30+ meters prevents parasite transmission from shared water/pasture.

**Speed of isolation:**
- Immediately (within hours of recognizing signs).
- Do not delay to see if animal recovers.
- Contagious disease spreads exponentially (Day 1: 1 animal; Day 3: 10+ animals).

### Quarantine of Sick Animal

**Duration:** Until animal recovers or dies.

**Monitoring:**
- Check 2-3 times daily (more frequent than new animals).
- Observe clinical progression (is it improving or worsening?).
- Monitor vital signs (temperature, pulse, respiration).

**Treatment:**
- Provide supportive care (food, water, shelter).
- Treat disease if possible (antibiotics, herbal remedies, fluid therapy).
- Manage pain (herbal analgesics if available).

**Decision points:**
- Day 3-5: Is animal improving?
  - Yes: Continue treatment; isolation continues until normal.
  - No: Consider culling (if prognosis is poor).
- Day 10-14: Has animal recovered?
  - Yes: Reintroduce to herd gradually (or keep isolated longer if you prefer).
  - No: Reassess diagnosis; consider culling (suffering is burden).

### Recovery and Return to Herd

**Criteria for ending isolation:**
- Normal temperature (38-38.5°C for cattle).
- Normal appetite (eating readily).
- Normal manure (formed, not diarrheal).
- Normal breathing (no cough, no discharge).
- Normal energy (active, alert).
- No signs of disease for 5+ days.

**Reintroduction:**
- Gradual: Return to main grazing area over 3-5 days.
- Monitor closely for first 2 weeks (relapse is possible).

**Carrier animals:**
- Some animals may recover but remain "carriers" (shed disease without showing signs).
- If disease reoccurs in herd, carrier animal may be the source.
- Options: Keep isolated permanently, cull, or accept ongoing disease risk.

## Part 3: Biosecurity Practices

### Preventing Disease Spread Within Your Operation

**Separate management:**
- Keep animal groups separate (breeding herd, young stock, sick animals).
- Separate grazing areas (rotate use so no shared contamination).
- Separate water sources if possible (or ensure water is clean).

**Personal hygiene:**
- Wash hands after handling sick animals.
- Change clothes after handling sick animals (parasite eggs, bacteria).
- Do not handle sick animals before handling healthy animals.
- If you must handle both, handle healthy animals first.

**Tool and equipment hygiene:**
- Use separate tools/buckets for sick animal area.
- Clean tools with water and soap (or bleach solution 1:10 after handling sick animals).
- Do not share equipment between groups.

**Manure management:**
- Do not spread sick animal manure on pasture where healthy animals graze.
- Compost sick animal manure separately (4+ weeks at high temperature kills most pathogens).
- Or, spread on pasture and leave fallow for 2+ months before grazing.

### Restricting Visitor Access

**Post-collapse, few visitors will arrive. But if any do:**
- Restrict access to animal areas.
- Provide clean boots or require footwash.
- Explain disease risks (most visitors will understand).
- Do not allow visitors to touch animals without explicit permission.

### Managing Multiple Species

**Different species have different parasites/diseases:**
- Cattle diseases sometimes do not affect sheep/goats (and vice versa).
- Segregation by species reduces cross-infection risk.

**Shared grazing (not recommended if disease is present):**
- Cattle → sheep/goats → poultry (sequential grazing is safer; breaks parasite cycles).
- Simultaneous mixed grazing increases parasite transmission between species.

**Feeding areas:**
- Separate feeding reduces disease transmission (animals do not compete; no stress).
- Shared feeding is acceptable if you monitor closely and clean troughs daily.

### Monitoring for Disease in Main Herd

**During quarantine of animals, watch main herd for:**
- Any new respiratory signs (cough, nasal discharge).
- Any new GI signs (diarrhea in multiple animals).
- Fever (elevated temperature in any animal).
- Lethargy or behavior change.

**Action if signs appear:**
- If new quarantine animal is source, cull it immediately.
- If main herd already exposed, isolation is too late; focus on treatment.
- If no clear source, investigate (was there a breach in quarantine?).

## Part 4: Quarantine for Specific Diseases

### Highly Contagious Diseases (If Present in Your Region)

**Diseases to watch for (region-dependent):**
- Foot-and-mouth disease (cattle, sheep, goats): Extreme contagion; spreads in days.
- Brucellosis: Spread through birthing fluids; affects reproduction.
- Tuberculosis: Slow spread; long incubation; serious zoonotic risk.

**Management:**
- If suspected in new animal: 4-6 week quarantine minimum.
- If confirmed: Cull immediately (disease is incurable post-collapse; risk to humans).
- Verify your region (do not expect diseases that are not present; adapt to regional threats).

### Parasite-Heavy Animals

**New animal from parasite-endemic region:**
- Quarantine 2-4 weeks as usual.
- During quarantine: Treat for parasites (herbal or pharmaceutical).
- Re-treat in 2-3 weeks (catches late-stage larvae).
- Monitor post-treatment for recurrence.

### Stressed/Weak Animals

**Animals arriving weak or stressed (transport, injury, malnutrition):**
- Quarantine longer (4-6 weeks).
- Provide excellent nutrition and shelter (promote recovery).
- Monitor closely (stressed animals are prone to secondary infection).
- Do not rush return to main herd; ensure full recovery.

## Verification

Your quarantine protocol is adequate if:
1. **Space:** You have dedicated quarantine housing (separate from main herd).
2. **Duration:** You quarantine new animals for 2-4 weeks minimum.
3. **Monitoring:** You check quarantined animals 1-2 times daily.
4. **Isolation:** Sick animals are removed from main herd within hours of illness.
5. **Documentation:** You record observations (helps detect patterns).
6. **Decisions:** You know when to treat, when to extend quarantine, when to cull.
7. **Biosecurity:** You practice hand/tool hygiene to prevent disease spread.

## Common Mistakes

**Skipping quarantine.** "I trust the seller" or "I do not have space." One infected animal = epidemic. Quarantine is non-negotiable.

**Inadequate isolation.** Quarantine pen next to main herd. Visual proximity is not isolation; physical separation (30+ meters, separate water) is required.

**Too short quarantine.** 1 week is not enough for many diseases. 2-4 weeks is standard.

**Not monitoring closely.** Quarantine is useless if you do not observe animals daily. Disease signs can be subtle.

**Mixing groups during quarantine.** Isolating new animals while moving them through main herd. Separate them completely.

**Not culling sick animals.** Hoping for recovery while animal suffers. If prognosis is poor, end suffering promptly.

**Reusing sick animal equipment.** Using same buckets/tools for healthy animals. Cross-contamination spreads disease.

**Not isolating sick animals immediately.** Waiting to "see if it is serious." Contagious disease spreads exponentially in days.
