## Overview

A hard disk is a data storage device that uses rotating magnetic platters to record digital information. Hard disks are valuable in post-collapse civilization because they:

1. **Store large amounts of data:** A single disk can store gigabytes to terabytes of information (books, medical records, technical manuals)
2. **Persist data without power:** Data remains intact even when the device is unpowered
3. **Survive mechanical failure:** Unlike tape, hard disks can often be repaired or have data recovered even if damaged
4. **Are scalable:** Multiple disks can be combined for higher capacity

Hard disks work by magnetizing spots on a rotating platter to represent 1's and 0's (binary data). By understanding hard disk principles, you can maintain, repair, and recover data from storage media.

This chapter covers hard disk design, how data is written and read, encoding methods, common failures, and data recovery basics.

## Part 1: Understanding Hard Disk Structure and Operation

### Components of a Hard Disk

**Platter:**
- A flat, circular disk coated with magnetic material
- Typical diameter: 3.5 inches (desktop), 2.5 inches (laptop), or smaller (mobile)
- Surface: Very smooth (microscopic imperfections of under 1 micrometer)
- Coating: Iron oxide or other ferromagnetic material
- Rotates at 5,400 to 15,000 RPM (revolutions per minute)

**Read/write heads:**
- Tiny electromagnets suspended above the platter
- Floating on a thin cushion of air (typically 10–50 nanometers above surface)
- Write head: Creates magnetic field to record data
- Read head: Detects magnetic field from platter to retrieve data
- Positioned by actuator arm

**Actuator arm:**
- Moves read/write heads across the platter
- Controlled by voice coil motor (similar to loudspeaker mechanism)
- Positions heads at different disk locations (tracks)

**Spindle motor:**
- Rotates the platter at constant speed
- High precision; must maintain exact RPM

**Controller circuit:**
- Manages read/write operations
- Converts computer data to magnetic signals and vice versa
- Handles error correction

### How Data Is Written

1. **Head positioning:** Actuator positions read/write head at desired location on platter
2. **Magnetization:** Write head creates magnetic field, orienting iron oxide particles
3. **Direction encoding:** Direction of magnetization represents 1 or 0 (north = 1, south = 0, or vice versa)
4. **Sequential writing:** As platter rotates, head writes data in circular tracks
5. **Speed:** Modern drives write at rates of 100–300 megabytes per second

### How Data Is Read

1. **Head positioning:** Actuator positions read head over desired track
2. **Detection:** Read head detects changing magnetic field as platter rotates past
3. **Signal generation:** Magnetic changes induce electrical signals in read head
4. **Amplification:** Weak signals are amplified and sent to controller
5. **Decoding:** Controller interprets signals as 1's and 0's

### Data Organization

**Sectors:**
- Smallest addressable unit of data (typically 512 bytes or 4,096 bytes)
- Arranged in concentric circles (tracks) on the platter
- Each sector has header information (address, error correction code) and data area

**Tracks:**
- Concentric circles on the platter
- Outer tracks have more physical space, longer sectors
- Inner tracks are tighter, shorter sectors
- Number of tracks: thousands to millions, depending on disk size

**Cylinders:**
- Imaginary vertical planes through all platters (for multi-platter drives)
- Allows parallel access to multiple platters

**Capacity calculation:**
- Capacity = Number of platters × Tracks per platter × Sectors per track × Bytes per sector
- Example: 1 platter × 100,000 tracks × 200 sectors/track × 512 bytes/sector ≈ 10 GB

## Part 2: Encoding and Error Correction

### Magnetic Encoding Methods

**Simple magnetization (NRZ - Non-Return to Zero):**
- North polarity = 1, South polarity = 0 (or vice versa)
- Simple but difficult to detect boundaries between same-polarity regions
- Rarely used in modern drives

**Flux transitions (RLL - Run-Length Limited):**
- Data is represented by transitions in magnetic field, not absolute direction
- Transition = boundary between north and south
- Improves data density and error detection
- Standard encoding method for modern hard drives

**Encoding rules (example: 2,7 RLL):**
- Maximum 2 consecutive 0's (prevents excessive flux change)
- Maximum 7 consecutive 0's (prevents loss of synchronization)
- Increases effective capacity by ~30% compared to simple magnetization

### Error Correction Codes (ECC)

Hard disks include error correction codes to detect and correct read errors.

**Parity bit:**
- Simple error detection
- Each sector includes a bit that indicates odd/even parity
- Detects single-bit errors (not sufficient for recovery)

**Reed-Solomon codes:**
- More sophisticated error correction
- Can correct multiple bit errors per sector
- Typical: 32 bytes of error correction data per 512-byte sector
- Allows recovery even if significant portion of data is unreadable

**Example:**
- Sector with read error detected
- ECC decodes and corrects error using redundancy
- Data recovered successfully
- If errors exceed ECC capability: sector marked as bad, data lost

## Part 3: Disk Failures and Data Recovery

### Common Hard Disk Failures

**Mechanical failures:**
1. **Head crash:** Read/write head contacts platter, grinding away magnetic coating
   - Symptoms: Loud clicking sounds, inability to read
   - Severity: Data loss
   - Recovery: Requires professional head replacement

2. **Spindle motor failure:** Platter stops rotating
   - Symptoms: No sounds, no power to drive
   - Severity: Data inaccessible without rotation
   - Recovery: Spindle motor replacement (delicate)

3. **Actuator arm failure:** Head positioning mechanism breaks
   - Symptoms: Head unable to move to correct location
   - Severity: Data inaccessible
   - Recovery: Actuator arm replacement

**Electronic failures:**
1. **Controller circuit failure:** Control electronics malfunction
   - Symptoms: Drive not recognized by computer, no spinning
   - Severity: Depends on failure type
   - Recovery: Possible if failure is isolated to controller (firmware reset or board replacement)

2. **Firmware corruption:** Software on the drive is corrupted
   - Symptoms: Drive spins but not recognized
   - Severity: Can sometimes be repaired without hardware replacement
   - Recovery: Firmware rewrite (if possible)

**Environmental failures:**
1. **Heat damage:** Excessive heat degrades components and magnetic coating
   - Symptoms: Degraded performance, eventual failure
   - Prevention: Maintain cool environment (under 25°C preferred)

2. **Humidity damage:** Moisture causes corrosion
   - Symptoms: Electrical failures, head crashes
   - Prevention: Keep relative humidity under 60%

3. **Magnetic field exposure:** Strong external magnetic field erases data
   - Symptoms: Data loss without drive damage
   - Prevention: Keep away from strong magnets and electromagnetic sources

### Data Recovery Attempts

**Basic recovery (no physical damage):**

1. **Connect to different computer:**
   - Try the disk on another computer with different controller
   - If recognized: data may be recoverable using software tools
   - If not recognized: likely firmware or controller failure

2. **Replace controller board (if available):**
   - Remove circuit board from failed drive
   - Install on identical or compatible donor drive
   - May allow access to data if controller failure was the problem

**Intermediate recovery (minor physical damage):**

1. **Head replacement (requires expertise):**
   - Open drive in clean environment (specialized clean room ideal)
   - Replace damaged read/write head with compatible donor head
   - Reassemble and attempt to read data

2. **Platter inspection and cleaning:**
   - If platter is scratched but coating is partially intact, carefully clean surface
   - May restore readability of damaged sectors

**Advanced recovery (major physical damage):**

- Requires professional data recovery service
- Involves replacing spindle motor, rebuilding drive mechanics
- Cost is often high but valuable for critical data

### Data Preservation

**Short-term (months to years):**
- Store disks in cool, dry environment
- Avoid temperature fluctuations
- Protect from vibration and physical shock
- Keep away from magnetic sources

**Medium-term (years to decades):**
- Periodically test disk functionality
- Keep multiple copies on different media
- Monitor for signs of failure (slow operation, noise)

**Long-term (decades or longer):**
- No single storage method is proven reliable for very long periods
- Migrate data to new media every 5–10 years
- Maintain multiple copies
- Keep documentation of data format and encoding

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Diagnosing a Failing Hard Disk

**Symptoms:** Drive makes clicking sounds, computer cannot access files.

**Diagnosis:**
- Clicking sound suggests head striking platter (head crash)
- Data inaccessible: platter or head damage likely
- Severity: Serious mechanical failure

**Recovery options:**
1. **Stop using immediately:** Further operation will worsen damage
2. **Professional recovery:** Head crash typically requires professional repair (expensive but data can often be recovered)
3. **DIY recovery (risky):** Remove drive casing in clean environment, inspect platter for visible damage, attempt head replacement if experienced

**Outcome:**
- If damage is minor: Head replacement may recover most/all data
- If platter is scratched: Some data loss but partial recovery likely
- If damage is severe: Professional recovery remains the best option

### Example 2: Recovering Data from a Non-Recognized Drive

**Symptoms:** Drive powers on and spins, but computer does not recognize it.

**Diagnosis:**
- Likely controller circuit or firmware failure
- Platter and data are intact

**Recovery process:**
1. **Try different computer:** Confirms issue is with drive, not computer
2. **Check BIOS:** Does computer detect drive at all? (Basic Input/Output System, low-level hardware detection)
3. **If detected but not recognized by OS:**
   - Likely firmware issue
   - Try firmware reset (disconnect power, wait 30 seconds, reconnect)
   - Try controller board replacement if available

4. **Data extraction:** If computer can recognize disk (even partially):
   - Use data recovery software to scan for readable sectors
   - Copy recoverable data to another disk
   - Success depends on extent of controller failure

### Example 3: Calculating Hard Disk Capacity

**Specification of a 20 GB disk:**
- 2 platters (two surfaces each = 4 surfaces)
- 50,000 tracks per surface
- 250 sectors per track
- 512 bytes per sector

**Calculation:**
- Capacity = 4 surfaces × 50,000 tracks × 250 sectors × 512 bytes
- Capacity = 4 × 50,000 × 250 × 512 = 25,600,000,000 bytes ≈ 25.6 GB

Note: Manufacturer advertising often rounds and excludes system overhead (file system, bad sectors), resulting in slightly lower usable capacity.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Attempting recovery of head-crashed drive without preparation:** Operating a drive with mechanical damage worsens the problem. Stop using it immediately and assess damage carefully before attempting recovery.

2. **Ignoring environmental conditions:** Heat and humidity damage hard disks slowly. Maintaining proper storage conditions extends disk lifespan significantly.

3. **Replacing controller board without compatibility check:** Controller boards are often not interchangeable. Replacing with incompatible board wastes time; verify compatibility or consult documentation first.

4. **Over-cleaning platter surface:** Cleaning may remove protective coating. Use only specialized, non-abrasive materials designed for disk surfaces.

5. **Attempting drive repair without static protection:** Static electricity can damage sensitive electronics. Always ground yourself before opening a drive or touching components.

6. **Ignoring data backup:** The best recovery method is not needing recovery. Maintain regular backups on different media.

7. **Assuming firmware corruption can be easily fixed:** Firmware repair requires specialized tools and knowledge. Amateur attempts can make the problem worse.

8. **Leaving a failing drive connected to computer:** If a drive is making clicking sounds or behaving erratically, disconnect it immediately. Operation can destroy remaining recoverable data.

9. **Exceeding safe operating temperature:** Hard disks rated for operation under 55°C. Extended operation above this temperature accelerates component failure.

10. **Stacking or storing disks without protection:** Physical shock can cause head crashes. Store disks carefully, ideally in cases or padded environments.
