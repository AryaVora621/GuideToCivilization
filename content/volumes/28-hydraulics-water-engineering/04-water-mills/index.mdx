## Overview

Water mills harness flowing water to power machinery. A water wheel turns via the force of flowing water; gears transfer this rotation to millstones that grind grain, or to other mechanical devices (saws, hammers, looms).

Water mills are among history's most important inventions. One mill can grind grain for 100 to 500 people, eliminating hand-grinding labor. Water mills enable industrial processes: sawing lumber, fulling cloth, pumping water, hammering iron.

By year 2, your settlement can build a water mill serving the entire population.

## Materials & Tools

**For the mill structure:**

- Stone or brick (foundation, walls)
- Timber (beams, wheel support, axles)
- Iron or wood (gears, axles, pulleys)

**For the water wheel:**

- Timber (hub, arms, buckets/paddles)
- Iron bands or fasteners

**For the mechanism:**

- Millstones (see Vol 34, Ch 1: Ceramic Applications for stone production OR use naturally occurring stone)
- Gears (metal or wood-toothed)
- Bearings (wood or iron)
- Shafts and axles (iron or strong timber)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Design the Mill Power System

Determine power output and water requirements.

**Water wheel power output:**

- Water flow rate × water pressure = power available
- Example: 100 liters per second of water falling 1 meter
- Power: roughly 1 kilowatt (about 1.3 horsepower)
- Practical output: 0.7 horsepower after friction losses

**Power needed for tasks:**

- Grinding grain: 0.5 to 2 horsepower (depending on grain hardness and grinding speed)
- Sawing lumber: 1 to 3 horsepower
- Pumping water: 0.5 to 1 horsepower
- Fulling cloth: 0.5 to 1 horsepower

**Water flow requirement:**

- Gentle mill (1 HP): 200 to 400 liters per minute
- Typical aqueduct: 500 to 1000 liters per minute
- A mill can be powered by a portion of an aqueduct

### Step 2: Select Water Wheel Type

Three types of water wheels: overshot, undershot, and breast wheel.

**Overshot wheel (best efficiency):**

- Water enters at the top; gravity and the weight of water power the wheel
- Efficiency: 50 to 80 percent
- Works with modest flow and moderate height (1 to 3 meters of water fall)
- Requires channel control to direct water into buckets

**Undershot wheel (simplest):**

- Water strikes the wheel at the bottom; force and momentum power the wheel
- Efficiency: 20 to 40 percent
- Works with high flow and modest height (0.5 to 1.5 meters)
- No need to control water entry precisely; any flowing water works

**Breast wheel (moderate):**

- Water enters at the side near the wheel axle
- Efficiency: 40 to 60 percent
- Works with moderate flow and moderate height (1 to 2 meters)

**Choice:**

- If you have elevation drop (water falling down): overshot is best
- If you have high flow on flat terrain: undershot
- If moderate both: breast wheel or overshot depending on site

### Step 3: Design the Water Wheel

Detailed dimensions for an undershot wheel (simplest to build).

**Undershot wheel design:**

- **Diameter:** 3 to 6 meters (larger wheels turn slower, provide more torque)
- **Width:** 1 to 2 meters (wider wheels use more water and provide more power)
- **Hub:** Center wooden cylinder, 30 to 50 cm diameter
- **Arms:** 6 to 8 wooden spokes radiating from hub to rim
- **Rim:** Wooden or iron ring forming the outer edge
- **Buckets/paddles:** Wooden boards attached to the rim
  - For undershot wheels: flat paddles 20 to 40 cm long, angled to catch water
  - Spacing: 10 to 20 cm apart around the rim
  - Number: 16 to 32 paddles depending on wheel size

**Overshot wheel design (for elevation drop):**

- **Diameter:** 4 to 8 meters (larger wheels are heavier but provide more power)
- **Hub, arms, rim:** same as undershot
- **Buckets:** Deep, cup-shaped buckets 20 to 40 cm diameter
  - Attached to the rim to catch water at the top
  - Spacing: 30 to 50 cm apart
  - Number: 10 to 20 buckets depending on wheel size

**Wheel specifications:**

- **Axle:** Iron or heavy timber, 5 to 10 cm diameter, extending 1 meter beyond the wheel on each side
- **Bearings:** Support the axle at the mill walls (see Vol 11, Ch 2: Gears and Bearings)
- **Speed:** Overshot wheels turn 3 to 10 rpm; undershot wheels 5 to 20 rpm
- **Torque:** Speed × power = constant; lower speeds provide more turning force for grinding

### Step 4: Build the Mill Structure

The mill building houses the wheel and mechanisms.

**Foundation:**

- Built on stable ground near the water course
- Can be partially in water (wheel extends through a side opening)
- Must be above flood level

**Building design:**

```
Side view:

       Water channel (from aqueduct)
              |
              v
    +----+----+----+
    |    Water wheel |
    |   (turning)   |
    +----+----+----+
         |
       Axle (extends into building)
         |
      [Gears and mechanism inside]
         |
       Millstones (grinding grain)
```

**Structural elements:**

1. **Wheel pit:** Pit or channel where water wheel is located
2. **Wall structure:** Stone or wood walls supporting the building
3. **Roof:** Protects mechanism from weather
4. **Openings:** Channel for water to flow in; outlet for water to flow out

### Step 5: Install the Power Transmission (Gears)

Transfer the rotating motion from the wheel to the millstones or other machinery.

**Gear system (for grain mill):**

1. **Main gear (on wheel axle):**
   - Large gear wheel, 1 to 2 meters diameter
   - Teeth: 50 to 100 teeth
   - Turns slowly (3 to 10 rpm from the water wheel)

2. **Pinion gear (on millstone shaft):**
   - Small gear, 20 to 40 cm diameter
   - Teeth: 15 to 30 teeth
   - Speed increase: ratio = main gear teeth / pinion teeth
   - Example: 80-tooth main gear / 20-tooth pinion = 4× speed increase
   - Millstone rotates at 40 rpm (faster grinding)

3. **Gear material:**
   - Wood gears with wooden teeth (adequate; see Vol 11: Mechanical Engineering)
   - Iron gears (stronger; lasts longer)
   - Iron teeth on wooden bodies (compromise)

4. **Alignment:**
   - Main gear and pinion must be aligned precisely
   - Misalignment causes noise, vibration, and wear

**Alternative: Direct shaft (no gears):**

- If millstone speed matches wheel speed, connect directly
- Requires cardan joint or flexible coupling
- Simpler but less power transfer

### Step 6: Install Millstones and Grinding Mechanism

For grain mills, the millstones do the actual grinding.

**Millstones:**

1. **Lower stone (stationary):**
   - Circular, 60 to 120 cm diameter, 15 to 30 cm thick
   - Made of hard stone (granite, sandstone, or ceramic hardstone)
   - Carved or formed with slight conical surface

2. **Upper stone (rotating):**
   - Same size and material as lower stone
   - Rotates above the lower stone, 2 to 10 mm clearance
   - Connected to the rotating shaft via wooden supports

3. **Furrows (grinding surface):**
   - Grooves carved into both stone surfaces
   - Pattern radiates from center (like wheel spokes)
   - Grain is dragged along furrows by rotation, crushed into flour

**Adjustments:**

1. **Distance between stones:** Controls flour fineness
   - Close (2 to 3 mm): fine flour, slow grinding
   - Wider (5 to 10 mm): coarse flour, fast grinding

2. **Stone rotation:** Can be adjusted with a lever or screw mechanism for finer control

### Step 7: Control Water Flow and Regulate Speed

Control the mill's operation.

**Water control:**

1. **Gate or dam:** Diverts water into the wheel
   - Gate can be fully or partially opened to control water flow
   - More flow = faster wheel speed = faster grinding

2. **Spillway:** Allows excess water to bypass the wheel
   - Prevents flooding in high water conditions
   - Can be used seasonally (summer uses less water than spring)

**Speed regulation:**

1. **Load control:** More grain to grind = slower rotation (mechanical load)
2. **Water control:** Adjust gate to increase or decrease wheel speed
3. **Brake mechanism:** Optional but useful for safety
   - Allows quick shutdown (emergency)
   - Allows controlled stopping when not grinding

### Step 8: Maintain the Mill

Regular maintenance keeps the mill operating.

**Daily maintenance:**

- Inspect stone alignment (listen for grinding sounds; they should be even)
- Check for grain dust buildup (fire hazard)
- Verify gate function and water control
- Listen for gear noise (indicates misalignment or wear)

**Weekly maintenance:**

- Clean grain dust from mechanism
- Check bearing lubrication (use oil or grease)
- Inspect belt or gear connections for wear

**Monthly maintenance:**

- Clean the entire mill inside
- Inspect and dress the millstones (retool grooves if they become worn and smooth)
- Check water control system for blockages
- Verify building structure for cracks or damage

**Annual major maintenance:**

- Redress millstones (re-carve the grinding furrows)
- Inspect wheel for damage or wear
- Replace worn gears or belts
- Inspect bearings; replace if worn
- Check and repair any structural damage

**Miller role:**

- Operate the mill
- Maintain the building and equipment
- Grind grain for the settlement (may charge a percentage of the grain as payment)
- Keep records of grain processed

## Verification

**Success looks like:**

- Water wheel rotates smoothly and continuously
- Millstones grind grain evenly (flour is consistent fineness)
- Grinding speed is 1 to 5 bushels per hour (1 to 2.5 tons per day)
- Building is dry and structure is sound
- Gears mesh without excessive noise or vibration
- Water control system allows adjustment of grinding speed

**Failure looks like:**

- Water wheel jamms or rotates erratically
- Millstones wear unevenly or produce inconsistent flour
- Grinding is very slow (under 0.5 bushels per hour)
- Building leaks or shows structural damage
- Gear noise is loud or grinding is jerky
- Water control doesn't work; mill cannot be regulated

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll make the water wheel too large"** — Large wheels are heavy and hard to turn. 3 to 6 meters diameter is adequate.

2. **"I'll use soft wood for the wheel axle"** — Soft wood bends under load. Use hard oak or iron for axles.

3. **"Millstones don't need dressing"** — Stones wear smooth and stop grinding efficiently. Dress (retool) the furrows annually.

4. **"I won't control water flow; the wheel will self-regulate"** — Water wheels need speed control for efficient operation. Install gates and adjust water flow.

5. **"I'll make the building small to save effort"** — The mill needs space for the mechanism and grain storage. Build generously.

6. **"I don't need to maintain the gear meshes"** — Loose or misaligned gears wear quickly and are noisy. Check alignment monthly.

7. **"I'll use unseasoned (wet) wood for the wheel"** — Wet wood rots and warps. Use well-seasoned wood or build the wheel underground initially to soak and season it.

8. **"One mill is enough for the settlement"** — A mill can fail or need maintenance. Build 2 to 3 mills so grain processing doesn't stop.
