## Overview

Floods are a natural hazard in many regions. Rivers swell after heavy rain; coastal areas flood during storms. Settlements built on floodplains face regular inundation unless they prepare.

Flood control uses three strategies: prevention (keep water away), protection (keep water out), and accommodation (allow flooding but limit damage). Post-collapse settlements can employ all three, from dikes and channels to elevated buildings and evacuation plans.

This chapter covers practical flood control: assessing flood risk, building dikes and levees, designing retention channels, and planning for inevitable floods.

- Measuring tools (level, tape)

**For emergency response:**

- Boats or rafts (for evacuation)
- Food stores (for survival during flood)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Assess Flood Risk

Determine the likelihood and severity of flooding.

**Information gathering:**

1. **Historical records:**
   - Ask longtime residents: how often do floods occur?
   - When was the last major flood?
   - How high did water rise?
   - What damage resulted?

2. **River behavior:**
   - Walk along the river at different seasons (spring high water vs. summer low flow)
   - Look for debris lines on trees (indicating past flood height)
   - Observe erosion patterns (undercutting banks, driftwood jams)

3. **Topography:**
   - Map the floodplain (lowland area that floods)
   - Identify high ground (safe areas)
   - Assess escape routes (can people reach high ground quickly?)

4. **Climate:**
   - Understand seasonal flooding (spring snowmelt, summer monsoons, etc.)
   - Identify storm patterns (hurricanes, intense rainstorms)
   - Consider long-term climate trends (increasing rainfall, sea-level rise)

**Flood classification:**

- **Frequent floods (annually or several times per year):** Floodplain must accommodate regular inundation
- **Occasional floods (once per 5 to 20 years):** Significant damage; dikes or levees justified
- **Rare floods (once per 100+ years):** Design for protection or plan for limited damage

### Step 2: Design Flood-Resistant Settlement Layout

Choose settlement locations and designs that minimize flood risk.

**Site selection:**

1. **High ground:** Build on elevated terrain above historical flood heights
2. **Avoid bottlenecks:** Don't settle in narrow valleys (funnel water, worsen flooding)
3. **Distance from rivers:** Even 100+ meters on high ground is safer than floodplain
4. **Plan escape routes:** Settlements should have clear paths to high ground

**Building design:**

1. **Elevated structures:**
   - Buildings on stilts or elevated foundations
   - Allows floodwater to flow underneath
   - Reduces structural damage

2. **Flood-resistant materials:**
   - Stone and brick: resist water damage
   - Elevated mechanical equipment (mills, pumps above flood height)
   - Avoid basement storage (floods reach basements first)

3. **Accessible high ground:**
   - Keep high areas clear for emergency use (refuge during floods)
   - Don't build on all available high ground; reserve some for safety

### Step 3: Build Dikes and Levees

Dikes are barriers that prevent water from reaching protected areas.

**Dike design:**

1. **Height:**
   - Must exceed the historical maximum flood height
   - Add 1 to 2 meters of freeboard (safety margin above expected flood)
   - Example: if historical flood was 5 meters, build dike to 6 to 7 meters

2. **Location:**
   - Runs parallel to the river
   - Should be set back from the bank (10 to 50 meters minimum)
   - Allows the river to access its natural floodplain partially

3. **Cross-section:**
   - Sloping sides (not vertical; reduces collapse risk)
   - Base width: at least as high as the dike (e.g., 7-meter-tall dike needs 7-meter base)
   - Crest (top) width: 3 to 5 meters (allows patrols and maintenance)
   - Slope angle: 30 to 45 degrees from horizontal (steeper is cheaper but less stable)

**Dike materials:**

1. **Earthen dike (most common):**
   - Built from clay-rich soil
   - Compacted in layers (prevents settling)
   - Grass planted on the surface (stabilizes soil, prevents erosion)

2. **Stone facing (for strength):**
   - Outer layer of stone (prevents erosion during high water)
   - Inner core of clay and soil (cheaper, adequate strength)

3. **Timber reinforcement:**
   - Pilings (wooden stakes) driven through the dike
   - Prevents sliding on steep slopes
   - Used in weak soil or steep dikes

**Dike construction:**

1. **Soil preparation:**
   - Clear vegetation from the dike alignment
   - Compact the ground beneath the dike

2. **Layer construction:**
   - Build in 30 to 50 cm layers
   - Compact each layer (tamp or roll with animals)
   - Add clay layers (bind the material, prevent seepage)

3. **Vegetation stabilization:**
   - Plant grass or brush on the dike surface
   - Roots prevent soil from eroding during high water
   - Spacing: 30 to 50 cm apart

4. **Maintenance slope:**
   - Gentle upstream slope (1:2 or 1:3, i.e., 1 meter rise per 2 to 3 meters horizontal)
   - Steep downstream slope (1:1 to 1:2)
   - Asymmetry provides strength where needed (steep side resists water pressure)

### Step 4: Control Seepage

Dikes must prevent water from seeping through or under them.

**Seepage issues:**

- Water finds pathways through soil (sand layers, cracks)
- Seepage at the dike base erodes the dike from below
- Seepage through the dike weakens it

**Seepage control:**

1. **Clay core:**
   - Clay-rich soil is impermeable
   - Core layer runs through the center of the dike
   - Blocks seepage pathways

2. **Cutoff wall:**
   - Wall of clay or concrete driven deep into the ground
   - Extends from dike base into the bedrock below
   - Blocks water seepage under the dike

3. **Toe drain (for seepage beneath):**
   - Gravel-filled trench at the dike base (downstream side)
   - Allows seeping water to exit without eroding the dike
   - Drains water safely away from the dike base

### Step 5: Design Retention Channels

Retention channels (floodways) allow water to flood in a controlled manner.

**Floodway design:**

1. **Purpose:**
   - Allow water to flood during extreme events
   - Prevents water from overtopping dikes (catastrophic failure)
   - Water fills the floodway, reducing pressure on the dike

2. **Location:**
   - Between the river and the dike (allows water to spread)
   - Or in designated low areas (sacrifice zones where flooding is acceptable)

3. **Characteristics:**
   - Must be large enough to store expected flood volume
   - Should drain back to river naturally (when water level drops)
   - May be agricultural land (allowed to flood occasionally)

4. **Access control:**
   - Gates or barriers control water entry
   - Opens automatically when water level reaches a threshold
   - Allows deliberate flooding of sacrifice zones

**Alternative: spillways (for channels and aqueducts):**

- Channels with overflow sections that spill water into retention areas
- Prevents channel overtopping
- Allows controlled flood management

### Step 6: Prepare for Inevitable Floods

Even good dikes fail. Settlements must plan for floods.

**Warning systems:**

1. **Upstream monitoring:**
   - Watch for heavy rain or snowmelt
   - Observe river levels (rising water is a warning sign)
   - Plan evacuation 24 to 48 hours before major flood

2. **Communication:**
   - Signal (bell, drum, horn) to warn residents
   - Multiple warnings (some may miss the first)

3. **Evacuation plan:**
   - Designated assembly points on high ground
   - Plans for moving vulnerable people (children, elderly, sick)
   - Routes to high ground (multiple paths in case some are blocked)

**Flood survival items:**

- Store food in high areas (not basements or floodplain)
- Keep boats or rafts available (for rescue)
- Maintain sanitation supplies (toilets may flood)
- Store drinking water (surface water becomes contaminated)

**Damage mitigation:**

- Move valuables to high ground before floods
- Store records above flood height
- Have repair supplies ready (after flood cleanup)
- Know how to assess structural damage (report to authorities)

### Step 7: Maintain Flood Control Systems

Dikes and channels require regular maintenance.

**Monthly:**

- Walk the dike; look for cracks, erosion, or settlement
- Check vegetation coverage (re-seed bare spots)
- Inspect gates and spillways

**Seasonally (before flood season):**

- Major inspection and repair
- Strengthen weak sections
- Clear vegetation from channels (allows water flow)
- Test warning systems

**After each flood:**

- Inspect damage and make repairs
- Update flood records (height, duration, damage)
- Plan improvements based on lessons learned
- Assess casualties or property damage

**10-year cycle:**

- Rebuild or strengthen dikes as needed
- Upgrade gates and spillways
- Improve warning systems
- Update community evacuation plans

## Verification

**Success looks like:**

- Dikes are well-maintained with no visible cracks or settlement
- Vegetation covers dike surfaces (prevents erosion)
- Water seepage is minimal (dry ground at dike toe)
- Retention channels drain properly (no standing water except during floods)
- Community evacuation plan is known and practiced

**Failure looks like:**

- Dike shows settlement or cracks
- Water seeps from dike (wet spots, erosion at base)
- Vegetation is sparse (dike surface eroding)
- Retention channels are clogged (water cannot flow)
- Floods cause damage despite dikes (indicates design or maintenance failure)

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll build a dike but not maintain it"** — Unmaintained dikes fail catastrophically. Plan for annual maintenance.

2. **"I'll make the dike as steep as possible to save materials"** — Steep dikes slide in weak soil. Keep slopes gentle (1:2 or shallower).

3. **"I won't allow floodway overflow"** — Dikes that cannot overflow fail from overtopping. Design spillways and retention areas.

4. **"I'll build on the floodplain because it's convenient"** — Floodplains flood; unavoidable. Build on high ground or on stilts.

5. **"Clay-rich soil from the river will work for the dike"** — Clay is good; but river clay may contain too much silt/sand. Test soil before using.

6. **"One dike is enough for the river"** — River can migrate, breaching dikes. Build multiple dikes with retention areas.

7. **"I don't need a warning system"** — Warning allows evacuation and mitigation. Build communication systems.

8. **"Dikes are permanent; I'll forget about them"** — Dikes require constant maintenance. Assign responsibility; budget for upkeep.
