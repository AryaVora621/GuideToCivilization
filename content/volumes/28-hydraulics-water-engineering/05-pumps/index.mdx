## Overview

Pumps lift water against gravity. Hand pumps use human muscle; water-powered pumps use flowing water or water wheels to lift water to reservoirs, houses, or fields.

Pumps are essential when gravity alone cannot deliver water. A deep well cannot deliver water using only a rope and bucket. A settlement on elevated ground cannot receive water from an aqueduct on lower ground without a pump to lift it.

Three pump types are practical post-collapse: hand pumps (cheap, manual), ram pumps (use flowing water's energy), and screw pumps (lift water in large quantities).

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Understand Pump Principles

Pumps work via pressure difference: low pressure on the intake side draws water up, high pressure on the discharge side pushes it out.

**Vacuum principle:**

- Pump creates a void (partial vacuum) at the intake
- Atmospheric pressure on the water surface pushes water up the intake pipe
- Height limit: roughly 10 meters (at sea level; less at higher elevations)
- Water cannot be lifted higher than atmospheric pressure can push it

**Pressure principle:**

- Pumps force water into a discharge pipe
- Discharge pressure can overcome gravity and height
- Mechanical advantage determines how much pressure is created

**One-way flow (check valves):**

- Intake valve: allows water in, prevents backflow
- Discharge valve: allows water out, prevents backflow
- These one-way valves are essential for pump function

### Step 2: Design a Hand Pump (Simplest and Most Useful)

A hand pump is a cylinder with a piston, operated by handle leverage.

**Pump design:**

1. **Cylinder:**
   - Length: 30 to 50 cm
   - Diameter: 5 to 10 cm
   - Material: wood (hollowed and sealed) or metal
   - Must be smooth inside (enables piston to move freely)

2. **Piston:**
   - Wooden cylinder slightly smaller than the pump cylinder
   - Leather seal wrapped around the piston (keeps water from leaking past)
   - Piston rod extends upward to the handle

3. **Valves (check valves):**
   - Intake valve at the bottom of the cylinder (allows water in, blocks backflow)
   - Discharge valve at the top (allows water out, blocks backflow)
   - Simple design: flapper valve (hinged disk) seals when pressure reverses

4. **Handle:**
   - Lever connected to piston rod
   - Mechanical advantage: 2:1 or 3:1 (effort arm twice as long as load arm)
   - Down stroke: piston moves down, intake valve opens, water enters
   - Up stroke: piston moves up, discharge valve opens, water is forced out

5. **Piping:**
   - Intake pipe: from water source (well) to pump intake valve
   - Discharge pipe: from pump discharge valve to destination
   - Pipe diameter: 2 to 5 cm
   - Intake pipe can have slight rise (gravity assists if intake is below pump)

**Hand pump specifications:**

- **Pumping rate:** 10 to 50 liters per minute (depending on piston size and stroke rate)
- **Lift height:** under 10 meters practical maximum (15 to 20 meters possible with two pumps in series)
- **Effort:** 10 to 30 kg per stroke (depending on lever mechanical advantage)
- **Operator:** one person can pump 1 to 2 hours continuously

### Step 3: Build a Hand Pump (Simple Design)

Step-by-step construction of a working hand pump.

**Cylinder:**

1. **Select material:** Wood (oak or ash) is acceptable; metal is better
2. **Hollow the cylinder:** Drill out lengthwise to create the piston chamber
   - Diameter: match the piston diameter (6 to 8 cm is typical)
   - Smooth the inside (may require stone honing or metal smoothing)
3. **Intake opening:** Drill a port near the bottom for water intake
4. **Discharge opening:** Drill a port near the top for water discharge

**Piston:**

1. **Wooden rod:** 5 to 6 cm diameter, 30 to 40 cm long (includes piston and rod)
2. **Seal:** Wrap leather or cloth around the piston part to create a water-tight seal
   - Leather is better (lasts longer)
   - Coat with grease to maintain flexibility and seal

3. **Piston rod:** The portion extending above the pump cylinder
   - Connected to the handle

**Valves (intake and discharge):**

1. **Simple flapper valve design:**
   - Metal or wood plate hinged at one edge
   - At the intake: allows water in when piston moves down; seals when piston moves up
   - At the discharge: allows water out when piston moves up; seals when piston moves down

2. **Valve installation:**
   - Intake valve in the bottom opening
   - Discharge valve in the top opening
   - Hinges on either side of the valve opening

**Assembly:**

1. Secure the cylinder vertically (piston moves up and down)
2. Insert the piston into the cylinder
3. Connect the piston rod to the lever handle
4. Attach the intake pipe at the bottom opening
5. Attach the discharge pipe at the top opening

### Step 4: Operate a Hand Pump

Simple procedure for using a hand pump.

**Priming (first use):**

1. Fill the pump cylinder with water from the source (pour through the discharge opening)
2. Push the handle down; water may be expelled or may start flowing
3. Repeat: down and up strokes until water flows from the discharge

**Continuous pumping:**

1. Down stroke: piston moves down, intake valve opens, water enters the cylinder
2. Up stroke: piston moves up, discharge valve opens, water is forced out through discharge pipe
3. Repeat at a comfortable pace (30 to 40 strokes per minute is sustainable)

**Maintenance:**

- Check that the piston seal (leather) remains moist (prevents drying and cracking)
- Grease the seal quarterly
- Clean the intake pipe (prevent sediment from blocking the valve)

### Step 5: Design an Archimedean Screw Pump (for Larger Flows)

A screw pump lifts water by trapping it in spiral chambers and rotating them.

**Screw pump design:**

1. **Spiral shaft:**
   - Wooden screw, 0.5 to 1 meter long
   - Diameter: 10 to 20 cm
   - Pitch (spacing of spiral): 10 to 15 cm
   - Built by wrapping a helical (spiral) wooden blade around a central shaft

2. **Cylinder:**
   - Wooden or metal tube surrounding the screw
   - Length: same as screw
   - Diameter: slightly larger than screw

3. **Angle:**
   - Typically 30 to 45 degrees from horizontal
   - Steeper angle: lifts higher but slower
   - Shallower angle: lifts lower but faster

4. **Drive:**
   - Crank handle connected to one end of the screw shaft
   - Or water wheel connection (see Vol 28, Ch 4: Water Mills)

**Screw pump operation:**

1. Water enters the bottom opening
2. As the screw rotates, water is trapped in the spiral chambers
3. Water is lifted up the inclined screw
4. Water exits the top opening into a reservoir or discharge pipe
5. Rotation speed: 20 to 50 rpm (depending on angle and desired lift)

**Pumping rate:**

- Moderate screw pump: 100 to 500 liters per minute
- Much higher than hand pumps
- Requires more mechanical power (crank force or water wheel)

### Step 6: Design a Ram Pump (Uses Flowing Water)

A ram pump uses the energy of flowing water to lift a portion of that water higher.

**Ram pump design:**

1. **Intake:**
   - Channel or pipe fed by flowing water (from aqueduct or stream)
   - Water flows downslope naturally

2. **Valve mechanism:**
   - Flapper valve in the intake (snaps closed when pressure increases)
   - When water speed increases, the flapper slams shut
   - Sudden pressure surge lifts water into an air chamber

3. **Air chamber:**
   - Enclosed space above the valve
   - Compressed air acts like a spring, pushing water into discharge pipe
   - Air is gradually absorbed, so a small air inlet re-supplies it

4. **Discharge:**
   - Pipe carrying lifted water to reservoir or final destination
   - Can deliver water higher than the intake level

**Ram pump operation:**

1. Water flows down the intake, pushing the flapper open
2. As water velocity increases, inertia causes the flapper to snap shut
3. Sudden pressure closes the flapper and forces water into the air chamber
4. Compressed air in the chamber pushes water up the discharge pipe
5. As pressure drops, the flapper opens again
6. Cycle repeats automatically (no human operation needed)

**Advantages:**

- Operates continuously without human effort
- Pumps roughly 10 to 20 percent of intake flow to higher elevation
- Reliable (simple mechanism, few parts)
- Lifespan: decades with minimal maintenance

**Limitations:**

- Requires constant flowing water (aqueduct or stream)
- Lifts only a fraction of the intake flow
- Lift height depends on water speed and pressure

### Step 7: Maintain Pumps

Regular maintenance keeps pumps working.

**Hand pump maintenance (weekly):**

- Inspect piston seal (leather) for cracking or drying
- Grease the seal with animal fat or oil
- Check intake pipe for blockages
- Verify valves function (listen for clicks when pumping)

**Monthly:**

- Check all fittings for leaks
- Inspect the handle and lever for cracks
- Clean sediment from intake filter (if present)

**Annual:**

- Replace piston seal if worn
- Clean internal passages of mineral deposits (lime buildup)
- Inspect and repair valves if they fail to seal

**Ram pump maintenance:**

- Minimal: check intake for blockages
- Inspect discharge pipe for leaks
- Verify the flapper valve moves freely (annual inspection)

## Verification

**Success looks like:**

- Hand pump flows water continuously at desired rate
- Water reaches desired height or destination
- Piston seal holds (no water leakage from pump body)
- Valves function properly (audible clicks when pumping)
- Screw or ram pump operates smoothly without excessive noise

**Failure looks like:**

- Hand pump produces little or no water
- Water leaks from pump cylinder
- Valves stick or make grinding noises
- Intake pipe cavitates (vacuum sound; indicates intake blockage)
- Piston moves but produces no discharge

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll make the intake pipe too narrow"** — Narrow pipes restrict flow and create vacuum. Use pipes at least 2 to 5 cm diameter.

2. **"I don't need an intake valve"** — Without the intake valve, water drains back down when the piston moves up. Intake valve is essential.

3. **"I'll lift water more than 10 meters with a single hand pump"** — Atmospheric pressure limits lift to about 10 meters. For greater height, use two pumps in series.

4. **"I'll make the piston seal from cloth"** — Cloth wears quickly. Use leather; it lasts years and maintains flexibility.

5. **"The valves can be complex"** — Simple flapper valves work well. Complex ball or spring valves are prone to sticking.

6. **"I won't grease the piston seal"** — Dry seals crack and allow water leakage. Grease quarterly.

7. **"I'll use metal pipes without sealing"** — Metal pipes must be sealed with putty or sealant at joints. Unsealed joints leak.

8. **"I'll install the pump in a cold place"** — In freezing climates, pumps must be protected. Freezing water cracks cylinders.
