## Overview

Aqueducts deliver reliable water to settlements using only gravity. Water flows from a higher elevation (spring, river, lake) to lower elevation settlements in pipes or channels. No pumps needed; the slope of the land does the work.

Aqueducts are among the most important engineering projects post-collapse. They enable permanent settlements to grow beyond immediate water sources. A well-built aqueduct delivers clean water reliably for centuries, supporting public health and supporting agriculture.

Roman aqueducts delivered water over 50 to 100 km; medieval aqueducts supplied entire regions. Your settlement can build a 5 to 20 km aqueduct in 6 to 12 months with basic labor and no advanced equipment.

## Materials & Tools

**For aqueduct surveying:**

- Water level or A-frame level (to determine slope)
- Measuring tape or rod (50 to 100 meters long)
- Stakes and string (for marking the line)
- Compass (for orientation)
- Notebook for recording measurements

**For aqueduct construction:**

- Water pipes (ceramic, wood, or stone; see Vol 15, Ch 7: Ceramic Applications for production)
- Stone or brick (for channels and structures)
- Lime mortar (for sealing joints)
- Timber (for supports, temporary forms)
- Soil and gravel (for bedding, backfill)

**For tools:**

- Shovel, pickaxe, wheelbarrow (digging and moving material)
- Level (to verify slope during construction)
- Saw, chisel (for cutting stone or pipe)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Survey the Route and Calculate Slope

Aqueducts work only if water flows downslope. A survey determines whether gravity flow is possible.

**Surveying procedure:**

1. **Identify water source:**
   - Spring: natural groundwater emergence (most reliable)
   - River intake: location along a river (requires sediment management)
   - Lake or reservoir: upper reservoir creates reliable supply

2. **Identify destination:**
   - Settlement location (where water is needed)
   - Elevation of final delivery point
   - Elevation of water source

3. **Measure elevation difference:**
   - Total relief: vertical distance from source to destination
   - Example: Spring at 500 m elevation, settlement at 400 m elevation
   - Relief: 100 meters

4. **Estimate distance:**
   - Map distance along the proposed route
   - Horizontal distance (ignore elevation for this calculation)
   - Example: 10 km horizontal distance, 100 m relief

5. **Calculate required slope:**
   - Slope = Relief ÷ Distance
   - Slope in percent = (Relief ÷ Distance) × 100
   - Example: (100 m ÷ 10 km) ÷ 10,000 m) × 100 = 1 percent

**Minimum slope required:**

- Water flows in pipes: 0.3 to 0.5 percent minimum
- Water flows in open channels: 0.1 to 0.3 percent minimum (less steep)
- Example: For 10 km, minimum slope is 30 to 50 meters of relief

**Route choice:**

- Direct route: steeper slope, shorter distance, easier engineering
- Contour route: follows the hillside to maintain slope, longer but less earthwork
- Trade-off: steep routes require stronger pipes/channels; longer routes cost more labor but use less materials per km

### Step 2: Survey the Route in Detail

A detailed survey maps the actual terrain and identifies obstacles.

**Detailed survey steps:**

1. **Walk the route:**
   - Identify hills, valleys, rocky areas, unstable slopes
   - Note obstacles: cliffs, gorges, forests (requires clearing), buildings (may need to go around)

2. **Set up surveying stations:**
   - Place stakes at regular intervals (every 100 to 500 meters, depending on terrain)
   - Use a level (A-frame or water level) to measure slope between stakes
   - Record the elevation at each stake

3. **Alternative: water level method (no tools needed):**
   - Fill a clear tube with water
   - Hold each end level (water surfaces are always level; gravity ensures this)
   - A helper holds one end at the starting point; you walk along the route holding the other end
   - When the water is level between your position and the starting point, you have marked one elevation contour
   - Mark this point; step downslope and repeat

4. **Create a profile:**
   - Draw the route as a line on paper
   - Mark elevations (vertical) and distances (horizontal)
   - This profile shows where the aqueduct must rise (bridges or tunnels) and where it slopes naturally

### Step 3: Design the Aqueduct: Pipe vs. Channel

Choose between closed pipes and open channels based on terrain and materials.

**Closed pipes (ceramic, wood, or stone):**

**Advantages:**
- Less water lost to evaporation
- Cleaner water (no contamination from air or ground)
- Works on steep slopes (pressure-rated pipes)
- Can cross gaps (bridges or siphons)

**Disadvantages:**
- More expensive (requires pipe production)
- Harder to clean or repair
- Pressure can exceed material strength on very steep slopes

**Open channels (carved or lined):**

**Advantages:**
- Cheaper (uses local stone or earth)
- Easier to repair or modify
- Self-cleaning (debris flows out)
- No pressure issues

**Disadvantages:**
- More water lost to evaporation
- Requires gravity slope (water must flow downhill)
- Cannot cross uphill sections (must tunnel or use a siphon)
- Water may be contaminated

**Choice guideline:**

- Gentle slopes (under 2 percent): open channels
- Moderate slopes (2 to 5 percent): either pipes or channels
- Steep slopes (over 5 percent): pipes preferred (open channels too dangerous)

### Step 4: Design Intake and Delivery Infrastructure

The intake captures source water; delivery provides clean water to the settlement.

**Intake design (for a spring):**

1. **Protect the spring:**
   - Stone or brick structure around the spring
   - Captures clean water before it enters the ground
   - Filters debris (gravel layer)
   - Has a valve or gate to control flow

2. **Settling basin:**
   - Optional but improves water quality
   - Allows sediment to settle before the water enters the aqueduct
   - Built as a shallow stone or clay-lined pool
   - Takes 4 to 8 hours for sediment to settle

3. **Flow rate:**
   - Measure the spring's flow (see Vol 28, Ch 5: Pumps for measurement methods)
   - Design aqueduct capacity equal to the spring flow
   - If spring flow varies seasonally, use the dry-season flow (worst case)

**Delivery design:**

1. **Terminal reservoir or cistern:**
   - Collects aqueduct water before distribution to the settlement
   - Allows settling of any sediment
   - Provides water storage (buffering supply and demand)
   - Built of stone with waterproof lime plaster

2. **Secondary distribution:**
   - From the terminal reservoir, smaller pipes or channels distribute to:
     - Public wells or fountains (for drinking)
     - Mills or industry (for power or washing)
     - Irrigation channels (for agriculture)
     - Firefighting reservoirs

### Step 5: Construct the Aqueduct Channel

For open-channel aqueducts, construction involves digging or building a channel with controlled slope.

**Channel construction procedure:**

1. **Prepare the alignment:**
   - Clear vegetation along the route
   - Mark the centerline with stakes
   - Mark the elevation (from surveying) at each stake

2. **Excavation:**
   - Dig a channel following the marked alignment
   - For pipes: trench is dug slightly deeper than the pipe (allows bedding and pipe-laying)
   - For open channels: excavate a V-shaped or trapezoidal channel, 0.5 to 1 meter deep, 0.5 to 1 meter wide
   - Check slope with a level at each step (must maintain slope toward settlement)

3. **Channel sides (optional but recommended):**
   - For open channels on unstable ground, line the sides with stone, brick, or clay
   - Prevents erosion and collapse
   - Improves durability
   - Use lime mortar to seal joints and prevent leaks

4. **Bedding (for pipes):**
   - Sand or fine gravel (5 cm layer)
   - Pipes sit on this bed, preventing damage from rocks below
   - Allows water to drain if pipes are removed

5. **Pipe laying:**
   - For ceramic pipes: lay end-to-end with sealed joints (clay mortar or lime mortar)
   - Joint should be tight (no gaps; tested by running water through before burial)
   - Support pipes on the sand bed; backfill gradually as you go

### Step 6: Handle Obstacles

Aqueducts often encounter obstacles: valleys, hills, unstable ground.

**Crossing valleys (water must descend then rise):**

1. **Siphon (simplest for small gaps):**
   - Pipes descend into the valley, then climb up the other side
   - Pressure inside the pipe (from the higher water source) pushes water up the opposite side
   - Works as long as the source elevation is higher than the destination elevation
   - Maximum practical siphon depth: 5 to 10 meters (beyond this, pipes must be very strong)

2. **Bridge (for small valleys):**
   - Stone or wooden structure carries the aqueduct across the valley
   - Supports the pipe or channel above ground
   - Must be strong enough to support the weight of water plus pipe/channel materials

3. **Tunnel (for hills blocking the way):**
   - Dig through the hill at the same elevation
   - Longer distance but avoids building a high bridge
   - Very labor-intensive (requires skilled miners)
   - Provides protection from contamination and freezing

**Handling steep slopes (water is too fast):**

1. **Stepped channels:**
   - Cascade water down in steps (like a waterfall)
   - Slows the flow, prevents erosion
   - Used in very mountainous terrain

2. **Slower materials:**
   - Rough stone channels (friction slows water)
   - Wooden channels (can absorb energy)

**Handling unstable ground:**

- Avoid if possible (choose a different route)
- If unavoidable: bridge or siphon instead of burying the pipe
- Risk of ground settling, breaking pipes underground

### Step 7: Maintenance and Management

Aqueducts require regular maintenance to function reliably.

**Monthly maintenance:**

- Inspect the intake for blockages (leaves, debris)
- Walk the aqueduct route looking for leaks (wet spots, damaged areas)
- Clear vegetation that might damage the aqueduct
- Check delivery system for blockages

**Annual maintenance:**

- Clean the entire aqueduct (flush debris from pipes or channels)
- Repair any damaged sections (mortar cracks, broken pipes)
- Inspect bridges or siphons for structural damage
- Reapply waterproof plaster to channels if needed
- Check and adjust gates and valves

**Major repairs (every 10 to 30 years):**

- Replace worn pipes or channels
- Rebuild bridges or siphons
- Realign settling sections
- Replaster channel walls

**Management:**

- Assign a person (aqueduct keeper) responsible for inspection and repair
- Keep records of maintenance work done
- Plan seasonal shutdowns if needed for major repairs (winter, when water demand is lower)

## Verification

**Success looks like:**

- Water flows continuously from source to destination
- Flow rate matches the source supply (no major leaks)
- Water is clean (transparent, no sediment)
- Aqueduct structure is intact (no major cracks or damage)
- Delivery reaches the settlement reliably year-round

**Failure looks like:**

- No water flow (blockage in intake or main line)
- Weak water flow (leaks along the route)
- Cloudy or sediment-laden water (intake not properly protected)
- Visible cracks, damage, or water seeping from the aqueduct structure
- Seasonal failure (water stops in dry season or during cold weather)

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll choose the shortest route regardless of slope"** — If the slope is insufficient, water will not flow or will flow too slowly. Survey first; choose the route that maintains slope.

2. **"I'll build the aqueduct steeper than calculated to save distance"** — Too-steep slopes cause erosion, noise, and water loss. Follow the calculated slope.

3. **"I won't protect the spring intake; I'll just dig at the source"** — Unprotected springs become contaminated. Build a stone structure to capture clean water.

4. **"I'll use low-quality pipes to save money"** — Leaking pipes waste water and waste the effort of building the aqueduct. Use strong, well-made pipes.

5. **"I'll bury the aqueduct everywhere to hide it"** — Buried aqueducts are hard to inspect and repair. Expose sections you can see; bury only where necessary.

6. **"Open channels are wasteful; I'll use pipes everywhere"** — Pipes are expensive. Use open channels on gentle slopes and pipes only where necessary (steep slopes, long spans).

7. **"I don't need to survey; I'll just follow the map"** — Maps are inaccurate. Actual terrain may not match maps. Survey before building.

8. **"A 1 percent slope is too steep; water will flow too fast"** — A 1 percent slope is ideal; water flows at under 1 meter per second (manageable). Without slope, water doesn't flow at all.
