## Overview

Irrigation channels distribute water from aqueducts or wells to fields. Well-designed channels double or triple crop yields by ensuring crops have water during dry seasons.

Channel design is simple: dig a sloped channel from the water source, branch it into smaller channels through the fields, and allow water to percolate into the soil. Ancient civilizations used irrigation channels to support millions of people; post-collapse settlements can use the same techniques.

This chapter teaches channel design: sizing for water flow, slope and grading, branching patterns, and field saturation. By year 2, your settlement can irrigate 10 to 100 hectares, supporting many times more people.

## Materials & Tools

**For channel construction:**

- Shovel, pickaxe (digging)
- Measuring tape (dimensions and slope)
- Level (to verify slope)
- Stakes and string (layout)

**For channel lining (optional):**

- Clay or lime plaster (sealing)
- Stones or bricks (for structure)
- Vegetation (reeds or grass for stabilization)

**For water distribution:**

- Gates or dams (for controlling water flow between channels)
- Buckets or containers (for manual distribution if needed)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Design the Channel Network

A channel network has main channels and secondary branches.

**Main channel:**

- Largest channel, carries water from source to the irrigation area
- Slope: 0.1 to 0.5 percent (gentle; water flows slowly, no erosion)
- Width: 0.5 to 2 meters
- Depth: 0.5 to 1 meter
- Carries bulk water for multiple fields

**Secondary channels:**

- Branch from main channel into individual fields
- Slope: 0.1 to 0.3 percent
- Width: 0.2 to 0.5 meters
- Depth: 0.3 to 0.5 meters

**Field ditches (optional):**

- Small channels within fields for fine distribution
- Follow field contours
- Allow water to percolate into soil

**Layout pattern:**

```
Water source (aqueduct or well)
    |
    v
Main channel (slopes gently downhill)
    |
    +---> Secondary channel 1 (to Field A)
    |
    +---> Secondary channel 2 (to Field B)
    |
    +---> Secondary channel 3 (to Field C)
    |
    v
Drainage or downslope outlet
```

### Step 2: Calculate Water Flow Rate

Determine how much water each channel must carry.

**Crop water needs:**

- Crops need 5 to 15 millimeters of water per day during growing season
- Example: Field A is 1 hectare (10,000 m²)
- Water need: 10 mm per day = 0.01 m per day = 100 cubic meters per day
- Flow rate needed: 100 m³ / 86,400 seconds per day = 0.001 m³/second = 1 liter per second

**Total settlement needs:**

- All fields combined determine main channel size
- Example: settlement irrigates 50 hectares total
- Total daily need: 500 cubic meters per day (water from aqueduct or well must provide this)

**Channel dimensions for flow rate:**

- Flow rate = width × depth × velocity
- Velocity in channels: 0.2 to 0.5 meters per second (gentle flow)
- Example: To carry 0.5 m³/second with velocity of 0.3 m/s
  - Cross-section area needed: 0.5 / 0.3 = 1.67 m²
  - A channel 1 meter wide and 1.67 meters deep works
  - Or 2 meters wide and 0.83 meters deep
  - Choose dimensions that fit available space

### Step 3: Design Channel Slope and Grading

Proper slope ensures water flows without erosion.

**Slope determination:**

1. **Gentle slope (preferred):** 0.1 to 0.3 percent
   - Water flows slowly (no erosion)
   - Easy to construct on irregular terrain
   - Requires longer channel distance

2. **Moderate slope:** 0.3 to 1 percent
   - Water flows faster (more risk of erosion)
   - Acceptable if channel is lined or stabilized
   - Covers distance more efficiently

3. **Steep slope:** over 1 percent
   - High flow velocity (significant erosion risk)
   - Should be lined or protected
   - Only use if necessary to reach distant fields

**Preventing erosion:**

- Line channels with clay or stone to prevent soil erosion
- Plant vegetation along channel sides (grass, reeds)
- Minimize slope; use multiple channels in steps rather than one steep channel

**Grading procedure:**

1. Mark the starting point (at the water source)
2. Determine elevation at the fields
3. Calculate required slope: (elevation drop) / (channel length)
4. Use a level at intervals to ensure slope is correct
5. Mark the channel alignment with stakes

### Step 4: Construct the Main Channel

Dig the main channel from source to irrigation area.

**Construction steps:**

1. **Mark alignment:**
   - Stake out the centerline from source to fields
   - Use a compass for general direction
   - Straight channels are easier to build and manage

2. **Excavate:**
   - Dig the channel to the marked dimensions
   - Remove soil; discard or use for fill elsewhere
   - Monitor slope with a level at regular intervals (every 50 to 100 meters)

3. **Protect the channel:**
   - For unlined channels in sandy soil: edge with stone to prevent collapse
   - For channels crossing unstable ground: line with clay plaster or bricks
   - Vegetation planted on the sides stabilizes the banks

4. **Branching points:**
   - At regular intervals (every 100 to 200 meters or at field entrances), install secondary channel junctions
   - Junction should be stable; reinforce with stone or bricks
   - Install a gate or dam to control water flow into secondary channels

### Step 5: Construct Secondary Channels

Secondary channels distribute water into individual fields.

**Secondary channel design:**

1. **Width and depth:**
   - Smaller than main channel (less water per field)
   - Width: 0.2 to 0.5 meters
   - Depth: 0.3 to 0.5 meters

2. **Layout:**
   - Run through or around fields
   - Follow the field contours (run along the side of fields at the same elevation)
   - Place at the highest point of the field; let water flow downslope

3. **Water entry into fields:**
   - Allow water to breach the channel bank where crops are grown
   - Simple gaps or openings in the channel allow water to flow into the field
   - Control these openings with small gates to regulate field flooding

4. **Multiple secondary channels:**
   - For large fields, install multiple secondary channels at different elevations
   - Top channel waters the upper part of the field
   - Lower channels water lower portions
   - Ensures even water distribution

### Step 6: Design Field Irrigation Methods

Water distribution within fields varies by crop and field shape.

**Flood irrigation (simplest):**

- Water fills the field; allowed to percolate and drain downslope
- Suitable for rice, pasture, or crops that tolerate standing water
- Requires level or nearly level fields
- Simple but water-wasteful (some evaporates or runs off)

**Furrow irrigation (better for rows):**

- Long ditches run between crop rows
- Water flows down the furrows
- Efficient; water reaches roots with minimal evaporation
- Requires sloped fields and regular maintenance (clearing blockages)

**Basin irrigation (for orchards):**

- Individual trees or shrubs surrounded by a low berm (raised edge)
- Water fills the basin around each plant
- Water percolates directly to roots
- Very efficient; minimal water loss

**Border irrigation (for flat fields):**

- Fields are divided by small berms (ridges) running downslope
- Each border strip is flooded until saturated
- Water advances downslope through repeated saturation
- Used for row crops on sloped land

### Step 7: Manage Water Flow and Drainage

Control water distribution and prevent waterlogging.

**Flow control:**

1. **Gates or dams:**
   - Simple wooden gates control water entry into secondary channels
   - Dams (low stone walls) divert water at junctions
   - Gates allow seasonal adjustment (more water in dry season, less in rainy season)

2. **Scheduling:**
   - Rotate water delivery: Field A gets water for 3 days, then Field B, etc.
   - Allows all fields to be irrigated from a single small water source
   - Requires coordination and record-keeping

**Drainage:**

1. **Downslope drainage:**
   - Excess water flows downslope naturally to the lowest point
   - May be captured for secondary use (mills, lower fields)

2. **Perennial drainage:**
   - If fields are waterlogged, dig drainage ditches at the downslope edge
   - Removes excess water before it causes damage
   - Drainage water can be reused downslope

3. **Seasonal adjustment:**
   - In rainy season: reduce irrigation; rely on rainfall
   - In dry season: irrigate fully
   - Prevents overwatering and waste

### Step 8: Maintain Channels

Channels require regular maintenance to function.

**Monthly maintenance:**

- Walk the channel; look for leaks, cracks, or damage
- Clear debris (leaves, branches) that blocks water flow
- Ensure gates and dams function

**Seasonal maintenance:**

- Before planting: clean channels; repair damage from winter
- During growing season: monitor water level and flow
- After harvest: drain channels; prepare for winter or dry season

**Annual major maintenance:**

- Regrade channels if they've settled or eroded
- Replaster or reline channels if sealing has failed
- Rebuild dams or gates if damaged
- Plant new vegetation on channel banks if erosion has exposed soil

**Channel cleaner role:**

- Assign a person responsible for daily channel inspection
- Maintain a log of water level and flow
- Respond immediately to blockages or damage
- Coordinate water distribution schedule with fields

## Verification

**Success looks like:**

- Water flows continuously from source to all fields
- All fields receive adequate water (crops are well-watered, not wilted)
- No flooding or waterlogging in fields
- Channel structure is intact (no cracks, damage, or leaks)
- Water remains clean (no excessive sediment or debris)
- Crop yields are 2 to 3 times higher than rain-fed farming

**Failure looks like:**

- Water flow stops (blockage in channels)
- Some fields get water while others are dry (uneven distribution)
- Fields are waterlogged or swampy (poor drainage)
- Channel structure is damaged (cracks, eroded banks, breaches)
- Water is muddy or carries excessive debris
- Crop yields do not improve

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll make channels too steep to save digging"** — Steep channels cause erosion and water loss. Gentle slopes (under 1 percent) are much better.

2. **"I'll skip lining channels; they'll work without it"** — Unlined channels lose water to seepage and collapse in sandy soil. Line channels in unstable soil.

3. **"I'll make all fields receive water from one large channel"** — Control is lost; water runs away or pools. Branch channels allow better control.

4. **"I won't install gates or dams"** — Without control, water distribution is uneven. Gates allow seasonal adjustment and field-by-field control.

5. **"I'll over-irrigate; water is cheap"** — Waterlogging kills crops. Irrigate just enough to saturate the soil; excess water should drain away.

6. **"I'll dig channels in summer when the ground is dry"** — Dry ground is hard to dig. Dig in spring or early season when soil is moist but not waterlogged.

7. **"I don't need drainage; the fields will self-drain"** — Some fields won't drain naturally. Drainage ditches are essential in low areas.

8. **"I'll build all channels at the same elevation"** — Fields at different elevations need channels at different heights. Match channel elevation to field elevation.
