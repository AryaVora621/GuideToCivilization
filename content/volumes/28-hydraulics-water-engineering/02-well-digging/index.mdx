## Overview

Wells access groundwater, providing reliable water independent of surface sources (springs, rivers). Unlike aqueducts which require hills and slopes, wells work on flat land. A good well can supply a settlement for decades.

Well depth depends on the water table (the level of groundwater underground). Shallow wells (3 to 10 meters) are quick and easy; deep wells (20 to 100 meters) reach more reliable aquifers. Post-collapse, most settlements can drill hand-dug wells in under a month.

This chapter teaches well-digging methods: hand digging, percussion boring (using a heavy tool dropped repeatedly), and rope-and-bucket well recovery. By year 1, your settlement can drill multiple wells serving 100 to 1000 people each.

## Materials & Tools

**For hand-dug wells:**

- Shovel, pickaxe (digging tools)
- Bucket and rope (removing excavated soil and water)
- Measuring tape (measuring depth)
- Light source (candle, lamp; for visibility in deep wells)

**For well structures:**

- Bricks or stones (lining the well; prevents collapse)
- Lime mortar (for sealing joints)
- Wood or metal rings (optional; for structure support)
- Timber (for support beams and cover)

**For deeper wells (percussion boring):**

- Heavy metal tool or stone weight (0.5 to 2 kg)
- Long rope or chain
- Wooden frame (to hang the boring tool)
- Bucket and rope (for removing cuttings)

**For recovery:**

- Rope or chain (strong, non-rotting materials)
- Bucket or container
- Hand pump (if available; see Vol 28, Ch 5: Pumps)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Locate the Well Site

Choose a location based on water table depth, soil conditions, and settlement needs.

**Site selection criteria:**

1. **Water table depth:**
   - Shallow wells (3 to 10 m): easier, faster to dig
   - Deep wells (20 to 100 m): harder but more reliable
   - Find the water table from local knowledge or from a test hole (see Vol 14, Ch 3: Groundwater Finding)

2. **Soil conditions:**
   - Sand or gravel: easier to dig but may collapse (needs lining)
   - Clay: hard to dig but stays stable (may not need lining)
   - Rock: very hard; may not be feasible without specialized equipment
   - Test by digging a small hole; assess soil type at various depths

3. **Distance from pollution sources:**
   - At least 30 meters downslope from latrines, manure piles, or burial sites
   - Water flows downslope through soil; avoid contamination paths
   - Higher elevation than nearby water sources (prevents contamination flowing upslope)

4. **Accessibility:**
   - Near where water is needed (settlement, mill, fields)
   - Away from low-lying areas (flooding risk)
   - On stable ground (no signs of settlement or erosion)

5. **Settlement needs:**
   - Estimate daily water needs: 50 to 100 liters per person per day
   - Well should provide this supply plus buffer for drought
   - Multiple wells (3 to 5) ensure reliability

**Test boring:**

Before committing to a full well, drill a small test hole:

1. Use a hand auger or small boring tool to reach 15 to 20 meters
2. Record soil type at each meter of depth
3. Note water table depth
4. Assess how many meters of digging work to expect

### Step 2: Hand Dig a Shallow Well

For water tables within 10 meters of the surface, hand digging is feasible and quick.

**Hand digging procedure:**

1. **Mark the hole location:**
   - Center point clearly marked
   - Diameter: 1 to 1.5 meters (large enough for a person to work inside)

2. **Dig carefully (safety is critical):**
   - Remove soil in buckets; do not let soil accumulate in the well (weight and suffocation hazard)
   - Use a bucket on a rope: filled by a person inside, pulled up by a person outside
   - Work in shifts; excavation is exhausting

3. **Monitor soil conditions:**
   - As you dig deeper, assess soil type
   - Sand: unstable; likely to collapse (needs lining as you go)
   - Clay: stable; can dig to depth and line afterward
   - Rock: very hard; may need to go elsewhere

4. **Reach the water table:**
   - Dig until water begins to seep into the hole
   - Continue digging 1 to 2 meters below the water table (ensures adequate flow)
   - Water level will stabilize at the water table elevation

5. **Dewater the well:**
   - Once you've dug to the desired depth, water will have accumulated
   - Bail out water with buckets until the water level stabilizes
   - This may take hours or days depending on water table and flow rate

### Step 3: Line a Well (for Collapsed Soil)

If the well walls collapse or the soil is loose, line the well with brick or stone.

**Lining procedure (for open wells with unstable soil):**

1. **As you dig, line the walls:**
   - For sand or loose soil, place bricks or stones as you go, every 0.5 to 1 meter of depth
   - Build a ring of bricks or stacked stones around the inside of the well (like a circular wall)
   - Use lime mortar between bricks to seal joints

2. **Lining materials:**
   - Fired bricks (strongest; see Vol 10, Ch 2: Brickmaking)
   - Stones (roughly circular; stacked in rings)
   - Wood (less durable; rots over time)

3. **Seal the lining:**
   - Lime mortar between bricks/stones prevents sand from falling through
   - Allow mortar to cure before excavating further

4. **Alternative: wood rings:**
   - Wooden rings (barrels with top and bottom removed) can be stacked
   - Lower each ring into the well as you dig
   - Allows faster digging but wood deteriorates

### Step 4: Bore a Deeper Well (Percussion Method)

For water tables 10 to 100 meters deep, use percussion boring (dropping a heavy tool repeatedly).

**Percussion boring setup:**

1. **Boring tool:**
   - Weight: 0.5 to 2 kg (heavier tools reach deeper but are harder to lift)
   - Shape: wide, flat bottom (better for cutting soil)
   - Attached to a rope or cable

2. **Lifting frame:**
   - Wooden tripod or frame standing over the hole
   - Pulley at the top (reduces effort needed to lift the boring tool)
   - Rope passed through the pulley

3. **Boring procedure:**
   - Drop the tool 1 to 2 meters, allow it to fall and strike the bottom
   - Lift the tool; repeat
   - Each strike advances the hole depth by 1 to 10 cm depending on soil
   - In sand: fast (1 to 2 meters per day)
   - In clay: slow (0.5 meters per day)
   - In rock: very slow or impossible

4. **Remove cuttings (soil/sand from boring):**
   - As the hole deepens, cuttings accumulate at the bottom
   - Pump or bail out cuttings periodically
   - Cuttings mixed with water create a slurry (easy to bail)
   - Alternate between boring and bailing

5. **Advance downward:**
   - Continue boring and bailing until water reaches the bottom of the hole
   - Once water is present, boring becomes easier (water lubricates and carries cuttings)
   - Continue boring 2 to 5 meters below the water table

**Depth estimate:**

- Hand digging: 1 to 10 meters (1 to 4 weeks)
- Percussion boring: 20 to 100 meters (8 to 24 weeks)
- Rate depends on soil type and weather (impossible in heavy rain or freezing weather)

### Step 5: Develop the Well (Improve Flow Rate)

A newly dug well may flow slowly because the soil is disturbed. Development improves flow.

**Development procedure:**

1. **Pumping or bailing:**
   - Rapidly pump or bail water for several hours
   - This disturbs the soil at the well bottom
   - Sediment clears out; aquifer compresses and improves flow

2. **Jetting (if available):**
   - High-pressure water from a pump directed at the well bottom
   - Removes fine particles and opens the aquifer
   - Improves flow rate significantly

3. **Surging:**
   - Move a plunger (flat disk on a pole) up and down in the well
   - Creates pressure waves that disturb the aquifer
   - Low-tech version of jetting; effective but labor-intensive

4. **Repeat until flow stabilizes:**
   - After development, the well should flow clearly (no sediment)
   - Flow rate stabilizes over a few days to a week

### Step 6: Seal the Well (for Contamination Prevention)

The well surface must be sealed to prevent contamination from rain, surface runoff, or animals.

**Sealing procedure:**

1. **Sanitary seal (for shallow wells):**
   - Stone or brick lining extends 30 to 60 cm above ground
   - Clay or lime mortar is packed around the lining at ground level
   - Creates a raised, sealed well head

2. **Cover:**
   - Wooden or stone cover (lid) over the well opening
   - Prevents debris from falling in
   - Allows removal for water recovery

3. **Drain away from well:**
   - Build a slight mound of soil around the well head
   - Slopes water away from the opening
   - Prevents standing water near the well

4. **Distance from pollution sources:**
   - Contamination flows downslope through soil
   - Wells should be higher than pollution sources
   - Minimum distance: 30 to 50 meters downslope from latrines or manure heaps

### Step 7: Install Recovery Equipment

Extract water from the well for use.

**Rope and bucket (simplest):**

1. **Rope:**
   - Non-rotting material (hemp, wool, or synthetic)
   - Length equal to well depth plus 5 to 10 meters (reaches the surface with extra)
   - Attached to a bucket

2. **Bucket:**
   - Any container (ceramic, wood, metal)
   - Capacity: 10 to 20 liters
   - Attached securely to the rope

3. **Lifting:**
   - Haul by hand (one person, for shallow wells)
   - Use pulley (reduces effort; for deeper wells)
   - Multiple buckets (one rising, one descending) increase efficiency

4. **Yield:**
   - Shallow well (3 m deep, sandy soil): 100 to 500 liters per hour
   - Deep well (30 m deep, clay soil): 20 to 100 liters per hour
   - Depends on aquifer flow rate

**Hand pump (if available):**

See Vol 28, Ch 5: Pumps for hand pump design and construction.

**Alternatives:**

- Shaduf (lever pump; see Vol 11: Mechanical Engineering)
- Animal-powered pump (using oxen or horses)
- Windmill or water wheel (if available; see Vol 33: Mechanical Engineering)

### Step 8: Maintain the Well

Wells require maintenance to function reliably and safely.

**Monthly maintenance:**

- Check that the well cover is secure (prevents contamination)
- Test water quality: it should be clear and taste fresh
- Inspect lining for cracks or damage

**Annual maintenance:**

- Pump or bail the well completely dry
- Clean the bottom (remove accumulated sediment)
- Inspect and repair any damage to the lining
- Check that the seal and drain are functioning

**Every 5 to 10 years:**

- May need redevelopment if flow rate has declined (repeat Step 5)
- Check and replace rope or pump parts as needed
- Inspect structural integrity of the lining

**Well keeper role:**

- Assign a person responsible for daily well operation
- Maintain a log of water level (seasonal variation)
- Record maintenance performed
- Respond immediately to any concerns (drops in water level, cloudiness, odors)

## Verification

**Success looks like:**

- Well flows continuously throughout the year
- Water is clear, fresh-tasting, and free of sediment
- Water level is stable (rises and falls with season, but consistently available)
- Flow rate meets settlement needs
- Well structure is intact (no visible cracks, damage, or leaks)
- Water quality remains safe (no contamination from surface sources)

**Failure looks like:**

- Well flow drops significantly during dry season (indicates shallow water table or low aquifer yield)
- Water is cloudy or contains sediment
- Well runs dry during drought (inadequate depth or aquifer yield)
- Water has odor or unusual taste (contamination from pollution sources)
- Well structure shows cracks, settlement, or damage
- Water level changes unexpectedly or erratically

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll dig the well too shallow to save time"** — Shallow wells dry up in drought. Dig to 3 to 5 meters minimum (deeper if water table allows).

2. **"I'll dig the well without lining it"** — Without lining, sandy soil collapses, killing workers and destroying the well. Always line unstable soil.

3. **"I'll put the well near the latrine for convenience"** — Contamination flows through soil from latrines to wells. Place wells upslope or at least 30 to 50 meters away.

4. **"I'll use soft wood for the well lining"** — Soft wood rots within a few years. Use fired brick or stone; they last centuries.

5. **"I'll skip development; the well will clear on its own"** — New wells flow slowly because soil is disturbed. Development (pumping out sediment) is essential for good flow.

6. **"One well is enough for the settlement"** — One well may fail or fall into disrepair. Build 3 to 5 wells so the settlement survives if one breaks.

7. **"I don't need to seal the well; nobody will contaminate it"** — Surface runoff, animals, and dust contaminate wells. Always seal the well head.

8. **"Deeper wells are always better"** — Deeper wells are harder to dig and may not be necessary. Dig to reliable water table; going deeper costs more with little benefit.
