## Overview

Sewage systems collect wastewater from settlements and safely dispose of it. Without sewers, human waste accumulates, causing disease, contamination, and health disaster. Ancient Rome's sewer system (cloaca maxima) supported millions; medieval cities without sewers had frequent plague and cholera.

Post-collapse settlements must choose: individual latrines (Vol 5, Ch 1: Latrine Design) or communal sewers. Sewers are expensive (require extensive channels and treatment) but enable larger, denser settlements. This chapter covers sewer design, treatment, and safe discharge.

By year 2, your settlement can build a modest sewer system serving 500 to 1000 people.

- Channels (dug in earth, lined with stone or clay)
- Gravel and stone (for filter beds)

**For treatment:**

- Tanks or basins (excavated and lined, or built from stone)
- Filters (gravel, sand, charcoal)
- Biological treatment: reeds, bacteria

**For discharge:**

- Outlet channel or river access
- Flow control (gates, weirs)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Plan the Sewer System

Design the collection network and treatment facility.

**System layout:**

```
Individual houses
  |
  +---> Local collection lines (to-do)
          |
          +---> Main sewer (gravity slope)
                 |
                 v
            Treatment facility
                 |
                 +---> Final discharge (to river or land)
```

**Collection network:**

1. **House drains:**
   - Pipes from each house to the local collection line
   - Slope: at least 0.5 percent (ensures flow)
   - Diameter: 5 to 10 cm (carries combined sewage and greywater)

2. **Local collection lines:**
   - 15 to 30 cm diameter pipes or channels
   - Run through streets or alleys
   - Slope: 0.5 to 1 percent (gravity flow to main sewer)
   - Spaced 100 to 200 meters apart

3. **Main sewer:**
   - Largest pipe or channel (30 to 50 cm diameter or equivalent)
   - Runs from the settlement to the treatment facility
   - Slope: 0.3 to 0.5 percent (gentle but sufficient)
   - Must be below ground (avoids odors and contamination)

**Treatment facility:**

- Located downwind and downstream from the settlement
- Treats wastewater before discharge to prevent pollution

**Discharge:**

- To a river (if available)
- To land (if treated adequately)
- Must be below the settlement (gravity flow)

### Step 2: Design Sewer Pipes and Channels

Choose between pipes (costly but compact) and open channels (cheaper but require more space).

**Ceramic or stone pipes (preferred):**

- Diameter: 15 to 50 cm (depending on flow volume)
- Material: ceramic (fired) or stone (if ceramic unavailable)
- Joints: clay mortar or lime mortar (must be watertight)
- Installation: buried 1 to 2 meters deep (protects from freezing and contamination)
- Slope: at least 0.3 percent to allow gravity flow

**Open channels (cheaper alternative):**

- Excavated channel, 1 to 2 meters wide, 1 to 1.5 meters deep
- Lined with stone or clay (prevents erosion and infiltration)
- Slope: 0.5 to 1 percent
- Covered or open (open channels smell; covered is better)

**Flow capacity:**

- Estimate water use: 100 to 200 liters per person per day
- Settlement size: 500 people = 50 to 100 cubic meters per day
- Flow rate: roughly 0.6 to 1.2 liters per second average
- Peak flow (morning/evening): 2 to 3 times average
- Sewer must handle peak flow: design for 3 to 4 liters per second for 500 people

**Pipe sizing:**

- Flow rate = area × velocity
- Velocity in sewers: 0.3 to 0.6 meters per second (prevents sediment settling and odors)
- For 1 liter per second: area needed = 1/0.5 = 2 liters per second / 0.5 m/s = roughly 0.002 m² = 50 cm² per liter
- A 10 cm diameter pipe (area roughly 80 cm²) works for 1 to 2 liters per second

### Step 3: Install Sewer Pipes

Lay pipes from the settlement to the treatment facility.

**Excavation:**

1. **Alignment:**
   - Mark the sewer line from settlement to treatment facility
   - Slope should be continuous downward (no sags that trap sediment)
   - Plan for easy maintenance (straight lines, regular intervals)

2. **Trench:**
   - Dig to depth (1 to 2 meters below surface, depending on ground freezing)
   - Width: slightly wider than pipes (allow workspace)
   - Slope: match the designed sewer slope (use a level to check)

3. **Pipe installation:**
   - Place a sand bed at the bottom (2 to 5 cm; allows proper pipe seating)
   - Lay pipes end-to-end with slight overlap or sealed joints
   - For ceramic pipes: overlap joints, seal with clay mortar
   - For stone channels: align carefully, seal with clay or lime mortar

4. **Backfill:**
   - Cover pipes with sand or gravel (prevents pipe breakage from above)
   - Cover with soil
   - Compact gradually

**Access points (maintenance):**

- Install access points (manholes) every 30 to 50 meters
- At changes in grade or direction
- Manholes are vertical shafts allowing a person to access the sewer
- Covers prevent odors and contamination

### Step 4: Design and Build a Treatment Facility

Treat wastewater before discharge to prevent pollution.

**Primary treatment (settling):**

1. **Settling tank:**
   - Large tank or basin (10 to 20 cubic meters for 500 people)
   - Wastewater flows in slowly; solids settle to the bottom
   - Residence time: 2 to 4 hours (allows sediment to settle)
   - Overflow outlet at the top (clarified water continues to secondary treatment)
   - Settled solids (sludge) accumulate at the bottom (removed monthly or quarterly)

2. **Construction:**
   - Excavated basin, lined with clay or stone
   - Sloped bottom (allows sludge removal)
   - Inlet and outlet pipes (inlet submerged below surface)

**Secondary treatment (biological):**

1. **Constructed wetland (simplest and most effective):**
   - Shallow basin (30 to 60 cm deep)
   - Filled with gravel and sand
   - Planted with reeds or marsh plants
   - Wastewater percolates through, bacteria and plants break down pollutants
   - Very effective; naturally low maintenance

2. **Construction:**
   - Excavate basin large enough for settlement flow (1 to 2 square meters per person)
   - Line with clay or plastic (prevents groundwater infiltration)
   - Fill with sand and gravel mix
   - Plant with reeds (typha, phragmites)
   - Inlet at one end, outlet at the other

3. **Alternative: lagoon (very simple):**
   - Large, shallow pond (0.5 to 1 meter deep)
   - Wastewater fills the pond; algae and bacteria treat it
   - Outlet after 3 to 7 days residence time
   - Very cheap; effective in warm climates
   - Less effective in cold climates

**Sludge handling:**

- Settled solids from the primary tank must be managed
- Options:
  - Dry in beds (leave for months; allows water drainage)
  - Compost (mix with plant material; becomes soil amendment)
  - Bury in designated area (disease-free after several months)

### Step 5: Design Final Discharge

Safely return treated water to the environment.

**River discharge:**

- If a river is available, discharge treated water downstream
- Intake point should be far above the outlet (prevents re-contamination)
- Quality standards: treated water should be clear, free of odor, and safe

**Land discharge:**

- If no river, discharge to land through a drain field
- Drain field: network of pipes buried in soil
- Water percolates into soil; soil bacteria continue treatment
- Unsuitability: if groundwater is near or if soil is clay (impermeable)

**Requirements for safe discharge:**

- Treated water quality:
  - Clear (no suspended solids)
  - Odorless (no odor indicates biological treatment has occurred)
  - Pathogen-free (disease-causing bacteria eliminated by treatment and settling)
- Flow management: avoid flooding downstream or stagnation in treatment system

### Step 6: Maintenance of Sewer Systems

Regular maintenance prevents blockage and odors.

**Daily:**

- Monitor for blockages (slow drainage or backups)
- Inspect treatment facility for normal operation

**Weekly:**

- Clean screens or filters (if present)
- Check for leaks or odors

**Monthly:**

- Access the main sewer at a manhole and inspect (use rope safety precautions)
- Remove accumulated sediment if necessary (use vacuum or bucket)
- Check treatment facility level (sludge accumulation)

**Quarterly:**

- Remove sludge from primary tank
- Dry in sludge beds or compost
- Inspect all pipes and channels for damage

**Annually:**

- Major cleaning of entire system
- Repair any cracks or damage
- Assess vegetation in constructed wetlands; maintain as needed

**Sewer keeper role:**

- Oversee system operation and maintenance
- Keep records of maintenance work
- Manage sludge disposal

### Step 7: Prevent System Failure

Common problems and solutions.

**Blockage (most common issue):**

- Caused by: grease, hair, solid waste
- Prevention: educate users (don't dump grease, don't dispose of solids)
- Solution: access manhole and remove blockage (hand or mechanical)

**Leakage:**

- Caused by: cracked pipes, poor joints, root infiltration
- Prevention: proper installation, regular inspection
- Solution: replace damaged section

**Odor:**

- Caused by: anaerobic (oxygen-free) decomposition
- Prevention: ensure water flow (proper slope)
- Solution: aerate system (increase oxygen by adding air vents)

**Overflow (flooding):**

- Caused by: more water than system can handle (heavy rain) or blockage
- Prevention: design for peak flow; maintain system
- Solution: temporary storage during peak flow (overflow tanks)

## Verification

**Success looks like:**

- Wastewater flows continuously to treatment without backing up
- Treatment facility produces clear, odorless water
- No sewage in streets or around treatment facility
- Soil remains dry (no seeping sewage)
- Downstream water quality is good (no visible pollution, no odor)

**Failure looks like:**

- Sewage backs up in homes or streets
- Treatment facility produces murky or foul-smelling water
- Sewage seeps from pipes (wet spots on surface)
- Downstream water smells foul or has visible sewage
- System frequently overflows during rain

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll build sewers without slope"** — Without slope, sewage doesn't flow; settles in pipes. Minimum slope is 0.3 percent.

2. **"I'll use large pipes to move water faster"** — Large pipes slow water, allowing sediment to settle in the pipes (blockage). Design for proper velocity (0.3 to 0.6 m/s).

3. **"I won't treat sewage; I'll discharge directly to the river"** — Untreated sewage pollutes the river, causes disease downstream. Always treat.

4. **"I'll make treatment settling tanks small to save space"** — Small tanks don't allow settling; solids flow into the river. Size for 2 to 4 hours residence time.

5. **"Constructed wetlands need constant maintenance"** — Well-designed wetlands are self-maintaining. Plant reeds; they handle treatment naturally.

6. **"I'll hide the sewage system underground to avoid odors"** — Odors come from wastewater, not from exposure. Treat sewage properly; hiding it just prevents inspection.

7. **"One sewer main is enough"** — If the main sewer clogs, the entire system backs up. Build parallel sewers in large settlements.

8. **"I'll discharge treated water upstream of where people drink"** — Treated water must go downstream or to isolated land. Intake should be far from discharge.
