## Overview

Every integrated circuit begins as a drawing. Photolithography is the process that transfers that drawing — the layout of transistors, wires, and contacts — onto the wafer, over and over, layer by layer. It is, at its core, the same idea as printing a page: you have a master pattern, and you reproduce it. The difference is scale. Instead of millimeter letters you are printing features measured in micrometers or nanometers, and you are doing it in registration with patterns already on the wafer, to a fraction of the feature size.

Photolithography does not itself remove or add material. It creates a **stencil**. You coat the wafer with a light-sensitive polymer, shine light through a mask carrying your pattern, develop away the exposed (or unexposed) regions, and you are left with a patterned protective coating. The next step — etching (Ch 8) or doping (Ch 6's sibling on junctions) — acts only where the stencil leaves the surface open. Then you strip the stencil and do it again for the next layer.

This single capability is why a chip can hold billions of components. You are not building them one at a time; you are printing the whole pattern at once across the wafer. This chapter teaches the materials, the equipment, the step-by-step flow, and the optical limits that decide how small your circuit can be.

## Why Patterning Is the Bottleneck

A finished chip is built from perhaps 20 to 50 patterned layers, each requiring its own lithography step. The smallest feature you can reliably print sets the size of every transistor, which sets how many fit on a die, which sets cost and performance. For half a century, progress in computing has been, more than anything else, progress in lithography. Shrinking the printable feature is the engine of the whole field.

It is also the hardest step to get right. Patterning demands three things simultaneously:

- **Resolution** — printing features small enough.
- **Registration (overlay)** — aligning each new layer to the ones beneath it, within a fraction of a feature.
- **Repeatability** — doing both across the whole wafer and across thousands of wafers identically.

Miss any one and the circuit fails. This is where the most expensive, most precise machines in human manufacturing live.

## Photoresist: The Light-Sensitive Coating

The heart of the process is **photoresist**, a polymer whose solubility changes when light hits it. Two kinds:

- **Positive resist:** light makes the exposed region *more* soluble. After development, the pattern you exposed is removed, leaving resist everywhere the light did not reach. The mask is a direct picture of what stays. Positive resist gives sharper, finer features and dominates fine-line work.
- **Negative resist:** light makes the exposed region *less* soluble (it cross-links and hardens). After development, the exposed region stays and the rest washes away. Negative resist is tougher and cheaper but historically gives coarser resolution because the polymer can swell during development.

A resist has three parts: a **polymer** that gives mechanical body, a **photoactive compound** that drives the solubility change, and a **solvent** that keeps it liquid for coating. Choose positive for fine geometry, negative where ruggedness and cost matter more than the last micron.

## The Equipment

The lithography bay needs several specialized tools and a controlled environment:

- **A cleanroom under yellow light.** Resist is blind to yellow/red wavelengths but reacts to blue and ultraviolet, so the whole bay is lit yellow to avoid fogging the coating. Airborne particles are the enemy — a single dust speck larger than a feature ruins a die — so the air is filtered to extreme cleanliness.
- **A spin coater:** a vacuum chuck that holds the wafer and spins it thousands of revolutions per minute. A puddle of resist dropped in the center flings out into a thin, uniform film; the spin speed and resist viscosity set the thickness.
- **A hotplate or oven** for the bakes that drive off solvent and harden resist at controlled points.
- **An exposure tool** carrying the mask and the light source — the most critical and expensive machine (see below).
- **A developer station** with the chemical bath (often an alkaline solution) that dissolves the soluble resist.
- **An alignment microscope or automated alignment system** to register each layer to marks left by earlier layers.

## The Mask

The **mask** (or reticle) is the master pattern: a flat quartz plate carrying your circuit layout in an opaque material, usually chromium. Light passes where the chrome is absent and is blocked where it is present. One mask exists per layer; a full chip needs the whole set.

There are two ways to use it:

- **Contact/proximity printing:** the mask sits directly on or just above the wafer, printing the pattern one-to-one. Simple and cheap, but contact damages the mask and proximity gaps blur the image. Resolution is limited.
- **Projection printing (the modern method):** lenses image the mask onto the wafer, often *shrinking* it (a 4× larger mask projected down to 1× on the wafer), so mask defects shrink too and the mask never touches the wafer. A **stepper** exposes one small field, steps the wafer over, exposes the next, and tiles the whole wafer field by field. This is how real chips are made.

## Step-by-Step Procedure

1. **Clean and prime.** Start with a clean wafer (often freshly oxidized from Ch 5). Apply an adhesion promoter (historically HMDS) so resist sticks rather than lifting during development.
2. **Spin-coat resist.** Dispense resist in the center; spin to fling it into a uniform thin film. Higher speed and lower viscosity give thinner films. Uniformity is everything.
3. **Soft bake (pre-bake).** Gently heat to drive off solvent and solidify the film without triggering the photochemistry. Too little bake leaves the resist soft and sticky; too much degrades sensitivity.
4. **Align.** Position the mask so its pattern registers to alignment marks from previous layers, within a small fraction of the feature size. For the first layer there is nothing to align to; later layers are unforgiving.
5. **Expose.** Shine ultraviolet light through the mask for a precisely controlled dose. Too little and the pattern is incompletely developed; too much and features blur or bridge. Dose and focus together define the printed result.
6. **Post-exposure bake** (for many modern resists) to smooth out standing-wave effects and drive the chemical amplification reaction.
7. **Develop.** Immerse or spray with developer. The soluble regions dissolve away, leaving the patterned stencil. Time it precisely — over-development eats into features, under-development leaves residue.
8. **Hard bake.** A final, hotter bake toughens the remaining resist so it survives the etch or implant to come.
9. **Inspect.** Under a microscope, confirm the pattern is sharp, complete, correctly aligned, and free of bridges, gaps, and particles **before** committing to the irreversible etch step.
10. **Etch or dope through the openings,** then **strip the resist** (with solvent or oxygen plasma) and repeat for the next layer.

## A Worked Example

You need to open contact windows in a 500 nm oxide so metal can later touch the silicon.

- The oxide is already grown (Ch 5). Prime and spin-coat a **positive resist** — you want fine, clean openings, and positive resist prints the windows directly from a mask where those windows are clear.
- Soft bake to solidify.
- Align the contact mask to the alignment marks from the doping layer beneath; the windows must land exactly on the doped regions or the contact misses.
- Expose with the right UV dose, post-bake, develop. Now there are resist-free openings exactly where contacts belong; resist protects everywhere else.
- Inspect under the microscope: windows the right size, correctly placed, edges clean.
- Hand off to the etch step (Ch 8): HF or plasma removes oxide only in the open windows, baring silicon for the contacts. Strip the resist. The pattern is now permanent in the oxide.

## Resolution: How Small Can You Print?

The fineness of lithography is set by optics. The smallest resolvable feature scales with the **wavelength of light** divided by the **numerical aperture** of the lens (a measure of how steeply it gathers light). Two consequences follow:

- **Shorter wavelength prints smaller.** This drove the long march from visible light to ultraviolet, to deep ultraviolet, to extreme ultraviolet — each shorter wavelength enabling smaller features. If your only light source is a mercury lamp's UV lines, your minimum feature is on the order of the wavelength, roughly a fraction of a micrometer.
- **Bigger numerical aperture prints smaller,** but at the cost of **depth of focus** — the vertical range over which the image stays sharp shrinks as you push resolution. A wafer that is not flat, or resist that is too thick, falls out of focus and the pattern degrades. This trade-off between resolution and focus latitude governs the whole craft.

Other levers, in rough order of sophistication you might reach: keeping the wafer extremely flat, thinning the resist, and using contrast-enhancing tricks. The headline fact to internalize: **your light source's wavelength and your lens quality set the ultimate size of your circuit.** Improve the optics and everything downstream gets smaller and denser.

## Verification Criteria

- **Pattern fidelity:** under the microscope, printed features match the mask in size and shape, with sharp edges and no rounding of corners beyond tolerance.
- **No bridging or breaks:** lines that should be separate are separate; lines that should be continuous are continuous.
- **Overlay/registration:** the new layer sits on the previous layer within the allowed fraction of a feature; check alignment-mark vernier patterns.
- **Critical dimension (CD):** measure the width of the smallest features; they must fall within spec across the wafer, not just at the center.
- **No particles or resist residue** in the openings; residue blocks the etch and leaves defects.

## Common Mistakes

- **Particles and poor cleanliness.** Lithography is ruined by dust more than anything else. One particle equals one dead die. Cleanroom discipline is not optional.
- **Wrong exposure dose or focus.** Under-exposure leaves residue; over-exposure blurs and bridges features; out-of-focus loses the smallest lines. Dose and focus must be characterized and held.
- **Bad adhesion.** Skipping the primer or under-baking lets resist lift during development, destroying fine features. Prime and bake properly.
- **Coating non-uniformity.** Uneven resist thickness means uneven exposure and development across the wafer. Control spin speed, viscosity, and dispense.
- **Alignment error.** A perfectly printed layer in the wrong place is useless. Registration is as important as resolution; never rush alignment.
- **White light leaks.** Any blue/UV light in the bay fogs the resist before exposure. Keep the bay yellow and shield resist-coated wafers.
- **Skipping inspection before etch.** Lithography is the last reversible step — you can strip and recoat. Once you etch, mistakes are permanent. Always inspect first.

Everything else in fabrication exists to act on the openings that lithography defines. Learn to print clean, aligned, well-resolved patterns, and you can build any circuit your masks describe.
