## Overview

A silicon wafer is a blank slate. To create transistors and circuits, you must selectively remove material.

Etching is the controlled removal of material from a wafer surface. By using a mask (a pattern that protects some areas while exposing others), you can etch complex shapes and patterns.

There are two main approaches:

1. **Wet etching:** Chemical solutions that dissolve specific materials
2. **Dry etching:** Physical or chemical processes in a plasma environment

Both are essential for semiconductor fabrication.

This chapter teaches etching principles, materials, equipment, and techniques.

## Materials & Tools

**For wet etching:**

- Silicon tetrachloride (SiCl₄) or other etchants
- Hydrofluoric acid (HF) solutions
- Nitric acid (HNO₃)
- Etching baths (glass or plastic vessels, chemical-resistant)
- Agitation equipment (air bubbler or stirrer)
- Temperature control (thermostated water bath)

**For dry etching:**

- Plasma chamber (vacuum vessel with electrodes)
- Reactive gas source (fluorine compounds, chlorine compounds, or oxygen)
- Vacuum pump (capable of achieving 0.1–10 torr)
- Radio frequency (RF) power supply (13.56 MHz typical)
- Electrode systems (parallel plate or other geometry)

**For masking:**

- Photoresist (light-sensitive polymer)
- Masks (physical patterns to define etch regions)
- UV light source (for exposing resist)
- Developing solutions (to dissolve exposed or unexposed resist)

## Part 1: Wet Etching

### Wet Etch Chemistry

**Silicon etching:**

Silicon can be etched by various solutions:

**Hydrofluoric acid (HF):**

HF attacks both silicon dioxide (SiO₂) and silicon:
- SiO₂ + 6HF → H₂SiF₆ + 3H₂O (oxide etches rapidly)
- Si + 6HF → H₂SiF₆ + 2H₂ (silicon etches more slowly)

Hydrofluoric acid is selective in the presence of an oxidizer (like HNO₃):
- With HNO₃, silicon oxidizes at surface
- HF then selectively removes the oxide
- Net result: SiO₂ etches, Si etches much slower

**Nitric acid + Hydrofluoric acid mixture:**

Ratio is critical. Typical:
- HNO₃: 70% (by volume or weight)
- HF: 49% (diluted to 5–20% in final mixture)
- Water: balance

This mixture etches silicon rapidly (several micrometers per minute) while attacking oxide preferentially.

### Wet Etch Process

**Procedure:**

1. **Prepare etchant:** Mix acids in glass or plastic container (never metal—acids corrode it)
   - Example: 70% HNO₃ + 20% HF (diluted to 10%) + 10% water
   - Temperature: Room temperature to 50°C (higher temp increases etch rate)

2. **Prepare wafer:** Mount wafer in etching holder (plastic clamp or specialized holder)

3. **Immerse and agitate:** Place wafer in etchant solution
   - Agitate gently (air bubbler or rotate holder) to refresh solution at wafer surface
   - Etch time: Depends on desired depth and etchant concentration

4. **Monitor etch depth:** Remove wafer periodically to check etch progress
   - Optical inspection or mechanical measurement

5. **Stop etching:** Remove wafer from solution when desired depth is reached
   - Immediately rinse with copious deionized water
   - Rinse multiple times (remove all etchant)
   - Dry with nitrogen gas

**Etch rate:** With standard HNO₃/HF mixture, approximately 1–5 micrometers per minute at room temperature.

### Selective Etching with Masks

To create patterns, use a mask to protect some areas while exposing others.

**Masking material:**

Photoresist is a light-sensitive polymer that:
- Hardens (or dissolves) when exposed to UV light
- Can be patterned using a photomask
- Protects underlying material during etching
- Can be removed after etching is complete

**Process:**

1. **Spin photoresist:** Coat wafer with liquid photoresist (approximately 1 micrometer thick)

2. **Align mask:** Place photomask over wafer (photomask has pattern of opaque and transparent regions)

3. **Expose:** Shine UV light through mask
   - Transparent regions expose resist underneath
   - Opaque regions leave resist unexposed

4. **Develop:** Immerse in developer solution
   - Exposed resist dissolves (for positive resist)
   - Unexposed resist remains (forms the mask)

5. **Etch:** Immerse in etchant
   - Etchant attacks exposed silicon
   - Resist protects silicon underneath the mask pattern
   - Etchant cannot reach silicon covered by resist

6. **Strip resist:** Remove hardened resist with solvent (acetone or specialized stripper)

**Result:** Etched pattern in silicon, matching the photomask pattern.

## Part 2: Dry Etching (Plasma Etching)

### Plasma Etching Principles

Dry etching uses ionized gas (plasma) to remove material.

**Plasma:** Partially ionized gas containing electrons, ions, and reactive neutral particles.

**Mechanism:**

1. Electrons are accelerated by electric field
2. Electrons collide with neutral gas atoms, ionizing them
3. Ions are accelerated toward the wafer (cathode)
4. High-energy ions bombard the wafer surface
5. Physical sputtering or chemical reaction removes material
6. Neutral reactive species also participate in chemical etching

**Advantages over wet etching:**

- **Anisotropic:** Can create vertical sidewalls (not rounded, as in wet etching)
- **Precise:** Can etch very small features
- **Selective:** Some plasmas etch one material while leaving others untouched

**Disadvantages:**

- Requires vacuum equipment and power supply
- Can damage wafer surface (ion bombardment causes defects)
- More complex to control

### Plasma Etch Equipment

**Basic setup:**

1. **Vacuum chamber:** Stainless steel or aluminum vessel, capable of reaching 0.1–10 torr

2. **Electrodes:** Parallel plate design typical
   - Lower electrode (cathode): Wafer sits here
   - Upper electrode (anode): Grounded
   - Distance between electrodes: 1–10 cm

3. **RF power supply:** Generates 13.56 MHz radio frequency voltage
   - Power: 50–500 watts
   - Voltage: Accelerates ions toward wafer

4. **Gas inlet:** Supplies reactive gas (fluorine compounds like CF₄, chlorine compounds like Cl₂)

5. **Vacuum pump:** Maintains pressure at desired level

6. **Temperature control:** Heating or cooling of cathode to control etch rate

### Plasma Etch Process

**Procedure:**

1. **Load wafer:** Place wafer on cathode (lower electrode)

2. **Evacuate chamber:** Pump down to desired pressure (typically 0.5 torr for silicon etching)

3. **Introduce gas:** Flow reactive gas (e.g., CF₄ or Cl₂) into chamber
   - Gas flow rate: 10–100 cm³/min (depends on chamber size)

4. **Apply RF power:** Turn on 13.56 MHz power supply
   - Power: 50–200 watts typical
   - Voltage at cathode: 100–500 volts

5. **Monitor etch:** Observe etching process
   - Plasma glows (light emission from excited particles)
   - Etch rate depends on power, pressure, gas flow, and electrode temperature

6. **Stop etching:** Turn off RF power when desired depth is reached
   - Stop gas flow
   - Vent chamber to atmospheric pressure
   - Remove wafer

**Etch rate:** Depends on conditions, but typically 0.1–1 micrometer per minute for silicon.

## Part 3: Materials and Selectivity

### Materials and Etchants

Different materials etch at different rates under the same conditions.

**Wet etching selectivity:**

| Material | HF (dilute) | HF + HNO₃ | Remarks |
|----------|-------------|-----------|---------|
| SiO₂ | Etches rapidly | Etches rapidly | Most useful for oxide removal |
| Si | Minimal | Moderate | Depends on HNO₃ ratio |
| Photoresist | No | No | Used as etch mask |
| Metal | Varies | Varies | Generally resistant |

**Dry etching selectivity:**

In CF₄ plasma:
- Silicon etches rapidly
- SiO₂ etches much slower (can be used as etch mask)
- Photoresist etches (slower than Si, but eventually consumed)

### Achieving Selectivity

**For wet etching:**

Control the etchant composition and temperature:
- Dilute HF: Primarily etches SiO₂, barely touches Si
- HF + HNO₃: Etches both, but ratio controls relative rates

**For dry etching:**

Choose appropriate gas:
- CF₄ plasma: Etches Si faster than SiO₂
- O₂ plasma: Primarily etches organic materials (photoresist)
- Cl₂ plasma: Etches Si, moderate SiO₂ selectivity

## Worked Example: Etching a Trench

**Scenario:** Create a trench (approximately 2 micrometers deep, 1 micrometer wide) in silicon wafer.

**Process using wet etching and photoresist:**

**Step 1 — Prepare wafer:**

1. Start with clean silicon wafer
2. Grow thin oxide layer (approximately 0.5 micrometers) by heating in steam or dry oxygen

**Step 2 — Pattern with photoresist:**

1. Spin photoresist onto wafer (approximately 1 micrometer thick)
2. Place photomask on resist (mask has pattern of 1 micrometer wide lines)
3. Expose to UV light (approximately 365 nm wavelength)
4. Develop in developer solution (removes exposed resist)
5. Result: Thin layer of resist in line pattern

**Step 3 — Etch oxide:**

1. Immerse in dilute HF solution (approximately 1:10 HF:water)
2. Etch time: approximately 30 seconds (oxide is approximately 0.5 micrometers thick)
3. Oxide under the line patterns is removed
4. Silicon underneath is exposed
5. Oxide under resist remains (protects silicon)
6. Result: Oxide-free regions where trench will be cut

**Step 4 — Etch silicon:**

1. Immerse in HNO₃ + HF mixture (approximately 70:10 ratio)
2. Etch time: approximately 20 minutes (to create 2 micrometer deep trench)
3. Etch only attacks exposed silicon (not resist or oxide)
4. Etches to approximately 2 micrometers deep
5. Result: Trench pattern etched into silicon

**Step 5 — Strip resist:**

1. Immerse in acetone or specialized resist stripper
2. Photoresist dissolves
3. Oxide pattern remains
4. Result: Silicon wafer with trenches, oxide mask removed or partially removed

**Final result:** Silicon wafer with precisely etched trenches, pattern defined by photomask.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Using metal containers for acid etching.** Acids corrode metal. Use only glass or plastic containers.

2. **Inadequate rinsing after etching.** Residual acid continues attacking wafer. Rinse thoroughly with deionized water; rinse multiple times.

3. **Etching too long.** Etch rates are difficult to predict; easy to over-etch. Monitor progress by removing wafer periodically.

4. **Inadequate agitation in wet etching.** Without agitation, etchant becomes depleted at wafer surface; etch rate slows. Use air bubbler or rotation.

5. **Resist mask not adequately hardened.** If resist is partially developed or too thin, etchant can undercut it. Ensure resist is fully developed and adequate thickness.

6. **Plasma pressure too high or too low.** Both extremes reduce etch rate and selectivity. Maintain at typical pressure (0.5–2 torr) for best results.

7. **RF power too low.** Insufficient power prevents plasma from igniting or maintaining stable etch. Use 50 watts minimum for typical chamber.

8. **Photomask not in good contact with resist.** If mask is held away from surface, light diffracts around mask edges; patterns become blurry. Press mask against resist.

9. **Using expired or degraded etchant.** HF and HNO₃ can absorb water or degrade over time. Use fresh chemicals; store properly.

10. **Not controlling temperature in wet etching.** Temperature affects etch rate significantly (roughly doubles for every 10°C increase). Control temperature for reproducibility.
