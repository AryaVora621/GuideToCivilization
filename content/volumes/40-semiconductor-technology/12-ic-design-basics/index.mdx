## Overview

An integrated circuit begins not with atoms on silicon, but with ideas on paper.

The IC designer must decide:
- **What should the chip do?** (specification)
- **How should it do it?** (architecture)
- **How will we verify it works?** (testing strategy)
- **Will it fit on the die?** (area estimation)
- **Will it consume acceptable power?** (power budget)
- **How fast must it operate?** (timing requirements)

IC design is the bridge between system requirements and physical silicon. A poor design becomes a $100 million mistake in the fab. A good design becomes a successful product.

This chapter teaches IC design methodology: from specification to verification, the tools designers use, and the decision-making process that leads to manufacturable, functional circuits.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of transistor-level and gate-level circuit design (from Vol 45, Ch 11)
- Knowledge of Boolean logic and digital systems (from Vol 12, Ch 5)

- Ability to think abstractly about large systems
- Mathematical comfort with estimates and approximations

## Materials & Tools

**For IC design:**

- **Hardware Description Language (HDL):** Verilog or VHDL for specifying behavior and structure
- **Simulation tools:** ModelSim, VCS, or open-source simulators for functional verification
- **Synthesis tools:** Logic synthesis converts HDL to gate-level netlist (Synopsys Design Compiler, Cadence, or open-source alternatives)
- **Place & Route:** Layout tools that position cells and route interconnects (Cadence Innovus, Mentor Graphics, or open-source tools like OpenROAD)
- **Static Timing Analysis:** STA tools verify timing closure (PrimeTime, Tempus, or open-source sta)
- **Power Analysis:** Tools estimate dynamic and static power consumption
- **Design Rule Checking (DRC):** Verifies physical design meets foundry rules
- **Schematic entry:** For manual circuit design (analog circuits, custom cells)
- **Process Design Kit (PDK):** Foundry-provided descriptions of transistors, interconnects, and rules

**For planning:**

- Whiteboard or design notebook for sketching architecture
- Spreadsheet for area/power/timing budget estimates
- Version control (git) for design files
- Documentation system for specifications and decisions

## Part 1: IC Design Methodology

### The Design Hierarchy

IC design proceeds through layers of abstraction:

**1. Specification (System Level)**

Define what the chip must do without saying how.

**Example:** "A 32-bit microprocessor that executes 1 billion instructions per second, consumes under 10 watts, fits in a 10 mm × 10 mm die, and operates at 3.3 V."

This is not a design—it's a requirement. The specification allocates resources:

| Resource | Budget | Allocation |
|----------|--------|------------|
| Area | 100 mm² | CPU (60%), Memory (30%), I/O (10%) |
| Power | 10 W | Dynamic (7 W), Static (3 W) |
| Timing | 1 ns | Logic (0.6 ns), Routing (0.4 ns) |
| Frequency | 1 GHz | Clock cycle: 1 ns |

**2. Architecture (System/Microarchitecture)**

Divide the chip into major blocks: CPU, memory controller, I/O interface, power management.

For each block, describe:
- **Inputs and outputs:** What signals enter and exit?
- **Functionality:** What operations does it perform?
- **Timing:** How many clock cycles for each operation?
- **Interface:** How do blocks communicate?

**Example:** ALU block processes arithmetic operations in 1 clock cycle. Inputs: two 32-bit operands, 5-bit operation code. Output: 32-bit result, flags.

**3. Logic Design (Gate Level)**

Describe logic using Boolean equations, truth tables, or HDL.

**Example (HDL):**

```verilog
module alu_32bit(
  input [31:0] a, b,
  input [4:0] operation,
  output [31:0] result,
  output [3:0] flags
);
  always @(*) begin
    case(operation)
      5'b00000: result = a + b;        // ADD
      5'b00001: result = a - b;        // SUB
      5'b00010: result = a & b;        // AND
      5'b00011: result = a | b;        // OR
      // ... more operations
    endcase
  end
endmodule
```

**4. Transistor Design (Cell Level)**

For custom high-performance cells (not synthesized), design transistor-level circuits. Example: custom adder, multiplier, memory compiler output.

**5. Physical Design (Layout)**

Synthesis and place-and-route tools convert the gate-level design into physical layout on silicon.

**6. Verification at Each Level**

- **Logic verification:** Simulate HDL with test vectors; does behavior match specification?
- **Gate-level verification:** Verify synthesized design matches pre-synthesis HDL
- **Timing verification:** Check all paths meet timing constraints
- **Physical verification:** Ensure layout obeys design rules and contains no shorts
- **Manufacturing checks:** Verify design is manufacturable with expected yield

## Part 2: Specification and Planning

### Writing a Specification

A good specification is precise, verifiable, and allocates resources.

**Components:**

1. **Functional requirements:** What does the chip do?
   - Instruction set (if a processor)
   - I/O protocols and pinout
   - Operating modes (normal, low-power, debug)

2. **Performance requirements:**
   - Frequency (clock rate in MHz/GHz)
   - Latency (how long for a computation)
   - Throughput (operations per second)

3. **Power requirements:**
   - Maximum power dissipation (watts)
   - Operating voltage (V)
   - Supply current (A)

4. **Area budget:**
   - Die size (mm²)
   - Allocation by function (CPU, memory, I/O)

5. **Reliability:**
   - Operating temperature range
   - Voltage margins (min-max supply voltage)
   - Expected lifespan or mission duration

### Planning and Estimation

Before detailed design, estimate whether the goals are achievable.

**Area estimation:**

- 1 transistor occupies approximately 0.01–0.1 µm² in modern processes
- 1-bit of SRAM memory: approximately 100–200 transistors
- 1 logic gate: approximately 4–20 transistors
- Routing and interconnect overhead: 50–200% of cell area

**Example:** A 32-bit 4-stage processor pipeline:
- Registers and logic: approximately 50,000 transistors
- Cache (if present): approximately 100,000+ transistors
- Total: approximately 200,000 transistors minimum

In a 28 nm process, 200,000 transistors occupy approximately 0.2–0.5 mm².

**Power estimation:**

- **Dynamic power:** P = C × V² × f × A (capacitance, voltage, frequency, activity factor)
- **Static power:** P = I_leak × V (leakage current, supply voltage)

Example for 1 GHz operation at 1 V with 50% activity:
- C = 10 nF (estimated), V = 1 V, f = 1 GHz, A = 0.5
- P_dynamic = 10e-9 × 1² × 1e9 × 0.5 = 5 W
- P_static = 0.1 mA × 1 V = 0.1 W
- Total ≈ 5.1 W

**Timing estimation:**

- Simple logic: 50–100 ps per stage
- Memory access: 100–500 ps
- Routing delay: 50–200 ps per mm of interconnect

Example: A 32-bit adder path:
- Ripple-carry: approximately 16 stages × 100 ps = 1600 ps (too slow for 1 GHz)
- Optimized carry-lookahead: approximately 200 ps (suitable for 1 GHz)

## Part 3: HDL Design Flow

### Hardware Description Language (HDL)

HDL describes circuits using a language similar to programming, but with concurrency.

**Verilog (most common):**

```verilog
module counter_32bit(
  input clk, reset,
  output [31:0] count
);
  reg [31:0] counter;
  
  always @(posedge clk) begin
    if (reset)
      counter <= 32'b0;
    else
      counter <= counter + 1;
  end
  
  assign count = counter;
endmodule
```

**Key concepts:**

- **always blocks:** Describe sequential logic (triggered on clock edges or events)
- **assign statements:** Describe combinational logic
- **Modules:** Encapsulate circuits; can be instantiated many times
- **Ports:** Inputs and outputs of modules

### Design Flow: HDL to Netlist

1. **Write HDL:** Describe behavior or structure at register-transfer level (RTL)

2. **Simulate (Behavioral Verification):**
   - Write test benches (input stimuli)
   - Run simulation
   - Compare output against expected behavior
   - Debug if behavior is incorrect

3. **Synthesize:**
   - Logic synthesis tool reads HDL
   - Maps Boolean functions to gate library
   - Produces gate-level netlist (list of gates and connections)
   - Optimization passes reduce area, power, timing

4. **Verify Synthesis:**
   - Verify gate-level netlist matches HDL behavior
   - Formal verification or simulation

5. **Place & Route:**
   - Positions logic cells on die
   - Routes interconnect between cells
   - Respects design rules (spacing, width, antenna rules)

6. **Static Timing Analysis:**
   - Calculates path delays (without simulation)
   - Checks all paths meet clock timing constraints
   - Reports timing violations (if any)

7. **Physical Verification:**
   - Design rule check (DRC): Ensures layout follows foundry rules
   - Layout versus schematic (LVS): Verifies layout matches netlist topology
   - Antenna checks: Ensures metal routing won't cause plasma damage during manufacturing

## Part 4: Timing Closure and Constraints

### Understanding Timing

A synchronous circuit operates on a clock. Logic must complete within one clock cycle.

**Setup time:** The time before a clock edge that data must be stable.

**Hold time:** The time after a clock edge that data must remain stable.

**Propagation delay:** The time a signal takes to propagate through logic.

**Example:** 1 GHz clock = 1 ns per cycle.

If a path from one register to another takes 0.8 ns, it can work at 1 GHz (0.8 ns &lt; 1 ns).

If a path takes 1.2 ns, it violates timing (1.2 ns &gt; 1 ns). The circuit fails.

### Timing Constraints

Designers specify timing requirements:

**Clock constraint:**

```verilog
// Clock period: 1 ns (1 GHz)
create_clock -period 1 -name clk [get_ports clk]
```

**Input/output delay constraints:**

```verilog
// Input data arrives 0.1 ns before clock edge
set_input_delay -clock clk 0.1 [get_ports data_in]

// Output must be stable 0.2 ns after clock edge
set_output_delay -clock clk 0.2 [get_ports data_out]
```

**Path exceptions:**

```verilog
// This path is not critical; relax timing
set_false_path -from [get_pins stage1/Q] -to [get_pins stage2/D]
```

### Static Timing Analysis

Tools compute delay for every path in the design:

- **Critical path:** The slowest path that determines maximum frequency
- **Setup slack:** How much margin before timing violation (positive = safe)
- **Hold slack:** Margin to prevent hold violations

If setup slack is negative, timing is violated; the design must be optimized.

### Optimization Techniques

1. **Increase clock period:** Slow clock down (reduce frequency)

2. **Reduce path delay:**
   - Use faster cells (larger transistors = faster, but larger area)
   - Reduce fanout (limit number of gates driven by one output)
   - Shorten paths (restructure logic)
   - Insert pipeline stages (break long paths into multiple cycles)

3. **Reduce capacitive load:**
   - Smaller gates
   - Shorter wires
   - Fewer connections

## Part 5: Power and Area Management

### Dynamic Power Reduction

Power is dissipated when transistors switch (charge/discharge capacitance).

**P_dynamic = C × V² × f × A**

Where A is activity factor (what fraction of transistors switch each cycle).

**Reduction strategies:**

1. **Lower voltage:** 1 V → 0.5 V = 4× power reduction (V²)
2. **Lower frequency:** Reduce clock speed
3. **Reduce switching activity:** Gate clocks to idle blocks (clock gating)
4. **Use efficient logic:** Some circuits consume less power than others

### Static Power (Leakage)

Even idle transistors consume power through leakage current.

**P_static = I_leak × V**

Leakage doubles approximately every 5°C.

**Reduction strategies:**

1. **Use high-Vth transistors:** Higher threshold voltage = less leakage (but slower)
2. **Reduce transistor width:** Narrower transistors leak less
3. **Power gating:** Disconnect power from unused blocks

### Area Optimization

Smaller chips are cheaper and more reliable.

**Optimization:**

1. **Reduce logic:** Simplify Boolean equations; remove redundant logic
2. **Reuse:** Use the same cell multiple times rather than custom designs
3. **Library cell selection:** Choose compact cells from technology library
4. **Memory compilers:** Use optimized memory generators

## Worked Example: Designing a 16-Bit Counter

**Specification:**

- Counts from 0 to 65535 (16-bit)
- Increments on every clock edge
- Asynchronous reset
- Clock frequency: 100 MHz (10 ns period)
- Target area: under 5000 µm²
- Power: under 50 mW

**Step 1 — Architecture:**

A simple 16-bit counter with one output:

| Signal | Direction | Width | Purpose |
|--------|-----------|-------|---------|
| clk | Input | 1 | Clock signal |
| reset_n | Input | 1 | Active-low reset |
| count | Output | 16 | Counter value |

**Step 2 — HDL Design:**

```verilog
module counter_16bit(
  input clk, reset_n,
  output [15:0] count
);
  reg [15:0] counter;
  
  always @(posedge clk or negedge reset_n) begin
    if (!reset_n)
      counter <= 16'b0;
    else
      counter <= counter + 1;
  end
  
  assign count = counter;
endmodule
```

**Step 3 — Timing Analysis:**

- Adder delay: approximately 200 ps (ripple carry)
- Register setup time: approximately 150 ps
- Total path delay: approximately 350 ps
- Clock period: 10 ns
- Setup slack: 10 ns - 0.35 ns = 9.65 ns (margin = excellent)

Design easily meets 100 MHz.

**Step 4 — Power Estimation:**

- Registers: 16 flip-flops, each switches at clock frequency
- Adder: one addition per cycle

Dynamic power:
- 16 flip-flops × 0.5 pF × (1 V)² × 100 MHz × activity = approx 0.8 mW
- Adder: approx 2 mW
- Total: approx 3 mW (well under 50 mW budget)

**Step 5 — Area Estimation:**

- 16 D flip-flops: 16 × 300 µm² = 4800 µm²
- Adder logic: approx 500 µm²
- Total: approx 5300 µm² (slightly over budget, but acceptable for this example)

**Step 6 — Verification:**

Test vectors:

| clk cycles | reset_n | count |
|------------|---------|-------|
| 0 | 0 | 0000 |
| 1 | 1 | 0001 |
| 2 | 1 | 0002 |
| ... | ... | ... |
| 65535 | 1 | FFFF |
| 65536 | 1 | 0000 (wraps) |

Simulate and verify all test cases pass.

## Verification

**Understanding IC design means:**

1. You can write a specification for a digital circuit (functional, performance, area, power)
2. You understand design hierarchy (specification to transistor level)
3. You can write HDL to describe digital circuits
4. You can estimate area, power, and timing before detailed design
5. You understand timing constraints and how to verify timing closure
6. You can identify critical paths and optimize them
7. You recognize tradeoffs: area vs. speed, power vs. performance
8. You understand the role of place-and-route, DRC, and verification

If you can specify a digital system, design it in HDL, estimate its characteristics, and identify how to optimize for area/power/timing, you understand IC design.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Underestimating complexity.** A simple specification grows quickly when detailed. Budget 2–3× the estimated effort.

2. **Ignoring timing constraints until late.** Timing must be planned early. Late fixes are expensive and often impossible.

3. **Assuming synthesis is perfect.** Synthesis can produce inefficient logic. Review synthesized netlist; sometimes hand-tuning helps.

4. **Not budgeting for routing delay.** Metal routing adds significant delay, especially across large chips. Account for it early.

5. **Overoptimizing non-critical paths.** Focus optimization on the critical path. Improving slower paths wastes effort.

6. **Ignoring power from the start.** Late power reduction is difficult. Plan power budgets during architecture phase.

7. **Insufficient test coverage.** Simulation can miss corner cases. Use formal verification for critical paths; use multiple simulation tools.

8. **Not considering manufacturing yield.** Design choices affect yield. Consult foundry design guides; use conservative margins.

9. **Poor documentation.** Undocumented design decisions are lost when team members leave. Document WHY, not just WHAT.

10. **Scope creep.** Adding features during design delays tape-out. Freeze specification early; additions go to next version.
