## Overview

A Czochralski-grown silicon crystal is a rough cylinder with defects, contamination, and irregular surfaces.

A semiconductor wafer is a flat, smooth, mirror-polished disk with no visible defects, atomically clean surface, and precise thickness.

Transforming one into the other is the wafer preparation process—a sequence of precise mechanical and chemical operations.

Proper wafer preparation is essential. Surface defects, contamination, or uneven thickness cause transistors to fail. A single impurity in the top micrometer of silicon can destroy an entire integrated circuit.

This chapter teaches wafer preparation: cutting, grinding, polishing, and cleaning.

## Materials & Tools

**For cutting wafers:**

- Diamond-blade saw (thin blade, 0.5–1 mm thick, rotating at 5000–10,000 RPM)
- Cooling fluid (deionized water or specialized cutting oil)
- Holding fixture (clamps to hold crystal steady during cutting)

**For mechanical processing:**

- Grinding wheels (diamond or carbide, various grits: 100, 50, 25 μm)
- Polishing wheels (soft cloth or felt, for final polishing)
- Polishing compounds (colloidal silica or cerium oxide, 0.1–1 μm particle size)
- Abrasives (silicon carbide or aluminum oxide, progressively finer grits)

**For chemical cleaning:**

- Hydrofluoric acid (HF, for oxide removal)
- Nitric acid (HNO₃, for metal oxide removal)
- Sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄, for organic contamination)
- Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂, for oxidation)
- Hydrochloric acid (HCl, for metallic contamination)
- Deionized water (for rinsing)
- Nitrogen gas (for drying)

**For measurement and inspection:**

- Thickness gauge (micrometer, accurate to 0.01 mm)
- Surface flatness gauge (optical or mechanical)
- Surface inspection microscope (for defect detection)
- Atomic force microscope (AFM, for atomic-scale surface roughness)

## Part 1: Crystal Orientation and Cutting Plan

### Crystal Orientation

Czochralski crystals grow in a specific crystallographic direction (usually &lt;100&gt; or &lt;111&gt;).

**Why orientation matters:**

Different crystal planes have different properties:
- **&lt;100&gt; planes:** Most common for semiconductor processing; good for oxide growth; relatively easy to polish
- **&lt;111&gt; planes:** Smoother surface; used for specialized applications
- **Defect planes:** Some orientations have more defects or higher atomic step density

**Identifying orientation:**

1. Use X-ray diffraction to determine the crystal axis
2. Mark the crystal with the axis direction
3. Plan cutting to produce wafers with desired orientation

### Cutting Strategy

An ingot 20–30 cm long yields 100–200 wafers (depending on thickness).

**Typical wafer thickness:** 0.5–1 mm for standard wafers.

**Saw blade thickness:** 0.5 mm.

**Total material removed per wafer:** 0.5 mm (blade) + 0.75 mm (wafer) = approximately 1.25 mm.

**Efficiency:** Approximately 40–60% of the ingot becomes usable wafers; the rest is lost to kerf (cutting loss) and defective material.

## Part 2: Mechanical Processing

### Step 1: Cut the Ingot into Wafers

**Procedure:**

1. **Secure crystal:** Clamp the Czochralski ingot in a precision holder

2. **Setup saw:** Install diamond-blade saw in a precision cutting machine
   - Blade speed: 5000–10,000 RPM
   - Feed rate: Slow and steady (approximately 1–5 mm/minute)
   - Cooling: Continuous flow of deionized water to cool blade and crystal

3. **Cut wafers:** Feed the crystal into the blade, advancing slowly
   - Blade cuts through approximately 1 mm of material per cut
   - As each wafer is separated, it falls into a collection container
   - Continue until the entire ingot is sliced

4. **Cool wafers:** Allow cut wafers to cool in deionized water before handling

**Result:** Stack of wafers, approximately 0.5–1 mm thick, with rough edges and saw-damaged surface.

### Step 2: Grind Wafer Surfaces

Saw-cutting creates a damaged layer (approximately 10–50 micrometers deep) of fractured silicon.

**Grinding removes this damaged layer:**

1. **Coarse grinding (100 micrometer grit):**
   - Mount wafer on grinding wheel
   - Apply light pressure; let the wheel do the work
   - Grind both surfaces (approximately 30–50 micrometers removed)
   - Check flatness frequently

2. **Medium grinding (50 micrometer grit):**
   - Repeat, removing another 20–30 micrometers
   - Surface becomes smoother

3. **Fine grinding (25 micrometer grit):**
   - Final grinding step, removing remaining saw damage
   - Surface is now visibly smooth (but still not mirror-polished)

**Inspection:** Look for uniform gray color across entire surface (indicates uniform material removal). Watch for blue or dark areas (may indicate cracking or incomplete damage removal).

### Step 3: Polish Wafer Surfaces

Grinding leaves scratches and micro-defects. Polishing removes these and creates a smooth, reflective surface.

**Polishing procedure:**

1. **Mount wafer:** Attach wafer to a polishing head with adhesive (wax or polymer backing)

2. **Setup polishing wheel:** Soft cloth or felt wheel impregnated with polishing compound
   - Wheel speed: 50–200 RPM
   - Wafer pressure: Light (approximately 1–10 PSI)

3. **Apply polishing compound:** Colloidal silica or cerium oxide suspension (1 micrometer particles)

4. **Polish:** Move the wafer across the wheel in a random pattern (prevents directional scratches)
   - Polish time: 10–30 minutes depending on starting surface condition
   - Add more polishing compound as needed (keep surface wet)

5. **Monitor progress:** Wafer surface becomes increasingly reflective. Surface should eventually look like a mirror.

6. **Final polish:** Clean wafer with deionized water; dry with nitrogen gas

**Result:** Smooth, mirror-polished silicon surface with surface roughness under 1 nanometer (atomic scale).

## Part 3: Chemical Cleaning

After mechanical processing, the wafer has:
- Metal contamination (from saw blade, grinding wheel)
- Silicon oxide (forms naturally on exposed silicon)
- Organic contamination (fingerprints, dust)

Chemical cleaning removes all contamination down to pure silicon.

### Step 1: Oxide Removal (RCA SC-1)

This is the first chemical clean, removing organic contamination and loosening metallic particles.

**Solution:**

- Deionized water: 5 parts
- Ammonia solution (28% NH₃): 1 part
- Hydrogen peroxide (30% H₂O₂): 1 part

**Procedure:**

1. Heat solution to 70–80°C
2. Immerse wafer for 5–10 minutes
3. The solution oxidizes organic matter and metallic contaminants
4. Metallic particles are loosened and removed
5. A thin oxide layer forms on silicon surface
6. Remove wafer; rinse immediately with deionized water
7. Dry with nitrogen gas

### Step 2: Metallic Contamination Removal (RCA SC-2)

Remove any remaining metallic ions.

**Solution:**

- Deionized water: 6 parts
- Hydrochloric acid (37% HCl): 1 part
- Hydrogen peroxide (30% H₂O₂): 1 part

**Procedure:**

1. Heat solution to 70–80°C
2. Immerse wafer for 5–10 minutes
3. The solution dissolves metal oxides and complexes metallic ions
4. Remove wafer; rinse with deionized water
5. Dry with nitrogen gas

### Step 3: Final Oxide Removal (Dip in Dilute HF)

Remove the native silicon oxide layer, exposing pure silicon underneath.

**Solution:**

- Deionized water: 100 parts
- Hydrofluoric acid (49% HF): 1 part
- (Final dip concentration: approximately 0.5% HF)

**Procedure:**

1. Dip wafer in HF solution for 30–60 seconds
2. Silicon oxide dissolves immediately: SiO₂ + 2HF → SiF₂ + H₂O
3. Pure silicon is NOT dissolved by dilute HF (HF attacks SiO₂, not Si)
4. Remove wafer quickly
5. Rinse thoroughly with deionized water
6. Dry immediately with nitrogen gas

**Critical point:** If wafer is not dried immediately, atmospheric oxygen re-forms a native oxide layer (which you just removed).

**Result:** Atomically clean silicon surface, free of contamination or oxide.

## Part 4: Quality Inspection and Acceptance

### Surface Inspection

**Visual inspection:**

1. Look at wafer under bright light
2. Surface should be mirror-polished, reflective
3. No visible scratches or defects
4. Uniform gray color across entire surface (indicates uniform material)

**Microscopic inspection:**

1. Use optical microscope (100–1000x magnification)
2. Look for:
   - Scratches (should be &lt;1 micrometer wide, from final polishing)
   - Pits or holes (indicate defective material or incomplete cleaning)
   - Particles (dust or contamination)
   - Discoloration (indicates oxidation or contamination)

**Atomic force microscopy (AFM):**

For critical applications, measure surface roughness atomically:
- Surface should have RMS roughness &lt;1 nm (nanometer)
- Atomic-scale terraces and steps should be visible
- No large-scale waviness or variations

### Thickness Verification

**Measurement:**

Use a precision micrometer (accurate to 0.01 mm):
1. Measure thickness at center of wafer
2. Measure at four points around the edge (90° apart)
3. Calculate flatness: Maximum variation should be &lt;0.05 mm across a standard 100 mm diameter wafer

**Acceptance:**

Standard thickness: 0.5–0.75 mm. Variation under 0.02 mm is acceptable.

### Flatness Verification

**Optical method:**

1. Place wafer on optical flat (extremely flat glass reference)
2. Look at light interference pattern between wafer and flat
3. Flat wafer shows uniform pattern; curved wafer shows distorted pattern

**Mechanical method:**

1. Place wafer on precision V-groove support
2. Use dial gauge to measure height variation
3. Standard flatness: &lt;0.05 mm across entire wafer

## Worked Example: Preparing a 100 mm Wafer

**Starting material:** 20 cm long Czochralski-grown silicon crystal, &lt;100&gt; orientation.

**Timeline:**

**Day 1 — Cutting (1 day):**

1. Cut crystal into 200 wafers (approximately 0.5–0.6 mm thick each)
2. Cutting takes approximately 6–8 hours (slow speed to minimize damage)
3. Sort wafers; discard any with visible cracks

**Day 2–3 — Grinding (1 day):**

1. Grind all 200 wafers with coarse, medium, and fine grits
2. Approximately 50 micrometers of material removed per surface
3. Wafers now appear uniformly gray

**Day 4–5 — Polishing (1 day):**

1. Polish all 200 wafers with colloidal silica
2. Each wafer polishes for approximately 15–20 minutes
3. Wafers now mirror-polished

**Day 6 — Chemical Cleaning (1 day):**

1. RCA SC-1 clean (organic removal)
2. RCA SC-2 clean (metal removal)
3. Final dilute HF dip (oxide removal)
4. Dry immediately
5. All wafers now atomically clean

**Day 7 — Quality Inspection (1 day):**

1. Visual inspection of all wafers
2. Thickness measurement (verify ±0.02 mm tolerance)
3. Flatness check (verify &lt;0.05 mm variation)
4. Microscopic inspection for defects
5. Approximately 95% of wafers pass inspection (5% are rejected due to defects or excessive variations)

**Result:** 190 high-quality wafers ready for doping and transistor fabrication.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Saw blade too fast or too slow.** Too fast causes overheating and crystal cracking. Too slow takes forever. Optimal: 5000–10,000 RPM with steady feed.

2. **Inadequate cooling during cutting.** Friction heats the blade and crystal. Without continuous water cooling, the crystal cracks.

3. **Grinding too aggressively.** Heavy pressure and coarse grit remove material too fast, creating new damage. Use light pressure and let the wheel do the work.

4. **Polishing with inadequate compound.** Without enough polishing compound, the wheel dry-polishes and generates heat, potentially cracking the wafer. Keep the surface wet.

5. **Skipping intermediate grinding grits.** Jumping from 100 μm directly to polishing leaves large scratches. Use all grits in sequence.

6. **Incomplete oxide/metal removal before final HF dip.** If oxides and metals remain, the final HF dip doesn't reach pure silicon. Complete RCA cleaning first.

7. **Allowing wafer to air-dry after HF dip.** Atmospheric oxygen immediately re-oxidizes the clean silicon. Dry with nitrogen immediately.

8. **Using contaminated deionized water.** If water has dissolved ions or particles, it re-contaminates the wafer. Use only high-purity deionized water (resistivity &gt;18 MΩ·cm).

9. **Handling wafer with bare hands.** Skin oils and salts contaminate the surface. Always use clean gloves or tools.

10. **Storing finished wafers improperly.** Store in clean, dry environment. Cover with lens paper to prevent dust. A cleaned wafer left exposed for days accumulates contamination.
