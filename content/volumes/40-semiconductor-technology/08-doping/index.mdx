## Overview

Perfectly pure silicon is almost useless as an electronic material. It conducts a little, but not in a controllable way. The magic of the semiconductor is that you can change its electrical behavior by enormous factors — a million-fold or more — by adding astonishingly tiny amounts of specific impurities. This deliberate, controlled contamination is called **doping**, and it is the step that converts inert crystal into transistors, diodes, and the junctions at the heart of every chip.

The numbers are striking. A useful dopant concentration might be one impurity atom per hundred thousand to one per billion silicon atoms. At those levels silicon's conductivity swings over a vast range, and — crucially — you can make it conduct by two different mechanisms. Add one kind of impurity and current flows by extra electrons. Add another and it flows by the *absence* of electrons, called holes. Put a region of each next to one another and you get a **p-n junction**, the one-way valve and switching element that underlies all of electronics.

This chapter teaches what dopants do, the two main ways to put them in (diffusion and ion implantation), how patterning makes doping selective, and how junctions form. Combined with oxidation (Ch 5) and lithography (Ch 6), doping completes the toolkit for building active devices.

## Donors and Acceptors: n-type and p-type

Silicon sits in column IV of the periodic table; each atom forms four bonds with its neighbors. Doping works by substituting an atom with a different number of outer electrons.

- **Donors (n-type).** Elements from column V — **phosphorus, arsenic, antimony** — have five outer electrons. Four go into bonds; the fifth is loosely held and easily freed to roam as a conduction electron. The dopant "donates" a mobile negative carrier. Silicon doped this way is **n-type** (negative carriers).
- **Acceptors (p-type).** Elements from column III — **boron, aluminum, gallium** — have three outer electrons. They form only three of the four bonds, leaving a vacancy, a "hole," that behaves like a mobile positive charge as neighboring electrons hop into it. The dopant "accepts" an electron, creating a hole. Silicon doped this way is **p-type** (positive carriers).

The key practical point: the **type** depends on which column the dopant comes from, and the **conductivity** depends on how much you add. A heavily doped region (sometimes written n+ or p+) conducts strongly and is used for contacts; a lightly doped region conducts weakly and is used where you need controlled, high-resistance behavior. You choose both type and dose deliberately for every region of the device.

## The p-n Junction

Bring p-type and n-type silicon together in one continuous crystal and something remarkable happens at the boundary. Electrons from the n-side and holes from the p-side meet and cancel near the interface, leaving a thin **depletion region** with no free carriers and a built-in electric field. That field makes the junction a one-way valve:

- **Forward bias** (positive to the p-side) pushes carriers across and current flows easily.
- **Reverse bias** widens the depletion region and current is essentially blocked.

This rectifying behavior is the **diode**. Stack the doping the other way and add a third region and you build a **transistor** — the amplifying, switching device that everything digital is made from. Every transistor on a chip is, at bottom, a carefully arranged set of doped regions and junctions. Doping is how you draw them.

## Selective Doping: Why Masking Matters

You almost never dope a whole wafer uniformly. You dope **specific regions** — the source and drain of a transistor here, a resistor there, a contact region elsewhere — while leaving the rest alone. Selectivity comes from the oxide mask.

Recall from Ch 5 that dopants diffuse far more slowly through silicon dioxide than through silicon. So the recipe is:

1. Grow an oxide over the whole wafer (Ch 5).
2. Use photolithography (Ch 6) and etching (Ch 8 sibling) to open windows in the oxide exactly where you want dopant to enter.
3. Apply the dopant. It penetrates the bare silicon in the windows but is blocked by the oxide everywhere else.

This **oxide masking** is what makes integrated circuits possible: it lets you define different doped regions, side by side, with the precision of lithography. The whole process flow — oxidize, pattern, dope, repeat — is built around it.

## Method 1: Diffusion

The older method is **thermal diffusion.** Expose the bare silicon to a high concentration of dopant at high temperature (roughly 900–1100 °C) and the dopant atoms migrate into the crystal, driven by the concentration gradient — many atoms outside, few inside, so they flow in. The hotter and longer the process, the deeper they go.

Diffusion is usually done in two steps:

- **Predeposition:** a short, hot exposure to a dopant source loads a high, fixed concentration into a shallow surface layer. The source can be a gas, a doped-oxide solid wafer placed nearby, or a spun-on dopant film.
- **Drive-in:** the source is removed and the wafer is heated further (often in an oxidizing ambient that regrows a capping oxide), driving the already-deposited atoms deeper and lowering the surface concentration to the target profile.

Diffusion is simple and needs only a furnace much like the oxidation tube. Its drawbacks: it spreads **sideways** as well as down (dopant creeps under the mask edges, limiting how small features can be), and it gives you limited independent control over dose and depth, since both are tied to temperature and time.

## Method 2: Ion Implantation

The modern, precise method is **ion implantation.** Ionize the dopant, accelerate the ions to high energy in an electric field, and fire them as a beam directly into the wafer. They embed at a depth set by their energy; the dose is set by the beam current and time. Because you control energy and dose independently and measure the beam directly, implantation gives **far more precise, repeatable, and uniform** doping than diffusion, with little sideways spread.

The price is two complications:

- **Crystal damage.** Slamming energetic ions into the lattice knocks silicon atoms out of place, damaging the crystal and leaving the dopant in non-active sites.
- **Activation anneal.** A controlled heat step afterward repairs the lattice and moves the implanted atoms onto proper crystal sites where they actually donate or accept carriers — "activating" them. This anneal also slightly redistributes the dopant, which must be budgeted.

Ion implantation took over for fine geometries because of its precision and its shallow, sharply controlled profiles. Diffusion remains useful for deep or heavily doped regions and where simplicity matters more than precision.

## Step-by-Step Procedure (Implantation Flow)

1. **Start with a masked wafer.** Oxide grown and patterned so that dopant can only reach the silicon through the intended windows. (Photoresist itself can also serve as an implant mask.)
2. **Choose dopant, dose, and energy.** Pick the element for the type you need (e.g., boron for p, phosphorus for n), the dose for the conductivity you want, and the energy for the depth.
3. **Implant.** Expose the wafer to the ion beam, scanning to cover it uniformly, until the target dose is delivered.
4. **Strip the mask** if using resist; remove or keep the oxide as the flow requires.
5. **Anneal to activate.** Heat the wafer to repair lattice damage and place dopants on active sites. Use the lowest temperature and shortest time that activates fully, to limit unwanted diffusion of this and earlier layers (the thermal budget again).
6. **Verify** the result before proceeding (below).

For a diffusion flow, replace steps 2–5 with predeposition and drive-in in a furnace, masked by oxide.

## A Worked Example: Making a Diode

To build a simple p-n diode in an n-type wafer:

- Start with a lightly doped **n-type** wafer (donors throughout).
- Grow an oxide (Ch 5) and pattern a window (Ch 6, Ch 8 etch) where the junction should be.
- Implant or diffuse **boron** (an acceptor) through the window. Where enough boron goes in, the silicon flips to **p-type**; below it, the wafer remains n-type. The boundary between them is your **p-n junction.**
- Anneal to activate the boron and define the junction depth.
- Open contact windows and add metal (Ch 9) to both regions.

Apply voltage and you have a working rectifier: current flows one way, blocks the other. Arrange a more elaborate doping pattern and the same toolkit yields a transistor.

## Verification Criteria

- **Sheet resistance.** Measure with a four-point probe: it tells you how much active dopant you put in. Values must match the target across the wafer, confirming correct dose and activation.
- **Junction depth.** Confirm the doped region reached the intended depth (by angle-lap-and-stain or profiling). Too shallow or too deep changes device behavior.
- **Type check.** A hot-point probe (thermoelectric) or simple diode test confirms a region is the n or p type you intended — catching the catastrophic error of the wrong dopant.
- **Uniformity.** Dose and depth should vary by only a small percentage across the wafer; under 5 percent is a reasonable aim for a controlled process.
- **No residual damage** (for implants): the activation anneal should restore good crystal quality, verifiable by leakage current at junctions being low.

## Common Mistakes

- **Wrong dopant type.** Using a donor where you needed an acceptor inverts the device. Double-check the column of the periodic table before loading the source.
- **Wrong dose.** Too little dopant and the region is too resistive; too much and you lose the controlled behavior or cause excess leakage. Dose is a primary spec, not an afterthought.
- **Blowing the thermal budget.** Every doping heat step also moves dopants placed earlier. Late steps must run cool and short or earlier junctions smear out. Plan the whole thermal sequence, not each step in isolation.
- **Skipping or under-doing the activation anneal** (implant): un-annealed implants leave damaged, electrically dead silicon. The anneal is mandatory, not optional.
- **Relying on diffusion for tiny features.** Diffusion's sideways spread undercuts the mask and limits resolution; for small geometry, implant.
- **Inadequate masking.** A too-thin oxide or resist lets dopant leak through where it should be blocked, doping regions you meant to protect. Size the mask thickness to the dopant and dose.

Doping is where silicon stops being a rock and starts being a device. By placing trace amounts of the right element in the right places at the right concentrations, you draw diodes and transistors into the crystal itself. Combined with the ability to grow insulators and print patterns, controlled doping gives you everything you need to build an integrated circuit.
