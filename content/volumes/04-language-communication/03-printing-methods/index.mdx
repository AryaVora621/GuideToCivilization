## Overview

A scribe writes a book by hand. It takes months. One copy exists. It is precious, guarded, read by few.

A printer sets type once. That type is used to print hundreds of copies in weeks. The books are cheaper and more available. Literacy spreads. Knowledge becomes common.

This chapter teaches:

1. **Why printing matters:** Speed and accessibility
2. **Block printing:** The simplest method
3. **Movable type:** The flexible method
4. **The printing press:** The mechanical amplification
5. **Practical printing:** Setting up and operating a printshop

Printing is how knowledge escapes the control of elites and reaches the common person.

## Prerequisites

- Ability to read and write
- Understanding of basic hand tools (chisel, hammer, file)
- Ability to understand hand-eye coordination and alignment
- Patience for precise, repetitive work
- Some understanding of ink, paper, and surface preparation

## Materials & Tools

**For block printing:**
- Woodblock or linoleum block
- Carving tools (knife, chisels, burins)
- Ink (oil-based or water-based)
- Paper or cloth
- Inking surface (stone, glass, or wooden plate)
- Roller or pad for applying ink

**For movable type printing:**
- Lead or wood type pieces
- Type-setting composing stick
- Printing press (screw press or lever press)
- Ink and paper

**For advanced printing:**
- Metalworking tools (to cast type)
- Presses and mechanical equipment
- Knowledge of chemistry (for inks)

## Part 1: Why Printing Matters

### The Scribe Economy

Before printing, written text was scarce:

1. A scholar or cleric writes a book by hand, copying existing texts
2. It takes months to copy one book
3. Few copies exist (expensive, time-consuming)
4. Only institutions (church, nobility, wealthy scholars) own books
5. Knowledge is concentrated in few hands

**Cost:** A hand-copied book cost as much as a house.

**Access:** Less than 5% of the population could read. Few saw a book.

**Knowledge growth:** Knowledge spread slowly. Each generation re-discovered what the previous one knew.

### The Printing Revolution

Printing changed everything:

1. Set type once
2. Print hundreds or thousands of copies
3. Copies are cheaper (cost drops from house-price to week's wages)
4. More people can afford books
5. Literacy spreads
6. Knowledge is democratized

**Cost:** Printed book costs a fraction of hand-copied book.

**Access:** Within 100 years of printing's invention, literacy doubled. Knowledge escaped the control of church and nobility.

**Knowledge growth:** Scientists and thinkers could read others' work, build on it, and accelerate progress.

Printing is one of history's most impactful technologies because it changed who could access knowledge.

## Part 2: Block Printing

### The Principle

**Block printing** uses a single carved block:

1. Carve an image or text into a wooden or stone block (one surface raised, rest recessed)
2. Apply ink to the raised surface
3. Press paper or cloth against the inked block
4. The raised surface transfers ink to the paper; the recessed areas do not

### How It Works

**Carving:**
- Start with a wooden block (cherry, pear, or similar fine-grained wood)
- Use chisels and burins to carve away the areas that should NOT print
- Leave the text or image raised
- The carving is time-consuming but done only once

**Inking:**
- Use an ink-soaked pad (roller or dauber) to apply ink to the raised surface
- Ink must be consistent and not too wet (runs off) or too dry (does not transfer)

**Printing:**
- Place paper on inked block
- Press down firmly and evenly (by hand or with a press)
- Lift paper carefully
- Repeat with fresh ink for each print

### Advantages and Disadvantages

**Advantages:**
- Simple tools required (chisel, hammer, carving tools)
- Low cost to set up
- Can print hundreds of copies from one block
- Works for images and text

**Disadvantages:**
- Text and images are backwards on the block (mirror image)
- Carving is slow and skilled work
- Text is permanent (cannot change or fix)
- For books with many pages, need many blocks

**Best use:** Single prints (posters, single-page documents, decorative prints, images)

### Practical Block Printing

**Wood selection:** Cherry, pear, and boxwood have fine grain and take detail well. Avoid soft woods (pine) that splinter.

**Block preparation:** Sand smooth. Oil or seal to prevent warping.

**Carving:** Use sharp tools. Carve from center outward. Shallow carving (1–2 mm depth) is sufficient.

**Ink consistency:** Mix to resemble thick syrup. Too thin, it runs; too thick, it is hard to apply.

**Pressure:** Hand-printing works for small areas. Larger areas need a press.

## Part 3: Movable Type Printing

### The Innovation

**Movable type** breaks text into individual letters:

1. Cast individual letter pieces (type)
2. Arrange type in a frame (composing stick)
3. Apply ink and print
4. Disassemble and re-arrange letters for the next page

**Advantage:** Same type pieces are reused. No carving for each page.

### How Movable Type Works

**Type creation:**
- Punch a letter shape into steel (creates a stamp)
- Use the punch to make a mold
- Cast molten lead into the mold
- Trim the resulting type piece (one letter)
- Repeat for all letters, numbers, and punctuation

Each type piece has:
- **Face:** The raised letter (transfers ink)
- **Stem:** The body of the type
- **Feet:** Support the type in the frame

**Setting type:**
- Place a composing stick (a frame) on the work surface
- Select letters from a type case (cabinet with compartments, each holding one letter)
- Arrange letters into lines, right-to-left (mirror image for printing)
- When a line is full, slide it into a galley (a larger frame)
- When a page is complete, place the galley in the printing press

**Printing:**
- Apply ink to the type surface
- Press paper against the inked type
- The raised letters transfer ink to paper

**Resetting:**
- Remove paper
- Take the galley apart
- Return letters to the type case
- Type is ready for the next job

### Advantages and Disadvantages

**Advantages:**
- Text is changeable (rearrange letters for corrections or new text)
- Same type pieces used thousands of times
- Pages can be reprinted indefinitely
- More efficient than block printing for books

**Disadvantages:**
- Type creation is skilled, time-consuming work
- Type pieces must be uniform (spacing and alignment)
- Setting type is slow (one letter at a time)
- Movable type is more expensive than a block

**Best use:** Books, pamphlets, any document where multiple pages or reprints are needed

### Type Case and Organization

A printer uses a **type case**—a cabinet with many compartments, each holding one letter of the alphabet (and multiple copies of frequent letters like "e" and "a").

**Organization:**
- Uppercase letters (capitals) in the upper half
- Lowercase letters in the lower half
- Numbers and punctuation in dedicated areas
- Most-used letters in the most accessible positions (center)

**Efficiency:** A skilled typesetter can set 2,000–3,000 characters per day by hand.

## Part 4: The Printing Press

### The Machine

A **printing press** applies consistent, even pressure, improving print quality and speed.

**Simple screw press:**
- Type is locked in a frame on a platen (flat surface)
- Paper is placed on top
- A screw presses a heavy plate downward
- Pressure transfers ink to paper
- The screw is backed off, paper is removed
- Repeat

**Lever press:**
- Similar to screw press but uses a long lever arm instead of a screw
- Lever provides mechanical advantage (easier to generate pressure)
- Faster than screw press

**Early presses** (Gutenberg's design, ~1440):
- Screw mechanism similar to wine presses
- 1–2 impressions per minute (slow by modern standards, fast for the time)

### Improvements Over Hand Printing

1. **Consistency:** Press applies same pressure everywhere, producing uniform prints
2. **Speed:** Press is faster than hand-pressing
3. **Quality:** More even ink transfer, sharper images
4. **Scalability:** Makes printing commercially viable

### Operating a Press

**Setup:**
1. Set and lock type in the chase (frame)
2. Place chase in the press bed
3. Ink the type using an inking roller
4. Position paper

**Printing:**
1. Roll paper or cloth onto the inked type
2. Engage the press mechanism (pull lever or turn screw)
3. Apply full pressure
4. Release pressure
5. Remove printed sheet
6. Repeat

**Maintenance:**
- Clean type regularly (dried ink reduces sharpness)
- Oil mechanical parts (friction wears them out)
- Check alignment (type can shift)

## Part 5: Inks, Paper, and Finishing

### Inks

**Oil-based ink (best):**
- Durable, rich color
- Good transfer to paper
- Difficult to make (requires chemistry knowledge)

**Water-based ink:**
- Easier to make (mix pigment with water and gum)
- Dries slower
- Less vibrant than oil-based
- Washes off tools easier

**Making basic ink:**
- Lampblack (soot from burning oil) = pigment
- Linseed oil = carrier
- Mix to thick, consistent paste
- Store in sealed container

### Paper

**Hand-made paper (for post-collapse):**
- Beat plant fibers (hemp, cotton, flax) into pulp
- Mix pulp with water
- Dip a fine-mesh screen into the mixture
- Drain water; fibers bond to screen
- Flip screen onto felt cloth
- Press to remove water
- Dry on racks
- (See Vol 21: Textile and Fiber Arts for details)

**Paper requirements:**
- Smooth surface (rough paper does not accept fine detail)
- Strong enough not to tear under pressure
- Not too absorbent (ink stays on surface, not absorbed)

### Finishing

**Trimming:**
- Printed sheets are trimmed to size using a guillotine or knife
- Edges should be square and clean

**Collating:**
- Pages are arranged in order (page 1, page 2, etc.)

**Binding:**
- Pages are sewn together with thread
- Covers are added (leather, boards, or cloth)
- (See Ch 4 for binding details)

## Part 6: Practical Printshop Setup

### Space and Layout

**Composition area:**
- Work tables with type cases
- Composing sticks and galleys
- Good light

**Press area:**
- Printing press
- Ink application surface
- Drying racks for wet prints

**Storage:**
- Type stored safely (protected from damage)
- Paper stored flat (protected from humidity)
- Inks stored sealed (protected from drying)

### Labor Organization

**A small printshop needs:**
1. **Master printer** (oversees operation, handles difficult jobs)
2. **Typesetters** (arrange type)
3. **Pressmen** (operate press)
4. **Apprentices** (learn the trade, perform simple tasks)

**Division of labor improves efficiency:** Each person specializes.

### Production Workflow

1. **Manuscript received** (author's handwritten text)
2. **Typesetting** (typesetters arrange type to match manuscript)
3. **Proofing** (print a sample; check for errors)
4. **Correction** (fix type errors)
5. **Printing run** (print full quantity)
6. **Drying** (printed sheets dry on racks)
7. **Collating** (arrange pages in order)
8. **Binding** (sew and bind)
9. **Delivery** (finished books sent to customer)

Timeline: A 200-page book takes 2–4 weeks from manuscript to finished copies.

## Part 7: Worked Examples

### Example 1: Setting Type for a Paragraph

**Manuscript paragraph:**
"The printing press changed the world by making books available to ordinary people."

**Process:**
1. Count letters: approximately 75 letters + spaces
2. Select type pieces for each letter
3. Arrange left-to-right (remembering final print will be right-to-left because of mirror image)
4. Adjust spacing (tight spacing makes words hard to read; loose spacing wastes space)
5. Lock in composing stick
6. Transfer to galley

**Time:** A skilled typesetter takes 5–10 minutes for this paragraph.

### Example 2: Estimating Cost of Printed Book

**Book: 200 pages**

**Costs:**
- Paper: 200 pages × 2 sheets = 400 sheets. Hand-made paper costs roughly 1 day's labor per 100 sheets = 4 days labor
- Type setting: 200 pages × 75 characters = 15,000 characters. At 2,500 characters per day = 6 days labor
- Printing: 200 pages × 100 copies = 20,000 impressions. At 100 impressions per day = 200 days labor (this is the bottleneck—printing is slow)
- Binding: 1 day per 20 books = 5 days labor for 100 copies
- **Total labor: 4 + 6 + 200 + 5 = 215 days**
- **Cost for 100 copies: 215 days labor / 100 = 2.15 days per book**

**Compared to hand-copying:** 200 pages takes 60–90 days by hand (one book), so 2.15 days per printed copy is 28–42 times faster. This is the efficiency gain.

## Part 8: Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Inconsistent Letter Spacing

Letters crowded together are hard to read. Letters too spread out waste space. Consistent spacing is essential for legibility.

### Mistake 2: Damaged Type

Worn or chipped type produces blurry prints. Inspect type regularly. Replace damaged pieces. Clean type between jobs.

### Mistake 3: Uneven Ink Application

Too much ink produces blotchy, uneven prints. Too little produces faint prints. Practice to develop consistent technique.

### Mistake 4: Loose Type

Type that is not locked firmly shifts under pressure, producing misaligned or doubled prints. Always lock type securely.

### Mistake 5: Wet Paper

Paper that is too damp sticks to type or stretches. Paper should be dry. Store in dry location.
