## Overview

Language is humanity's most important technology for transmitting knowledge. But language is fragile: meanings change, new words appear, old words fall out of use. Words from a parent's generation may be unknown to a child.

A dictionary is a permanent record of language: what words exist, what they mean, how they're used. A dictionary enables:

- **Consistency:** Everyone uses the same word for the same concept
- **Precision:** Technical terms have exact definitions, not vague approximations
- **Preservation:** Future generations inherit the language as it was understood
- **Translation:** Words can be mapped between languages

This chapter teaches how to build a dictionary: collecting words, defining them precisely, organizing for easy reference.

## Part 1: Planning a Dictionary

### Scope: What Words to Include?

A dictionary must decide:

**Comprehensiveness:**

- **Exhaustive dictionary:** Every word in use, including slang, technical terms, obsolete words (example: Oxford English Dictionary, 171,000+ words)
- **Standard dictionary:** Common words used by educated speakers (example: Merriam-Webster's Collegiate Dictionary, 70,000+ words)
- **Concise dictionary:** Essential words only (example: basic dictionary for learning language, 1,000–5,000 words)

For a post-collapse community, start with a **concise dictionary** (2,000–5,000 words) covering:
- Common nouns (objects, animals, plants)
- Common verbs (actions)
- Common adjectives (qualities)
- Technical terms specific to your community (agriculture, medicine, tools)

**Specialized vs. general:**

- **General dictionary:** Words across all topics (the most common approach)
- **Technical dictionary:** Words in a specific field (engineering, medicine, mathematics)
- **Bilingual dictionary:** Words mapped between two languages

Start with general (teach people your language), then add specialized dictionaries later (teach technical language to specialists).

### Dictionary Format

**Entry structure:**

Each entry (one word) typically includes:

1. **Headword:** The word itself, spelled correctly
2. **Part of speech:** Noun, verb, adjective, adverb, etc.
3. **Pronunciation:** How to say the word (optional if written language is phonetic)
4. **Definition:** What the word means, in clear language
5. **Example:** A sentence showing the word in use
6. **Etymology:** Origin of the word (optional, but useful for understanding)
7. **Related words:** Synonyms, antonyms, or words in the same family

**Example entry:**

```
TREE (noun)
Definition: A large plant with a woody stem and branches, typically growing to a height of more than 3 meters.
Example: "We built a shelter under the large tree."
Related words: oak, birch, elm (types of trees); bush (smaller plant); shrub
```

### Alphabetical Organization

Standard order: A, B, C, D, ...

But organize sub-entries consistently:

1. By first letter (A words, B words, etc.)
2. By second letter (AB words, AC words, etc.)
3. By subsequent letters (ABA, ABB, ABC, etc.)

Example ordering:
- TALK
- TALK-DOWN (compound word or variant)
- TALLER
- TALL (if not already listed)

Decide in advance:

- Are compound words separate entries or sub-entries under the main word?
- Are different forms of a word (verb/noun/adjective versions) separate entries or variants of one entry?
- How are hyphens, apostrophes, and special characters handled?

Consistency is crucial.

## Part 2: Collecting Words

### Step 1: Source Identification

Where do words come from?

**Observational:** Spend time listening. Encounter words in conversation and note them.

**Text-based:** Read all available written material (stories, technical manuals, records, laws) and extract unfamiliar or important words.

**Expert consultation:** Ask elders, specialists, and knowledgeable community members about their field-specific vocabulary.

**Field-specific:** For technical dictionaries, spend time with practitioners and document their terminology.

### Step 2: Recording Words

As you encounter words:

1. **Write them down immediately:** Don't rely on memory; you'll forget.

2. **Note context:** Write down how the word was used (the sentence it appeared in). This helps understand its meaning.

3. **Include speaker/source:** Who said this word? When and where? This helps verify the word is legitimate.

4. **Mark priority:** Which words are essential (must include) vs. optional (nice to have)?

**Recording template:**

```
Word: HAMMER
Context: "Use a hammer to drive the nail into the wood."
Speaker/Source: Master carpenter, June 2034
Priority: Essential
Notes: Common tool, multiple types (ball-peen, sledge, claw)
```

### Step 3: Deduplication

As you collect words:

1. **Check if word already exists:** Look through your collection. Are you collecting the same word multiple times?

2. **Merge related words:** "WALK," "WALKS," "WALKING," "WALKED" might be variants of one root word. Decide: Do you list all forms separately or combine under one entry?

3. **Remove duplicates:** If you encounter the same word twice, keep only one entry.

**Decision rule:** For a basic dictionary, list only the base form (e.g., "WALK") and note that other forms exist. For a comprehensive dictionary, list all forms separately (better for people learning the language).

### Step 4: Sourcing Definitions

For each word, find or create a definition.

**Options:**

1. **Consult existing dictionaries:** If you have access to old dictionaries or reference materials, use their definitions as a starting point.

2. **Expert definition:** Ask someone knowledgeable in that domain to define the word.

3. **Synthesize from context:** If you have multiple examples of the word being used, infer the meaning.

4. **Create from first principles:** Understand what the word refers to and write a clear definition.

**Quality check:** The definition should be:
- **Clear:** Written in simple language that someone learning the language can understand
- **Concise:** Not longer than 1–2 sentences
- **Accurate:** Captures the core meaning without unnecessary details
- **Exemplary:** Accompanied by an example showing the word in use

## Part 3: Defining Words Precisely

### Writing Effective Definitions

**Bad definition:** "A tree is a big plant."
- Vague (how big?), imprecise (not all big plants are trees)

**Good definition:** "A tree is a large woody plant with a defined trunk, branches, and foliage, typically growing more than 3 meters tall."
- Specific (woody, tall, structure), distinguishes from shrubs and small plants

### Strategies for Difficult Words

**Abstract concepts (love, justice):**

Define by:
- Core meaning: "Love is a strong affection or attachment."
- Examples: "Parents love their children. Communities love their land."
- Antonym: "The opposite of love is indifference."

**Technical terms (ferrite, photosynthesis):**

Define by:
- Category: "What type of thing is it?"
- Function: "What does it do?"
- Composition: "What is it made of?"

Example: "Ferrite is a ceramic magnetic material composed of iron oxide combined with other metal oxides. It is used in transformers and inductors."

**Verbs (actions):**

Define by:
- What happens: "To grow means to increase in size."
- Who does it: "Plants grow; people grow."
- Example: "A plant grows when its roots absorb water and nutrients."

### Handling Ambiguous Words

Some words have multiple meanings:

**BARK** (noun):
1. The outer covering of a tree
2. The sound a dog makes

**RUN** (verb):
1. To move fast on foot
2. To operate (a machine runs)
3. To manage (run a business)

**Solution:** Create separate entries or clearly mark multiple definitions within one entry.

```
RUN (verb)
1. To move quickly on foot: "She ran to the store."
2. To operate or function: "The machine runs on electricity."
3. To manage or oversee: "He runs the workshop."
```

## Part 4: Organizing and Preserving

### Creating the Final Dictionary

**Layout decisions:**

1. **One entry per page** (easy to read, uses much paper)
2. **Multiple entries per page** (saves paper, more compact)
3. **Card system** (index cards, one word per card, easy to reorganize)

**For durability:**

Use rag paper (lasts centuries), leather binding (withstands use), and clear ink (doesn't fade).

### Indexing

For fast reference:

1. **Main alphabetical index:** All headwords listed in order
2. **Keyword index:** Entries organized by topic (animals, tools, foods) for thematic browsing
3. **Related words index:** Synonyms and antonyms cross-referenced

### Cross-Referencing

Link related words:

```
TREE (noun): A large woody plant with a defined trunk...
  See also: OAK, BIRCH, FOREST, WOOD, PLANT

FOREST (noun): A large area of land covered with trees...
  See also: TREE, WOODS, DENSE
```

This helps people understand relationships between words.

### Multiple Copies

Create multiple copies of the dictionary:

1. **Master copy:** Kept safe, only consulted for verification
2. **Working copies:** Used for reference by the community
3. **Specialist copies:** Copies kept by people whose work depends on specialized vocabulary

## Worked Example: Building a Technical Dictionary for Metalworking

**Scope:** 200 essential metalworking terms

**Sources:**

1. Observe master blacksmith at work (4 weeks)
2. Read existing metalworking documentation (2 weeks)
3. Interview 3 experienced metalworkers (1 week)

**Collection phase (6 weeks):**

- Document 300 unique terms encountered
- Identify 200 essential terms (tools, techniques, materials, qualities)
- Cross-reference with existing terminology to avoid duplication

**Definition phase (8 weeks):**

- For each term: define, provide example, note related words
- Examples:
  - QUENCH: To rapidly cool hot metal in water, oil, or other liquid to harden it. Example: "After heating the steel to red, quench it in water." Related: TEMPER (less severe heating)
  - FORGE: To shape metal by heating and hammering. Example: "The blacksmith forges horseshoes." Related: ANVIL, HAMMER
  - TEMPER: To heat hardened metal to a specific temperature to increase toughness. Example: "Tempering prevents the steel from being too brittle." Related: QUENCH

**Organization phase (2 weeks):**

- Arrange 200 entries alphabetically
- Create cross-references
- Verify definitions with metalworkers

**Documentation (2 weeks):**

- Write cleanly on rag paper
- Bind in leather
- Create 5 copies (one master, four for community use)

**Result:** A 200-entry metalworking dictionary enabling:
- Consistent terminology (everyone uses same words for same techniques)
- Knowledge transmission (apprentices learn from standardized reference)
- Future preservation (technique descriptions outlive the people who invented them)

## Verification

**Understanding dictionary construction means:**

1. You can plan a dictionary (scope, format, organization)
2. You can collect words systematically (observation, documentation, deduplication)
3. You can write clear, concise definitions
4. You can organize words alphabetically with cross-references
5. You can handle ambiguous or technical words
6. You can create durable physical copies
7. You understand why dictionaries matter for a post-collapse community

If you have created a basic dictionary for a domain (100+ entries) and can use it to transmit vocabulary to others, you understand dictionary construction.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Incomplete definitions.** "Apple: a fruit" is too vague. Better: "Apple: a round red, green, or yellow fruit that grows on apple trees. Used for food and cider."

2. **Using undefined words in definitions.** Defining "photosynthesis" using words like "metabolic process" that others don't know. Define using common words only.

3. **Inconsistent format.** Some entries are one sentence, some are three paragraphs. Choose a format and stick to it.

4. **No alphabetical consistency.** If words starting with "ST" are scattered throughout instead of grouped, the dictionary is hard to use.

5. **Including too many obscure words.** A dictionary of 500,000 words is overwhelming. Start with essential words (2,000–5,000) and expand gradually.

6. **No examples.** Definitions without examples are abstract. Examples make meanings concrete.

7. **Failing to cross-reference.** Without connections between related words, the dictionary doesn't show relationships.

8. **One master copy only.** If the single copy is lost, burned, or damaged, the dictionary is gone. Create multiple copies.

9. **Using non-durable materials.** Modern paper deteriorates in 50 years. Use rag paper or parchment (lasts centuries).

10. **Treating the dictionary as static.** Languages evolve. Designate someone to update the dictionary annually, adding new words and refining definitions.
