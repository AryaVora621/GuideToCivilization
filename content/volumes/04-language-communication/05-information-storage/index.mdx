## Overview

A person dies. They take their knowledge with them.

A person writes something down. The knowledge survives them.

But what's written can be destroyed: fire consumes paper, water rots leather, insects eat books, mold spreads on parchment. To preserve information truly, it must be stored in ways that resist destruction.

This chapter teaches information storage techniques—how to write, bind, and preserve documents so they survive centuries and reach future generations.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of writing systems (from Vol 4, Ch 1)
- Experience keeping records (from Vol 2, Ch 3)
- Knowledge of materials (from Vol 10, Ch 2)
- Ability to think about long-term preservation (decades to centuries)
- Practical skills: writing, sewing, basic chemistry

## Materials & Tools

**For creating records:**

- Writing surfaces (parchment, rag paper, or bark paper)
- Inks (permanent, fade-resistant)
- Writing instruments (quill, brush, or charcoal stick)
- Binders (leather, cloth, or string)
- Seals (wax or clay, for securing documents)

**For preservation:**

- Dry storage space (low humidity, stable temperature)
- Pest-resistant containers (sealed boxes, ceramic vessels)
- Climate control (even if just a cool, dark place)
- Moisture control (lime powder, charcoal, or silica to absorb moisture)

**For handling and restoration:**

- Clean work surface (for handling fragile documents)
- Archival tools (bone folder for smoothing, soft brush for cleaning)
- Repair materials (wheat paste, Japanese tissue for mending)

## Part 1: Understanding Information Decay

### How Information Dies

**Biological decay:**

- **Mold and fungi:** Attack paper, parchment, and leather in humid environments
- **Insects:** Silverfish, beetles, and termites eat paper, leather, and glue
- **Rodents:** Destroy documents by chewing and nesting
- **Bacterial growth:** Causes stains and structural degradation

**Chemical decay:**

- **Acid hydrolysis:** Paper made from wood pulp contains acid that breaks down fibers over decades (50–100 years)
- **Oxidation:** Inks fade when exposed to oxygen (reason old documents look faded)
- **UV damage:** Sunlight fades inks and weakens paper

**Physical damage:**

- **Fire:** Total loss in minutes
- **Water:** Causes swelling, warping, mold growth
- **Mechanical damage:** Bending, folding, rubbing causes tears and loss of text

### Factors Affecting Lifespan

**Without preservation, typical lifespans:**

- Modern paper (wood pulp): 50–100 years
- Newspaper: 10–50 years
- Regular ink on modern paper: 50–100 years
- Leather binding: 100–200 years (if kept dry)

**With preservation, extended lifespans:**

- Rag paper with permanent ink: 500+ years
- Parchment with iron-gall ink: 1000+ years
- Stone carving: 10,000+ years (essentially permanent)

## Part 2: Selecting Durable Materials

### Writing Surfaces

**Rag paper:**

- Made from linen or cotton fibers, NOT wood pulp
- Lifespan: 500+ years (tested; some ancient examples survive 600+ years)
- Cost: More expensive than modern paper
- How to identify: Doesn't break easily when folded; watercolor paper or fine writing paper often is rag-based
- In post-collapse: Must make from cloth scraps (see Vol 10, Papermaking)

**Parchment:**

- Made from animal skin (usually calfskin), specially prepared
- Lifespan: 1000+ years (documented medieval manuscripts survive)
- Cost: Expensive; requires animal resources
- How to verify: Can be written on both sides; doesn't fray at edges; feels smooth but slightly waxy
- Advantage: Extremely durable; can be scrubbed and reused (palimpsest)

**Vellum:**

- Higher-quality parchment made from very young calfskin
- Lifespan: 1000+ years
- Cost: Very expensive; requires careful animal selection
- Properties: Finer, more uniform than regular parchment

**Bark paper:**

- Made from inner bark of trees (used historically in Polynesia and Mesoamerica)
- Lifespan: 200–300 years
- Cost: Moderate (if materials are available)
- Properties: Rough texture, but durable if well-made

**Stone:**

- Carved or inscribed stone
- Lifespan: Essentially permanent (10,000+ years)
- Cost: Very high; slow to produce
- Best for: Most important records (laws, genealogies, founding principles)

### Inks

**Iron-gall ink:**

- Standard permanent ink in pre-industrial Europe
- Composition: Iron sulfate (or iron oxide) + tannic acid (from oak galls) + gum arabic
- Permanence: 500+ years (tested; medieval manuscripts still legible)
- Color evolution: Starts brown/red, turns black over months as the chemical reaction continues
- Preparation: Requires oak galls (collect in autumn) and iron sulfate (see below)

**Charcoal ink:**

- Finely ground charcoal suspended in gum arabic
- Permanence: 500+ years (permanent black color)
- Advantage: Simpler to make than iron-gall ink
- Disadvantage: Can smudge if not sealed; may be erased with water
- Best for: Non-critical documents or when iron-gall ink is unavailable

**Walnut ink:**

- Made from walnut husks
- Permanence: 300–400 years
- Advantage: Simple to make; brown-black color
- Disadvantage: Less permanent than iron-gall

**Avoid:**

- Modern ballpoint pen ink: Unstable; fades in 50–100 years
- Pencil: Smudges and can be erased
- Vegetable-based inks without fixative: Fade quickly

## Part 3: Creating Permanent Records

### Step 1: Prepare Writing Materials

**Making rag paper (see Vol 10: Papermaking):**

1. Collect linen or cotton scraps (old cloth, worn-out textiles)
2. Bleach or soak to remove dyes
3. Chop into small pieces
4. Boil and macerate to break down fibers
5. Suspend in water and form sheets
6. Press and dry

Result: Durable paper suitable for 500+ year preservation.

**Preparing parchment (if available):**

1. Obtain animal skin (calf, goat, or sheep)
2. Soak in lime water to remove hair
3. Stretch on frame and scrape clean
4. Dry under tension
5. Smooth with pumice stone

Result: Writing surface suitable for 1000+ year preservation.

### Step 2: Prepare Ink

**Making iron-gall ink:**

1. **Gather materials:**
   - Oak galls (approximately 50g; collect in autumn)
   - Ferrous sulfate/iron vitriol (approximately 15g; can be made by rusting iron in vinegar)
   - Gum arabic (approximately 5g; resin from acacia trees)
   - Water (approximately 500ml)

2. **Prepare gall extract:**
   - Crush oak galls into powder
   - Soak in water for 24 hours
   - Strain through cloth
   - Collect the dark liquid

3. **Combine:**
   - Mix gall extract with ferrous sulfate solution
   - Add gum arabic (acts as binder)
   - Stir well

4. **Age (optional but recommended):**
   - Let mixture sit for 1–2 weeks
   - The reaction continues; color darkens from brown to black

Result: Permanent ink suitable for rag paper or parchment.

### Step 3: Write and Seal

1. **Use proper technique:**
   - Write neatly with a quill or brush
   - Allow ink to dry completely (takes 1–2 hours)
   - Don't touch the ink while drying (prevents smudging)

2. **Fixative (optional but recommended):**
   - For charcoal ink only (iron-gall ink doesn't need it)
   - Spray or brush a thin layer of gum arabic solution over the text
   - This prevents smudging

3. **Initial sealing:**
   - Once ink is completely dry, apply wax seal or clay seal
   - This protects from accidental damage during storage

## Part 4: Binding and Storage

### Binding Techniques

**Simple string binding (for pamphlets):**

1. Stack pages in order
2. Punch two or three holes along the spine
3. Thread string or twine through holes
4. Tie tightly on the outside
5. Seal knot with wax (prevents untying)

Suitable for: Documents up to 20 pages.

**Sewn binding with leather spine (for books):**

1. Stack pages in signatures (folded groups)
2. Sew signatures together along the spine with strong thread
3. Wrap leather around the spine
4. Paste leather to the first and last pages
5. Add wooden cover boards (optional, for rigidity)

Suitable for: Permanent books, 50+ pages.

**Leather case binding (for valuable documents):**

1. Prepare pages and spine
2. Create a leather case (custom-fitted box)
3. Paste pages inside the case
4. Add latch or closure to keep it shut

Suitable for: Most important documents; provides maximum protection.

### Storage Environment

**Climate:**

- Temperature: Stable, cool (10–15°C ideal; avoid fluctuations)
- Humidity: Low (30–50%; use desiccants if needed)
- Avoid heat, moisture, and direct sunlight

**Containers:**

- Acid-free boxes or containers
- Wooden chests lined with paper (not plastic)
- Sealed ceramic vessels (for very long-term storage)

**Organization:**

- Store documents flat (not standing upright)
- Separate heavy documents with protective paper
- Label containers clearly with contents and date
- Keep an index of what's stored where

**Pest prevention:**

- Store in a sealed, clean space
- No food or organic material nearby (prevents insects)
- Ventilation to prevent mold (not hermetically sealed at room temperature)
- Check quarterly for signs of infestation

### Handling and Access

**Rules for handling:**

1. Clean hands before touching
2. No eating or drinking near documents
3. Turn pages carefully from the top edge
4. Support the spine when opening books
5. Never force pages open (let them unfold naturally)

**Access control:**

- Only trained handlers access original documents
- Create copies for regular use (preserve original)
- Document every access (who, when, why)
- Limit handling to essential scholars

## Worked Example: Archiving Community Records

**Scenario:** A village wants to preserve critical records (laws, genealogies, agricultural knowledge) for 500+ years.

**Selection:**

- Village charter (founding document)
- Laws and legal precedents
- Master genealogy (family trees)
- Agricultural practices (best methods discovered)
- Medical knowledge (effective treatments)
- Technical records (blacksmithing, carpentry)

Total: Approximately 200 pages.

**Materials:**

- Rag paper (make from cloth scraps over 2 weeks)
- Iron-gall ink (prepare; oak galls are available)
- Leather binding (prepare from tanned hide)
- Wooden case (carved and fitted)

**Preparation (2 months):**

1. Copy all records by hand in neat script (trained scribe does this)
2. Use rag paper and iron-gall ink
3. Allow ink to dry completely
4. Bind 50 pages per book (4 books total)
5. Sew with strong thread through leather spine
6. Add wooden covers

**Sealing (1 month):**

1. Place each book in leather case
2. Seal with wax, stamping with village symbol
3. Document contents on outside

**Storage (permanent):**

1. Build stone vault (fire-resistant, waterproof)
2. Store books flat, separated by paper
3. Include desiccant (lime powder, changed annually)
4. Seal vault, but allow for quarterly inspection
5. Train 3 backup keepers (in case primary keeper dies)
6. Keep master index of contents

**Projected preservation:**

- Original documents: 500+ years (rag paper + iron-gall ink)
- Binding: 200+ years (well-made leather)
- Storage conditions: Protect from fire, water, pests
- Renewal: At 300 years, copy records to fresh materials (refresh the "500-year clock")

Result: Community knowledge survives centuries and is reliably accessible to future generations.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Using modern paper for important documents.** Wood pulp paper deteriorates in 50–100 years. Use rag paper for anything meant to last over 200 years.

2. **Using fading inks.** Ballpoint ink and pencil are unsuitable. Use iron-gall or charcoal ink only.

3. **Inadequate binding.** Weak binding falls apart within years. Sew properly with strong thread and quality leather.

4. **Poor storage conditions.** Damp, warm storage promotes mold. Keep documents cool, dry, and dark.

5. **Overcrowding storage.** Stacking documents too tightly damages them and prevents air circulation. Store with space between items.

6. **No backup copies.** Keep at least two copies of critical documents in different locations.

7. **Poor organization.** An unindexed archive is useless. Document what's stored and where.

8. **Allowing untrained access.** Handling documents carelessly causes damage. Restrict access to trained keepers.

9. **No renewal plan.** Even durable materials degrade eventually. Plan to recopy documents every 300–500 years.

10. **Treating storage as one-time task.** Maintenance is continuous. Check storage quarterly for problems (pests, moisture, degradation).
