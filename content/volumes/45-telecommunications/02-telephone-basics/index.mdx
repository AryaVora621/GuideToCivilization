## Overview

The telegraph transmits coded messages. The telephone transmits voice directly—human conversation across distance.

A telephone system has three essential parts:

1. **Microphone:** Converts sound waves (voice) into varying electrical current
2. **Transmission line:** Carries the electrical signal across distance
3. **Speaker:** Converts electrical current back into sound waves

The revolution of the telephone is simplicity: just speak, and the other person hears you. No coding, no translation.

This chapter teaches how to build a functioning telephone system and understand the physics underlying voice transmission.

## Part 1: How Telephone Works

### From Voice to Electrical Current

**Sound waves:**

Voice is sound—pressure variations in air traveling at approximately 340 meters per second. A sound wave has:

- **Frequency:** How many cycles per second (Hz). Voice ranges from approximately 80 Hz (deep male voice) to 3000 Hz (high female voice). Most speech information is between 300–3000 Hz.
- **Amplitude:** How strong the pressure variation (loudness). Quiet speech is 60 dB, normal speech is 70 dB, loud speech is 80 dB.

**Microphone function:**

A microphone responds to sound pressure and converts it to varying electrical current:

- **High pressure (loud sound):** Higher current
- **Low pressure (quiet sound):** Lower current
- **Frequency of variation:** Electrical current changes at the same frequency as the sound wave

**Key principle:** The electrical signal is an analog of the sound wave—it "looks like" the sound wave if displayed on an oscilloscope.

### From Electrical Current to Voice

**Speaker function:**

A speaker is essentially a reversed microphone. It responds to varying electrical current and converts it back to sound:

- **High current:** Stronger cone movement, higher pressure
- **Low current:** Weaker cone movement, lower pressure
- **Current frequency:** Cone vibrates at the same frequency as current variation

**Key principle:** If the electrical signal represents the sound wave, the speaker reproduces the sound wave.

### Transmission System

**Simple telephone circuit:**

```
[Microphone] → [Transmission Line] → [Speaker]
     ↑                                    ↓
[Battery] ← ← ← ← ← ← ← ← ← ← ← ← [Ground Return]
```

Current flows:
1. Battery → Microphone → Transmission line → Speaker → Ground return → Battery

When you speak, the microphone varies the current in this loop. The speaker responds to current variations. The listener hears your voice.

## Part 2: Building Telephone Components

### Step 1: Build a Carbon Microphone

A carbon microphone is the simplest practical microphone. It uses the resistance of carbon granules to convert sound into varying current.

**Materials:**

- Carbon powder or carbon granules (0.5–2 mm diameter, approximately 1–2 grams)
- Thin metal diaphragm (aluminum or steel foil, 0.1 mm thick, 3–5 cm diameter)
- Metal cup or container (brass or aluminum, approximately 3–5 cm diameter, 1 cm deep)
- Spring (light spring or springy metal strip to hold diaphragm against granules)
- Metal rod (2–3 mm diameter, 2–4 cm long, for electrical contact)

**Construction:**

1. Fill container with carbon granules, leaving 5 mm of space at the top

2. Place metal rod into granules (rod acts as electrical contact point)

3. Mount diaphragm on top of container (diaphragm should be approximately 1–2 mm above granules)

4. Install spring to push diaphragm down gently against granules (spring tension: light, enough to maintain contact)

5. Wire connections:
   - One wire: From container (body) to external circuit
   - Second wire: From rod in granules to external circuit

6. Test: When you speak, the diaphragm vibrates, pressing and releasing against granules. This changes resistance, varying current.

**Properties:**

- Sensitivity: High (responds well to faint sounds)
- Frequency response: 300–3000 Hz (adequate for speech)
- Impedance: 100–500 ohms (varies with granule packing)
- Bias current required: 20–100 mA (DC current through granules) for operation

### Step 2: Build an Electromagnetic Speaker

A speaker converts electrical current into sound by moving a cone.

**Materials:**

- Permanent magnet (ceramic or neodymium, 2–5 cm diameter)
- Coil of wire (1000–5000 turns of 32–36 gauge wire, wound on paper or plastic form)
- Paper or plastic cone (diameter 5–10 cm, tapering to small diameter at top)
- Metal pole piece (if permanent magnet doesn't have built-in pole pieces)
- Suspension system (flexible rubber or cloth ring to support cone edges)
- Backing enclosure (cardboard or wood box)

**Construction:**

1. Mount permanent magnet inside backing enclosure

2. Mount coil on a form (coil floats in the magnetic field, attached to cone)

3. Attach cone to coil (cone moves with coil)

4. Install suspension around cone edges (holds cone centered while allowing free movement)

5. Wire connections: From coil to external circuit

6. Test: When AC current flows through coil, magnetic field from coil interacts with permanent magnet field, creating force that moves coil and cone. Cone moves back and forth, creating sound.

**Properties:**

- Sensitivity: Depends on magnet strength and coil design (typically 85–95 dB SPL at 1 watt input)
- Frequency response: 100–10,000 Hz (adequate for telephone)
- Impedance: 4–16 ohms (typical speaker impedance)
- Power handling: 0.5–5 watts (depending on design)

### Step 3: Select Transmission Line

The transmission line carries voice signals between telephone stations.

**Cable options:**

- **Two-wire cable:** Two insulated copper conductors in a twisted pair (simplest, works up to approximately 10 km)
- **Four-wire cable:** Two pairs (one for each direction) separated by transformer (better for longer distances)
- **Coaxial cable:** Central conductor surrounded by shield (minimizes electromagnetic interference, used for longer runs or high-frequency signals)

**For post-collapse systems, start with two-wire twisted pair:**

- Gauge: 18–22 AWG (thicker = longer distances without signal loss)
- Insulation: Rubber or plastic, rated for outdoor exposure
- Twist rate: 2–5 twists per inch (reduces electromagnetic interference)
- Distance: Up to 10 km for practical voice communication

### Step 4: Impedance Matching

Impedance matching optimizes power transfer between microphone, transmission line, and speaker.

**Carbon microphone:** Approximately 200–500 ohms
**Transmission line:** Characteristic impedance approximately 600 ohms (standard telephone line impedance)
**Speaker:** Approximately 4–16 ohms

**To match impedances:**

Use transformers (step-up and step-down transformers) to convert between impedances:

- **Microphone transformer:** 500 ohms to 600 ohms (step-up transformer, approximately 1:1 ratio)
- **Speaker transformer:** 600 ohms to 8 ohms (step-down transformer, approximately 1:0.1 ratio)

Proper impedance matching maximizes power transfer and signal strength.

## Part 3: Building a Complete Telephone System

### Simple Two-Station Telephone

**Setup:**

Two telephone stations connected by a transmission line (copper wire).

**Station A (transmitting):**

```
[Microphone] → [Transformer] → [Transmission Line] → [Station B]
    ↑                                ↓
[Battery 6V] ← ← ← ← ← ← ← ← [Return Wire]
```

**Station B (receiving):**

```
[Transmission Line] → [Transformer] → [Speaker]
       ↓
[Battery 6V] → ← ← ← ← [Return Wire]
```

**Procedure:**

1. Connect microphone at Station A to transformer (output impedance matching)
2. Connect transformer to transmission line
3. Transmission line carries signal to Station B
4. At Station B, transmission line connects to second transformer (impedance matching for speaker)
5. Speaker at Station B is connected to transformer output
6. Both stations have batteries providing bias current
7. Ground rods at each station complete the return path

**Results:**

When you speak into the microphone at Station A, the speaker at Station B reproduces your voice.

### Two-Way Telephone (Simultaneous Conversation)

To allow both people to talk at the same time, use four-wire system with transformer:

**Configuration:**

```
Station A Microphone → [Transformer] → [Transmission Line A→B] → [Transformer] → Station B Speaker
Station B Microphone → [Transformer] → [Transmission Line B→A] → [Transformer] → Station A Speaker
```

Two separate transmission lines (or two pairs of wires in one cable), one for each direction.

**Advantages:**

- Full-duplex communication (both can talk simultaneously)
- No waiting for turn to speak
- More natural conversation

### Amplification (For Longer Distances)

As transmission line length increases, signal weakens due to resistance. Amplification boosts the signal.

**Using vacuum tube amplifier (from Vol 41, Ch 3):**

1. Microphone signal (approximately 0.1 volts) is too weak for long-distance transmission
2. Feed signal to vacuum tube amplifier (grid input)
3. Amplified output (approximately 10–100 volts) is strong enough to drive transmission line
4. At receiving end, weak signal from line is amplified again before speaker
5. Result: Clear voice communication over 100+ km

## Part 4: Operating and Troubleshooting

### Operating Procedure

1. **Establish connection:** Both stations must have phones connected and ready

2. **Ring signal:** Station A sends a ringing signal (AC current pulse) to alert Station B operator

3. **Pickup:** Station B operator answers (picks up handset)

4. **Conversation:** Both speak into microphones; both hear through speakers

5. **Hang up:** Either station hangs up (disconnects), ending the call

### Common Problems and Solutions

**No sound:**

- Check battery voltage (should be 6–12 volts depending on microphone type)
- Check wire connections (loose or corroded connections kill signal)
- Test microphone and speaker separately with known working battery
- Check transmission line for breaks (use continuity tester)

**Weak sound:**

- Increase battery voltage (up to maximum rating)
- Check microphone for dirt or damage (clean or replace)
- Check speaker for damage (torn cone, rusted magnet)
- Add amplifier if transmission line is long

**Noise or distortion:**

- Reduce microphone gain if too high
- Move transmission line away from electrical cables (reduce interference)
- Use shielded cable if available
- Check for battery noise (try different battery)
- Check for ground loops (ensure only one ground connection between stations)

**Feedback (howling sound):**

- Reduce microphone gain
- Move microphone away from speaker
- Use four-wire system instead of two-wire

## Worked Example: Setting Up a Telephone Line

**Scenario:** Connect two villages 15 km apart by telephone.

**Planning:**

- Village A to Village B: 15 km of overhead transmission line on poles
- Expected signal loss: approximately 30 dB over 15 km (depends on wire gauge)
- Solution: Install amplifier at midpoint (approximately 7.5 km) to boost signal

**Station A (Transmitting):**

- Carbon microphone (500 ohm impedance)
- 6V battery (for microphone bias)
- Microphone transformer (500 ohm to 600 ohm)
- Transmission line to Station B

**Midpoint Amplifier:**

- Incoming signal from Station A (weak, approximately 0.01 volts)
- Vacuum tube amplifier (vacuum triode, 6.3V filament, 200V plate)
- Amplified output (approximately 10 volts) sent to Station B
- Also receives signal from Station B, amplifies back to Station A

**Station B (Receiving):**

- Speaker transformer (600 ohm to 8 ohm)
- Dynamic speaker (8 ohm)
- Return path through ground rods at each end

**Construction timeline:**

- Week 1: Poles installed, transmission line strung
- Week 2: Stations built, amplifier installed and tested
- Week 3: Full system test over the 15 km distance

**Testing:**

1. Short distance (1 km): Verify both stations work
2. Medium distance (5 km): Verify signal quality
3. Full distance (15 km): Verify amplifier boosts signal adequately
4. Two-way test: Both operators speak simultaneously

**Result:** Working telephone line connecting two villages, allowing real-time voice communication

## Common Mistakes

1. **Carbon granules contaminated or dampened.** Moisture changes resistance unpredictably. Keep carbon dry and clean.

2. **Microphone diaphragm not tensioned correctly.** Too loose and no signal; too tight and diaphragm distorts. Tension should be light—just enough to maintain contact.

3. **Speaker magnet not strong enough.** Weak magnet produces quiet sound. Use strong permanent magnets (ceramic or neodymium).

4. **Transmission line too thin.** High resistance causes signal loss. Use at least 18 AWG for distances above 5 km.

5. **Impedance mismatch causing signal loss.** Without impedance matching transformers, signal transfer is inefficient. Use proper transformers.

6. **Battery voltage too low.** Carbon microphone needs 20–100 mA bias current. At low voltage, current is insufficient. Use 6–12 volts.

7. **Loose connections in circuit.** Corrosion or loose solder joints break circuit. Solder carefully and check all connections.

8. **No ground return path.** Telephone circuit must be a complete loop. Ensure good ground connections at both ends.

9. **Transmission line picking up noise.** Unshielded line picks up interference from nearby electrical equipment. Run line away from power lines or use shielded cable.

10. **Attempting three-way calling without proper equipment.** Three-way conference requires special bridge circuit. Simple two-wire system can't support three stations. Use proper multi-station switchboard.
