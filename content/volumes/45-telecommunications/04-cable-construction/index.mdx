## Overview

A signal travels through a wire from sender to receiver. But not all wires work equally. A bare copper wire loses signal to the environment. A properly constructed cable—with conductor, insulation, and shielding—preserves the signal and transmits it reliably over distance.

This chapter teaches:

1. **Cable components:** Conductor, insulation, shielding, and their functions
2. **Cable types:** Single wire, twisted pair, coaxial, and their uses
3. **Material selection:** Why copper, why specific insulations
4. **Cable construction:** How to build cables by hand
5. **Testing and quality:** How to verify a cable works

Cables are the invisible infrastructure of communication. Good cables last decades. Bad cables fail in weeks.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of electrical current and conductivity
- Basic knowledge of material properties (flexibility, strength, conductivity)
- Ability to work with hand tools (knife, pliers, crimper)
- Patience for detailed, repetitive work

## Materials & Tools

**For cable construction:**
- Copper wire (appropriate gauge for application)
- Insulation material (rubber, cloth, plastic, or natural alternatives)
- Shielding material (copper foil, braid, or multiple-layer wrapping)

- Connectors (appropriate for application)

**For testing:**
- Multimeter (to test continuity and resistance)
- Light source (to test signal transmission)

## Part 1: Cable Components

### The Conductor

**Function:** Carries the electrical signal.

**Material:** Copper is ideal because:
- High conductivity (electrons move easily)
- Ductile (can be drawn into fine wire)
- Available and relatively cheap
- Resistant to corrosion (with proper handling)

**Alternatives:** Aluminum (lighter, cheaper but less conductive), gold-plated copper (better contact, more expensive).

**Gauge (diameter):**
- Thicker wire (lower gauge number, e.g., 2 AWG) = lower resistance, more current capacity
- Thinner wire (higher gauge number, e.g., 22 AWG) = higher resistance, less current capacity

**Choice depends on:**
- Distance to transmit (longer distance = thicker wire to reduce signal loss)
- Current required (more current = thicker wire)
- Flexibility needed (thinner wire more flexible)

### Insulation

**Function:** Prevents the conductor from contacting other conductors or the environment. This prevents:
- Short circuits (signal lost)
- Signal leaking into the environment
- Environmental damage to the conductor

**Materials:**

**Rubber (natural or synthetic):**
- Advantages: Flexible, good insulation, durable
- Disadvantages: Degrades over time, absorbs moisture
- Use: General-purpose cables, outdoor applications

**Cloth or paper:**
- Advantages: Breathable, allows some moisture escape
- Disadvantages: Less protective, deteriorates
- Use: Historical cables, emergency applications

**Plastic (PVC, polyethylene):**
- Advantages: Excellent insulation, water-resistant, durable
- Disadvantages: Stiff (less flexible), can harden in cold
- Use: Modern cables, outdoor applications

**Natural materials (beeswax-coated cloth, tree resin):**
- Advantages: Post-collapse available, reasonably durable
- Disadvantages: Less reliable, variable quality
- Use: Emergency or survival applications

**Thickness:** Typically 0.5–2 mm, depending on voltage and application.

### Shielding

**Function:** Protects the signal from external electromagnetic interference (noise) and prevents the signal from radiating outward.

**How it works:** External electromagnetic fields induce currents in unshielded wires, corrupting the signal. A conductive shield (grounded) absorbs the interference before it reaches the conductor.

**Materials:**

**Copper foil:** Thin, flexible, good shielding. Wraps around the insulated conductor.

**Copper braid:** Woven copper wire, flexible, good coverage. Wraps around the insulated conductor.

**Multiple layers:** Multiple layers of foil and braid for high-interference environments.

**Grounding:** Shielding only works if one end is grounded (connected to earth or a common reference point). Without grounding, the shield is ineffective.

## Part 2: Cable Types

### Single Wire (Unshielded)

**Structure:** Just a conductor with insulation.

**Use:** Low-speed, short-distance, low-interference applications (bell wire, antenna leads).

**Pros:** Simple, cheap, easy to make.

**Cons:** Susceptible to interference, radiates signal.

### Twisted Pair

**Structure:** Two conductors twisted together, with insulation around each and a jacket around both.

**How it works:** Twisting causes external interference to couple equally to both conductors. When properly terminated, this interference cancels out. The twist rate (twists per meter) affects interference rejection.

**Use:** Telephone lines, short-distance data transmission (Ethernet), balanced audio.

**Pros:** Good noise rejection, standard, available.

**Cons:** More complex to make, slightly higher resistance.

**Twist rate:** Typically 1–3 twists per inch (25–75 mm). Tighter twists = better noise rejection but slightly stiffer cable.

### Coaxial Cable

**Structure:** Inner conductor, insulation, outer conductive shield (braid or foil), outer jacket.

**How it works:** The shield surrounds the inner conductor, completely protecting it from external fields. The center conductor and shield are concentric (same axis), hence "coaxial."

**Use:** High-frequency signals (radio, television, radar), long-distance applications.

**Pros:** Excellent shielding, can transmit high frequencies without loss.

**Cons:** More expensive, less flexible, more complex to terminate.

**Impedance:** Coaxial cables have a specific impedance (typically 50 or 75 ohms) determined by the ratio of inner and outer diameter. For signal integrity, connecting devices must match this impedance.

### Multi-Conductor Cable

**Structure:** Multiple conductors (twisted pair, shielded individually, or bundled together) in a single jacket.

**Use:** Equipment with multiple signals (power + data, multiple audio channels).

**Pros:** Organized, compact, single outer jacket.

**Cons:** More complex, harder to diagnose faults.

## Part 3: Cable Construction by Hand

### Making a Twisted Pair

**Materials:**
- Two insulated wires (same length, same gauge)
- Tape or clamp to hold them

**Method:**

1. **Prepare wires:** Strip a few cm of insulation from each end. Measure and cut to equal length.

2. **Secure one end:** Clamp one end of both wires in a vise or have someone hold them.

3. **Twist:** Grasp the other end. Twist the two wires together. Aim for 1–3 twists per inch.
   - Tight twists (high frequency) = better noise rejection but stiffer
   - Loose twists (low frequency) = more flexible but less noise rejection

4. **Maintain tension:** Keep both wires under equal tension during twisting (prevents one from bunching).

5. **Finish:** When fully twisted to desired length, secure the other end.

6. **Outer jacket:** Wrap the twisted pair with tape or cloth to form an outer jacket.

**Time:** 20–30 minutes for a 10-meter cable by hand.

### Making a Shielded Cable

**Materials:**
- Twisted pair (from above)
- Copper foil or braided shield
- Outer jacket material

**Method:**

1. **Start with twisted pair.**

2. **Add shield:** Wrap the twisted pair with copper foil or braid. Overlap the foil edges slightly to ensure complete coverage. If using braid, wrap in a helical pattern (diagonal, like a barber pole) to avoid gaps.

3. **Ground the shield:** At one end, solder a short wire to the shield (for later grounding). Do NOT ground both ends (can create a ground loop, which generates noise).

4. **Outer jacket:** Wrap the shielded pair with tape or cloth to protect the shield and create a finished appearance.

**Time:** 30–45 minutes for a 10-meter cable.

### Terminating a Cable

**Goal:** Connect the cable to a connector (plug or socket) so it can attach to equipment.

**For simple applications (low-speed):**
1. Strip 1 cm of outer jacket from the cable end.
2. Strip 5 mm of insulation from each conductor.
3. Twist bare copper to ensure full contact.
4. Solder the conductor to the connector terminal.
5. For shielded cables, solder the shield to the shield terminal.
6. Verify continuity with a multimeter (signal flows through).

**For professional applications:**
- Use crimped connectors (faster, more reliable, standard)
- Use a crimping tool to attach pre-made connector pieces
- This requires specific tools and connectors but produces repeatable, high-quality connections

## Part 4: Cable Quality and Testing

### Visual Inspection

**Signs of a good cable:**
- Uniform conductor diameter
- Even insulation thickness
- No damage or cracks in insulation
- Clean, straight twist pattern (for twisted pair)
- Complete shield coverage (for shielded cables)

**Signs of a bad cable:**
- Thin spots in insulation (risk of shorts)
- Exposed conductor (oxidation, signal loss)
- Uneven twist (noise rejection reduced)
- Gaps in shield (noise leaks through)

### Electrical Testing

**Continuity test:** Use a multimeter to verify signal flows from one end to the other.
- Connect multimeter across the conductor at one end
- Verify resistance is very low (less than 1 ohm per meter)
- If resistance is high, the conductor is damaged or connection is poor

**Insulation test:** Use a multimeter on high-resistance setting.
- Connect probes across the conductor and shield (or other conductor)
- Resistance should be very high (greater than 1 megohm)
- If resistance is low, insulation is damaged (risk of short circuit)

**Signal test:** For a working cable, transmit a signal and verify it arrives at the other end.
- Use a light, radio signal, or electrical pulse (depends on cable type)
- Compare signal strength at input and output (signal should not degrade significantly)

### Causes of Cable Failure

**Mechanical:** Crushed or bent conductor, cracked insulation from flexing, worn insulation from abrasion.

**Environmental:** Moisture (oxidizes copper, degrades insulation), temperature extremes (hardens plastic, dries rubber), chemical exposure (melts plastic, corrodes copper).

**Manufacturing:** Poor twist pattern, thin insulation, incomplete shielding, bad termination.

**Usage:** Exceeded current capacity (overheating, insulation melts), exceeded voltage (breakdown, arc), excessive bending (conductor breaks).

## Part 5: Cable Selection for Applications

### Low-Speed, Short-Distance (under 100 m)

**Best:** Single wire with good insulation
**Why:** Cheap, simple, works fine for slow signals
**Example:** Bell wire for telephone, intercom

### Medium-Speed, Medium-Distance (100–1000 m)

**Best:** Twisted pair with jacket
**Why:** Good balance of noise rejection, cost, and practicality
**Example:** Telephone trunk lines, short Ethernet runs

### High-Speed or Long-Distance (over 1 km)

**Best:** Coaxial cable or multi-pair shielded
**Why:** Excellent shielding preserves high-frequency signals
**Example:** Radio antenna feeds, television distribution, long-distance data

### High-Noise Environment

**Best:** Multi-layer shielded, twisted pair
**Why:** Redundant shielding catches interference that one layer might miss
**Example:** Industrial facilities with electric motors, RF equipment nearby

## Part 6: Worked Examples

### Example 1: Making a Telephone Cable

**Goal:** Connect a telephone handset to a base station, 50 meters away.

**Design:**
- Twisted pair (2 conductors): one for signal, one for return
- Twist rate: 2 twists per inch (good noise rejection, reasonable flexibility)
- Insulation: rubber or PVC (durable, weather-resistant)
- No shielding needed (low-frequency signal, moderate distance, low interference environment)

**Construction:**
1. Obtain 55 meters of insulated wire (extra for connections)
2. Twist the pair: 2 twists per inch over 50 meters
3. Wrap with tape or cloth
4. Terminate both ends with telephone connectors

**Testing:**
- Continuity: Resistance under 50 ohms over 50 meters ✓
- Insulation: Greater than 1 megohm resistance between conductors ✓
- Signal: Clear audio transmission ✓

### Example 2: Making a Radio Antenna Cable

**Goal:** Connect an antenna to a radio transmitter, 20 meters away, in a facility with electrical equipment nearby.

**Design:**
- Coaxial cable: 50-ohm impedance (matches radio equipment standard)
- Inner conductor: 18 AWG copper
- Insulation: polyethylene (standard for coax)
- Outer conductor: copper braid shield
- Jacket: PVC (weatherproof)

**Construction:**
1. Select standard coaxial cable (RG-58 type)
2. No twisting needed (coax design handles interference)
3. Terminate with coaxial connectors (SMA or N-type depending on equipment)

**Testing:**
- Continuity: Inner conductor continuous, shield continuous ✓
- Impedance match: Measure with impedance analyzer (should be 50 ohms) ✓
- Signal: Transmit test signal, verify antenna radiates efficiently ✓

## Part 7: Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Twist Rate Too Loose

Loose twists = poor noise rejection. If you twist only 1 turn per foot (very loose), external interference couples into the pair and corrupts the signal. Aim for 2–3 twists per inch minimum.

### Mistake 2: Not Grounding the Shield

A shield that is not grounded does nothing. One end of the shield must be connected to ground (earth or a common reference). Without grounding, the shield acts like an additional conductor and actually worsens noise rejection.

### Mistake 3: Exposing the Conductor

Bare copper oxidizes (forms a brown or green layer), increasing resistance and degrading signal quality. Always maintain insulation integrity. If insulation is damaged, replace the cable segment.

### Mistake 4: Using Too-Thin Wire for Long Distances

Thin wire has high resistance. Over long distances, the voltage drop (and signal loss) becomes significant. For distances over 1 km, use thicker wire or lower-impedance cable.

### Mistake 5: Mixing Cable Types

If you use different cable types in the same run (twisted pair in one section, coax in another), the impedance changes at the junction, reflecting some signal back. This causes signal loss. Use a single cable type or use impedance-matching devices at junctions.
