## Overview

In a post-collapse world, information travels at the speed of a messenger on foot or horseback. A message between distant villages takes days. A telegraph reverses this: the same message travels at the speed of electricity, arriving in minutes.

The telegraph is one of the most valuable technologies you can deploy. It connects communities, coordinates resource allocation, transmits emergency warnings, and preserves institutional knowledge across distances.

This chapter teaches:

1. **How telegraphs work:** The electrical principles underlying telegraph transmission
2. **How to build a telegraph:** Construction of a working telegraph sender and receiver
3. **How to network telegraphs:** Connecting multiple telegraph stations
4. **How to operate a telegraph system:** Code, procedure, and protocol

A single telegraph line connecting two villages provides more value than almost any other technology.

## Part 1: How Telegraph Works

### The Fundamental Principle

A telegraph is a circuit that is repeatedly closed and opened. When the circuit is closed, current flows through an electromagnet, creating a magnetic field. When the circuit is opened, the field collapses.

**The basic telegraph circuit:**

1. **Battery:** Provides electrical current
2. **Telegraph key (sender):** Switch controlled by the operator; pressing makes/breaks circuit
3. **Wire (transmission line):** Carries current from sender to receiver
4. **Electromagnet (receiver):** Current flows through coil, magnet attracts armature
5. **Armature:** Iron strip pulled toward the magnet, making/breaking contact with a marking device
6. **Ground return:** Current returns to battery through earth (or through a second wire)

### Why This Matters for Survival

Before telegraph: A messenger on horseback carries a message. Speed is limited by how fast a horse can travel (approximately 40 km per day). A message between two villages 100 km apart takes 2–3 days.

With telegraph: An operator presses the telegraph key repeatedly to create a pattern of pulses. The pulses travel through wire at the speed of electricity (approximately 300,000 km per second). The same message is transmitted in seconds.

Telegraph also carries signals that a messenger could not carry:
- Continuous transmission (no waiting for next messenger)
- Parallel communication (multiple messages on different lines)
- Real-time coordination (make decisions based on information that just arrived)
- Emergency signals (immediate warning of danger)

### The Electromagnetic Transducer

The core of a telegraph is the transducer: a device that converts electrical signal into physical motion.

**The electromagnet:**

When current flows through a coil of wire wrapped around an iron core, the iron becomes magnetized. The strength of the magnet depends on:

- **Number of coil turns:** More turns = stronger magnet (approximately 100–500 turns for a telegraph electromagnet)
- **Current magnitude:** More current = stronger magnet (0.5–2 amps is typical)
- **Iron core quality:** Soft iron is better than hard iron (soft iron magnetizes and demagnetizes quickly)

**The armature:**

A thin piece of iron or steel positioned near the electromagnet. When the electromagnet is activated, the armature is pulled toward it. When the electromagnet is deactivated, a spring pushes the armature back.

This motion (pull and release) is the key to telegraph operation:
- The armature moves down (pulled toward magnet) = mark
- The armature moves up (spring pushes back) = space

The operator at the sending end controls how long the electromagnet is powered (continuous power = long mark; brief power = short mark). The receiver records marks and spaces.

### Morse Code: The Language of Telegraph

In the early days of telegraph, operators developed a coding system to represent letters and numbers using marks and spaces.

**Example codes:**

- A = dot-dash
- E = dot
- T = dash
- 1 = dot-dash-dash-dash-dash
- Period = dot-dash-dot-dash-dot-dash

The advantage of Morse code is efficiency. Common letters (E, T) are represented with fewer marks, while rare letters (Q, Z) use more marks. An experienced operator can transmit approximately 30–50 words per minute.

## Part 2: Building a Telegraph System

### Step 1: Construct the Telegraph Key (Sender)

The telegraph key is a switch controlled by the operator. Pressing the key closes the circuit; releasing opens it.

**Materials:**

- Wooden base (approximately 10 cm × 5 cm)
- Brass lever (approximately 7 cm long, 1 cm wide)
- Pivot point (screw or brass rod)
- Contact points (two brass or copper scraps, approximately 1 cm²)
- Spring (small steel spring or bent wire)
- Wire (for electrical connections)
- Solder and flux

**Construction:**

1. **Mount the lever:** Attach the brass lever to the wooden base with a pivot point (screw or rod). The lever should balance—pressing one end closes the circuit, releasing opens it.

2. **Install contact points:** Place one contact point under the lever (where it naturally rests) and another below it (where it moves when pressed). These two points make electrical contact when the lever is pressed.

3. **Add spring tension:** Install a spring to push the lever back to its resting position when released. The spring tension should require moderate pressure to press the key.

4. **Connect wires:** Solder wires to each contact point. These wires carry current from the battery to the transmission line.

5. **Test:** Connect the key to a battery and wire. Pressing the key should complete the circuit (battery current flows). Releasing should open it (current stops).

**Result:** A functioning telegraph key that can be pressed repeatedly to create dots and dashes.

### Step 2: Construct the Telegraph Receiver (Electromagnet + Armature + Marking Device)

The receiver converts electrical pulses into physical marks on paper.

**Materials:**

- Wooden frame (approximately 15 cm × 10 cm)
- Electromagnet (from Vol 12, Ch 6): iron core, ~500 turns of 20-gauge copper wire
- Steel armature (thin strip, 5 cm long, 1 cm wide, 1 mm thick)
- Pivot point (screw or brass rod)
- Spring (steel spring or bent strip)
- Marking device (pencil, pen, or stylus with ink)
- Paper feed mechanism (simple pulley system to move paper under the stylus)
- Wire for connections

**Construction:**

1. **Mount the electromagnet:** Attach the electromagnet coil to the frame with the iron core pointing horizontally. The coil should produce a magnetic field that attracts the armature.

2. **Mount the armature:** Position the armature 2–3 mm in front of the electromagnet core. Use a pivot point (screw or rod) to allow the armature to rotate. When the electromagnet is activated, the armature should be pulled toward the core approximately 2–3 mm.

3. **Install return spring:** Attach a spring to pull the armature back to its resting position when the electromagnet is deactivated. The spring should be strong enough to overcome magnetic attraction when the field is off.

4. **Attach marking device:** Attach a pencil, pen, or stylus to the armature (on the opposite side from the electromagnet). When the armature moves, the marking device traces a line on paper passing beneath it.

5. **Add paper feed:** Install a simple mechanism to move paper under the marking device. Options:
   - Hand crank (operator manually pulls paper)
   - Weight-driven pulley (a falling weight pulls paper continuously)
   - Clock mechanism (if available, pull paper at constant speed)

6. **Connect electromagnet:** Solder the electromagnet coil to incoming wires from the transmission line. The electromagnet should be in series with the circuit.

7. **Connect ground return:** The electromagnet circuit must return to the battery's negative terminal (or to ground). Use a grounding rod driven into earth, or a second wire.

8. **Test:** Connect the electromagnet to the battery. When you press the telegraph key, the electromagnet should activate, pulling the armature. The marking device should create a line on paper. When you release the key, the armature should spring back, and the line should end.

**Result:** A functioning telegraph receiver that marks paper with dots (short pulses) and dashes (long pulses).

### Step 3: Connect Sender and Receiver

**Simple point-to-point telegraph:**

1. Build one telegraph key (sender) and one telegraph receiver
2. Connect them with insulated copper wire (gauge 14–16)
3. Complete the circuit through a battery
4. Operator at the sender presses the key to control the electromagnet at the receiver

**Circuit diagram (conceptual):**

```
[Battery +] → [Telegraph Key] → [Transmission Wire] → [Electromagnet] → [Ground Rod]
                                                                              ↓
                                                                         [Earth]
                                                                              ↑
[Battery -] ← [Ground Rod Connection] ← [Earth]
```

**Transmission line:**

- Use insulated copper wire (20–100 meters for initial tests)
- Run wire between two locations (rooftops, poles, or direct underground burial)
- Support wire with insulators (ceramic, wood, or glass) every 1–2 meters to prevent contact with ground
- At each end, connect wire to the telegraph equipment with solder or brass clamps
- Ensure tight connections; loose connections cause signal loss

### Step 4: Test the Telegraph

Before using for real communication:

1. **Test at short distance (1 meter):** Connect sender and receiver with 1 meter of wire. Verify that pressing the key activates the receiver reliably.

2. **Test at medium distance (10–20 meters):** Run wire between two rooms. Test both directions.

3. **Observe signal quality:**
   - Does the electromagnet activate reliably when key is pressed?
   - Does the marking device mark clearly?
   - Are marks clearly separated?
   - Does the armature return reliably to rest?

4. **Diagnose problems:**
   - **Weak or no signal:** Check battery voltage (should be 6–12 volts). Check wire connections. Check electromagnet coil continuity (use another battery to test; if electromagnet pulls when battery is connected, coil is good).
   - **Armature sticks:** Spring may be too weak, or electromagnet pull may be too strong. Reduce number of coil turns or reduce battery voltage.
   - **Marking is unclear:** Marking device may be dull. Sharpen pencil or refill pen. Armature motion may be too small; increase electromagnet strength or reduce spring tension.

## Part 3: Morse Code and Operation

### Morse Code Fundamentals

In Morse code, every letter and number is represented by a sequence of dots (short marks) and dashes (long marks).

**Dot:** Electromagnet activated briefly (approximately 0.1 second)
**Dash:** Electromagnet activated longer (approximately 0.3 second)
**Space between symbols:** Electromagnet inactive (approximately 0.1 second)
**Space between letters:** Electromagnet inactive (approximately 0.3 second)
**Space between words:** Electromagnet inactive (approximately 0.7 second)

**Common Morse code examples:**

| Letter | Code |
|--------|------|
| A | •‐ |
| B | ‐••• |
| C | ‐•‐• |
| D | ‐•• |
| E | • |
| I | •• |
| O | ‐‐‐ |
| S | ••• |
| T | ‐ |
| Z | ‐‐•• |

(For a complete code chart, see Cross-References at the end of this chapter.)

### Operating Procedure

**To send a message:**

1. Operator composes message in plain language
2. Operator translates message to Morse code
3. Operator presses telegraph key to generate the code:
   - Press key briefly (approximately 0.1 second) for a dot
   - Press key longer (approximately 0.3 second) for a dash
   - Pause briefly (approximately 0.1 second) between symbols
   - Pause longer (approximately 0.3 second) between letters
   - Pause even longer (approximately 0.7 second) between words

4. Receiver observes marks on paper (or listens to clicking sound, if no marking device)
5. Receiver translates marks back to Morse code, then to plain language

**To receive a message:**

1. Receiver starts paper feed (hand crank or weight-driven pulley)
2. Receiver observes incoming signal as marks appear on paper
3. After transmission is complete, receiver reads the marks
4. Receiver translates marks to Morse code, then to plain language
5. Receiver writes plain-language message for the intended recipient

### Common Operating Procedures

**Initiating contact:**

The sending operator begins with "CQ" (Morse: ‐•‐• •‐ — a request to contact any listening station). The sending station repeats CQ three times, then the destination station's identifier (if known).

**Confirming receipt:**

When the receiving operator confirms they have received a message correctly, they send "R" (•‐•). When the message is complete, they send "K" (‐•‐) to indicate they are ready for more.

**Signaling problems:**

If a signal is unclear, the receiving operator sends "?" (•••‐‐) to request retransmission.

**Ending communication:**

When all messages are complete, the sending operator sends "SK" (•••‐•‐) to indicate "end of contact." Both stations then shut down.

## Part 4: Telegraph Networks

### Connecting Multiple Stations

A single telegraph line (sender to receiver) is valuable. Multiple lines create a network.

**Star topology:** One central station connects to multiple other stations

```
      [Station A]
         |
      [Station B]—[Central Hub]—[Station C]
         |
      [Station D]
```

Advantage: Central station can relay messages between any two stations.
Disadvantage: Central station is critical; if it fails, network fails.

**Chain topology:** Stations connect in a line

```
[Station A]—[Station B]—[Station C]—[Station D]
```

Advantage: Can extend very far (each station boosts the signal).
Disadvantage: Message must pass through intermediate stations.

**Relay stations:** At distance, signal weakens. Solution: install a relay station that automatically retransmits.

When signal arrives at a relay station:
1. Incoming signal activates electromagnet
2. Electromagnet pulls armature, which closes a new circuit
3. New circuit powers a local battery, sending signal to the next station
4. Signal is regenerated at full strength

This allows telegraph lines to extend hundreds or thousands of kilometers.

### Signal Loss and Amplification

Over long distances, electrical signal weakens due to wire resistance.

**To maximize signal distance:**

1. **Thicker wire (lower resistance):** Wire gauge 10–14 carries signal farther than gauge 18–20
2. **Lower voltage drop:** Use batteries capable of delivering consistent voltage over long distances
3. **Relay stations:** Regenerate signal at intervals (every 10–50 km, depending on wire thickness)
4. **Underground cable:** Insulated cable (metal conductor surrounded by rubber or fabric insulation) reduces signal loss due to environmental interference

**Practical telegraph network:**

- Station 1 sends signal to Station 2 (10 km away via gauge 14 wire)
- Signal weakens but is still strong enough to trigger electromagnet
- At Station 2, operator reads message
- If message is for Station 3, operator retransmits using local battery
- Signal travels from Station 2 to Station 3 (another 10 km) at full strength
- This pattern can extend the network indefinitely

### Operating a Network

With multiple stations connected:

1. **Central record-keeping:** Central station maintains log of all messages sent and received
2. **Message routing:** Each message is addressed to a specific station; central station or relay stations direct it appropriately
3. **Priority protocols:** Emergency messages (fire, injury, attack) are transmitted immediately; routine messages wait
4. **Scheduled transmission:** To avoid confusion, stations may have assigned times (e.g., morning broadcast, afternoon status update)

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Setting Up a Simple Telegraph

**Scenario:** Two villages 15 km apart want to establish communication.

**Planning:**

1. Survey the route between villages (note obstacles, find suitable path for wire)
2. Identify suitable attachment points (church steeples, tall trees, or dedicated poles)
3. Estimate wire needed: 15 km + 10% for loops and connections ≈ 16.5 km

**Materials:**

- 16.5 km of insulated copper wire (gauge 14)
- 200 insulators (ceramic or wooden, spaced every 80 meters)
- 2 telegraph keys
- 2 electromagnets with armature
- 2 marking devices
- 4 batteries (6-volt galvanic cells)
- Grounding rods (2)
- Miscellaneous: solder, connectors, wire for installation

**Construction:**

1. Build telegraph key and receiver at Village A
2. Build telegraph key and receiver at Village B
3. Install support structure (poles or use existing trees)
4. Run wire from Village A to Village B, securing with insulators every 80 meters
5. At Village A: Connect key to wire and battery (ground return through rod)
6. At Village B: Connect electromagnet to wire and battery (ground return through rod)

**Testing:**

1. Operator at A presses key 5 times (5 dots), observes if receiver at B marks 5 dots
2. Operator at B sends confirmation signal
3. Test at various speeds (slow 10 words per minute, fast 30 words per minute)
4. Test in different weather (rain, high wind) to ensure reliability

**Operational handover:**

- Train 2–3 operators at each village
- Post Morse code chart at each station
- Establish daily check-in times (e.g., 8 AM and 6 PM)
- Keep message log at each station

**Result:** A working telegraph line that transmits messages in seconds instead of days.

### Example 2: Emergency Signal Protocol

**Scenario:** A village discovers a fire approaching nearby forest. They need to warn downstream villages.

**Process:**

1. Observer spots fire (5 km away, approaching at 1 km/hour)
2. Observer runs to telegraph station
3. Operator sends emergency signal: "SOS" (Morse: •••‐‐‐•••) repeated 3 times
4. Downstream station receives SOS and immediately relays to next station (no waiting for operators)
5. All downstream stations receive warning within minutes (vs. hours for a messenger)

**Message sent:**

"SOS FIRE APPROACHING FROM NORTH BEARING 045 DEGREES MOVE LIVESTOCK SOUTH REPEAT MOVE SOUTH"

**Result:** Villages have time to evacuate livestock and prepare defenses instead of being surprised by fire.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Wire too thin or too long without relays.** Thin wire has high resistance; signal weakens over distance. Use thicker wire (gauge 10–14) or install relay stations every 10–50 km.

2. **Failing to ground the circuit.** Telegraph circuits need a return path for current. A grounding rod driven into earth provides this. Without grounding, signal may not return, and the circuit fails.

3. **Electromagnet too weak.** If you use too few coil turns (under 100) or too little current (under 0.5 amps), the electromagnet may not pull the armature reliably. Test with a simple battery before installation.

4. **Spring tension wrong.** If the return spring is too strong, the electromagnet cannot pull the armature. If too weak, the armature sticks and fails to return. Adjust spring tension empirically.

5. **Loose electrical connections.** Solder all connections tightly. Loose connections introduce resistance and signal loss.

6. **Incorrect Morse code.** Before transmitting, verify the code is correct. Sending wrong code wastes time and creates confusion. Use a reference chart.

7. **Operating too fast.** Dots and dashes bleed together, creating ambiguous marks. Maintain consistent speed with pauses between symbols and letters.

8. **Assuming first telegraph will work.** The first telegraph usually has problems. Build, test at short distance, diagnose and fix. Only then extend to long distances.

9. **Ignoring environmental interference.** Lightning, other electrical equipment, or nearby metal objects can create noise on the telegraph line. Shield wire if possible, use grounding to bleed off interference.

10. **Failing to keep message logs.** Without a written record of messages sent and received, critical information can be lost or duplicated. Keep a log at every station.
