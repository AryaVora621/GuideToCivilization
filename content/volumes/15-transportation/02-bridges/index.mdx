## Overview

A bridge is a structure that spans a physical obstacle (river, ravine, valley, water channel) while maintaining passage for people, animals, or goods. Bridges are essential infrastructure in a rebuilding civilization because they:

1. **Enable trade:** A road that cannot cross a river is useless
2. **Reduce travel time:** A direct crossing is faster than finding a ford or going miles upstream
3. **Enable defense:** Bridges can be controlled and defended
4. **Concentrate population:** River crossings become natural settlement points

Bridge design depends on three factors:

1. **The span:** The distance the bridge must cover (width of the river or ravine)
2. **The load:** How much weight must the bridge carry (foot traffic, wagons, heavy equipment)
3. **Available materials:** What is locally available (wood, stone, iron, rope)

A poor bridge fails, stranding people and goods or causing injury. This chapter covers bridge types, principles of design, and construction methods suitable for post-collapse conditions.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of material strength (tension, compression, shear)
- Familiarity with basic geometry and load calculations
- Knowledge of construction principles (footings, drainage, soil bearing capacity)
- Practical experience with tools and basic construction
- Willingness to calculate forces and safety margins

## Materials & Tools

**Design and survey:**
- Measuring tape (at least 30 meters)
- Angle gauge or transit (for slope measurement)
- Level (to ensure horizontal bridge deck)
- String and stakes (to mark alignment)
- Notebook for sketches and calculations

**Structural materials (vary by bridge type):**
- Timber (posts, beams, planking—must be sound, not rotted)
- Stone (for masonry arches and piers)
- Rope (manila, hemp, or plant-based fibers for suspension bridges)
- Iron or salvaged steel (for reinforcement and connectors)
- Clay mortar or lime mortar (for binding stone)

**Construction tools:**
- Axes, saws, chisels (for timber preparation)
- Hammers, mallets (for assembly)
- Measuring and marking tools
- Scaffolding or temporary supports (essential for safety during construction)
- Digging tools (for excavating foundations)

## Part 1: Bridge Types and When to Use Each

### Beam Bridge (Simplest)

A beam bridge is the most basic form: a horizontal beam (or series of beams) is supported at each end by abutments (supports at the shore). The beam carries the load by bending slightly, with the top compressed and the bottom in tension.

**Properties:**
- Simple to build and repair
- Cannot span large distances (wood beams fail if span exceeds 15–20 meters; stone beams, even less)
- Suitable for small streams, channels, and moderate-load roads
- Load limit depends on beam depth and material strength

**When to use:**
- Small streams under 10 meters wide
- Foot and animal traffic (avoid heavy wagons)
- Temporary or easily replaceable structures

**Common variation: Multi-span beam bridge**
- Instead of one long beam, use several shorter beams, each resting on piers (supports in the middle of the span)
- Example: a river 30 meters wide using three 10-meter spans, with two piers in the water

### Arch Bridge (High Strength, Long Lasting)

An arch is a curved structure that carries load by compression. The weight of the arch is directed along the curve toward the abutments (the supports at each end). Stone arches have been used for 2,000+ years because they are extremely strong in compression.

**Properties:**
- Very strong under load (can span 30–50 meters in stone)
- Durable (stone lasts centuries)
- Requires precise construction (settling or displacement causes failure)
- Requires abutments that can resist outward thrust (the arch pushes outward at the base)

**How it works:**
- The arch is a curved series of wedge-shaped stones, each tighter than the one before
- The weight of the arch and its load push along the curve
- The curve is designed so all forces are compression (pushing, not pulling)
- The abutments resist the outward thrust

**When to use:**
- Permanent, high-traffic crossings
- Large spans (over 15 meters)
- Stone-rich areas
- Rivers that must support heavily loaded traffic

**Common materials:**
- Stone blocks (most durable)
- Brick (faster to build than stone, less durable)
- Timber (can be arranged in a temporary arch shape, but less durable)

### Suspension Bridge (Long Span)

A suspension bridge hangs from cables supported by tall towers. The cables are in tension (pulling), transferring the load of the deck to the towers and then to the abutments.

**Properties:**
- Can span very long distances (over 100 meters in modern materials)
- Relatively light and less material than other types
- Requires strong rope or cable and solid towers
- More complex to design and build than other types

**When to use:**
- Very wide rivers, gorges, or canyons (100+ meters)
- When span is too large for beam or arch bridges
- When materials like rope are available but stone or large timber is not

**Critical requirement:** The cables must be strong enough to support the load and the deck. In a post-collapse setting, suspension bridges are difficult because they require:
- Very strong rope (plant fibers, animal sinew, or salvaged steel cable)
- Extremely solid anchorages (the ends of the cables must be firmly secured to rock or deep footings)

### Pontoon Bridge (Mobile, Temporary)

A pontoon bridge uses floating platforms (boats, barrels, logs) to support the bridge deck. The platforms are lashed together or weighted to keep them in place.

**Properties:**
- Can be built and removed quickly
- Suitable for temporary crossings
- Limited load capacity (depends on the pontoons' buoyancy and size)
- Can be destroyed or displaced by flooding or ice

**When to use:**
- Temporary military crossings (supplies, troops)
- Seasonal rivers (not needed year-round)
- Quick access during emergencies

**Disadvantage:** Not suitable for permanent settlement areas because the crossing is vulnerable to interruption.

### Trestle Bridge (Timber, Tall)

A trestle bridge is a series of timber supports (like a fence) holding up a deck. It is common for roads in mountainous terrain and can span across deep valleys.

**Properties:**
- Can be built entirely from timber
- Flexible and relatively light
- Requires regular maintenance (wood rot, weathering)
- Can span moderate distances (30–50 meters total, with trestle bents every 4–6 meters)

**When to use:**
- Roads through valleys or ravines
- Timber-rich areas with little stone
- Temporary infrastructure that will be upgraded later

## Part 2: Design Principles and Load Calculation

### Understanding Load

A bridge's load comes from:

1. **Dead load (self-weight):** The weight of the bridge structure itself (deck, supports, materials)
2. **Live load:** The weight of people, animals, goods, and wagons using the bridge
3. **Environmental load:** Wind, water pressure (during floods), and earthquakes

**Design safety:** A bridge is designed so the total stress (from all loads combined) does not exceed the material's strength. A typical safety factor is 2–3, meaning the bridge can support 2–3 times the expected load before failure.

### Calculating Simple Loads

**Dead load example:**
- A timber beam bridge: 10 meters span, 2 meters wide, 0.5 meters deep
- Timber density: approximately 600 kg per cubic meter
- Volume: 10 × 2 × 0.5 = 10 cubic meters
- Weight: 10 × 600 = 6,000 kg (6 metric tons)

**Live load example:**
- 50 people crossing the bridge: 50 × 80 kg average = 4,000 kg
- Or a loaded wagon: 1,000 kg wagon + 2,000 kg goods = 3,000 kg

**Bending stress in a beam:**
- The middle of the beam experiences the most stress
- Stress = (Load × Span) / (Material Strength × Beam Depth²)
- For a 10-meter beam with a 10,000 kg load and timber strength of 10 MPa (megapascals), the required beam depth is calculated through standard formulas (beyond this chapter)

**Practical rule (for common timber beams):**
- A timber beam 30 cm deep can safely span 10 meters under typical road loads
- A beam 20 cm deep spans about 7 meters
- A beam 40 cm deep spans about 13 meters

(These are approximate; actual capacity depends on timber quality and load type.)

### Factor of Safety

Design with redundancy:

1. **Multiple beams:** Instead of one 10-meter beam, use two 5-meter beams in parallel. If one fails, the other might hold long enough for people to evacuate.

2. **Over-sized members:** If you calculate a beam needs to be 30 cm deep, build it 40 cm deep. The extra strength protects against unknown factors (decay, hidden cracks, unusual loads).

3. **Drainage:** A bridge that collects water becomes heavier and rots faster. Always slope the deck and provide drainage to prevent water pooling.

### Foundation Design

Bridge failures often occur at the foundations, not the superstructure. The abutments must be:

1. **Deep:** Below the frost line (typically 1–2 meters in temperate climates) to prevent heave (lifting due to soil freezing and thawing)
2. **On solid ground:** Rock or compacted soil, not loose sand or silt
3. **Protected from erosion:** The water flow around the abutment must not undermine it; use riprap (large rocks) to disperse water

**Abutment failure modes:**
- **Scour:** Water flow erodes soil around the abutment, causing settling
- **Frost heave:** Soil under the abutment freezes and expands, lifting the bridge
- **Undermining:** Water flow removes soil from under the abutment, causing sudden collapse

Prevention:
- Place abutments on bedrock or deep, stable soil
- Armor the riverbed around abutments with stone to prevent scour
- Ensure drainage so water does not accumulate

## Part 3: Construction Methods

### Timber Beam Bridge (10–15 Meter Span)

**Materials:**
- Main beams: 4–6 wooden beams, 30–40 cm diameter, at least 10 meters long
- Cross beams: smaller timber connecting the main beams
- Decking: planks, usually 5 cm thick
- Nails or wooden pegs (rope lashing acceptable for temporary structures)

**Abutments:**
- Stone or timber supports at each bank
- Must be below the frost line

**Construction steps:**

1. **Prepare abutments (1–2 weeks):**
   - Dig to below frost line (1.5–2 meters)
   - Build stone or timber supports
   - Ensure level tops to support beams

2. **Fell and prepare timber (1–2 weeks):**
   - Select straight, sound logs (no rot, large knots, or checking)
   - Fell with crosscut saw or axe
   - Strip bark (reduces rot)
   - Cut to length (add 1 meter extra on each end to rest on abutments)
   - Square off rough sides if needed for alignment

3. **Transport and position (1–2 days):**
   - Use temporary scaffolding or wooden trestle to hold beams
   - Roll or slide beams into position
   - Ensure proper alignment and slope (usually 2–5% slope for drainage)

4. **Install cross bracing and decking (3–5 days):**
   - Lay cross beams (perpendicular to main beams) every 1.5–2 meters
   - Nail or peg cross beams to main beams
   - Lay decking planks perpendicular to cross beams
   - Nail planks firmly (loose planks are hazards)

5. **Install railings and drainage (1–2 days):**
   - Install railings to prevent falls (especially important for animals that might panic)
   - Ensure deck has slight crown (higher in center) to drain water
   - Use stones or debris to shield abutments from water erosion

**Maintenance plan:** Inspect annually. Replace decking every 5–10 years. Major structural repairs every 20–30 years.

### Stone Arch Bridge (20–40 Meter Span)

**Materials:**
- Ashlar (squared) or fieldstone, 30 cm × 30 cm × 50 cm average
- Lime mortar (lime + sand, mixed with water)
- Temporary support structure (wooden forms) during construction

**Construction steps:**

1. **Design the arch (1–2 weeks):**
   - Calculate radius and angle of the arch (requires basic geometry and trigonometry)
   - A typical arch for a 20-meter span has a radius of 15–20 meters
   - The arch should be high enough that ships or water can pass (minimum 5 meters clearance for major rivers)

2. **Build abutments (2–4 weeks):**
   - Excavate foundations to bedrock or stable soil (3–5 meters deep)
   - Build massive stone abutments (10+ meters thick) to resist outward thrust of the arch
   - Abutments must be higher than the arch's maximum flood level

3. **Construct temporary wooden supports (1–2 weeks):**
   - Build a wooden form (called a centering) in the shape of the arch
   - The form must be strong enough to support the weight of the stones while the mortar sets
   - The form is removed after the mortar has cured (2–4 weeks)

4. **Lay the arch (2–4 weeks):**
   - Start laying stones from each abutment toward the center (the keystone)
   - Use mortar between each stone
   - Ensure each stone is wedge-shaped so the outer edges are longer than the inner edges
   - The wedge shape ensures that the load is directed along the curve (all compression)

5. **Build the deck and approach ramps (1–2 weeks):**
   - Once the arch is complete and mortar has cured, remove the temporary form
   - Build the deck on top of the arch
   - Construct approach ramps on each bank at a gentle slope (5–10%)

6. **Final work and testing (1 week):**
   - Re-point mortar joints if needed
   - Inspect for cracks or movement
   - Test with loaded carts before opening to traffic

**Maintenance:** Stone arch bridges require little maintenance if well-built. Repoint mortar every 50–100 years. Replace damaged stones as needed.

### Suspension Bridge (Smaller Scale, 30–50 Meter Span)

**Materials:**
- Strong rope: hemp, manila, or salvaged steel cable (several centimeters diameter)
- Main cables: 2–4 parallel cables supporting the deck
- Deck: wooden planks or logs suspended by vertical cables from the main cables
- Anchors: deep footings at each end to hold the cables

**Construction steps:**

1. **Establish anchorages (2–4 weeks):**
   - Anchor the ends of the main cables to deep concrete footings or rock anchors
   - The cables must be held with extreme force (the tension can exceed 50 metric tons)
   - Wrapping rope around boulders and anchoring to bedrock is an option (less reliable than concrete)

2. **Erect towers (1–2 weeks):**
   - Build two towers (20–30 meters tall) at the center of the span
   - Towers carry the main cables and must be tall enough to provide the necessary sag (rope has some curve)

3. **String the main cables (1–2 weeks):**
   - Carefully raise the main cables over the towers and secure to the anchorages
   - Adjust tension to ensure proper curve (sag should be about 1/10 of the span)

4. **Hang the deck (1–2 weeks):**
   - String vertical cables from the main cables to the deck supports
   - Attach wooden beam or plank deck
   - Ensure the deck is level and rigid

5. **Add railings and drainage (1 week):**
   - Install railings to prevent falls
   - Slope the deck for drainage

**Challenges in a post-collapse setting:**
- Sourcing strong rope or cable is difficult
- Erecting tall towers requires significant labor and materials
- Suspension bridges sway in wind; some cultures find this uncomfortable
- Not suitable for heavily loaded traffic (unlike arches)

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Designing a Simple Timber Beam Bridge

**Situation:** A river 12 meters wide must be crossed by foot traffic and light wagons. Timber is available. Stone is limited.

**Analysis:**
- Span: 12 meters
- Load: occasional wagon (3,000 kg) plus people (assume 50 people × 80 kg = 4,000 kg simultaneously)
- Total live load: 7,000 kg
- Bridge deck area: 12 m × 2.5 m (2.5 meters wide for wagon passage)
- Dead load (estimated): bridge weight ~5,000 kg

**Design:**
- Use 4 main beams, each 0.4 meters in diameter, resting on stone abutments
- Estimated load capacity per beam: ~4,000 kg
- 4 beams × 4,000 kg = 16,000 kg capacity (2.3× safety factor over combined loads of 12,000 kg)
- Decking: 5 cm planks, nailed to cross beams

**Result:** The bridge can safely carry the intended traffic with adequate margin.

### Example 2: Arch Bridge Sizing

**Situation:** A river 30 meters wide requires a permanent, high-traffic crossing. Stone is abundant.

**Analysis:**
- Span: 30 meters
- Loads: multiple wagons, herds of animals, possibly future heavy machinery
- Design life: 100+ years

**Design:**
- Build a stone arch with a 35-meter radius (tall enough for flood clearance and low sag)
- Arch height: approximately 8 meters above the riverbed
- Abutment thickness: 15 meters (to resist outward thrust)
- Deck width: 5 meters (two-way wagon traffic)
- Width of arch: 6 meters (provides factor of safety and stability)

**Construction estimate:**
- Stone required: approximately 10,000 metric tons
- Labor: 50 workers × 6 months = intensive project
- Cost: very high in terms of labor, but bridge lasts centuries

**Result:** A permanent crossing that can support the heaviest traffic of a medieval economy.

### Example 3: Pontoon Bridge for Temporary Crossing

**Situation:** An army must cross a river quickly. A ford is not passable. Construction time is limited.

**Analysis:**
- Span: 50 meters
- Load: soldiers, light equipment, some animals (total ~10,000 kg sustained)
- Duration: needed for 2–3 weeks, then abandoned

**Design:**
- Use 20 wooden boats or rafts, each supporting 500 kg
- Lash boats together side-by-side
- Lay wooden beams across the boats to form a deck
- Rope lashings hold the deck to the boats
- Anchor the pontoon to both banks with ropes and poles

**Assembly time:** 3–5 days
**Cost:** Labor + materials, minimal permanent loss (boats are returned to normal use after)

**Result:** A functional crossing in weeks, suitable for armies or emergency traffic.

## Verification

**You understand bridge design and construction when:**

1. You can identify which bridge type suits a given situation (span, load, materials available, permanence)
2. You can estimate the load on a bridge (dead load + live load) and roughly calculate safety margins
3. You understand how different bridge types distribute forces (compression in arches, tension in suspension cables, bending in beams)
4. You can plan the construction of a simple timber bridge, including abutments, foundations, and drainage
5. You can identify potential failure modes (scour, frost heave, overload, decay) and explain prevention
6. You understand how to maintain a bridge and predict its lifespan

## Common Mistakes

1. **Ignoring abutment and foundation failures:** The structure itself might be strong, but if the ground shifts, the bridge falls. Always prioritize stable, deep foundations.

2. **Underestimating live load:** A wagon with goods is heavier than you estimate. A crowd of animals panics and concentrates weight on one spot. Design for worst-case loads.

3. **Neglecting drainage:** Water trapped in the bridge deck causes rot (wood) or erosion (stone). Ensure water runs off, not pools.

4. **Building too close to the water's normal level:** Floods are higher than normal water. Build the deck above the 100-year flood level if possible.

5. **Miscalculating arch geometry:** An arch that is not carefully designed settles or cracks. Slight errors in the curve can cause significant problems. Use tested designs or hire an engineer.

6. **Assuming dead load is negligible:** A bridge made from massive stone is heavy. The weight of the structure itself can exceed the live load. Include dead load in calculations.

7. **Underestimating construction time:** Bridge building is slow. A "quick" timber bridge is 3–4 weeks of intensive labor. A stone arch is 6+ months.

8. **Building alone:** Bridges require teams. Raising a heavy beam, erecting temporary supports, and laying stones safely all benefit from multiple people. Under-staffed projects are dangerous.

9. **Using poor-quality materials:** A rotten timber or cracked stone is a hidden hazard. Use only sound materials. Salvage is good; damage is not.

10. **Failing to maintain:** A bridge maintained every year lasts 100+ years. A bridge ignored deteriorates rapidly. Plan maintenance from the start.
