## Overview

Roads are the circulatory system of civilization. A network of roads connects communities, enables trade, distributes goods and knowledge, and allows rapid response to emergencies. Without roads, each settlement is isolated; with roads, civilization scales.

This chapter covers practical road construction from bare ground to finished surface—site selection, grading, drainage, surfacing, and maintenance.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of earth, stone, and materials (Vol 9)
- Basic surveying and level measurement
- Knowledge of tools and leverage
- Understanding of team coordination (organizing labor)

## Materials & Tools

### For planning and surveying
- Measuring rope or chain
- Stakes and string for marking
- Water level or improvised level (straight stick, spirit level equivalent)
- Compass (optional, for route planning)

### For grading and earthwork
- Shovels and picks (multiple, for labor)
- Wheelbarrows or drag sledges (for moving earth)
- Crowbars or pry bars (for breaking rock)
- Carts or animals (for hauling stone and gravel)

### For surfacing
- Stone (quarried or collected)
- Gravel or crushed rock
- Sand (if available)
- Binding materials: clay, lime mortar (see Vol 5: Chemistry), or compacted earth

### For drainage
- Shovels (for digging ditches)
- Stone or timber (for culverts and drainage structures)

## Part 1: Site Selection and Planning

### Route planning

Choose a route that:

- **Connects important places:** From settlement to settlement, resource site to settlement, or resource site to resource site.
- **Minimizes distance:** A shorter route is faster and requires less maintenance. However, a longer route that avoids steep hills may be faster in travel time.
- **Avoids hazards:** Stay away from flood-prone areas, avalanche zones, areas prone to rockfall, and extreme terrain.
- **Provides access to water and resources:** Routes that pass near water allow rest stops and water access.
- **Follows natural terrain:** Natural ridges and valleys guide routes efficiently.

### Grade and slope

- **Ideal grade:** Between 1% and 5% (1 foot of rise per 100 feet of distance is 1%). Roads steeper than 10% are difficult for carts and animals; above 15%, nearly impassable.
- **Drainage slope:** Even a slight slope (minimum 2%) ensures water does not pool on the road surface.
- **Avoiding grades above 10%:** If terrain requires steep crossing, consider switchbacks (zigzag pattern) to reduce the grade on each segment.

### Width

- **Footpath:** 1–2 feet wide (for pedestrians only)
- **Pack animal trail:** 3–4 feet wide (for single-file movement)
- **Cart road:** 10–15 feet wide (for two-way traffic, allowing carts to pass)
- **Major road:** 20–30 feet wide (for significant traffic and future expansion)

Wider roads are more expensive to build and maintain, so match width to expected traffic.

## Part 2: Grading and Earthwork

Grading is the process of shaping the earth to create the road bed.

### Step 1: Mark the centerline.

Use stakes and string to mark the intended centerline of the road. Space stakes every 50–100 feet along the route.

### Step 2: Clear vegetation.

Remove all trees, shrubs, and vegetation within the road width plus 2–3 feet on each side. Remove roots to prevent water-logging and root damage to the road surface.

### Step 3: Cut and fill.

- **Cutting:** Where the road is higher than the natural terrain, cut (excavate) earth from the high side.
- **Filling:** Where the road is lower than the natural terrain, fill by placing excavated earth or collected material.
- **Balance:** Ideally, cut and fill are balanced—material removed in one location is moved to another, minimizing material import or export.

### Step 4: Compact the subgrade.

The subgrade is the layer beneath the road surface. Compaction reduces settling and increases bearing capacity.

- **Method:** Drive animals back and forth, or ram the earth with heavy implements (a log suspended from a frame, dropped repeatedly).
- **Moisture:** Slightly moist earth compacts better than dry earth. If the ground is very dry, add water before compaction.
- **Depth:** Compact the top 6–12 inches of the subgrade.

### Step 5: Create drainage.

- **Crowned surface:** Build the road surface slightly higher in the center than at the edges (a crown). This sheds water to the sides, reducing pooling.
- **Ditches:** Dig shallow ditches (6–12 inches deep) on each side of the road to channel runoff away.
- **Cross-drains:** Where the road crosses a valley or depression, create a culvert (a small channel or tunnel) to allow water to flow under the road.

## Part 3: Road Surfacing

The surface is what traffic travels on. Different surfaces suit different conditions and climates.

### Type 1: Packed earth or gravel (simplest, least durable)

Suitable for dry climates and light traffic.

**Construction:**
1. Spread 4–6 inches of gravel or coarse sand over the prepared subgrade.
2. Rake smooth to remove rocks and large debris.
3. Wet the surface.
4. Drive animals or roll (drag a heavy log) to compact.
5. Repeat until the surface is solid and vehicles leave minimal ruts.

**Maintenance:** Raking and re-compacting after heavy rains or traffic is necessary. Dusty in dry weather; muddy in rain. Life span: 1–3 years before significant degradation.

### Type 2: Gravel with binder (improved)

Adding a binding material (clay, lime, or oil) improves durability.

**Construction:**
1. Prepare the subgrade as above.
2. Spread 4–6 inches of coarse gravel.
3. Spread 1–2 inches of smaller gravel (pea gravel or crushed stone).
4. Spread a thin layer of binding material (clay slurry, lime mortar, or animal fat if available).
5. Compact thoroughly with water.
6. Allow to cure (if using lime, it hardens over days to weeks).

**Maintenance:** Less frequent re-compacting needed. Life span: 3–10 years depending on traffic and climate.

### Type 3: Stone surface (most durable)

Suitable for high-traffic roads and wet climates.

**Construction (Roman method—effective but labor-intensive):**

**Layer 1: Subgrade (as above)**

**Layer 2: Statumen (roughly 6–9 inches)**
- Large stones (4–8 inches) laid as densely as possible to distribute weight.
- Fill gaps with smaller stones and sand.
- Compact with a rammer.

**Layer 3: Ruderatio (roughly 4–6 inches)**
- Smaller stones (1–4 inches) and gravel.
- Compact with water and rammer.

**Layer 4: Nucleus (roughly 3–4 inches)**
- Very small stones (0.5–1 inch) mixed with sand and binding material (lime mortar or clay).
- Spread in 1–2 inch layers, compacting each layer.

**Layer 5: Summum dorsum (surface layer, roughly 1–2 inches)**
- Small stones (0.5–1 inch) fitted closely together.
- Gaps filled with fine gravel and binding material.
- Finish is smooth and compact.

**Maintenance:** Stone roads require less frequent maintenance than gravel. Periodic replacement of worn surface stones is needed. Life span: 20–50+ years with proper maintenance.

### Type 4: Paved surface (if lime or clay mortar available)

**Construction:**
1. Prepare the subgrade and lower layers as in Type 3.
2. Create a 2–3 inch layer of well-compacted sand or gravel.
3. Mix and lay a binding layer of lime mortar (see Vol 5: Chemistry) or clay-based mortar.
4. Place cut or quarried stone in a regular pattern (though irregular stones also work if tightly fitted).
5. Allow the mortar to cure before opening to traffic.

**Maintenance:** Similar to Type 3. Repointing (replacing failed mortar) extends life. Life span: 20–100+ years.

## Part 4: Specific Situations

### Mountain roads

- **Switchbacks:** On steep terrain, use switchbacks—a hairpin curve that reverses direction. This reduces grade on each segment.
- **Width:** Narrower roads are acceptable on switchbacks (but avoid single-width—add passing areas at intervals).
- **Drainage:** Steep slopes require good drainage to prevent erosion. Ditch placement is critical.
- **Surfaces:** Stone surface is better than gravel on steep slopes (less slipping).

### Water crossings

**Bridges:**
- **Simple span:** Logs or stones bridging a small stream (under 20 feet wide).
- **Multiple span:** For wider streams, use multiple logs or stone piers to support spans.
- **Construction:** See Vol 9: Materials for bridge-building details.

**Fords:**
- Suitable for shallow streams (under 2–3 feet deep) where construction is difficult.
- Stone bedding under the ford reduces erosion and provides stable footing.
- Mark ford routes clearly to prevent traffic from wandering into deeper water.

### Swamps or wet areas

- **Causeway:** Build up the road surface above the water level using stone and earth fill.
- **Drainage:** Cut ditches on both sides to lower the water table.
- **Surface:** Use stone or firm gravel to avoid sinking into mud.
- **Maintenance:** Frequent maintenance is needed in wet areas; erosion is rapid.

## Part 5: Labor Organization and Timeline

### Labor requirements

Road construction is labor-intensive. Estimates (per mile):

- **Unpaved gravel road:** 200–500 person-days (depends on terrain and quality)
- **Stone surface road:** 1000–3000 person-days
- **Labor organization:** Assign teams: one to excavate, one to haul, one to lay surface, one to compact

### Timeline

- **Gravel road on easy terrain:** 2–4 weeks with 50 workers
- **Stone road:** 8–16 weeks with 50 workers
- **Mountain road with switchbacks:** 4–12 weeks depending on terrain

Timeline is highly dependent on terrain and labor availability.

## Part 6: Maintenance

Roads deteriorate under traffic, weather, and time.

### Annual maintenance

- **Raking and smoothing:** Remove ruts and holes; re-compact the surface.
- **Ditch cleaning:** Clear blocked ditches to maintain drainage.
- **Stone surface:** Replace worn or missing stones.
- **Vegetation control:** Remove encroaching plants.

### Major maintenance

- **Subgrade repair:** If the subgrade settles or shifts, excavate and re-compact.
- **Surfacing:** After 10–20 years, resurfacing may be needed.
- **Bridge repair:** Wooden bridges deteriorate; periodic repair or replacement is necessary.

### Maintenance crews

- Assign teams of 10–20 people to maintain each 10–20 mile stretch of road.
- Schedule maintenance in spring (after winter damage) and fall (to prepare for winter).
- Keep supplies (gravel, stone, tools) at depots along the road.

## Verification

A well-built road:

1. **Has minimal ruts:** Traffic has not created significant depressions.
2. **Sheds water:** The crown and ditches keep water from pooling.
3. **Is passable in all weather:** Rain does not make it impassable (for a stone road).
4. **Allows reasonable travel speed:** Walkers can cover 2–4 miles per hour; carts 1–3 miles per hour (slower on hills).
5. **Shows minimal erosion:** Banks have not washed away; ditches are not gullied.
6. **Is maintained regularly:** Evidence of recent work shows investment in upkeep.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Building too steep:** Grades above 10% make roads difficult for carts and animals.
2. **Poor drainage:** Roads that retain water quickly develop ruts and wash away.
3. **Inadequate surface:** A gravel road in a wet climate rapidly degrades to mud.
4. **Insufficient width:** Narrow roads create congestion and are dangerous.
5. **Lack of maintenance:** Roads not maintained quickly deteriorate beyond repair.
6. **Poor route choice:** A route that avoids one hill but adds 20 miles is not efficient.
