## Overview

A ship is a vessel designed to float on water and transport cargo, passengers, or materials over water routes. Building a functioning ship requires knowledge of:

1. **Materials:** Wood properties, decay resistance, strength
2. **Design:** Hull shape for stability, capacity, and speed
3. **Structure:** How to assemble wooden frames and planking to create a strong hull
4. **Seaworthiness:** How to design for safety in waves and bad weather
5. **Propulsion and steering:** Sails, oars, rudders, and their design
6. **Logistics:** Launching, maintenance, and repair

In a post-collapse civilization rebuilding maritime infrastructure, the ability to build ships is valuable for:

- **Trade:** Moving goods along rivers and coasts
- **Fishing:** Small boats for harvest and food security
- **Communication:** Transportation of messages and people
- **Exploration:** Reaching new lands and resources
- **Defense:** Maritime control and communication

This chapter covers practical ship construction: vessel types, hull design principles, selecting materials, assembly methods, and how to test a new ship for seaworthiness.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of wood and material properties
- Familiarity with tool use (axes, saws, chisels, augers)
- Basic geometry and proportions
- Construction experience (at least basic carpentry)
- Access to timber resources and a suitable building site
- Willingness to work with teams (ship building is labor-intensive)

## Materials & Tools

**Materials (will vary by ship size and type):**
- Timber: oak (frames, structural), pine or spruce (planking), ash (oars and poles)
- Pitch or tar (waterproofing, caulking)
- Animal hair or oakum (fiber for caulking seams)
- Rope (rigging, mooring)
- Nails or wooden dowels (fastening)

**Tools:**
- Saw (for cutting timber: ripsaw, crosscut saw, pit saw)
- Axe and adze (shaping timber)
- Chisel and mallet (fine shaping, joint work)
- Auger or drill (making holes for pegs and fasteners)
- Plane (smoothing surfaces)
- Measuring and marking tools (tape, rule, chalk line)
- Caulking tools (hammer and caulking iron for driving fiber between planks)

**Workspace and support equipment:**
- Shipyard with slipway or cradle (supporting the hull during construction)
- Scaffolding (safe access during construction)
- Shoring and supports (preventing collapse)
- Supply of water and tools for ongoing work

## Part 1: Ship Types and Design Principles

### Vessel Classification by Size

**Small boats (under 10 meters):**
- Dinghies, rowboats, small fishing boats
- Oar or sail propelled
- Can be built and maintained by individuals or small teams
- Suitable for river transport and coastal work

**Medium vessels (10–30 meters):**
- Merchant cogs, fishing boats, river barges
- Sail or oar propelled; some use cargo transport
- Require skilled builders and teams of 5–10 workers
- Carry 10–50 tons of cargo

**Large vessels (30 meters and above):**
- Trading ships, galleons, warships
- Sail or (later) engine propelled
- Require large, experienced shipyards and teams of 20–50+ workers
- Carry 50–500+ tons of cargo
- Can undertake long ocean voyages

**This chapter focuses on small to medium vessels**, as they are more practical for post-collapse civilization rebuilding.

### Hull Shapes and Stability

The **hull** is the main body of the ship that contacts water. Hull shape determines:

1. **Stability:** How resistant the ship is to tipping over
2. **Capacity:** How much cargo can fit inside
3. **Speed:** How fast the ship moves through water
4. **Maneuverability:** How easily the ship can turn

**Key hull features:**

**Beam:** The width of the ship. A wider beam (relative to length) increases stability but reduces speed.

**Draft:** The depth of the ship below the waterline. A deeper draft provides stability and load-carrying capacity but limits access to shallow waters.

**Freeboard:** The distance from the waterline to the top of the hull. Taller freeboard keeps water out during waves but increases the risk of wind capsizing and makes launching harder.

**Common hull shapes:**

1. **Flat-bottomed (barge hull):**
   - Flat bottom, nearly parallel sides
   - Maximum cargo capacity
   - Low draft (can navigate shallow rivers)
   - Poor stability; can tip easily in waves
   - Use for: river transport, protected harbors

2. **Rounded hull (displacement hull):**
   - Curved bottom, gradually widening sides
   - Moderate cargo capacity
   - Better stability and seakeeping
   - Can navigate waves and rough water
   - Use for: coastal trading, fishing, general transport

3. **Double-ender (pointed bow and stern):**
   - Pointed ends reduce waves around the ship
   - Reduces resistance and improves speed
   - Can navigate better in rough water
   - Slightly less cargo capacity due to pointed ends
   - Use for: fast sailing ships, fishing boats, naval vessels

### Load and Buoyancy Calculation

A ship floats because its weight is supported by water displacement. Understanding this relationship is critical for safe design.

**Archimedes' principle:** A floating object displaces a weight of water equal to its own weight.

**For a 10-ton ship:**
- Displacement: 10 metric tons of water
- Volume of water displaced: 10 cubic meters (at fresh water density; slightly less for salt water)

**Stability:** The weight distribution affects stability. A low center of gravity (cargo placed low) improves stability. A high center of gravity (cargo on deck) reduces stability and increases capsizing risk.

**Load calculation example:**

- Ship hull weight: 5 tons
- Crew and supplies: 1 ton
- Total empty weight (lightweight): 6 tons
- Maximum allowable cargo: determine by stability and safety
- If you want a maximum total weight of 12 tons, cargo capacity is 6 tons

**Freeboard requirement:** The ship must have enough freeboard that waves do not wash over the deck. For a 12-ton ship with 1.5 meters freeboard, rough seas could fill the deck. Freeboard of 0.8–1.0 meter is typical for small vessels.

### Step 1: Design the Ship

Before building, sketch and calculate the basic dimensions:

1. **Intended use:** What cargo? What waters? How many crew?
2. **Capacity target:** Desired cargo capacity
3. **Proportions:**
   - Length: 2–4x the beam for cargo vessels
   - Beam: 1/4 to 1/3 of the length
   - Depth: 1/2 to 2/3 of the beam
4. **Rough calculations:**
   - Estimate hull volume (length × beam × depth × shape factor: 0.5–0.7)
   - Estimate hull weight (volume × wood density)
   - Determine remaining capacity for cargo

**Example design: Small cargo vessel**
- Length: 15 meters
- Beam: 4 meters
- Depth: 2 meters
- Volume estimate: 15 × 4 × 2 × 0.6 = 72 cubic meters
- Hull weight estimate: 72 × 0.4 (wood is less dense than water) = approximately 28 tons
- Total displacement: aim for 40 tons
- Cargo capacity: 40 - 28 = 12 tons

(These are rough estimates; actual design requires more precision.)

## Part 2: Timber Selection and Preparation

### Choosing Wood Types

**Frame timber (primary structure):**
- Species: Oak, ash, or other hardwood with high strength
- Properties: Hard, strong in compression, resists rot
- Use: Keel, frames (ribs), beams
- Size: Large (30+ cm diameter logs preferred)

**Planking (hull outside):**
- Species: Pine, spruce, or fir (softer, easier to work)
- Properties: Moderate strength, workable, water-resistant when sealed
- Use: Hull planks, deck planking
- Size: Medium (15–25 cm diameter logs)

**Interior components (bulkheads, supports):**
- Species: Various; less critical than structural timber
- Can use smaller or lower-grade wood
- Use: Cabin walls, shelving, stowage

**Decay resistance:**
- Oak: Highly decay-resistant
- Elm: Resistant, especially in water (traditional for submerged components)
- Pine/Spruce: Moderately resistant, requires pitch/tar coating
- Avoid: Softwoods without waterproofing (rot within 5–10 years)

### Timber Seasoning and Moisture

**Green (freshly cut) wood:**
- Contains high moisture (over 50%)
- Shrinks as it dries (5–10% linear shrinkage)
- Dries unevenly, causing checking (cracks) and warping
- Heavier (higher water content)

**Partially seasoned wood (air-dried 6–12 months):**
- Moisture content: 20–30%
- Better stability
- Acceptable for ship building if defects are minor

**Well-seasoned wood (dried 1–2 years or kiln-dried):**
- Moisture content: 12–20%
- Best performance
- Less likely to crack or warp
- Ideal but time-consuming to produce

**For practical ship building:** Use partially seasoned wood if possible; allow drying time before assembly. If time is critical, use green wood but accept that some warping and checking will occur (plan reinforcement to compensate).

### Timber Processing

**Selecting logs:**
- Choose straight logs with minimal knots or defects
- Avoid logs with large cracks or rot
- Diameter: At least 30 cm for frame timber

**Sawing to shape:**
- Pit saw (two-person saw) cuts logs into planks and timbers
- Convert a log into usable pieces: sawing takes 2–4 days per large log, depending on size and number of pieces

**Shaping components:**
- Frame pieces: adze rough-shape, then plane smooth
- Planks: cut to width, taper if needed, plane smooth
- Allow time for each component: 2–8 hours per frame piece depending on complexity

## Part 3: Building the Ship

### Shipyard Preparation

**Slipway or building ways:**
- A sloped surface (5–10% slope) on which the ship will rest during construction
- Supported by wooden blocks and timbers every 2–3 meters
- Must be strong enough to support the full weight of the hull under construction

**Scaffolding:**
- Working platforms at various heights
- Allows workers access to the hull during assembly
- Built as the hull grows

**Tools and materials nearby:**
- Timber storage (protected from sun and rain to prevent drying distortion)
- Water for testing and soaking wooden pegs
- Pitch and tar for sealing
- Rope and rigging materials
- Nails or wooden dowels

### Hull Assembly (Overview of Process)

**Stage 1: Keel and main frames (2–4 weeks)**
1. Lay the keel (main longitudinal timber)
2. Build the skeleton: frames (ribs) attached perpendicular to the keel
3. Support the skeleton with bracing to maintain shape during construction

**Stage 2: Planking (4–8 weeks)**
1. Attach the lowest planks (garboard strakes) to the keel
2. Progress upward, overlapping each plank (clinker construction) or edge-to-edge (carvel construction)
3. Caulk (drive fiber) between planks and seal with pitch
4. Continue until the hull is complete

**Stage 3: Deck and structural supports (2–4 weeks)**
1. Install beams (transverse supports for the deck)
2. Lay deck planking
3. Install bulkheads and internal bracing

**Stage 4: Launching and outfitting (1–2 weeks)**
1. Remove temporary supports
2. Slide the hull down the slipway into water
3. Install masts, sails, rigging
4. Add cabin, navigation equipment, cargo
5. Test and adjust as needed

**Total construction time for a 15-meter trading vessel: 3–6 months** with an experienced crew of 10–20 workers.

### Key Construction Techniques

**Frame joints:**
- Mortise and tenon: A protruding piece (tenon) on one timber fits into a hole (mortise) on another
- Used where frame meets keel, and frame-to-frame connections
- Provides excellent strength and stability

**Fastening:**
- Wooden dowels (pegs): Drive a round peg through aligned holes, expanding as it dries
- Iron nails: Faster but requires iron availability; prone to rust if not sealed
- Lashing: Rope ties, flexible but labor-intensive; suitable for emergency repairs

**Sealing (caulking):**
1. Drive fiber (oakum or animal hair) between plank seams using a caulking iron and mallet
2. Seal the fiber with hot pitch or tar, which hardens and waterproofs the joint
3. Properly caulked seams last decades; poorly caulked seams leak within months

**Waterproofing:**
- Pitch or tar coating on the hull (bottom and sides) prevents water absorption
- Coating also prevents marine organism boring and wood rot
- Recoating is needed every 2–5 years

### Step 1: Practical Frame Assembly (Simplified)

For a small vessel (10 meters), simplified construction:

1. **Keel preparation (2–3 days):**
   - Select a long, straight timber (15+ meters)
   - Dimension: 30 cm × 30 cm cross-section
   - Lay on the slipway, supported by blocks every 2 meters

2. **Frame construction (4–6 weeks for 20 frames):**
   - Cut frame pieces: curved timbers that form the ribs
   - Shape with axe and adze to match the intended hull curve
   - Attach perpendicular to the keel using mortise-and-tenon joints or large dowels
   - Space frames 0.75 meters apart
   - Brace frames temporarily with wooden poles to maintain shape

3. **Planking (6–8 weeks):**
   - Start at the bottom (closest to keel)
   - Attach first plank, shaping it to conform to the frames
   - Fasten with dowels or nails every 30–45 cm
   - Overlap planks (clinker) or edge-to-edge (carvel), depending on tradition and available labor
   - Drive caulking fiber between seams and seal with pitch
   - Progress upward until the hull is closed

4. **Deck and finishing (2–3 weeks):**
   - Install deck beams and plank the deck
   - Add bulkheads (walls)
   - Install any necessary bracing
   - Finish edges and prepare for launching

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Designing a Small Trading Vessel

**Requirements:**
- Cargo capacity: 20 tons
- Waters: River and coastal trade
- Crew: 5 people
- Intended life: 20+ years

**Design:**
- Length: 18 meters
- Beam: 4.5 meters
- Depth: 2.5 meters
- Estimated hull volume: 18 × 4.5 × 2.5 × 0.6 = 121 cubic meters
- Estimated hull weight: 50 tons (accounting for all components, not just displacement volume)
- Total displacement: 75 tons
- Cargo capacity: 75 - 50 (hull) - 2 (crew and provisions) = 23 tons ✓

**Hull shape:** Rounded bottom with modest beam. Stable enough for river currents and coastal waves.

**Timber requirements:**
- Keel: 1 large oak log (30+ cm diameter, 20 m length)
- Frames: 20 frames × 0.4 cubic meters = 8 cubic meters of hardwood
- Planking: 800 linear meters × 0.2 meters wide × 0.05 meters thick = 8 cubic meters of softwood
- Deck and interior: 5 cubic meters mixed
- Total timber: approximately 25 cubic meters, requiring 5–10 large logs or equivalent

### Example 2: Building Timeline and Labor

**Small vessel (12 meters, 10 tons capacity):**

| Phase | Duration | Crew | Tasks |
|-------|----------|------|-------|
| Design & planning | 1 week | 1–2 | Sketches, calculations, timber selection |
| Timber preparation | 3–4 weeks | 5–8 | Felling, sawing, shaping |
| Keel & frame setup | 2 weeks | 8–10 | Laying keel, building frame skeleton |
| Planking | 6–8 weeks | 10–15 | Attaching planks, caulking seams |
| Deck & interior | 2–3 weeks | 10–12 | Installing deck, bulkheads, finishing |
| Testing & adjustment | 1–2 weeks | 5–10 | Launching, trimming, outfitting |
| **Total** | **15–21 weeks** | **Avg 10** | **Completed vessel** |

**Resource requirements:**
- Timber: 15–20 cubic meters
- Pitch/tar: 200–300 liters
- Nails or dowels: 5,000–10,000 pieces
- Rope: 500+ meters
- Labor: Approximately 4,000–6,000 person-days

### Example 3: Troubleshooting a Leaky Hull

**Situation:** A new vessel (3 months old) is leaking at the waterline despite proper caulking.

**Diagnosis:**
1. Inspect from inside: water visible dripping from a seam between planks
2. Test with markers or candles: water jets in at specific points, indicating specific leaks
3. Likely causes: caulking fiber compressed with age, hull movement, or poor initial caulking

**Solutions:**
1. **In-water repair:**
   - Divers or swimmers jam additional fiber into the seam
   - Seal the outside with hot pitch
   - Effective but risky if waves are strong

2. **Careening (beaching):**
   - Tip the ship on its side at high tide
   - When tide drops, expose the leaking seam
   - Remove old caulking, drive new fiber, reseal with pitch
   - Right the ship on next high tide
   - Risky but effective; requires calm weather and suitable beach

3. **Accepting minor leaks:**
   - Small leaks are pumped out continuously by hand pumps
   - Not ideal but ships often operate with minor leaks
   - Schedule drydocking (putting in a shipyard) every 5 years for major repairs

## Common Mistakes

1. **Underestimating hull weight:** A wooden hull is heavier than you expect. A 15-meter trading vessel hull can weigh 30–50 tons before cargo. Miscalculating this results in overloading and instability.

2. **Using unsuitable timber:** Fast-growing softwoods without waterproofing rot within 5 years. Use decay-resistant species (oak, elm) for frames and other critical structural components.

3. **Insufficient freeboard:** A ship with only 0.5 meters of freeboard will be swamped by moderate waves. Design for at least 0.8–1.0 meter of freeboard for ocean work.

4. **Poor caulking:** The most common cause of leaks is inadequate caulking. Drive fiber firmly until the seam is fully packed, then seal thoroughly with pitch.

5. **Insufficient bracing during construction:** A hull without proper internal bracing can collapse or distort during construction. Install diagonal bracing and tie-rods to maintain shape.

6. **Launching too early:** A hull that has not fully seasoned can develop leaks within weeks. Wait until the wood has partially dried and settled.

7. **Ignoring grain direction:** Wood shrinks more along the growth rings than across the grain. Misaligned planking causes excessive warping and creates gaps.

8. **Overloading:** Loading cargo above the designed waterline results in capsizing. Mark the waterline and do not exceed it.

9. **Neglecting maintenance:** A ship not hauled out for painting and caulking inspection every 2–3 years will develop serious leaks. Maintenance extends the lifespan from 10–15 years to 30–50 years.

10. **Building without a plan:** Beginning construction without detailed drawings and calculations leads to structural problems and waste. Invest time in design before starting construction.
