## Overview

Metals do not occur as pure metal in the earth; they occur as ores—rocks containing metallic compounds mixed with worthless rock (gangue). Your settlement will need metal for tools, weapons, and construction. Before you can smelt ore, you must find it and identify it correctly. Mining the wrong rock wastes labor; smelting the wrong ore wastes fuel and produces no metal.

This chapter teaches you how to recognize 20 key ores by sight and simple field tests. You will learn to use a streak plate (any unglazed ceramic), a hardness reference (copper coin, steel knife, quartz), and dilute acid (vinegar or battery acid) to distinguish metal-bearing ores from worthless rock. These tests take minutes, require no complex equipment, and are accurate enough for practical mining decisions.

Your goal is simple: by year 2 post-collapse, you can walk into a rocky area, recognize iron, copper, tin, lead, zinc, and sulfur ore when you see it, and judge whether the ore body is worth mining.

## Materials & Tools

**Essential for all field work:**
- Streak plate: an unglazed ceramic tile (the back of a bathroom tile, unglazed porcelain dish, or a ceramic kiln brick). Cost: none (scavenge). Used for streak testing.
- Hardness reference set:
  - Fingernail (hardness 2.5) — can scratch softer minerals
  - Copper penny (hardness 3.0) — scratches soft ores like copper and iron oxides
  - Steel knife blade or file (hardness 5.5) — scratches medium-hard ores
  - Quartz crystal or piece of flint (hardness 7.0) — does NOT scratch by copper, scratches by knife only barely
- Acid for testing: dilute hydrochloric acid (10% concentration, made by diluting commercial battery acid 1:1 with water) OR vinegar (5% acetic acid — weaker but still useful)
- Small glass vial or container to hold acid
- Wooden stick or glass rod for applying acid
- Hand lens or magnifying glass (10x) — optional but useful
- Geological hammer or chisel for breaking rocks
- Field notebook for recording observations

**For testing specific properties:**
- Weight comparison: scale or balance (roughly compare ore weight vs. non-ore rock of same volume)
- Magnetic test: lodestone or a magnet (checks for iron minerals)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Learn the Properties of the 20 Key Ores (Study, 1–2 hours)

Memorize or create a reference card for these 20 ores. For each, you need: color, luster, hardness, streak color, acid reaction, and typical crystal form.

#### IRON ORES

**1. Hematite (Fe₂O₃) — Iron oxide, 60–70% iron**

- **Color:** Steel-gray to red-brown to black
- **Luster:** Metallic (gray variety) to dull (red variety)
- **Hardness:** 5.5–6.5 (scratches by knife, difficult)
- **Streak:** Red to red-brown (very diagnostic)
- **Acid reaction:** None (does not fizz)
- **Magnetism:** Not magnetic (or very weakly)
- **Form:** Massive, botryoidal (grape-like clusters), tabular crystals
- **Diagnostic test:** Red streak is the key. If it leaves a red streak on ceramic, it is almost certainly hematite.
- **Value for smelting:** Excellent ore, 60–70% iron content

**2. Magnetite (Fe₃O₄) — Iron oxide, 72% iron**

- **Color:** Black
- **Luster:** Metallic to sub-metallic
- **Hardness:** 5.5–6.5 (scratches by knife)
- **Streak:** Black
- **Acid reaction:** None
- **Magnetism:** Strongly magnetic (a magnet will attract it immediately)
- **Form:** Octahedral crystals, massive
- **Diagnostic test:** Strong magnetism. If a magnet sticks to it, it is magnetite. Red streak is NOT present (unlike hematite).
- **Value for smelting:** Excellent ore, 72% iron content, easier to find than hematite due to magnetism

**3. Limonite (iron oxyhydroxide, FeO(OH)·nH₂O) — Iron oxide with water, 40–60% iron**

- **Color:** Yellow-brown to dark brown
- **Luster:** Dull to earthy
- **Hardness:** 1–2 (easily scratched by fingernail or copper penny)
- **Streak:** Yellow-brown (distinctive)
- **Acid reaction:** None (does not fizz)
- **Magnetism:** Not magnetic
- **Form:** Earthy masses, botryoidal, dense
- **Diagnostic test:** Soft (scratch by fingernail), yellow-brown streak, color. Often found in gossan (weathered iron-rich rock at the surface of ore bodies).
- **Value for smelting:** Lower grade but accessible; often the first iron ore found near the surface

#### COPPER ORES

**4. Chalcopyrite (CuFeS₂) — Copper-iron sulfide, 35% copper**

- **Color:** Brass-yellow (tarnishes to purple, blue, or brown)
- **Luster:** Metallic
- **Hardness:** 3.5–4.0 (scratches by copper penny)
- **Streak:** Greenish-black
- **Acid reaction:** Reacts slowly with dilute HCl (may not fizz obviously; may turn solution blue)
- **Magnetism:** Not magnetic
- **Form:** Tetrahedral crystals, massive
- **Diagnostic test:** Brass-yellow color + tarnish (purple/blue) is diagnostic. Greenish-black streak. May turn acid solution blue (copper ion).
- **Value for smelting:** One of the most important copper ores, 35% copper content

**5. Malachite (Cu₂CO₃(OH)₂) — Copper carbonate, 60% copper but harder to smelt**

- **Color:** Bright green to dark green, often banded
- **Luster:** Silky to dull
- **Hardness:** 3.5–4.0 (scratches by copper penny)
- **Streak:** Light green
- **Acid reaction:** Strong reaction: fizzes and bubbles with HCl (carbonate reacts with acid)
- **Magnetism:** Not magnetic
- **Form:** Botryoidal, banded, crystals
- **Diagnostic test:** Bright green color + banding + strong acid fizz = malachite. Carbonate makes it more chemically available than chalcopyrite but produces CO₂ during smelting (complicates the process).
- **Value for smelting:** Highest copper content (60%) but more difficult to smelt than sulfide ores; easier than native copper to process

**6. Azurite (Cu₃(CO₃)₂(OH)₂) — Copper carbonate, 55% copper**

- **Color:** Bright blue (distinctive and diagnostic)
- **Luster:** Silky to dull
- **Hardness:** 3.5–4.0 (scratches by copper penny)
- **Streak:** Light blue
- **Acid reaction:** Strong fizz with HCl
- **Magnetism:** Not magnetic
- **Form:** Botryoidal, crystals
- **Diagnostic test:** Bright blue color is unmistakable. Fizzes in acid. Often found with malachite.
- **Value for smelting:** High copper content, similar properties to malachite

**7. Chalcocite (Cu₂S) — Copper sulfide, 80% copper**

- **Color:** Black (shiny)
- **Luster:** Metallic
- **Hardness:** 2.5–3.0 (scratches by copper penny)
- **Streak:** Black, shiny
- **Acid reaction:** Weak reaction; may produce faint smell of H₂S (rotten egg)
- **Magnetism:** Not magnetic
- **Form:** Massive, pseudocubic crystals
- **Diagnostic test:** Black metallic sheen + low hardness + copper-rich. Sulfide odor in acid is diagnostic. Highest copper content of any common ore (80%).
- **Value for smelting:** Excellent ore, 80% copper content

#### TIN ORES

**8. Cassiterite (SnO₂) — Tin oxide, 78.6% tin**

- **Color:** Brown, black, or colorless
- **Luster:** Adamantine (diamond-like) to metallic
- **Hardness:** 6–7 (not scratched by knife, scratches by quartz)
- **Streak:** White (very diagnostic)
- **Acid reaction:** None
- **Magnetism:** Not magnetic
- **Form:** Tetragonal crystals, gravel-like (stream deposits), massive
- **Diagnostic test:** Very hard (harder than knife blade), white streak, high density (feels heavy for size). White streak is diagnostic for cassiterite.
- **Value for smelting:** Nearly pure tin oxide; 78.6% tin is excellent. Often found in alluvial deposits (streambeds).

#### LEAD ORES

**9. Galena (PbS) — Lead sulfide, 86.6% lead**

- **Color:** Silver-gray to black
- **Luster:** Metallic
- **Hardness:** 2.5 (soft, scratches by fingernail)
- **Streak:** Gray-black
- **Acid reaction:** Very weak (may not visibly react)
- **Magnetism:** Not magnetic
- **Form:** Perfect cubic crystals, massive, often cleaves into cubes
- **Diagnostic test:** Soft + metallic sheen + cubic form + high density. Lead is dense, so galena feels very heavy. Cubic cleavage is diagnostic.
- **Value for smelting:** The primary lead ore; 86.6% lead content. Very valuable.

#### ZINC ORES

**10. Sphalerite (ZnS) — Zinc sulfide, 67% zinc**

- **Color:** Black, brown, yellow, or red (color varies widely)
- **Luster:** Resinous to submetallic
- **Hardness:** 3.5–4.0 (scratches by copper penny)
- **Streak:** White, yellow, or brown (varies)
- **Acid reaction:** No visible reaction (weak)
- **Magnetism:** Not magnetic, but may be slightly magnetic if iron is present
- **Form:** Tetrahedral crystals, massive
- **Diagnostic test:** Color varies (not diagnostic alone). Resinous luster + perfect tetrahedral cleavage. Often found with galena.
- **Value for smelting:** Important zinc ore, 67% zinc content. Harder to smelt than copper or iron.

**11. Smithsonite (ZnCO₃) — Zinc carbonate, 52% zinc**

- **Color:** White, yellow, brown, or pink
- **Luster:** Vitreous to pearly
- **Hardness:** 4.5–5.0 (scratches by copper penny)
- **Streak:** White
- **Acid reaction:** Fizzes with HCl (strong carbonate reaction)
- **Magnetism:** Not magnetic
- **Form:** Botryoidal, rhombohedral crystals
- **Diagnostic test:** Fizzes in acid (carbonate), white streak, associated with lead-zinc deposits.
- **Value for smelting:** Zinc carbonate; easier to process than sphalerite in some smelting methods

#### SULFUR ORES

**12. Pyrite (FeS₂) — Iron disulfide**

- **Color:** Brass-yellow (metallic)
- **Luster:** Metallic
- **Hardness:** 6–6.5 (not scratched by knife)
- **Streak:** Greenish-black
- **Acid reaction:** No obvious reaction
- **Magnetism:** Not magnetic
- **Form:** Cubic or pentagonal dodecahedral crystals, very common
- **Diagnostic test:** Brass-yellow color (like chalcopyrite) but MUCH harder (quartz hardness vs. copper penny hardness). Cubic crystals. Sparks when struck (produces sparks; useful for fire-starting).
- **Value for smelting:** NOT a good ore for copper (often mistaken for chalcopyrite). But valuable as a source of sulfuric acid (in later stages). Can be used for sulfur extraction.

**13. Marcasite (FeS₂) — Iron disulfide, polymorph of pyrite**

- **Color:** Pale brass-yellow to light bronze
- **Luster:** Metallic
- **Hardness:** 6–6.5 (not scratched by knife)
- **Streak:** Greenish-black
- **Acid reaction:** No obvious reaction
- **Magnetism:** Not magnetic
- **Form:** Orthorhombic crystals (different from pyrite's cubic form), often tabular
- **Diagnostic test:** Similar to pyrite but lighter color and different crystal form. Less common than pyrite.
- **Value for smelting:** Similar to pyrite; not useful for iron extraction.

**14. Stibnite (Sb₂S₃) — Antimony sulfide**

- **Color:** Gray to black (metallic)
- **Luster:** Metallic, splendent
- **Hardness:** 2.0 (very soft, scratches by fingernail)
- **Streak:** Gray (metallic)
- **Acid reaction:** No obvious reaction
- **Magnetism:** Not magnetic
- **Form:** Long prismatic or acicular (needle-like) crystals, striated
- **Diagnostic test:** Very soft + metallic gray + long needle-like crystals. Antimony compounds are toxic; handle with care.
- **Value for smelting:** Antimony was used as a hardener in ancient metalwork. Not critical but useful.

#### NICKEL ORES

**15. Pentlandite ((Fe,Ni)₉S₈) — Nickel-iron sulfide, 30% nickel**

- **Color:** Yellow-bronze
- **Luster:** Metallic
- **Hardness:** 3.5–4.0 (scratches by copper penny)
- **Streak:** Bronze-brown
- **Acid reaction:** Weak, may smell of H₂S (rotten egg)
- **Magnetism:** Slightly magnetic
- **Form:** Massive (crystals rare)
- **Diagnostic test:** Bronze color + slightly magnetic + H₂S smell in acid. Often found with iron sulfides.
- **Value for smelting:** Nickel is valuable for hardening steel alloys. Less common than iron ores.

**16. Niccolite (NiAs) — Nickel arsenide, 43% nickel**

- **Color:** Pale copper-red
- **Luster:** Metallic
- **Hardness:** 5.0–5.5 (scratches by knife)
- **Streak:** Pale copper-red
- **Acid reaction:** May produce garlic odor (arsenic compound!)
- **Magnetism:** Not magnetic
- **Form:** Hexagonal crystals, massive
- **Diagnostic test:** Copper-red color + hardness + arsenic smell. WARNING: arsenic is toxic. Handle with care.
- **Value for smelting:** Nickel source, but arsenic makes it hazardous.

#### CHROMIUM ORES

**17. Chromite ((Fe,Mg)Cr₂O₄) — Chromium oxide, 46% chromium**

- **Color:** Black to brown
- **Luster:** Sub-metallic to metallic
- **Hardness:** 5.5–6.0 (scratches by knife)
- **Streak:** Brown
- **Acid reaction:** None
- **Magnetism:** Weakly magnetic
- **Form:** Octahedral crystals, massive
- **Diagnostic test:** Black color + brown streak + weak magnetism. Often found in dunite (olivine rock).
- **Value for smelting:** Chromium is used for hardening steel. Not as economically important as iron, copper, or tin.

#### MANGANESE ORES

**18. Pyrolusite (MnO₂) — Manganese dioxide, 63% manganese**

- **Color:** Black to dark gray
- **Luster:** Metallic to sub-metallic
- **Hardness:** 2.0–2.5 (soft, scratches by fingernail)
- **Streak:** Black (marks your fingers or notebook paper)
- **Acid reaction:** Weak, but reacts with strong acid; produces Cl₂ gas if mixed with HCl (dangerous!)
- **Magnetism:** Not magnetic
- **Form:** Cubic crystals, prismatic, massive
- **Diagnostic test:** Black streak on paper, very soft, marks fingers. Dangerous with HCl (do NOT test with acid).
- **Value for smelting:** Manganese hardens steel. Less critical than iron, copper, tin. Requires careful smelting.

**19. Rhodochrosite (MnCO₃) — Manganese carbonate, 48% manganese**

- **Color:** Pink to red
- **Luster:** Vitreous to pearly
- **Hardness:** 3.5–4.0 (scratches by copper penny)
- **Streak:** White
- **Acid reaction:** Fizzes with HCl (carbonate reaction)
- **Magnetism:** Not magnetic
- **Form:** Rhombohedral crystals, banded
- **Diagnostic test:** Pink color + banding + acid fizz. Beautiful but less common than pyrolusite.
- **Value for smelting:** Manganese source; easier to process than pyrolusite due to carbonate chemistry.

#### ALUMINUM ORES

**20. Bauxite (Al₂O₃·2H₂O + other aluminum oxyhydroxides) — Aluminum oxide, 30–60% aluminum**

- **Color:** White, yellow, red, or brown (varies widely)
- **Luster:** Earthy to dull
- **Hardness:** 1–3 (soft, scratches by copper penny or fingernail)
- **Streak:** White to pale yellow
- **Acid reaction:** None (resistant to weak acid, but reacts with strong base solutions)
- **Magnetism:** Not magnetic
- **Form:** Earthy, pisolitic (round pea-like grains), massive, botryoidal
- **Diagnostic test:** Earthy appearance + color variation + low hardness. Often found in tropical weathering environments.
- **Value for smelting:** The primary aluminum ore. Aluminum smelting is energy-intensive and requires electricity (requires electrolysis); impractical in early post-collapse. But bauxite is abundant and may be worth mining for later use.

### Step 2: Conduct Streak Test (First Test)

**Why streak test?** The color of a mineral in hand can be misleading (some ores are iridescent or tarnished). The streak (color of the powder) is more consistent.

**Procedure:**
1. Take the mineral specimen
2. Rub it firmly across the unglazed ceramic streak plate at a 45° angle
3. Observe the color of the powder left on the plate
4. Compare to your reference (e.g., hematite leaves RED, magnetite leaves BLACK, etc.)

**Interpretation:**
- Hematite → red streak (diagnostic)
- Magnetite → black streak + strong magnetism
- Limonite → yellow-brown streak
- Chalcopyrite → greenish-black streak
- Malachite → light green streak
- Azurite → light blue streak
- Cassiterite → white streak (very diagnostic)
- Galena → gray-black streak
- Sphalerite → white, yellow, or brown streak (color varies)

If the streak matches one of your key ores, you have made a tentative identification. Proceed to hardness test to confirm.

### Step 3: Conduct Hardness Test (Second Test)

**Why hardness test?** Some ores have similar colors but different hardness. Hardness is a reliable secondary test.

**Procedure:**
1. Try to scratch the ore with your fingernail (hardness 2.5)
2. If it scratches easily → ore is soft (hardness &lt;3)
3. If it does not scratch, try copper penny (hardness 3.0)
4. If copper penny scratches it → hardness 3–4
5. If not, try steel knife (hardness 5.5)
6. If knife barely scratches it → hardness 5–6
7. If knife does not scratch, try quartz/flint (hardness 7)
8. If quartz scratches the ore, it is harder than 7

**Hardness reference for your 20 ores:**
- **Soft (&lt;3):** Limonite, Galena, Pyrolusite, Rhodochrosite, Sphalerite, Smithsonite, Chalcopyrite, Malachite, Azurite, Chalcocite, Stibnite
- **Medium (3–4):** Chalcopyrite, Malachite, Azurite, Sphalerite, Smithsonite, Niccolite (5), Pentlandite, Galena, Stibnite
- **Hard (5–6):** Hematite, Magnetite, Pyrite, Marcasite, Niccolite, Chromite
- **Very hard (6–7):** Cassiterite

Hardness combined with color and streak significantly narrows your choices.

### Step 4: Conduct Acid Test (Third Test)

**Why acid test?** Carbonates (like malachite, azurite, smithsonite, rhodochrosite) fizz or bubble when exposed to dilute acid. Sulfides may produce a rotten-egg smell. These reactions help distinguish ores.

**Procedure:**
1. Place a small drop of dilute HCl (or vinegar) on the ore specimen
2. Observe:
   - **Fizzing/bubbling:** Strong acid reaction = carbonate ore (malachite, azurite, smithsonite, rhodochrosite, galena)
   - **Rotten-egg smell:** H₂S odor = sulfide ore (chalcopyrite, chalcocite, sphalerite, pyrite)
   - **No reaction:** Oxide ore (hematite, magnetite, cassiterite)
   - **Solution turns blue:** Copper ions (chalcopyrite, chalcocite, malachite, azurite)
   - **Dangerous gases:** Pyrolusite + HCl = toxic Cl₂ gas. Do NOT test pyrolusite with HCl.

**Caution:** Use acid in a well-ventilated area. Wear gloves. Do not inhale fumes.

### Step 5: Check Magnetism (Fourth Test)

**Why magnetism test?** Magnetite is uniquely strongly magnetic. This is the fastest way to identify it.

**Procedure:**
1. Bring a magnet near the ore specimen
2. If the magnet strongly attracts it → **magnetite**
3. If weak or no attraction → other ore

### Step 6: Combine Tests and Identify

Use your test results systematically:

**Flowchart example:**

```
Is it metallic and VERY hard (quartz hardness or harder)?
  → YES: Cassiterite (white streak) or Pyrite (brass-yellow, sparks)
  → NO: Continue

Does it fizz in acid?
  → YES: Carbonate ore
    Is it green or banded? → Malachite
    Is it bright blue? → Azurite
    Is it white? → Smithsonite
    Is it pink/banded? → Rhodochrosite
  → NO: Continue

Is it strongly magnetic?
  → YES: Magnetite
  → NO: Continue

Is it red or red-brown in color? Leaves red streak?
  → YES: Hematite
  → NO: Continue

Is it brass-yellow? Greenish-black streak? Soft?
  → YES: Chalcopyrite
  → NO: Continue

... (continue checking properties)
```

### Step 7: Record and Map Your Findings

Create a field notebook entry for each ore location:

```
Date: 2026-07-15
Location: 2 km northeast of settlement, 100m elevation
Ore type: Hematite (identified by red streak, hardness 6, non-magnetic)
Color: Red-brown
Estimated quantity: Large outcrop, visible for ~50m along hillside
Richness (visual): High density, ~40% ore in host rock

```

## Verification

**Success looks like:**
- You can identify each of the 20 ores by sight and simple field tests in &lt;5 minutes
- You have located at least one ore body (iron, copper, or tin) near your settlement by month 6
- Your mining crew is extracting from the ore body by month 12

**Failure looks like:**
- You mine the wrong rock (pyrite instead of chalcopyrite, for example)
- You waste fuel smelting ore that is too poor quality to yield metal
- You spend months searching for ore without a systematic approach

## Common Mistakes

1. **"Chalcopyrite and Pyrite are the same thing"** — Wrong. Chalcopyrite is soft (scratches by copper penny), contains 35% copper, and is a valuable ore. Pyrite is hard (not scratched by knife), contains NO copper, and is useless for copper smelting. Test hardness: copper penny scratches chalcopyrite but NOT pyrite.

2. **"If it's yellow and metallic, it's gold/copper"** — Brass-yellow metallic minerals are usually pyrite (useless) or chalcopyrite (valuable). Use hardness test to distinguish. Pyrite sparks when struck; chalcopyrite doesn't.

3. **"All black minerals are iron ore"** — Black minerals include magnetite (iron ore, magnetic), galena (lead ore, very soft), pyrolusite (manganese ore, very soft and black-streaking), and sphalerite (zinc ore, can be black). Test hardness, streak, and magnetism.

4. **"If it fizzes in acid, it's worthless"** — Wrong. Carbonates like malachite, azurite, and smithsonite fizz in acid and are valuable ores. Acid fizzing tells you it's a carbonate, not that it's worthless.

5. **"Ore identification requires a microscope"** — You can identify most ores in the field with streak plate, hardness tests, and acid. A hand lens helps, but is not required.

6. **"All green minerals are copper ore"** — Malachite and azurite are green copper ores, but other green minerals exist (olivine, serpentine, chlorite) that contain no copper. Confirm with streak (should be green) and acid test (should fizz and/or turn solution blue).

7. **"We should mine all ore bodies equally"** — No. High-grade, shallow, easily-accessible ore bodies are worth mining first. Deep or low-grade bodies should wait. Assess location, richness, and accessibility before committing mining resources.

8. **"Cassiterite is rare and hard to find"** — False. Cassiterite is common in alluvial deposits (streambeds). It is hard and dense, so it sinks to the bottom of streams. Pan streambeds in tin-bearing regions.
