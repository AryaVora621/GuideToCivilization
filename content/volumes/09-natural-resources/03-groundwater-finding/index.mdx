## Overview

Water is your most critical resource after collapse. Surface water (rivers, lakes) is visible but often contaminated. Groundwater—water stored underground in rock and soil—is usually cleaner, more reliable, and accessible year-round. The challenge is finding it. Your settlement cannot survive on guessing.

Dowsing (using rods or pendulums to find water) is myth, not geology. Dowsing works no better than random chance when tested scientifically. However, geology works. Understanding how water moves through rock, where it accumulates, and what surface signs indicate groundwater below allows you to find water reliably. This chapter teaches you the science of groundwater and the field techniques to locate it.

Your goal is simple: by year 1, you can walk any landscape, read the geology, and predict where groundwater exists. By year 2, you have dug at least one successful well or developed a spring.

## Materials & Tools

**For field reconnaissance:**
- Geological hammer or chisel for breaking rocks and examining subsurface
- Hand lens (10x magnifying glass) to examine rock texture and grain size
- Notebook and pencil for recording observations
- Topographic map or hand-drawn map of your area showing elevation changes
- Compass (to orient observations)
- Measuring tape (to estimate depth of features, distance to seeps)

**For testing groundwater presence (optional but useful):**
- Bucket and rope (for testing wells)
- pH testing strips (groundwater is usually slightly acidic; pH 6–8 is good)
- Taste (groundwater often has minerals; taste tells you if it is fresh or salty)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Understand Aquifers and How Groundwater Forms

Groundwater is not an underground lake. It is water filling the pores (small spaces) in rock and soil. Understanding aquifer types tells you where to look.

**Aquifer type 1: Unconfined aquifer (most common, easiest to access)**

- **What it is:** A layer of permeable rock (sand, gravel, porous sandstone) saturated with water, sitting above an impermeable layer (clay, bedrock).
- **How it forms:** Rain soaks into permeable rock, fills the pores, and sits on top of clay (which does not allow water to pass through).
- **Characteristics:** The water table (top of the water-filled zone) is relatively shallow (5–30 meters in many places). Water table rises and falls with seasons (high in spring/wet season, low in summer/dry season).
- **How to find it:** Dig a well until you hit water. Usually 10–20 meters deep.
- **Storage:** Can hold significant water, but depletes if overused (especially in dry seasons).

**Aquifer type 2: Confined aquifer (deeper, longer-lasting)**

- **What it is:** A layer of permeable rock (sandstone, gravel) sandwiched between two impermeable layers (clay, shale).
- **How it forms:** Water entered the porous layer millions of years ago, got trapped by overlying clay, and remains under pressure.
- **Characteristics:** Deep (20–100+ meters), water is often artesian (flows upward on its own due to pressure).
- **How to find it:** Dig or drill past multiple rock layers until you hit permeable rock with water.
- **Storage:** Can hold enormous quantities of water; lasts longer than unconfined aquifers.
- **Danger:** Confined aquifers recharge very slowly (over centuries to millennia); overpumping depletes them permanently on human timescales.

**Aquifer type 3: Fractured rock aquifer (common in uplands)**

- **What it is:** Water stored in cracks and fractures within hard rock (granite, basalt, limestone).
- **How it forms:** Rain seeps into cracks and fractures in bedrock. Water follows cracks downward.
- **Characteristics:** Water yield is often low (small flow rates), but present in many upland locations.
- **How to find it:** Locate fractures (visible cracks in rock faces), follow them downward, and dig near the base of fractures.
- **Storage:** Moderate; depends on fracture density and rock depth.

### Step 2: Use Geology to Predict Groundwater Depth

Before you dig, predict how deep you must dig.

**Step 2A: Examine visible rock types**

Walk your settlement area and note the rock types:
- **Sand and gravel:** Highly permeable; water table is usually 5–15 meters deep
- **Silty clay:** Low permeability; water table may be deeper (15–50 meters) or absent in shallow layers
- **Solid bedrock (granite, basalt):** Impermeable (unless fractured); water is in cracks, may be deep or absent
- **Limestone:** Permeable (dissolves in weak acid from rainwater), often has caves; water can be shallow or deep
- **Sandstone:** Permeable if well-sorted (grains are similar size); may hold water at moderate depth

**Step 2B: Examine topography (elevation and slope)**

- **Hilltops:** Water table is usually deeper (gravity pulls water downward)
- **Valleys:** Water table is usually shallower (groundwater flows toward valleys)
- **Slopes:** Water flows downslope; look for seeps lower on the slope

**Step 2C: Use existing wells (if any)**

If your settlement has any wells dug before collapse or by previous inhabitants:
- Measure their depth
- Determine how long they stayed full
- Use this as a baseline for your area

### Step 3: Look for Natural Spring Indicators

Springs are places where groundwater naturally flows to the surface. Finding springs is the easiest way to locate water.

**Indicator 1: Seeps and wet patches**

- **What to look for:** Areas of permanently damp ground, wet rocks, or flowing water in small trickles
- **Geology:** Seeps occur where permeable rock meets impermeable rock (e.g., sand over clay), forcing water upward
- **Clue:** If the seep flows all year (even in dry season), it indicates reliable groundwater
- **How to follow it:** Trace water upslope; it often indicates an aquifer above

**Indicator 2: Vegetation anomalies**

- **What to look for:** Lush vegetation (green trees, grass) in otherwise dry areas; willows or cottonwoods growing in unexpected places
- **Geology:** Plants indicate shallow water tables. Where you see willows far from surface rivers, groundwater is likely 3–10 meters down.
- **Clue:** In semi-arid regions, a band of green vegetation often marks a groundwater zone
- **Caution:** Not all vegetation is at groundwater zones; some plants have deep roots or access surface water

**Indicator 3: Rock color and weathering**

- **What to look for:** Rust-colored (iron oxide) staining, white salt deposits, or mineral crusts on rock
- **Geology:** Mineral deposits indicate water has moved through the rock for a long time; water is often present below the surface
- **Clue:** Rust staining (red or orange streaks) indicates iron minerals were oxidized by groundwater; water is likely near

**Indicator 4: Slope breaks and terraces**

- **What to look for:** An abrupt change in slope angle, a natural bench or terrace, or a line of springs across a hillside
- **Geology:** Springs often align on hillsides where an impermeable layer (clay) meets a permeable layer (sand). This creates a line of seeps.
- **Clue:** Walk upslope from any seep; you may find more seeps higher up, indicating the aquifer

**Indicator 5: Rock type transitions**

- **What to look for:** A visible boundary between two different rock types (e.g., sandstone over shale, gravel over clay)
- **Geology:** Water accumulates at the contact between permeable and impermeable layers
- **Clue:** Dig or probe at rock type boundaries

### Step 4: Identify Artesian Wells (Pressurized Groundwater)

Artesian wells flow on their own without pumping. They are rare but incredibly valuable.

**Conditions for an artesian well:**
1. An unconfined or confined aquifer uphill from your location
2. Permeable rock that lets water seep down to a deeper aquifer
3. An impermeable cap (clay, shale) trapping water under pressure
4. A low point (your location) where the cap is broken, allowing water to flow up

**How to recognize an artesian location:**
- **Slope:** Your location is lower than nearby hills where rain falls
- **Rock layers:** If you dig, you will hit layers of sand (permeable) and clay (impermeable) in alternation
- **Existing wells:** If any existing wells in your area flow without pumping, an artesian aquifer likely exists

**How to develop an artesian well:**
1. Dig or drill through the confining layer (clay, shale)
2. Once you break through to the pressurized aquifer below, water should flow upward
3. If water does not flow immediately, you may need to develop the well by pumping to clear fine sediment

**Caution:** Artesian wells are not permanent; they can be depleted if overpumped or if the source aquifer recharges slowly.

### Step 5: Conduct a Hydrogeological Survey

Perform this survey systematically in your settlement area to find the best well location.

**Survey procedure (3–5 days of fieldwork):**

**Day 1: Walk the entire settlement area and mark all water sources and indicators**
- Springs (flowing water)
- Seeps (damp ground)
- Vegetation anomalies (unexpected green patches)
- Rock type boundaries
- Slope breaks and terraces
- Existing wells (if any)
- Mark each on a hand-drawn map with coordinates

**Day 2: Examine each water source closely**
- Measure flow rate: collect water in a container, time it (e.g., 1 liter in 10 seconds = flow rate of 0.1 L/sec)
- Measure temperature: cool water (5–10°C) suggests deep aquifer; warm water (15+°C) suggests shallow
- Observe color: clear is good; brown or orange suggests iron; milky suggests clay
- Test pH: use pH strips if available
- Look for algae or moss: indicates long-term presence of water

**Day 3: Dig test holes or examine exposed rock**
- At 2–3 locations (near water indicators), dig or use a auger to bore 5–10 meters deep
- Note the rock types and depth of the first water-bearing layer
- If water is found, measure the depth and note the rock type

**Day 4: Identify the best well location**
- Compare all findings
- Prioritize locations with:
  - Shallowest water table (&lt;30 meters)
  - Most reliable water source (perennial, year-round flow)
  - Best geology (sandy/gravelly aquifer, not fractured rock)
  - Highest flow rate or lowest evaporation risk
  - Closest to settlement (minimum pumping distance)

**Day 5: Mark the well location and plan construction**
- Mark the chosen location with a cairn or flag
- Record bearing and distance from settlement landmarks
- Sketch the subsurface geology (rock layers, water depth)
- Plan well construction method (hand-dug, bored, drilled)

### Step 6: Distinguish Dowsing Myth from Geology

**What dowsing claims:**
- A Y-shaped stick (willow, hazel) or metal rods will twist or point downward near groundwater, allowing you to find water without geology knowledge.

**Why dowsing appears to work:**
- In areas with shallow groundwater, almost any location has water 10–30 meters down, so the dowser will eventually find it
- Confirmation bias: people remember the rare times the dowser found water, forget the many times they didn't
- In humid regions with high rainfall, water is everywhere in the subsurface; dowsing "works" by chance

**Why dowsing fails scientifically:**
- Controlled tests (double-blind, randomized): dowsers perform no better than random chance (~50% success in finding water in areas where water is present at all locations)
- No physical mechanism: water creates no magnetic or electrical field strong enough to affect a stick from several meters away
- Dowsers disagree: different dowsers mark different locations as "water," all claiming to find water

**What works (geology):**
- Understand rock types and water movement
- Look for natural springs and seeps (surface evidence of groundwater)
- Use topography to predict depth
- Dig in the right places based on geology, not chance

**Practical recommendation:**
Use geology to narrow your search to 2–3 likely locations. Dig at the most promising location first. If it fails (dry well at 30 meters), move to the second location. This is faster and more reliable than dowsing.

### Step 7: Assess Well Sustainability

Once you find water, assess whether it is sustainable for your settlement's needs.

**Calculate daily water demand:**
- Drinking and cooking: 2 liters per person per day = 2,000 L for 1,000 people
- Sanitation (washing, latrines): 10 liters per person per day = 10,000 L
- Agriculture (if irrigating): 5,000–20,000 L per day per hectare (highly variable)
- **Total: 12,000–40,000 L per day for a small settlement**

**Assess well capacity:**
- Measure flow rate: how many liters per hour can you pump from the well?
- Measure depth to water: how long do you need to pump before the well runs dry?
- Estimate recharge rate: how long does it take for the water table to recover after pumping?

**Example:**
- Well flow rate: 1,000 L per hour (17 L per minute)
- Settlement demand: 20,000 L per day
- Pumping needed: 20 hours per day (out of 24 hours)
- Conclusion: Sustainable with moderate effort, but not luxurious

**If well capacity is insufficient:**
- Dig a second well (if geology allows)
- Reduce demand (collect rainwater, use grey water for irrigation)
- Use shallow seeps or springs for non-potable uses (agriculture, washing)

## Verification

**Success looks like:**
- By month 3: You have located at least 2–3 groundwater sources (springs, seeps, or indicators)
- By month 6: You have dug a test hole and confirmed the water table depth
- By month 12: You have a functional well providing 80%+ of your settlement's water demand

**Failure looks like:**
- You dig wells randomly (no geological basis) and fail to find water
- You find water but it is too deep or flows too slowly to meet demand
- Water quality is poor (salty, iron-rich, contaminated)
- Well fails in dry season, forcing you to rely on dangerous surface sources

## Common Mistakes

1. **"Dowsing works; I'll use a Y-stick to find water"** — Dowsing has no scientific basis. You will waste time and resources chasing random locations. Use geology instead.

2. **"Water is the same everywhere underground; I'll dig anywhere"** — Wrong. Aquifers exist in specific geological settings. Dig in the wrong rock type and you will drill past all water layers.

3. **"If a spring flows now, it will flow forever"** — Seasonal springs dry up in dry season. Before committing to a spring, observe it for a full year (wet and dry season) to confirm it is perennial.

4. **"Deeper is better; I'll dig a 100-meter well to ensure plenty of water"** — Digging 100 meters is labor-intensive. Most settlements find adequate water at 10–30 meters. Use geology to find shallow water first.

5. **"Salt water is underground; I need to worry about salty wells"** — In continental (non-coastal) areas, groundwater is usually fresh. Salt water is found in coastal aquifers or very old, deep aquifers. Look for natural seeps; if they are fresh, your well water will be too.

6. **"That green patch in the desert means a big aquifer"** — Not necessarily. A single seep supporting vegetation is valuable but may not yield enough for a full settlement. Develop multiple water sources.

7. **"Once we dig a well, we can pump it dry if we need to"** — Overpumping lowers the water table for neighboring wells (if you have them) and can permanently damage the aquifer's recharge capacity. Pump sustainably.

8. **"Iron-stained rock means iron ore, not water"** — Iron staining can indicate both ore and water. Look at the whole picture: if the iron staining is associated with seeps, water is present.
