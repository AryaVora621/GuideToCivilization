## Overview

Geology is the study of Earth materials and structures. Post-collapse, geology determines where resources are found (metals, clays, water, fuel). Understanding basic geology allows settlements to locate and extract resources.

This chapter covers:
1. **Rocks and minerals:** Basic classification and identification.
2. **Geological structures:** How rocks are arranged (layers, faults, formations).
3. **Reading the landscape:** Interpreting visible geology.
4. **Resource discovery:** Finding ores, gems, water, useful materials.
5. **Settlement planning:** Locating resources near settlements.

## Prerequisites

Understand environmental systems (Vol 13, Ch 1-3). Know survival basics (Vol 3, Ch 1-2).

## Part 1: Rocks and Minerals Fundamentals

### What is a mineral?

**Definition:** Naturally occurring, solid, crystalline material with specific chemical composition.

**Common minerals post-collapse:**
- Quartz (SiO2): Hard; abundant; used for tools, glass.
- Feldspar: Common rock-forming mineral; slightly softer than quartz.
- Mica: Shiny; splits into sheets; used for insulation, writing surfaces.
- Calcite (CaCO3): Soft; reacts with acid; forms limestone, marble.
- Hematite (Fe2O3): Iron ore; dark red/black; dense.
- Galena (PbS): Lead ore; shiny; dense; heavy.

### What is a rock?

**Definition:** Solid aggregate of minerals; forms through geological processes.

**Three rock types:**

**Igneous rocks (formed from cooling magma):**
- Granite: Large crystals; visible minerals; hard (mountains, tools).
- Basalt: Fine crystals; dark; very hard (lava flows, tools).
- Obsidian: Glass-like; sharp edges; volcanic (cutting tools, weapons).

**Sedimentary rocks (formed from compressed sediment):**
- Sandstone: Medium hardness; feels sandy; porous (building stone).
- Limestone: Soft to medium; reacts with acid; light colored; from shells/coral.
- Shale: Soft; flakes easily; formed from clay and mud.

**Metamorphic rocks (formed from heat/pressure on existing rocks):**
- Marble: Hard; from compressed limestone; takes polish (carving, building).
- Slate: Splits into flat sheets; dark; from compressed shale (roofing).
- Schist: Foliated; visible minerals; medium to hard.

### Identifying rocks and minerals

**Hardness test:**
- Fingernail (hardness 2.5): Can scratch soft minerals.
- Copper coin (hardness 3-3.5): Scratches calcite; doesn't scratch feldspar.
- Steel knife (hardness 5-6): Scratches quartz; doesn't scratch harder minerals.
- Scratch glass: Glass has hardness 5.5; tests harder minerals.

**Color and luster:**
- Color: Natural hue; some minerals vary.
- Luster: Shiny (metallic ores), dull (many rocks), glassy (quartz), or silky (mica).

**Weight (density):**
- Light: Calcite, coal, pumice.
- Medium: Most common rocks (granite, basalt).
- Heavy: Metals ores (hematite, galena), magnetite.

**Crystal structure:**
- Cubic: Halite (salt), galena.
- Hexagonal: Quartz.
- Flat sheets: Mica, graphite.

## Part 2: Geological Structures and Formations

### Rock layers (strata)

**Formation:** Rocks form in layers over time (older rocks below, newer above).

**Significance:**
- Each layer represents a time period and environment (desert, ocean, river).
- Useful resources often found at layer boundaries (ores in transition zones).
- Layer slope indicates geological history (flat = stable; tilted = mountain building).

**Reading layers:**
- Walk a hillside; note exposed rock layers in road cuts or cliffs.
- Count layers; note color/texture differences.
- Estimate thickness (cm to meters per layer).
- Tilted layers = deformation; horizontal = undisturbed.

### Faults and fractures

**Fault:** Break in rock where movement occurred.

**Significance:**
- Faults can displace mineral-rich layers (creates step patterns visible).
- Hot water flows along faults, sometimes depositing ores.
- Fault zones are zones of weakness (erosion follows faults; creates valleys, streams).

**Identifying faults:**
- Offset rock layers (different elevations on either side).
- Linear valleys or stream patterns (streams follow fault lines).
- Broken/crushed rock visible along the line.

### Folding

**Fold:** Rock layers bent (not broken) by pressure.

**Types:**
- Anticline: Arch-shaped; older rocks in center; forms ridges.
- Syncline: Bowl-shaped; younger rocks in center; forms valleys.

**Significance:**
- Folded layers can trap oil, gas, and water.
- Fold crests are good sites for cave exploration, springs.

## Part 3: Reading the Landscape

### Topography tells geological story

**Mountains (steep, high terrain):**
- Active uplift (rock rising faster than erosion).
- Often granite or metamorphic core (hard rocks).
- Minerals concentrated in mountain roots (deep, inaccessible).

**Valleys (flat, low terrain):**
- Often follow faults or soft rock formations (easier to erode).
- Streams concentrate here; good water access.
- May have springs (water emerging from fault or layer boundary).

**Plateaus (flat-topped high terrain):**
- Horizontal rock layers (lava flows or sedimentary strata).
- Cliff faces expose geology clearly.
- Often have mineral deposits at layer boundaries.

**Plains (flat, low terrain):**
- Soft sedimentary rocks or soils.
- Groundwater close to surface.
- Poor for hard rock resources; good for clay, water wells.

### Soil color and type reveal rock below

**Red soil:** Iron-rich minerals breaking down; hematite or laterite present.

**Yellow/brown soil:** Clay and sand; formed from sedimentary rocks.

**Black soil:** Organic matter rich; good for farming (indicates prior vegetation, stable weather).

**Rocky soil:** Hard bedrock close to surface; limited soil depth.

### Vegetation patterns indicate water and rock

**Lush vegetation:** Reliable water (spring, seepage, high water table).

**Sparse vegetation:** Dry or poor soil (exposed bedrock, sandy soil, salt).

**Tree types:** Oak, hardwoods prefer clay-rich soil; pine, conifers prefer sandy/rocky.

## Part 4: Finding Resources Through Geology

### Locating water sources

**Springs:** Water emerges at rock layer boundaries, faults, or where water table meets surface.

**Indicators:**
- Lush vegetation in otherwise dry area.
- Soil always moist; ferns or mosses present.
- Animals converge at certain locations.
- Seepage visible on hillsides.

**Wells:** Dig where sedimentary layers are present (water table higher).
- Avoid steep terrain (water table deep; hard to dig).
- Prefer flat/valley terrain (water table accessible).

### Locating metal ores

**Hematite (iron ore):** Red rocks, deposits in bands within rock layers.
- Visible as reddish/rust coloring on rocks and soil.
- Often found in metamorphic belts (mountain regions).

**Galena (lead ore):** Gray/metallic mineral; heavy.
- Often found with other ores in mountainous regions.
- Indicates complex geology (faulting, hot water activity).

**Copper ore (malachite, chalcopyrite):** Green or shiny coloring on rocks.
- Associated with faults and hot springs.
- Oxidizes to blue-green color near surface (visible indicator).

**Tin ore (cassiterite):** Heavy, dark mineral in granite.
- Associated with granite intrusions (mountains).
- Found in stream sediments downstream of tin deposits.

### Locating useful materials

**Clay:** Fine sediment; light colored; sticky when wet.
- Found in low areas, stream beds, lake beds.
- Indicates prior water environment.

**Limestone:** Light colored; reacts with acid.
- Used for cement, agriculture (pH adjustment), building stone.
- Often in horizontal layers; easy to quarry.

**Sand and gravel:** Rounded particles; found in stream beds, plains.
- Used for concrete, glass, construction.

**Coal and peat:** Dark, organic-rich layers.
- Coal: Hard; found in deep sedimentary deposits (mountain regions).
- Peat: Soft; found in wet areas with thick vegetation (bogs, marshes).

## Part 5: Geological Assessment for Settlement Planning

### Site evaluation checklist

**Water:**
- Is spring or well water accessible (distance, depth)?
- Water quality (color, smell, taste—indicates contamination risks)?

**Metals:**
- Are visible ores present (reddish staining, metallic rocks)?
- Are mountainous regions nearby (ore typically in mountains)?

**Building materials:**
- Stone suitable for construction (not soft shale; use granite, basalt)?
- Clay available for bricks, pottery?
- Sand for concrete?

**Energy:**
- Coal deposits present (dark layers in sedimentary regions)?
- Peat available (wetland regions)?

**Soil quality:**
- Deep soil for agriculture (dark color; high organic matter)?
- Or shallow soil (poor for crops; good for pasture)?

### Regional geology assessment

**Step 1: Obtain rock and soil map (if available from prior industrial era).**

**Step 2: Walk territory; record:**
- Visible rock outcrops (expose bedrock; see actual geology).
- Soil color and depth (dig test pit).
- Water sources (springs, streams, seepage areas).
- Vegetation patterns (lush vs. sparse; tree types).

**Step 3: Infer geology:**
- What rock types are visible?
- Are layers horizontal or tilted?
- Are faults present (offset layers, linear features)?
- What resources are indicated by geology?

**Step 4: Map resources:**
- Water sources (primary, backup).
- Potential metal deposits (ore colors, mountain proximity).
- Building materials (stone, clay).
- Energy sources (coal, peat).

**Step 5: Plan extraction:**
- Which resources are accessible (easy to mine)?
- Which require major effort (deep deposits)?
- Which can be developed first vs. later?

## Part 6: Practical Prospecting for Post-Collapse Settlements

### Systematic prospecting method

**1. Desktop phase (use any available maps/documents):**
- Study old geological maps or industrial site records.
- Identify known mining areas, quarries, industrial sites.
- Note water sources, springs on maps.

**2. Walk-and-observe phase:**
- Walk entire settlement territory (1-2 weeks effort).
- Record all visible rock outcrops.
- Note soil color, vegetation patterns.
- Locate springs, seepage areas.
- Collect rock samples from visible deposits.

**3. Testing phase:**
- Test samples for hardness, density, color.
- Try acid (vinegar) on samples (soft rocks fizz = limestone/marble).
- Pan stream sediments for heavy minerals (gold, tin, gem stones).
- Judge magnetic properties (magnetite sticks to magnet).

**4. Targeted digging phase:**
- Dig test pits in promising areas (1-2 meters deep).
- Expose and examine subsurface geology.
- Assess ore thickness and purity.
- Judge excavation effort needed.

### Reading ore indicator signs

**Oxidized ore (at surface):**
- Iron: Rusty red, brown discoloration on rocks and soil.
- Copper: Blue-green (malachite) or bright blue (azurite) discoloration.
- Lead: Heavy rocks, no color change (hard to detect).

**Stream sampling:**
- Pan sediments from streams flowing down from hills.
- Look for heavy particles that don't wash away (ores, gems).
- Find stream panning deposits; source usually uphill.

**Mineral associations:**
- Ores rarely occur alone; specific minerals indicate ore types.
- Quartz veins often associated with metal ores (worth examining).
- Clay near base of soil; look for pottery-grade deposits.

## Part 7: Post-Collapse Geological Reality

### Limited exploration technology

**What you can do:**
- Visual assessment (see exposed geology).
- Simple tests (hardness, density, acid reaction).
- Trenching/digging (expose deeper geology).
- Panning (heavy sediment separation).
- Walking surveys (detailed observation).

**What you cannot do (without industrial equipment):**
- Deep boreholes (requires drilling equipment).
- Seismic surveys (requires sensitive instruments).
- Chemical analysis (requires lab).
- Mapping at depth (only surface geology visible).

### Implications for post-collapse resource discovery

**Accept surface-level deposits first:** Deep ores require mining technology; start with easily accessible sources.

**Prioritize obvious deposits:** Mountains have ores; stone cliffs have building material; clay beds are visible.

**Use trial-and-error learning:** Dig test wells; map resources by exploration; document what works.

**Leverage community knowledge:** Ask settlement members about prior observations; combine personal knowledge with systematic assessment.

**Plan long-term exploration:** Resource discovery takes years; balance current needs with future prospecting.

## Verification

Your geology understanding is adequate if:
1. **Rocks:** You can identify common rock types (granite, basalt, sandstone, limestone).
2. **Minerals:** You can recognize iron ore (hematite), copper (malachite), lead (galena).
3. **Structures:** You understand rock layers, faults, and folding.
4. **Reading landscape:** You can infer geology from topography, soil, vegetation.
5. **Resource finding:** You can locate water, metals, clay, building stone using geological signs.
6. **Planning:** You can assess geology of settlement territory and map resources.

## Common Mistakes

**Assuming all mountains have metal ore.** Mountains have concentrated minerals only if geological history supports it (collision zones, hot springs). Random mountains may have little ore.

**Trusting color alone.** Red soil might be iron-rich (ore) or just oxidized common rock. Test hardness and weight.

**Ignoring water table.** Wells dug on hilltops hit water table 50+ meters down (impractical). Dig in valleys where water table is shallow.

**Expecting easy access.** Ore deposits often exposed only deep underground. Post-collapse mining is labor-intensive.

**Not mapping faults.** Faults are weak zones where ore can be concentrated and water flows. Fault zones worth exploring.
