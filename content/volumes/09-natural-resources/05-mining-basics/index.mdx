## Overview

Mining is essential but extraordinarily dangerous. Miners die from cave-ins, poisoned air, flooding, and exhaustion. Your settlement will need ore and coal to develop metallurgical industries, but you cannot afford to lose skilled workers to preventable deaths. This chapter teaches you mining methods, how to support tunnels with timber to prevent collapse, and how to manage air quality underground so miners survive.

You will learn three mining methods (open-pit, shaft, drift mining), when to use each, how to calculate timber requirements for supporting rock, and how to ventilate underground workings safely. The goal is simple: extract the maximum ore or coal with the minimum loss of life, while maintaining the mine's long-term productivity.

## Materials & Tools

**For all mining:**
- Picks and shovels (basic extraction tools)
- Pry bars and crowbars (for moving heavy rock)
- Baskets or buckets (for transporting ore/coal)
- Ropes and pulleys (for raising material from shafts)
- Light sources: torches or oil lamps (mines are dark)
- Air testing: candles (used to detect poisoned air)

**For underground mining (shafts and drifts):**
- Timber (logs, planks) for shoring (see Step 2 for specifications)
- Nails, wedges, rope (for securing timber)
- Measurement tools: rope, marked stick (to assess chamber dimensions)
- Drainage: buckets for bailing water, or pumps (if available)

**For ventilation:**
- Furnace or brazier (for creating air movement by heating)
- Chimney or pipe (to direct fresh air and exhaust foul air)
- Bellows (optional; speeds up ventilation)

**Safety:**
- Rope with loops (for securing miners to prevent falls)
- First aid supplies (for immediate treatment of injuries)
- Backup light sources (in case primary light fails)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Choose Your Mining Method

Three primary methods exist. Each has different costs, risks, and yields.

**Method 1: Open-pit mining (surface mining)**

**Use when:**
- Ore/coal is at shallow depth (0–20 meters)
- Deposit is large and worth the labor
- No environmental constraints (not near water sources or settlements)

**How it works:**
1. Remove overburden (rock and soil above the ore)
2. Strip mine: extract ore layer by layer
3. Load ore into carts and transport away

**Advantages:**
- Safest method (no roof to collapse)
- Fastest extraction (no underground support needed)
- Easier to monitor ore quality (visible layers)
- Better ventilation (natural air movement)

**Disadvantages:**
- Destroys the landscape
- May expose deep layers to groundwater (contaminating aquifers)
- Limited size (can only go as deep as you can shovel)
- Hard to expand: once you've removed the overburden, deepening the mine becomes exponentially harder

**Labor requirements:**
- 10–20 workers for a medium pit
- 5–10 person-years to extract 10,000 kg ore (varies by rock hardness and depth)

**Timeline:**
- Small deposit (1,000–5,000 kg available): 2–6 months
- Medium deposit (10,000–50,000 kg): 1–2 years

**Method 2: Shaft mining (underground vertical access)**

**Use when:**
- Ore/coal is 20–100+ meters deep
- Deposit is large (worth the sustained effort)
- You have experienced miners and adequate timber

**How it works:**
1. Sink a vertical shaft straight down to the ore seam
2. At the seam level, tunnel horizontally (using room-and-pillar method)
3. Extract ore from rooms, leaving pillars of ore/rock to support roof
4. Install timber props to prevent collapse
5. Manage air with ventilation system

**Advantages:**
- Access very deep ore (eventually limited only by your ability to support the shaft)
- Preserves surface landscape
- Can sustain operations for decades (room-and-pillar method prevents depletion of entire seam)

**Disadvantages:**
- Most dangerous (risk of shaft collapse, poisoned air, flooding)
- Requires significant timber investment
- Slower extraction rate (lateral tunneling is slower than open-pit)
- Complex ventilation requirements

**Labor requirements:**
- 20–50 workers (depending on depth and seam size)
- Requires skilled miners (foreman, timbermen, ventilation manager)
- 10–20 person-years to establish a productive shaft

**Timeline:**
- Sinking shaft to 50 meters: 3–6 months
- Developing horizontal workings: 6–12 months after shaft sinking
- Production at scale: year 2+

**Method 3: Drift mining (horizontal underground access)**

**Use when:**
- Ore/coal is on a hillside at moderate depth
- A natural or artificial horizontal tunnel can access the seam
- You want to avoid sinking a deep vertical shaft

**How it works:**
1. Tunnel horizontally into a hillside (or along a cliff face) to intersect the ore seam
2. Use the natural slope to reach the ore with minimal vertical shaft sinking
3. Once the seam is reached, extract using room-and-pillar method

**Advantages:**
- Lower labor cost than shaft sinking (no need to dig as deep vertically)
- Natural gravity-assisted drainage (water flows out of drift, not down shaft)
- Easier ventilation (natural draft through horizontal passage)
- Safer than shaft mining (less risk of collapse from great height)

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires suitable topography (hillside or cliff with ore at appropriate elevation)
- Requires correct angle (too steep or too shallow and the drift is inefficient)
- Still dangerous (collapse risk, air quality issues)

**Labor requirements:**
- 15–30 workers
- 5–15 person-years to develop a productive drift mine

**Timeline:**
- Initial drift tunnel (50–100 meters): 2–4 months
- Development to production: 6–12 months
- Scale production: year 2+

### Step 2: Calculate and Construct Timber Support (Shoring)

Unsupported rock underground collapses. Timber prevents collapse by transferring rock weight downward to solid ground. Timber requirements depend on:
- Rock type (hard rock like granite needs less support than soft rock like shale)
- Room size (wider rooms need more support)
- Depth (deeper = more weight = need more support)

**Basic timber support formula:**

For a room (chamber) in a mine:

```
Number of props (vertical wooden posts) needed = (room width in meters) × (room length in meters) / 4

Examples:
- 3m wide × 3m long room = 9÷4 = 2–3 props needed
- 5m wide × 5m long room = 25÷4 = 6–7 props needed
- 10m wide × 10m long room = 100÷4 = 25 props needed
```

**Timber specifications (rule of thumb):**

**For prop posts (vertical supports):**
- Diameter: 15–30 cm (6–12 inches) depending on load
- Material: hardwood (oak, maple) preferred, softwood (pine) acceptable
- Length: slightly longer than floor-to-ceiling height, so you can jam them tight (e.g., if ceiling is 2.5m high, use a 2.6m prop)
- Quantity: 1 prop per 4–8 square meters of floor (varies by depth and rock type)

**For cross-beams (horizontal supports between props):**
- Diameter: 10–20 cm
- Length: span between props
- Placement: typically one beam per 1–2 meters of height on the props
- Purpose: spreads weight across multiple props and prevents pillars from shifting sideways

**For roof support (above beams):**
- Material: planks (20–30 cm wide, 5–10 cm thick) or woven branches
- Placement: tight against the ceiling, supported by beams
- Purpose: prevents small rocks from falling; spreads weight

**Assembly process:**

1. Place vertical props 4–8 meters apart
2. Attach horizontal cross-beams to props at regular intervals (every 1–2 meters up the prop)
3. Place planks or branches above cross-beams, supporting the immediate ceiling
4. Ensure each prop is jammed tight against floor and ceiling (no gaps)
5. Check daily: props may settle; adjust wedges to keep tight

**Timber yield calculation (how much timber you need):**

For a 50m × 5m horizontal drift mine with 3m ceiling height:

```
Estimated room length = 50 meters
Room width = 5 meters
Props needed = (5 × 50) / 4 = 62 props

Props per prop: 3m each = 186 linear meters of 15cm-diameter timber
Cross-beams per room: 5 beams across the width (5m each) × (3 height / 1.5m spacing) = 10 beams per row × (50m length / spacing) = ~100 cross-beams, 5m each = 500 linear meters of 10cm-diameter timber
Roof planking: 5m wide × 50m long = 250 m² of planking, roughly 2,500 linear meters of plank material

TOTAL: ~3,200 linear meters of timber
Tree yield: one 30cm-diameter, 20m-tall tree yields roughly 5–8 m³ of usable timber
Volume needed: estimated 150 m³ of timber for this mine
Trees needed: ~20–30 large trees

This is a significant resource commitment, requiring a dedicated timber-cutting operation.
```

**Labor timeline for timber harvesting and installation:**
- Felling and trimming trees: 1–2 person-weeks per tree
- Installing shoring: 10–20 person-days per 100 m² of underground space
- Total labor: 2–3 months of preparation before mining can safely begin

### Step 3: Manage Water in Underground Mines

Water underground kills miners (through drowning or hypothermia) and corrodes ore. Manage it actively.

**Sources of water underground:**
- Infiltration from surface (rain seeping through rock cracks)
- Groundwater (rising water table hitting the mine)
- Springs (if the mine intersects a groundwater-bearing layer)

**Prevention:**
- Avoid mining below the water table if possible
- If the mine is wet, ensure it is not because water is rising dangerously
- Monitor water level daily

**Pumping if water is present:**
- Use a hand pump or bucket-and-rope system to remove water
- Assign one worker to pump continuously (exhausting work)
- If water rises faster than you can pump, evacuate and cease mining until water level drops
- Never ignore rising water (it is a sign of imminent danger: floor collapse, flooding, or gas eruption)

### Step 4: Ventilate Underground Workings

Poisoned air kills miners silently. Underground air becomes toxic from:
- **Methane (CH₄):** Colorless, odorless, explosive at 5–15% concentration
- **Carbon dioxide (CO₂):** Heavier than air, pools in low spots, causes suffocation
- **Hydrogen sulfide (H₂S):** Rotten-egg smell, highly toxic, can kill in seconds at high concentration
- **Carbon monoxide (CO):** Colorless, odorless, deadly (from coal fires or incomplete combustion)

**Ventilation principles:**

**Natural draft (simplest):**
- Shaft or drift slopes upward → hot air rises → fresh air is drawn in to replace it
- Example: drift slopes 5° upward → slope over 100m = 8m elevation gain → creates air circulation
- Effectiveness: Limited; depends on temperature difference

**Furnace draft (more reliable):**
- Place a furnace or brazier at the exit of the mine (or at the base of a shaft)
- Heat creates convection: hot air rises, pulls fresh air in from the entrance
- Install chimney above furnace to direct exhaust away from the entrance
- Result: continuous air movement through the mine

**Bellows boost (if natural draft is insufficient):**
- Use bellows (hand-operated or water-powered) to force fresh air into the mine entrance
- This pushes stale air out of the opposite entrance (if two entrances exist)

**Air testing (critical safety):**

Use candles to test air:
- Lower a lit candle into the mine before workers enter
- If the candle remains bright and steady: air is good
- If the candle flickers or goes out: air is poisoned or low oxygen → Do NOT enter
- Repeat this test every hour while miners are working

**Dangerous gas indicators:**
- **Rotten-egg smell:** Hydrogen sulfide → evacuate immediately
- **Metallic/acrid taste in the air:** Sulfur dioxide or nitrogen oxides → evacuate
- **Soot or white smoke:** Fire/combustion underground → evacuate, extinguish the fire if possible

### Step 5: Organize Mining Labor and Safety

Mining is organized labor with clear roles and hierarchies.

**Organization structure (for a 30-person mine):**

- **Mine foreman (1):** Overall authority, makes safety decisions, directs work
- **Shift supervisor (2, one per shift):** Directly supervises miners, ensures timber is sound, manages water/ventilation
- **Timbermen (3):** Install and maintain shoring, repair damage
- **Ventilation manager (1):** Monitors air, maintains furnace/bellows, tests with candles
- **Miners (20):** Extract ore/coal, load baskets, transport material
- **Support crew (3–5):** Maintain tools, cook meals, haul water, manage records

**Daily routine (12-hour shift):**
- **6:00 AM:** Crew arrives, eats breakfast
- **6:30 AM:** Safety check: inspect shoring, test air with candle
- **7:00 AM–4:00 PM:** Active mining (7 hours)
- **4:00 PM:** Lunch break (1 hour)
- **5:00 PM–6:00 PM:** Inspect mine, check shoring, close up
- **6:00 PM:** Crew leaves

**Weekly schedule:**
- 5 days mining, 2 days rest
- One day dedicated to timber repair and inspection
- One day dedicated to tool maintenance

**Safety rules (non-negotiable):**

1. **Air testing before work:** Every morning and every 2 hours during work
2. **Shoring inspection:** Every shift supervisor inspects shoring before work; repairs immediately if damage is seen
3. **No single worker rule:** Never work alone underground
4. **Equipment check:** All tools inspected daily; broken tools discarded
5. **Water pumping:** Continuous monitoring; if water rises dangerously, evacuate
6. **Emergency exits:** Every worker knows the primary and secondary exits
7. **Limits on depth:** No one digs beyond support installed (always work behind properly shored areas)
8. **Rest limits:** Miners work maximum 7 hours per day (exhaustion = accidents)
9. **Health monitoring:** Sickness or injury = immediate evacuation and rest

**Injury and death protocol:**
- Minor injuries (cuts, bruises): treat with first aid, rest the worker for 1–2 days
- Serious injuries (broken bones, deep wounds): evacuate, seek medical care from healer
- Death: recover the body (if possible), bury respectfully, investigate the accident to prevent recurrence

### Step 6: Monitor Mine Productivity and Sustainability

Not all mining operations succeed. Monitor performance and adjust.

**Metrics to track:**

**Extraction rate:**
- Kg of ore/coal per worker per day
- Target: 10–50 kg per worker per day (varies by ore type and depth)
- If rate falls below 10 kg/day: mine may be becoming less productive (seam narrowing, depth increasing, quality declining)

**Safety:**
- Injuries per 1,000 worker-days
- Target: &lt;10 injuries per 1,000 worker-days (roughly 1 injury per 100 days of work)
- If injuries spike: investigate causes and enforce safety rules

**Timber consumption:**
- Linear meters of timber installed per kg ore extracted
- Monitor because excessive timber consumption may indicate shoring is failing
- If timber consumption is rising: inspect the mine for damage (water infiltration, rock pressure increasing, supports failing)

**Long-term viability:**
- Estimated reserve remaining (kg) ÷ extraction rate (kg/day) = days of mining remaining
- If reserve is &lt;1 year: begin planning transition to new mine or other work

### Step 7: Phase Out Mining Operations

Eventually, mines are exhausted. Plan for closure.

**End-of-mine procedures:**
1. Remove all portable equipment (tools, baskets, rope)
2. Leave final shoring intact (so no sudden collapse if someone wanders in)
3. Seal the shaft or drift (pile rocks, cave it in partially) so people cannot easily enter an abandoned mine
4. Document the location and any hazards (water accumulation, bad air, unstable sections)
5. Redirect the labor force to new projects (farming, construction, other industries)

## Verification

**Success looks like:**
- By month 6: You have excavated a small open-pit mine (1,000 kg ore extracted)
- By month 12: You are operating a shaft mine with proper shoring and ventilation (100+ kg ore per week)
- By year 2: Your mine has produced 20,000+ kg ore without major accidents
- By year 3: Mining labor is productive and safety incidents are rare (&lt;1 per month)

**Failure looks like:**
- Miners are injured regularly (cave-ins, poisoned air)
- Extraction rate is declining (mine is exhausted or becoming dangerous)
- Shoring is failing (visible damage, settling, cracking)
- Air is bad (candles going out, miners getting sick)

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I will mine without shoring to save timber"** — The mine will collapse, killing workers and burying the ore. Shoring is non-negotiable. Budget timber from the start.

2. **"Air quality is good enough if miners say they feel OK"** — Poisoned air kills silently. Miners will be unconscious before they realize the danger. Use candles. Never rely on how people feel.

3. **"We will mine the richest area first and ignore less-rich areas"** — This causes uneven excavation and collapse. Use room-and-pillar method: extract systematically, leaving pillars, so the mine stays stable.

4. **"Water in the mine is no big deal"** — Rising water is a critical danger sign. It indicates structural failure or imminent flooding. Pump actively or cease mining.

5. **"One experienced miner per shift is enough"** — Never work alone underground. Accidents happen (collapse, poisoning). You need a partner to call for help.

6. **"I will use cheap wood for shoring to save cost"** — Rotting wood fails. Use strong, dry wood. Inspect props daily.

7. **"Ventilation will happen naturally; I don't need a furnace"** — Natural draft is unreliable. Install a furnace-driven ventilation system if you want to guarantee good air.

8. **"Mines are like excavation; I can just dig deeper indefinitely"** — Mines have limits. Depth increases support requirements exponentially. A 100m mine needs vastly more timber than a 30m mine. Plan limits realistically.
