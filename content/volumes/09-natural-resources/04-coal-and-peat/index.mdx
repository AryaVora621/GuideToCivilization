## Overview

Wood is your early energy source, but it is finite. By year 2–3 post-collapse, your settlement will need alternative fuel for metalworking, heating, and power. Coal and peat are fossilized carbon reserves that burn hotter and longer than wood, making them essential for smelting metal and industrial work.

Coal is the compressed remnant of ancient forests. It burns hotter than wood and contains more energy per volume. Peat is incompletely decomposed plant matter—coal in early stages of formation. Both are found in accessible deposits in many regions. Learning to identify, extract, and use them is the key to industrial development beyond survival.

This chapter teaches you how to find coal and peat deposits, assess their quality, extract them sustainably, and convert coal into coke—a near-pure carbon fuel essential for advanced metalworking. By year 3–5 post-collapse, coal mining and coke production will support your settlement's transition to metal tools, weapons, and infrastructure.

- Light source: torch, candles, or oil lamp (underground work requires light)
- Ventilation: props for supporting tunnels, emergency exit plan
- Safety: ropes, pulley system for raising coal bags

**For coke production (see Step 4):**

- Coal fuel (high-quality coal, preferably bituminous)
- Sealing material: clay, mud (to seal the kiln against air leaks)
- Chimney or ventilation for smoke
- Temperature indicator (visual assessment; color of escaping smoke)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Identify Coal Deposits in Your Region

Coal forms in specific geological environments. Understanding these allows you to predict where coal exists even before finding surface deposits.

**Geological setting for coal formation (all required):**

1. **Ancient swamps or wetlands:** Coal forms from compressed plant matter accumulated in swamps. Evidence: sedimentary rock layers with fossil plant remains.
2. **Anoxic (oxygen-poor) water:** Swamps prevent wood decomposition; oxygen-depleted water slows decay. Coal cannot form in aerated soils.
3. **Burial and pressure:** Overlying rock layers compress the plant matter into coal (peat → lignite → bituminous → anthracite).
4. **Long time:** Coal formation requires millions of years.

**Where to look for coal (regional clues):**

- **Rock type:** Coal occurs in sedimentary rock layers (sandstone, shale, mudstone), NOT in granite, basalt, or other igneous rock
- **Stratigraphy:** Coal is sandwiched between shale (black/gray mud) layers in most deposits
- **Age:** Most economically important coal is from the Carboniferous period (360–300 million years ago) or other coal-forming periods (Permian, Cretaceous, Tertiary)
- **Historical knowledge:** If your region had coal mining before collapse (even 100+ years ago), coal exists. Look for old mines, mining towns, or railway lines (built to transport coal)
- **Topography:** Coal deposits create distinctive topography: ridges capping coal (harder layer above), valleys where coal was mined out

**Surface indicators of coal nearby:**

- **Exposed coal seams:** Black or dark brown layers in road cuts, riverbeds, cliffs
- **Coal fragments:** Black fragments (coaly shale) in surface debris, stream beds
- **Distinctive rock types:** Thick layers of shale (soft, black, breaks into thin sheets) are associated with coal
- **Gas seeps:** Methane gas emerging from rock fractures (dangerous but indicates coal below)
- **Mineral water:** Springs with acidic pH or iron-rich water (formed by coal-bearing layers)

### Step 2: Assess Coal Quality

Not all coal is equally useful. Quality depends on rank (degree of heat and pressure) and composition.

**Coal rank (from lowest to highest quality):**

**Peat (not technically coal, but important)**
- **Age:** Recently formed (thousands of years, not millions)
- **Carbon content:** 40–50%
- **Heat value:** ~8–10 MJ/kg (megajoules per kilogram)
- **Burn characteristics:** Slow, cool, high moisture, smoky
- **Use:** Emergency fuel, only if coal is unavailable. Produces poor metallurgical coke.
- **Identification:** Brown, fibrous, visible plant matter, light and spongy

**Lignite (brown coal)**
- **Age:** 2–5 million years
- **Carbon content:** 50–75%
- **Heat value:** ~10–15 MJ/kg
- **Burn characteristics:** Smoky, high moisture, crumbles easily
- **Use:** Fuel for steam, heat, not ideal for coke production
- **Identification:** Dark brown to black, powdery, breaks easily, visible plant structure in some samples

**Bituminous coal (soft coal)**
- **Age:** 100–300 million years (Carboniferous to Permian)
- **Carbon content:** 75–90%
- **Heat value:** ~20–30 MJ/kg
- **Burn characteristics:** Hot, produces good coke, moderate smoke, binds well
- **Use:** Metallurgical fuel, ideal for coke production
- **Identification:** Black, shiny or dull, hardness 2–2.5 (scratches by knife), banded or bony structure

**Anthracite (hard coal)**
- **Age:** 200–400 million years, deeply buried
- **Carbon content:** 85–95%
- **Heat value:** ~25–35 MJ/kg
- **Burn characteristics:** Very hot, clean, produces excellent coke, slow burn
- **Use:** Premium fuel for forges, furnaces, coke production
- **Identification:** Black, bright metallic luster, hardness 2–2.5, conchoidal fracture (curved break surface)

**How to assess coal quality in the field:**

**Test 1: Hardness test**
- Scratch with fingernail: easily scratches → lignite or peat
- Scratch with copper penny: scratches → bituminous coal
- Does not scratch by copper penny → anthracite
- This is a rough test; bituminous coal varies in hardness

**Test 2: Burn test (small sample)**
- Burn a small piece of coal in a hot flame or furnace
- **Peat:** burns yellow-orange, lots of smoke, leaves ash
- **Lignite:** burns with yellow-orange flame, moderate smoke, leaves ash
- **Bituminous:** burns with yellow-white flame, less smoke, leaves small amount of ash, may glow red after flame is removed
- **Anthracite:** burns with small yellow flame, little smoke, leaves minimal ash, does not glow red
- Note: Bituminous coal that sticks together when heated (forms clinker) is especially good for coke production

**Test 3: Coke production (small sample)**
- Heat a piece of coal in a closed container (covered crucible in a furnace) to 1000°C
- Remove and examine the residue:
- **Peat/lignite:** disintegrates, leaves powdery ash
- **Bituminous:** leaves coherent gray mass (coke) that is lighter than original coal
- **Anthracite:** changes little; already so pure that coking does not change it much
- This test tells you which coal will produce usable coke

**Test 4: Visual examination under hand lens**
- **Peat:** visible plant fibers, woody structure
- **Lignite:** layered, woody structure visible, duller black
- **Bituminous:** banded layers, bright and dull layers, no visible plant structure, may show cleat (natural fracture lines)
- **Anthracite:** uniform black, vitreous (glassy) luster, conchoidal fracture

**Priority ranking for use:**
1. **Anthracite:** Best for metallurgy and coke; use first
2. **Bituminous:** Ideal for coke production; use second
3. **Lignite:** Acceptable for heating; use third
4. **Peat:** Emergency fuel only

### Step 3: Extract Coal Sustainably

Coal mining can be surface (open-pit) or underground (shaft). Choice depends on seam depth.

**When to use surface mining (coal seam 0–20 meters deep):**

**Advantages:**
- Less dangerous than underground mining
- Faster to extract
- Lower cost in labor

**Disadvantages:**
- Destroys surface landscape
- May contaminate groundwater if rock layers are disturbed
- Limited size (once you dig out the shallow coal, you must stop or go underground)

**Procedure:**
1. Locate the coal seam (use field mapping from Step 1)
2. Strip overburden (rock layers above the coal) using picks and shovels
3. Extract coal from exposed seam using picks and shovels
4. Load coal into baskets or carts
5. Transport to surface or storage area

**When to use underground mining (coal seam 20+ meters deep):**

**Advantages:**
- Access deeper, larger coal reserves
- Preserves surface landscape

**Disadvantages:**
- Very dangerous (cave-in risk, toxic gases, flooding)
- Requires shoring (wooden supports) to prevent collapse
- Requires ventilation (natural or mechanical) to manage gases
- Slower extraction rate

**Procedure (simplified; for detailed safety, see Vol 14, Ch 5: Mining Basics):**

1. Sink a shaft straight down to the coal seam (dangerous; requires wooden bracing at all times)
2. At the coal seam level, tunnel horizontally (room-and-pillar method: extract coal in rectangular rooms, leave pillars of coal to support roof)
3. Install wooden props (wooden posts) supporting roof every 2–3 meters
4. Extract coal from rooms, working away from shaft
5. Never work alone in the mine; always have two people per team
6. Monitor air: if you smell rotten eggs (H₂S), sulfur dioxide (acrid smell), or methane (odorless but dangerous), evacuate immediately
7. Provide constant ventilation: install a chimney or natural draft to pull fresh air into shaft and stale air out

**Sustainable extraction principles:**

- **Do not over-mine one seam:** Leaving pillars of coal supports the roof and allows gradual extraction over many years rather than catastrophic collapse
- **Monitor water:** If water enters the mine, pump it out or cease work. Flooding is a major hazard.
- **Assess reserve size:** Before committing labor, estimate the seam's total coal content. A small seam may not justify the mining effort.

**Example calculation:**
- Coal seam: 1 meter thick, 2 km long, 500 meters deep in accessible region
- Estimated coal volume: 1 × 2,000 × 500 = 1,000,000 cubic meters
- Bulk density of coal: ~1,300 kg/m³ (varies; bituminous is denser than lignite)
- Total coal mass: 1,000,000 × 1,300 = 1.3 billion kg
- **This is a major coal deposit worth decades of mining effort**

### Step 4: Convert Coal to Coke

Coke is coal with volatiles (water, tar, gas) removed, leaving behind nearly pure carbon. Coke is superior to coal for metalworking because it burns hotter and leaves minimal ash.

**Why coke matters for metallurgy:**
- Smelting iron ore requires temperatures >1,200°C; bituminous coal alone cannot reach this
- Coke can reach 1,200–1,400°C in a blast furnace
- Coal leaves ash and tar that contaminates metal; coke is nearly pure carbon, leaving clean metal
- Coke production also yields tar and gas as byproducts (valuable for later industrial use)

**Coke production method (coking oven or beehive kiln):**

**Simple beehive coking (most practical for post-collapse):**

**Design:**
- Brick or stone dome-shaped structure, ~2 meters diameter
- Loading hole at top
- Flue opening near bottom (for draft)
- Output door at bottom (removable brick sealing)

**Procedure:**
1. Fill oven 3/4 full with broken bituminous coal (chunk size: fist-sized, not powder)
2. Seal the top loading hole with a clay brick
3. Leave the flue opening uncovered; it provides draft for natural ventilation
4. Heat the oven by igniting coal in the flue opening (use wood kindling, then add coal)
5. Keep the flue hot (active combustion) for 6–12 hours (timing varies by coal type and oven size)
6. Monitor: white/clear smoke from flue indicates complete volatilization; thick black smoke indicates under-heating
7. When smoke clears (volatiles are driven off), cool the oven for 12–24 hours (do not quench with water; it causes cracking)
8. Open the output door and remove coke

**Yield:**
- ~100 kg bituminous coal yields ~70–75 kg coke
- Byproducts: coal tar (~3–5 kg per 100 kg coal), coal gas (mostly methane and hydrogen)

**Quality assessment:**
- Good coke is gray, porous, light, and chunks break with a ringing sound
- Poor coke is soft, crumbles, or retains oily smell (indicates incomplete coking)
- Test coke by heating it with iron ore in a furnace: good coke produces hot, clean metal

**Scaling up coke production:**

If you need large quantities (for heavy industry), build multiple ovens:
- 10 ovens running continuously: ~500 kg coke per day
- Enough to support a small iron foundry (5–10 smithies)
- Requires dedicated labor (2–3 people per oven managing heating and loading)

**Danger: carbon monoxide**

Coal gas produced during coking contains carbon monoxide (CO), a deadly, odorless gas. Symptoms of CO poisoning: headache, dizziness, confusion, collapse. Ventilation is critical. Never work near the coking oven without air movement away from you.

### Step 5: Plan Long-Term Coal Use Strategy

Coal mining is not indefinite. Eventually, accessible coal is depleted. Plan accordingly.

**Timeline:**
- **Years 1–2:** Find coal, assess deposit, develop surface mining or shallow shaft
- **Years 3–5:** Increase coal production, begin coke production, develop metallurgical industry
- **Years 5–20:** Exploit coal reserves while transitioning to renewable energy (water power, wind power, biomass)
- **Years 20+:** Coal reserves become scarce; rely on renewable energy, recycled metal

**Sustainable transition:**
- Every unit of coal you use should contribute to infrastructure that lasts (tools, buildings, defensive works)
- Do not waste coal on luxury items or poorly-designed projects
- Invest coal profits (in metallurgical products) in renewable energy systems (water mills, wind mills)
- By year 10, you should be transitioning away from coal dependence, not toward it

## Verification

**Success looks like:**
- By month 3: You have located a coal deposit with visible surface exposure
- By month 6: You have assessed the coal quality and estimated reserve size
- By month 12: You are extracting coal (surface mining) at a rate of 50–200 kg/month
- By year 2: You have produced your first batch of coke and smelted metal with it
- By year 3: Coal is supporting metalwork at scale (20+ tools/weapons per month)

**Failure looks like:**
- You find coal but cannot reach it (too deep without underground mining infrastructure)
- Coal quality is poor (lignite or peat), insufficient for coke production
- You over-mine and cause a cave-in, losing access to the deposit
- You rely on coal exclusively, ignoring renewable energy systems

## Common Mistakes

1. **"Any black rock is coal; I'll burn it immediately"** — Black shale and other rocks are NOT coal. Test with hardness, burn test, and visual exam. Burning shale produces nothing useful.

2. **"I will mine coal without ventilation or shoring"** — Underground mining without proper support collapses. Without ventilation, you will be poisoned by methane or carbon monoxide. Never mine underground without these safety measures.

3. **"Coke production can wait; I'll use coal directly in my furnace"** — Coal leaves ash, tar, and smoke in metal, ruining it. For quality metalwork, you NEED coke. Prioritize coke production.

4. **"I will dig the deepest mine possible to access the most coal"** — Deep mines are exponentially more dangerous. Start with surface mining and shallow shafts (&lt;30 meters). Only go deeper after you have infrastructure and experience.

5. **"If coal is nearby, I should open a mine immediately"** — Assess the coal deposit first. Is it economic? Is it bituminous or lignite? Can you reach it safely? Jumping into mining before assessment wastes labor.

6. **"Once I start mining, I will mine continuously until the seam is exhausted"** — This causes collapse and environmental damage. Use room-and-pillar mining, leaving pillars to support the roof. Mine sustainably.

7. **"Coal smoke is just dirty; I don't need to ventilate the coking oven"** — Coal smoke contains carbon monoxide, a deadly poison. Ventilation is not optional.

8. **"Peat is as good as coal for metalworking"** — Peat produces poor coke and burns cooler. Use it as emergency fuel only. Prioritize bituminous coal or anthracite.
