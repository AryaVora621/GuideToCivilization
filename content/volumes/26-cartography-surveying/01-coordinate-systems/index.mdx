## Overview

You are lost. You tell someone "I am north of the town." They ask, "How far north?" You do not know. Now tell them "I am at latitude 45°N, longitude 120°W." Suddenly, they can pinpoint your exact location on a map or globe.

A **coordinate system** is a set of numbers that describes location. This chapter teaches:

1. **Why coordinates matter:** Precision and reproducibility
2. **Latitude and longitude:** The global system
3. **Degrees, minutes, and seconds:** Breaking down coordinates
4. **Local coordinate systems:** Grids and rectangles
5. **Using coordinates to find locations:** Practical navigation

Coordinates are the language of location. Learn this language, and the world becomes locatable.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of compass directions (north, south, east, west)
- Familiarity with numbers and basic arithmetic
- Ability to measure distance or understand ratios
- Experience with maps or globes (helpful but not required)

## Part 1: Why Coordinates Matter

### The Problem with Descriptions

**Imprecise:** "North of the town." But how far north? Miles? Kilometers? And which town?

**Still imprecise:** "20 km north of Springfield." But where on the 20 km line? West? East? Straight north?

**Vague and ambiguous:** Different people might interpret the same description differently. "Near the river" could mean within 100 meters or within 1 kilometer.

### The Solution: Coordinates

**Precise:** "Latitude 45.5°N, Longitude 120.3°W."

This describes a single location, unique on Earth. Anyone with a map and these numbers can find the exact spot. This is why coordinates are essential for:
- Navigation (ships, aircraft, hikers)
- Mapping (creating accurate maps)
- Communication ("Meet me at these coordinates")
- Record-keeping (historical locations, disaster sites, resources)

## Part 2: Latitude and Longitude

### Latitude: North and South Position

**Latitude** describes how far north or south a location is from the equator.

**The equator** is an imaginary line around Earth's middle, halfway between the North Pole and South Pole.

- Equator latitude = 0°
- North Pole latitude = 90°N (north)
- South Pole latitude = 90°S (south)
- Any location between: somewhere between 0° and 90°N or 0° and 90°S

**Example:**
- 45°N means 45 degrees north of the equator (halfway between equator and North Pole)
- 30°S means 30 degrees south of the equator

**How it works visually:**
Imagine Earth as a sphere. Draw lines parallel to the equator, each representing a different latitude. These are called **parallels**. All locations on a parallel (line of latitude) are the same distance from the equator.

**Key insight:** Latitude alone tells you north-south position but not east-west position. Many locations share the same latitude (they form a circle around Earth).

### Longitude: East and West Position

**Longitude** describes how far east or west a location is from a reference line called the **prime meridian**.

**The prime meridian** is an imaginary line running north-south through Greenwich, London (chosen by international agreement). It represents 0° longitude.

- Prime meridian longitude = 0°
- 180° east and 180° west are the same line (on the opposite side of Earth)
- Locations east of the prime meridian: 1°E to 180°E
- Locations west of the prime meridian: 1°W to 180°W

**Example:**
- 120°W means 120 degrees west of the prime meridian
- 30°E means 30 degrees east of the prime meridian

**How it works visually:**
Imagine lines running north-south, each representing a different longitude. These are called **meridians**. All locations on a meridian (line of longitude) are the same distance east or west from the prime meridian.

**Key insight:** Longitude alone tells you east-west position but not north-south position. Many locations share the same longitude (they form a north-south line).

### Combining Latitude and Longitude

A location requires BOTH:
- Latitude (north-south position): 45°N
- Longitude (west-east position): 120°W

Together, they describe a unique point: 45°N, 120°W.

**Analogy:** Like a grid on paper, where one number is the row and another is the column. You need both to specify a cell.

## Part 3: Degrees, Minutes, and Seconds

### Breaking Down a Degree

One degree (1°) can be subdivided for greater precision.

**1 degree = 60 minutes (60')**
**1 minute = 60 seconds (60")**

### Why Subdivision?

The surface of Earth is large. One degree of latitude or longitude represents a large area. For precise navigation (find an object 100 meters away, not 100 kilometers), you need finer precision.

### Examples

**Coordinate with degrees only:**
45°N, 120°W
Precision: approximately 100 km (a large area)

**Coordinate with degrees and minutes:**
45°30'N, 120°45'W
Precision: approximately 1–2 km (a smaller area)

**Coordinate with degrees, minutes, and seconds:**
45°30'45"N, 120°45'30"W
Precision: approximately 30 meters (useful for navigation)

### Decimal Degrees (Modern Alternative)

Modern GPS and digital maps use **decimal degrees**: fractional parts of a degree written as decimals.

45.5125°N, 120.7583°W

This is equivalent to 45°30'45"N, 120°45'30"W but in decimal form.

To convert:
- 0.5125 degrees × 60 min/degree = 30.75 minutes
- 0.75 minutes × 60 sec/minute = 45 seconds
- Result: 30 minutes 45 seconds = 30'45"

Decimal degrees are easier to work with mathematically (computers prefer them).

## Part 4: Local Coordinate Systems

### Why Local Systems?

Global latitude-longitude is useful for navigation across the world, but local systems are often better for surveying and mapping a specific region (a town, a territory, a property).

### Grid Systems

A **grid system** divides a map into rectangles using east-west (x) and north-south (y) axes.

**Example: City Block Grid**
- X-axis: Numbered east-west (Avenue 1, Avenue 2, Avenue 3...)
- Y-axis: Numbered north-south (Street 1, Street 2, Street 3...)
- Location: "Avenue 5, Street 3" uniquely identifies a block

**Advantages over latitude-longitude:**
- Easier to remember and communicate ("meet at Avenue 3, Street 5")
- Simpler to navigate (gridded cities feel orderly)
- Easier to calculate distances (distance between Avenue 2 and Avenue 5 is clearly 3 avenue-widths)

### Map Grid Systems (UTM, State Plane, etc.)

Large regions use standardized grid systems for mapping:

**UTM (Universal Transverse Mercator):**
- Divides Earth into 60 vertical bands (zones)
- Each zone has a local x-y coordinate grid
- Useful for military, surveying, GIS (geographic information systems)

**State Plane Coordinates (US):**
- Each state has its own grid system
- Designed to minimize distortion within that state
- Used for property surveying and local mapping

**Choosing a system:** For a small region, a local grid is simpler. For a large region or worldwide work, use latitude-longitude or UTM.

## Part 5: Practical Coordinate Use

### Reading Coordinates from a Map

**Method 1: Using the Grid Lines**

1. Locate the feature on the map (a town, landmark, or resource)
2. Find the nearest latitude line to the north and south
3. Find the nearest longitude line to the east and west
4. Estimate the feature's position between these lines
5. Record the coordinates

**Example:** A location appears to be:
- Between 45°N and 46°N, closer to 45°N (estimate 45°25'N)
- Between 120°W and 121°W, closer to 120°W (estimate 120°30'W)
- Coordinates: 45°25'N, 120°30'W

### Finding a Location Given Coordinates

1. Locate the latitude line on the map (find 45°N)
2. Locate the longitude line (find 120°W)
3. The intersection (or nearby intersection) is approximately the location
4. For finer precision, subdivide (45°25'N is between 45°N and 46°N, 25/60 of the way from 45°N to 46°N)

### Recording Locations

When documenting a location, include:
- Coordinates (latitude, longitude)
- Map name and date
- Landmark or description (for verification)
- Accuracy (e.g., "accurate to within 100 meters")

Example:
"Water well at 45°30'15"N, 120°45'00"W (Clearwater topographic map, June 2026). Located 200 meters north of the old mill ruins. Measured with compass and pacing; accuracy ±50 meters."

## Part 6: Distortion and Limitations

### Earth Is Not Flat

Maps are flat representations of a spherical Earth. Converting a sphere to a flat surface requires **projection**, which introduces **distortion**.

**Common projections:**
- **Mercator:** Preserves angles and compass directions but distorts area (Greenland appears much larger than it really is)
- **Albers Equal-Area:** Preserves area but distorts shapes
- **Gnomonic:** Shows great circles (shortest paths) as straight lines, useful for navigation

Different projections introduce different distortions. Know which projection your map uses.

### Magnetic vs. True North

A compass points to **magnetic north** (where Earth's magnetic field points), not **true north** (the geographic North Pole).

The difference (called **declination**) varies by location and changes over time.

**Survival implication:** If using a compass and old map, adjust for declination. If off by even a few degrees, you will miss your destination.

### Accuracy of Coordinates

Coordinates are only as accurate as your measurement tool and method:
- **Pacing and compass:** Accurate to ±100 meters
- **Surveying tools:** Accurate to ±1–10 meters
- **GPS:** Accurate to ±5–10 meters (civilian), ±1–2 meters (military-grade)
- **High-precision survey equipment:** Accurate to centimeters

Always record your measurement accuracy with your coordinates.

## Part 7: Worked Examples

### Example 1: Locating a Resource

**Scenario:** You discover a mineral deposit. You want to record its location so you can return to it later.

**Steps:**

1. You have a map (topographic map of your region)
2. You measure your location: Latitude approximately 45°30', Longitude approximately 120°45'
3. You mark the location on your map
4. You note the coordinates: 45°30'N, 120°45'W
5. You note landmarks: "200 meters northeast of the old mill, at the base of Rocky Ridge"
6. You note accuracy: "Measured with compass and pacing; accurate to ±100 meters"

**Later:** You retrieve your map and notes. You find latitude 45°30'N, longitude 120°45'W. You navigate to that location. You look for Rocky Ridge and the mill. You find the mineral deposit.

### Example 2: Comparing Two Locations

**Location A:** 45°30'N, 120°00'W
**Location B:** 45°30'N, 121°00'W

Both are at the same latitude (45°30'N), so they are on an east-west line. Location B is 1 degree farther west. At this latitude, approximately 74 km difference.

**Location C:** 45°00'N, 120°00'W

Location C is at the same longitude as Location A, so they are on a north-south line. Location C is 30 minutes south of Location A (approximately 55 km).

## Part 8: Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Confusing Latitude and Longitude

Latitude is north-south. Longitude is east-west. Memorization aid: "Latitude is like the rungs of a ladder (horizontal appearance), Longitude is like long lines (vertical appearance)."

### Mistake 2: Forgetting the Direction Indicator (N/S, E/W)

45°, 120° is incomplete. Is it 45°N or 45°S? 120°E or 120°W?

**Always include direction.**

### Mistake 3: Mixing Degrees/Minutes/Seconds Formatting

45°30'45"N is clear. But writing 45°30'45"N as "45 30 45" or "45-30-45" without the symbols is ambiguous.

**Use standard notation or decimal degrees consistently.**

### Mistake 4: Assuming Degrees Are Equal Distance

One degree of latitude is always approximately 111 km. But one degree of longitude varies: 111 km at the equator, but 0 km at the poles (all meridians meet there).

**Understand that "distance per degree" depends on latitude.**

### Mistake 5: Not Accounting for Magnetic Declination

A compass does not point to true north (geographic north pole). It points to magnetic north. The difference (declination) can be 5–20 degrees or more depending on location.

If you use an old map or relocate, check the current declination and adjust your compass readings.

## Verification

You understand coordinate systems when:

1. You can define latitude and longitude and explain what each represents
2. You can read coordinates from a map
3. You can locate a point on a map given its coordinates
4. You can convert between degrees/minutes/seconds and decimal degrees
5. You understand that latitude alone or longitude alone does not uniquely identify a location
6. You can choose an appropriate coordinate system for a specific task (local grid vs. latitude-longitude)
7. You recognize limitations and sources of error in coordinate measurement
