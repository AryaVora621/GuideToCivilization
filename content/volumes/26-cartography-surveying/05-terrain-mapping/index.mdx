## Overview

A flat map shows where things are. A terrain map shows what the land looks like—where it is high, low, steep, or gentle. This information is essential for agriculture, construction, water management, and navigation.

This chapter teaches:

1. **Why terrain matters:** How shape affects land use
2. **Measuring elevation:** Recording height above a reference level
3. **Slope and steepness:** Understanding and measuring gradient
4. **Contouring:** Representing terrain on a flat map
5. **Practical terrain mapping:** How to survey and draw terrain maps

Terrain mapping transforms abstract numbers (coordinates, elevations) into understandable pictures of the land.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of coordinate systems (from Ch 1)
- Ability to measure distances with chains or rope
- Ability to measure angles or slopes
- Patience for detailed fieldwork
- Understanding that all elevations are relative to a chosen reference point (sea level, a benchmark, arbitrary starting height)

## Materials & Tools

**For terrain measurement:**
- Surveying instrument (clinometer, transit, or improvised level)
- Measuring chain or rope
- Level and plumb bob (for verticality check)
- Notebook (to record measurements)
- Stakes or markers (to mark survey points)

**For terrain mapping:**
- Paper and pencil
- Ruler and scale
- Protractor (for angles)
- Ability to draw neatly

## Part 1: Understanding Terrain

### What Is Terrain?

Terrain is the physical shape of the land surface. It includes:
- **Elevation:** Height above a reference point (sea level, arbitrary datum)
- **Slope:** Steepness (angle or percentage gradient)
- **Aspect:** Direction the slope faces (north-facing, south-facing, etc.)
- **Features:** Peaks, valleys, ridges, basins, cliffs

### Why Terrain Matters

**For agriculture:**
- Steep slopes = erosion risk = poor farming
- Gentle slopes = better for crops
- Valley bottoms = water availability = good for irrigation

**For water:**
- Water flows downhill (toward lower elevation)
- Terrain determines drainage patterns and flood risk
- Understanding terrain predicts where water collects

**For construction:**
- Steep slopes are expensive to build on (requires terracing or deep foundations)
- Unstable slopes are dangerous (landslide risk)
- Flat terrain is easiest and cheapest

**For navigation:**
- Steep terrain is hard to traverse (slower travel, more dangerous)
- Terrain hides you from enemies or reveals you to them
- Understanding terrain lets you choose safe routes

### Reference Levels (Datum)

All elevation measurements are relative to a **datum**—a chosen reference point.

**Common datums:**
- Sea level (absolute reference, used for navigation charts)
- Nearest river or stream (local reference for a region)
- An arbitrary point (e.g., "the well at the village center is elevation 0")

**Why it matters:** Two maps with different datums show the same terrain but with different numbers. A peak that is "1000 m above sea level" might be "500 m above the river valley" using a different datum. Choose a datum and document it.

## Part 2: Measuring Elevation

### Absolute Elevation (Height Above Sea Level)

Measuring absolute elevation requires:
1. Starting from a known point (e.g., a port city at sea level)
2. Measuring the vertical change to each new location
3. Accumulating the changes to determine absolute elevation

**Method 1: Barometer**
- Air pressure decreases with elevation
- A barometer can estimate elevation from pressure
- Accuracy: ±100 meters (crude but useful)

**Method 2: Astronomical observation**
- The angle of the sun or stars above the horizon changes with latitude
- Latitude can be converted to elevation using maps
- Accuracy: depends on precision of angle measurement

**Method 3: Triangulation from known points**
- If two known-elevation points are visible, you can calculate elevation of a third using angles
- Requires precise angle measurement

### Relative Elevation (Height Difference Between Two Points)

Measuring relative elevation is simpler and more practical.

**Method 1: Clinometer**
- Measures the angle of slope between two points
- Angle = elevation difference / horizontal distance

**Example:**
- Horizontal distance between two points: 100 meters
- Angle of slope (clinometer reading): 10 degrees
- Elevation difference = 100 × tan(10°) ≈ 17.6 meters

**Method 2: Level and staff**
- A level ensures a perfectly horizontal line of sight
- A staff (marked rod) shows vertical height at a distance
- Difference in staff readings = elevation difference

**Example:**
- Position level midway between two points (100 m apart)
- Look through level at Point A: staff reads 2.5 meters
- Look through level at Point B: staff reads 1.0 meter
- Elevation difference: 2.5 - 1.0 = 1.5 meters (Point A is 1.5 m higher)

**Method 3: Water level**
- Water finds a horizontal level (gravity)
- Mark where water level intersects a vertical staff
- Difference in marks = elevation difference

**Example (field method):**
1. Fill a tube with water
2. Hold one end at Point A, mark where water level is
3. Extend tube to Point B, mark where water level is
4. Difference in marks = elevation difference

## Part 3: Slope and Steepness

### Measuring Slope

**Slope** is the steepness of a surface. It can be expressed as:

**Angle (degrees):**
- 0° = flat
- 45° = equal rise and run (45 meters up per 45 meters horizontal)
- 90° = vertical cliff

**Percentage gradient:**
- Gradient = (rise / run) × 100
- 5% grade = 5 meters up per 100 meters horizontal
- 45° angle = 100% grade

**Ratio:**
- 1:2 slope = 1 meter up per 2 meters horizontal (26.6° angle)

### Practical Slope Measurement

**Method 1: Clinometer**
1. Stand at the base of a slope
2. Look horizontally through the clinometer
3. Tilt until the sight aligns with the top of the slope
4. Read the angle
5. This angle is the slope

**Method 2: Level staff measurement**
1. Place a level staff vertically at the top of the slope
2. From the bottom, position level (straight horizontal sight line)
3. Note the height where your sight line hits the staff
4. Measure horizontal distance
5. Calculate slope: slope angle = arctan(height / distance)

### Why Slope Matters

**Agricultural suitability:**
- 0–2%: Excellent (flat land, good for any crop)
- 2–5%: Good (slight slope, okay for most crops, minimal erosion)
- 5–10%: Moderate (needs care, some erosion risk, steep for machinery)
- 10–30%: Poor (significant erosion risk, labor-intensive)
- 30%+: Unsuitable (steep, dangerous, not farmable without terracing)

## Part 4: Contouring (Contour Lines)

### What Are Contour Lines?

A **contour line** connects all points at the same elevation. On a map, contour lines show terrain shape.

**Example:** If you walk along a contour line (same elevation), you neither go uphill nor downhill.

### How Contour Lines Represent Terrain

**Flat area:** Contours are far apart (little elevation change in a given distance).

**Steep area:** Contours are close together (much elevation change in a short distance).

**Slope direction:** Contours are perpendicular to the steepest slope.

**Valley:** Contours form V-shapes pointing uphill (water flows downhill, so the valley points up).

**Ridge:** Contours form inverted V-shapes pointing downhill.

### Creating a Contour Map

**Method 1: Direct surveying**
1. Identify contour intervals (e.g., every 10 meters elevation)
2. Mark all points at elevation 100m (the first contour)
3. Mark all points at elevation 110m (next contour)
4. Continue for all elevations
5. Connect points of same elevation to form contour lines
6. Draw lines between marked points, estimating intermediate terrain

**Method 2: From grid of elevations**
1. Survey a grid of points (every 50m × 50m)
2. Measure elevation at each grid point
3. On a map, mark each point with its elevation
4. Draw contour lines connecting points of similar elevation (interpolating between them)
5. Spacing of lines reflects terrain steepness

### Contour Interval

The vertical distance between contour lines is the **contour interval**.

**Choosing interval:**
- Flat terrain: 1–5 meter intervals (small interval shows gentle slopes)
- Hilly terrain: 10–50 meter intervals (larger interval prevents crowding)
- Mountainous terrain: 100+ meter intervals (large intervals necessary)

**Rule of thumb:** Choose interval small enough to show important features but not so small that lines crowd.

## Part 5: Practical Terrain Mapping Workflow

### Step 1: Choose Reference Point and Datum

- **Reference point:** A location with known (or assumed) elevation
- **Datum:** Sea level (if known), arbitrary starting height, or nearest river
- Document this in your map notes

### Step 2: Establish Survey Points

- **Mark locations:** Place stakes or marks at regular intervals (grid: every 50–100 meters)
- **Record coordinates:** Use coordinate system from Ch 1
- **Number the points:** For easy reference in notes

### Step 3: Measure Elevations

For each survey point:
1. Measure elevation relative to reference point (using clinometer, level, or water level)
2. Record elevation in field notes
3. Verify measurements (measure twice, average if different)

### Step 4: Create Map

1. Draw map outline showing survey area
2. Mark all survey points with their coordinates and elevations
3. Draw contour lines connecting points of similar elevation
4. Interpolate between measured points (estimate terrain between survey points)
5. Add features (rivers, cliffs, known landmarks)

### Step 5: Label and Document

- **Title:** Name of area and date
- **Scale:** How many map centimeters = how many real meters
- **Contour interval:** What vertical distance each line represents
- **Datum:** Reference elevation chosen
- **Legend:** Symbols used (springs, peaks, cliffs, etc.)

## Part 6: Worked Examples

### Example 1: Mapping a Small Valley

**Survey area:** A valley 200m × 300m

**Reference point:** The stream at the valley bottom (elevation = 0m)

**Survey points:** Grid every 50m, resulting in 6 × 7 = 42 points

**Measurements (sample):**
- Southwest corner: 15 meters elevation
- Center: 5 meters elevation
- Northeast corner: 20 meters elevation

**Contours (at 5m intervals):**
- 5m contour: runs through center and follows stream
- 10m contour: forms a ring around the center, halfway up the slopes
- 15m contour: another ring higher up
- 20m contour: near the corners

**Interpretation:**
- Contours are close together on steep slopes (the valley sides)
- Contours are far apart in the gentle stream valley center
- V-shaped contours point uphill (up the valley)

### Example 2: Mapping a Hillside for Agricultural Assessment

**Survey area:** A proposed farm on a hillside (500m × 500m)

**Reference point:** Base of hill (elevation = 0m)

**Measurements (sample):**
- Base area: 0–10m elevation
- Middle area: 20–40m elevation
- Top area: 60–80m elevation

**Slope analysis:**
- Lower third (0–20m over 200m horizontal = 5.7% grade): Good for farming
- Middle third (20–60m over 200m horizontal = 11% grade): Moderate, needs erosion control
- Upper third (60–80m over 100m horizontal = 20% grade): Steep, unsuitable for traditional farming without terracing

**Recommendation:** Farm the lower area, use middle for pasture or terraced crops, leave upper area forested.

## Part 7: Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Confusing Elevation and Slope

Elevation is absolute height above a datum. Slope is the steepness. A point can be high elevation but on flat terrain, or low elevation but on steep slope.

### Mistake 2: Inconsistent Datum

If you measure elevations relative to different reference points without converting, your map will be wrong. Choose one datum and stick with it.

### Mistake 3: Sparse Survey Points

Too few measurements = interpolation guesses are wrong. Survey points should be close enough that you can estimate terrain confidently between them.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Uncertainty

Measurements are imperfect. Clinometer angles might be off by 1–2 degrees. Document your measurement accuracy, and note where uncertainty is highest.

### Mistake 5: Contour Lines Crossing

Contour lines should never cross each other (except in overhanging cliffs, rare). If lines cross, check your measurements—something is wrong.
