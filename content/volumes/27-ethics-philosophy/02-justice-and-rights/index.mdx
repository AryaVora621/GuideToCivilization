## Overview

What rights do people have? The right to live? The right to property? The right to vote? In collapse, these questions return to fundamentals. You cannot appeal to a higher authority. You must decide.

This chapter covers three approaches to rights (natural rights, utilitarian rights, and collective rights) and how to apply them when building legal systems. It also covers basic justice principles: proportionality, fairness, rehabilitation, and deterrence.

A just legal system is not perfect justice. It is consistency, transparency, and restraint. People accept punishment they believe is deserved; they accept losing disputes decided fairly. Injustice breeds resistance.

## Prerequisites

- Experience with conflict (you have been party to a dispute or witnessed one)
- Community role with some authority
- Understanding of ethical frameworks (Chapter 1 helps)

## Materials & Tools

- Written law (pen and paper, or carved in stone)
- Court or dispute-resolution process
- Record-keeping system

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Define Basic Rights

A right is something a person is entitled to, that others must not violate.

**Three approaches to what rights people have:**

**Approach 1 — Natural Rights (universal, inalienable):**

Certain rights exist by virtue of being human. No one can take them away legitimately. Examples:
- Right to life (no execution without due process)
- Right to bodily autonomy (no slavery, forced labor)
- Right to practice beliefs (religion, speech)

Natural rights are powerful: they constrain what the community can do. You cannot vote to enslave people because enslaving people violates a natural right.

**Weakness:** Who decides what is natural? Different communities have different answers.

**Approach 2 — Utilitarian Rights (derived from outcomes):**

Rights are whatever arrangements produce the best outcomes. If slavery produces starvation and misery, people do not have the "right" to enslave. If private property produces more total wealth, people have the right to own property.

**Weakness:** Outcomes are uncertain. You can justify almost anything by claiming it will have good outcomes.

**Approach 3 — Collective/Constitutional Rights (decided by community):**

People have the rights your community decides they have. The community writes a constitution listing them. These rights can change if the community agrees.

Advantages: flexible, community-driven. Weaknesses: can marginalize minorities (if 90% vote to oppress 10%, the 10% have no protection).

**Practical approach (hybrid):**

Start with natural rights (life, basic bodily autonomy, freedom from torture). These are non-negotiable.

Add rights your community values (property, speech, religious practice, political participation). These can be debated and adjusted.

Explicitly state who has rights (all humans? All community members? Only property-owners? This is your choice, and it matters).

### Step 2: Establish Principles of Justice

Justice answers: "When someone wrongs another, what response is just?"

**Principle 1 — Proportionality**

Punishment should fit the crime. Execution for theft is unjust. No punishment for assault is unjust.

Create a scale:
- Minor wrongs (insult, minor theft): Warning, apology, compensation
- Moderate wrongs (serious theft, injury): Restitution (pay back), reduced status, limited labor
- Serious wrongs (assault, murder): Long imprisonment, exile, or (in extreme collapse contexts) execution

**Principle 2 — Fairness/Equality Before Law**

The same crime receives the same punishment regardless of the person's status. A leader who steals receives the same punishment as a commoner. A rich person who harms a poor person is judged by the same standard as vice versa.

This is hard. People naturally favor their allies. Resist this. Fairness builds trust; favoritism breeds resentment.

**Principle 3 — Rehabilitation**

Where possible, the goal is to return the wrongdoer to the community as a productive member. Punishment should include education or work that corrects the behavior.

- Thief → restitution + teaching about property rights + monitoring
- Drunkard who caused harm → requirement to assist in damage repair + community oversight
- Violent person → separated from community (temporarily) + training in conflict resolution + supervised re-entry

**Principle 4 — Deterrence**

Punishment should discourage future crime. But excessive punishment is unjust and often backfires (harsh punishment can make someone desperate enough to harm others).

Effective deterrence is swift and certain. A theft that always results in consequences is more deterrent than random harsh punishment. A murderer certain to face trial and punishment is more deterrent than a murderer who escapes (certain punishment > harsh punishment).

**Principle 5 — Victim Compensation**

The wronged person should be compensated when possible. If someone steals, they pay back. If someone injures, they provide labor or goods to help the victim recover.

### Step 3: Design a Court System

A court system needs:

**Decision-makers:**

- Single judge (fast, but can be arbitrary)
- Panel of judges (slows decisions, adds perspective)
- Jury of community members (slow, but community accepts verdicts they participated in)

Start with panels of 3-5 respected community members. They hear the case, discuss, and decide together.

**Procedures:**

- Both sides present their version of events
- Witnesses speak
- Judges ask questions to understand what happened
- Judges discuss and decide
- Decision is announced with reasoning (why, not just what)
- Appeal process exists if new evidence emerges or procedure was violated

**Standards of evidence:**

- Criminal (murder, serious theft): "Beyond reasonable doubt" (very confident the person did it)
- Civil (property disputes): "Balance of probabilities" (more likely than not)

Clear standards prevent arbitrary judgment.

### Step 4: Address Specific Justice Problems

**Problem: Revenge cycles**

When someone is wronged, they want revenge. Revenge on the wrongdoer's family feeds new cycles of violence.

Solution: Law must replace revenge. "Justice is achieved through courts, not personal vengeance." Establish this principle early. Anyone who harms someone in revenge (not self-defense) is themselves guilty of assault or murder.

**Problem: False accusations**

Someone falsely accuses another of a crime to eliminate a rival.

Solution: False accusers face consequences. If you accuse someone of theft and cannot prove it, you must compensate them for harm to their reputation. This discourages false accusations.

**Problem: Inability to pay restitution**

A thief stole grain. They cannot repay it all at once. What happens?

Solution: Labor-for-restitution. The thief works (under supervision) to produce value to repay. Over years, they repay through work.

Alternatively, the thief's family helps repay, or the community absorbs the loss if the thief dies before repayment. (This depends on your values: do family members bear responsibility for debts? Decide and state it clearly.)

**Problem: Capital crimes**

When someone commits murder (or equivalent), is execution justice?

This depends on your ethical framework:
- Natural rights view: Execution violates the murderer's right to life. Murder is wrong; executing the murderer makes you a murderer too.
- Utilitarian view: If execution deters murder more than imprisonment, it is justified. If not, it is not.
- Community view: Your community decides. Some execute murderers, some imprison, some exile.

There is no objectively right answer. Decide based on your values and state it clearly.

**Problem: Evidence and certainty**

In modern justice, DNA proves guilt. In collapse, you have eyewitnesses (fallible) and circumstantial evidence (indirect).

Solution: High standard of evidence for serious crimes. Require multiple witnesses, or circumstantial evidence so strong that reasonable doubt is impossible. When unsure, the accused goes free. ("Better 10 guilty people go free than one innocent person is wrongly punished.")

### Step 5: Document Your Justice System

Write down:

1. **Rights:** What rights do all community members have? (Life, property, speech, etc.)
2. **Crimes and punishments:** What actions are illegal, and what is the consequence?
3. **Court procedures:** How are disputes resolved?
4. **Appeal process:** How can someone challenge a verdict?

Example:

```
COMMUNITY JUSTICE SYSTEM

RIGHTS:
All community members have the right to life, bodily autonomy, property ownership, and participation in community decisions.

CRIMES AND PUNISHMENTS:
- Theft: Restitution (repay 1.5x the value) + public apology
- Assault: Restitution (cover medical costs) + labor (20 days community service) + compensation to victim
- Murder: Exile (exile from community for 10 years) or Imprisonment (10 years in secure location)

COURT PROCEDURE:
1. Both parties present their case
2. Witnesses speak
3. Three-person panel discusses
4. Panel announces verdict and reasoning
5. Appeal allowed if new evidence emerges within 30 days

APPEALS:
A different three-person panel reviews the case. If they find error, the case is retried.
```

Publish this. Let people know what to expect.

### Step 6: Practice Justice Over Years

Initially, your system will be imperfect. You will:
- Discover unintended consequences of punishments
- Learn that some crimes you didn't predict happen
- Find that some community members reject your court's authority

Adjust. Every 2-3 years, review:
- What punishments worked? What backfired?
- Are people accepting verdicts?
- Are new types of crime appearing?
- Is the system being used fairly (or are wealthy/powerful people getting lenient treatment)?

Update the system based on experience.

## Verification

**Successful justice system results in:**

- Clear rules (people know what behavior is punished and how)
- Community acceptance (people accept verdicts they believe are fair)
- Low crime rate (people comply because they fear consistent punishment, not because they fear harsh punishment)
- Victim satisfaction (wronged people feel they received justice, not vengeance)
- Rehabilitation (people who are punished learn and improve)

**If the system fails:**

- Vigilante justice (people ignore courts and seek revenge)
- Arbitrary punishment (people believe decisions are biased)
- High recidivism (people commit the same crime again)
- Excessive punishment (people see the system as cruel, leading to rebellion)

If failure appears, convene the community. Discuss what went wrong and what needs to change.

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Confusing justice with vengeance.**

Justice is fair, proportional response to wrongdoing. Vengeance is inflicting suffering because you are angry. Justice has rules; vengeance does not. Make this distinction clear and enforce it.

**Mistake 2 — Punishing without understanding the cause.**

Someone steals food. Before punishing, ask why. Are they hungry? Is the food distribution system unfair? Is there a misunderstanding about what is community property vs. personal? Punishment without addressing the cause leads to repeat offenses.

**Mistake 3 — Executing people for economic crimes.**

Execution for theft is unjust (disproportionate) and counterproductive (it does not prevent theft better than other punishments, and it creates martyrs). Reserve execution for only the most serious crimes, if you use it at all.

**Mistake 4 — Allowing wealth or status to affect justice.**

A leader who commits the same crime as a commoner must face the same consequence. If justice is applied unequally, people lose faith in the system.

**Mistake 5 — Ignoring victim needs.**

Justice must include compensation or restoration to the victim. A sentence that removes the wrongdoer but leaves the victim uncompensated is incomplete.

**Mistake 6 — Permanence of punishment.**

A crime should not permanently bar someone from the community. After appropriate punishment (and rehabilitation), allow reintegration. Permanent exile makes someone desperate and dangerous.
