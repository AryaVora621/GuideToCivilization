## Overview

Post-collapse, you will face choices with no good answer. Do you save five sick people (using resources that could feed ten healthy people for a week)? Do you execute a thief, or exile them to almost-certain death? Do you break a promise to save a life? Do you allow someone to starve rather than violate your community's rules?

These are not abstract philosophy problems. They are your weekly reality. This chapter gives you three frameworks for thinking through these choices. None is perfect. All are useful.

The goal is not to find the "right" answer—there is none. The goal is to decide clearly, explain your reasoning to your community, and live with the consequences. A consistent framework prevents arbitrary leadership and builds trust.

## Prerequisites

- Experience with scarcity (you have lived through wanting something you could not afford or obtain)
- Leadership role (you make decisions affecting others, not just yourself)
- Basic understanding of community needs and tradeoffs (Vol 8: Society & Governance helps)

## Materials & Tools

This chapter requires no physical tools. It requires:
- Pen and paper (to record your framework and key decisions)
- Willingness to state your reasoning clearly
- Acceptance that you cannot please everyone

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Understand Consequentialism (Results-Based Ethics)

**Core idea:** The right action produces the best outcomes. Judge actions by their consequences, not by rules or intentions.

**How it works:**

You evaluate outcomes. What happens if you do X? What happens if you do Y? Pick the action that produces the best result.

"Best" usually means: maximizes wellbeing, minimizes suffering, preserves population, maintains security, or advances civilization progress (depending on what you value).

**Example: Scarce medicine**

Your settlement has medicine enough for one person. Two are dying: a child with pneumonia (50% recovery rate) and an elder with infected wound (80% recovery rate).

Consequentialist reasoning:
- Give it to the elder. The elder has a better chance of survival (80% vs 50%). Net result: higher expected survival.

**Strengths:**
- Clear and practical. You count outcomes and pick the best.
- Adaptable. You can change the weights (what counts as good outcome) based on community values.
- Efficient. Focuses resources on results, not rules.

**Weaknesses:**
- You rarely know the true consequences. Medicine that saves an elder might enable them to teach survival skills to 50 people, or might enable them to sabotage the settlement. The future is uncertain.
- It can justify atrocities. "Killing this person will reduce conflict" is a consequence. Consequentialism, taken to extremes, says that's fine if the outcome is good.
- It removes moral constraints. Lying, stealing, killing are all okay if outcomes are better.

**When to use:**
- Resource allocation (who gets medicine, food, shelter)
- Triage in crisis (who do we help first when we can't help everyone)
- Long-term planning (what actions advance the settlement's future)

**When NOT to use (alone):**
- Deciding whether to allow torture, slavery, or killing of innocents
- Making promises or contracts (consequences-only reasoning breaks trust)
- Protecting vulnerable people (consequentialism might sacrifice them for the "greater good")

### Step 2: Understand Deontology (Rules-Based Ethics)

**Core idea:** The right action follows the right rules or duties, regardless of consequences. Some things are always wrong, others always right.

**How it works:**

You identify core duties or rules. Then you follow them, even if consequences are bad.

Common duties:
- Do not kill innocents (even if killing one would save five)
- Keep promises (even if breaking them would improve outcomes)
- Treat others with respect (even if dominating them would be more efficient)
- Do not steal (even if stealing saves a life)

**Example: Scarce medicine (again)**

Same situation: medicine for the elder (80% recovery) or the child (50% recovery).

Deontological reasoning (with "protect children" as a core duty):
- Give it to the child. Children are especially vulnerable; you have a duty to protect them. This duty holds even though outcomes are worse.

**Strengths:**
- Creates stable, predictable systems. If people know you always keep promises, they trust you.
- Protects vulnerable people. A rule like "never harm children" protects them even when harm would have good consequences.
- Simple to teach. Rules are easier to teach and remember than consequence calculation.
- Moral clarity. It answers "is this action ever okay?" with a clear yes or no.

**Weaknesses:**
- Conflicts between rules. "Do not lie" conflicts with "protect the innocent." What if lying saves a life?
- Rigidity. Rules that made sense in one context may cause harm in another. "Do not take food" might lead to starvation.
- No mechanism to handle true dilemmas. If two duties conflict and both are inviolable, you cannot make a decision.

**When to use:**
- Establishing laws and rules (laws work better when they're not constantly overridden by consequence-calculation)
- Protecting vulnerable people (establish rules that protect them even when breaking the rules would have good outcomes)
- Building trust and stability (rules people can count on create cooperation)
- Personal integrity (if you promise something, you keep it)

**When NOT to use (alone):**
- Allocating scarce resources (rigid rules don't account for variation)
- Crisis triage (a rule like "first come, first served" might waste resources)
- Evolving threats (rules written for old situations may not work for new ones)

### Step 3: Understand Virtue Ethics (Character-Based Ethics)

**Core idea:** The right action flows from good character. Develop virtues (courage, honesty, generosity, prudence, justice) and act consistently with them.

**How it works:**

You do not ask "What are the consequences?" or "What does the rule say?" Instead you ask: "What would a courageous, honest, generous, prudent person do here?"

You cultivate virtues through practice. You become courageous by acting courageously. You become generous by giving. You become just by making fair decisions.

**Example: Scarce medicine (again)**

Virtue ethics reasoning:
- A just leader distributes according to need and merit.
- The child needs medicine more (children are more vulnerable).
- The elder has merit through experience and responsibility.
- A just person weighs both, considers the community's future (what does the settlement need more?), and decides.

There's no single "right" answer—a wise leader uses judgment based on the full context.

**Strengths:**
- Accounts for context and nuance. Virtues guide you but don't override judgment.
- Develops resilience. Practicing virtues builds character that serves you across many situations.
- Balances rules and consequences. A virtuous person follows good rules but can break them when necessary for a good outcome.
- Builds community cohesion. People respect leaders with strong character and follow them.

**Weaknesses:**
- Vague. "What would a virtuous person do?" is less clear than "follow rule X" or "maximize outcome Y."
- Slow. Building virtue takes years; you need an ethical framework now.
- Subjective. Different people have different intuitions about what virtue requires.
- Vulnerable to bias. You think you are virtuous but are actually rationalizing selfishness.

**When to use:**
- Long-term leadership (building a virtuous character makes you a better leader over decades)
- Training younger people (teach them virtues, not just rules)
- Making nuanced decisions (when rules conflict and consequences are unclear, ask what a wise person would do)
- Building culture (communities where people admire virtue and practice it are more cohesive)

**When NOT to use (alone):**
- Crisis decisions (no time for lengthy character reflection)
- Complex resource allocation (virtue doesn't tell you precisely who gets the medicine)
- Establishing clear public rules (rules are clearer than "act virtuously")

---

### Step 4: Choose Your Framework (or Combine Them)

Most effective communities use all three, in layers:

**Layer 1 — Rules (Deontology):**

Establish core rules: "Do not kill innocents." "Keep contracts." "Respect property." These are non-negotiable. They build trust and stability.

**Layer 2 — Virtues (Virtue Ethics):**

Within those rules, encourage people to develop virtues: courage (face difficulties), honesty (admit mistakes), generosity (share), prudence (plan ahead), justice (treat others fairly).

**Layer 3 — Consequences (Consequentialism):**

When rules conflict or don't cover a situation, ask: "What action produces the best outcome for the community while respecting our core rules and virtues?"

**Example decision tree:**

1. Is this covered by our core rules? If yes, follow the rule.
2. If not, or if rules conflict: What would a virtuous, wise person do?
3. If still unclear: What action produces the best outcome?

### Step 5: Apply the Frameworks to Common Scenarios

**Scenario 1: Theft during starvation**

Someone stole food to feed their family. Your rule is "theft is forbidden." Your virtue is "mercy." Your consequence is "if you execute this person, their family starves; if you don't, others may steal."

Decision:
- Rule says: Punish according to law.
- Virtue says: Show mercy; understand desperation.
- Consequence says: Punishment might prevent future theft, but it guarantees the family's starvation.

Synthesis: Mercy-based punishment (reduced rations for a time rather than execution). This respects the rule (theft has consequences), honors virtue (mercy), and produces good outcomes (family doesn't starve, future theft is discouraged, community sees justice tempered with compassion).

**Scenario 2: Medicine for the elderly vs. the young**

- Rule: "Save all who can be saved" (conflicts—you can only save one).
- Virtue: Protect the young; respect the elderly.
- Consequence: Young person has longer lifespan ahead; elderly person's death may be natural; but elder's knowledge is valuable.

Synthesis: Ask the community. If this is a values question (whom do we prioritize?), it should be decided publicly, not by a leader alone. The process (how you decide) matters as much as the outcome.

**Scenario 3: Breaking a promise to save a life**

- Rule: "Keep promises."
- Virtue: Courage to break a bad promise; honesty about why.
- Consequence: Breaking the promise saves a life but undermines trust in promises.

Synthesis: If the promise was made under duress or was immoral (e.g., "promise to let me enslave you"), you can break it. If it was a good-faith promise, the consequences of breaking it (lost trust) are often worse than the immediate harm of keeping it. Unless saving a life is truly more important than the entire trust system, keep the promise and address the life-threat another way.

### Step 6: Document Your Framework

Write down your community's ethical framework:

**Inviolable rules (Deontology):**
- (List 3-5 core rules: "Do not kill innocents," "Keep contracts," etc.)

**Core virtues (Virtue Ethics):**
- (List 3-5: courage, honesty, generosity, justice, prudence)

**Decision process (Consequentialism with constraints):**
- When rules don't apply, ask: What action respects our rules and virtues while producing the best outcome?

**Example document (for a settlement):**

```
COMMUNITY ETHICS FRAMEWORK

Inviolable Rules:
- Life: We do not execute people for economic crimes.
- Property: We respect individual ownership of personal goods.
- Promise: We keep agreements made in good faith.

Core Virtues:
- Courage: We face difficulties without panic.
- Honesty: We admit mistakes and speak truth.
- Generosity: We share in times of abundance.
- Justice: We treat people fairly, proportionate to their actions.

Decision Process:
1. Does a core rule apply? Follow it.
2. If not, ask: What would a courageous, honest, generous, just person do?
3. If still unclear, choose the action that best serves community survival and wellbeing, respecting steps 1 and 2.
```

Publish this. Let people know what your community believes. Consistency builds trust.

---

### Step 7: Revisit and Revise

Over years, you will learn that some rules are too rigid, some virtues are hard to practice, some consequences were worse than you expected.

Review your framework every 2-3 years:
- What rules have caused problems?
- Which virtues do people most admire?
- Where did your consequence-predictions fail?
- What would you change?

Update the framework with the community. Stability is important, but so is learning.

## Verification

**Successful ethical framework results in:**

- Clear community standards (people know what is expected)
- Consistency in decisions (similar situations are judged similarly)
- Trust in leadership (people believe you are trying to be fair, not arbitrary)
- Resilience (when new situations arise, people know how to reason about them)
- Flourishing (people develop virtues and make better decisions over time)

**If the framework fails:**

- Arbitrary decisions (similar situations treated differently)
- Loss of trust (people believe leaders are making self-serving choices)
- Conflict (disagreement about what is just or right)
- Rigidity (rules that made sense in crisis now cause harm, but people refuse to change)

If any of these appears, convene a community meeting. Discuss what is failing and how the framework should change.

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Assuming one framework is right.**

None is. Consequentialism, deontology, and virtue ethics each have strengths and blind spots. Use all three.

**Mistake 2 — Applying the same framework to all decisions.**

Rules work for establishing law. Virtue works for developing character. Consequences work for crisis triage. Use the right tool for the right problem.

**Mistake 3 — Claiming you are consequentialist while actually following rules.**

You say "I make decisions based on outcomes," but you always side with your friends, never break promises, or refuse to kill even when it would save lives. You are actually rule-driven or relationship-driven, not consequentialist. Own your actual framework.

**Mistake 4 — Ignoring community input.**

You decide your community's ethics alone and announce them. People do not buy into rules they did not help create. Develop the framework with community input.

**Mistake 5 — Changing the framework when it is inconvenient.**

You say "We follow our rules" but then break them when consequences seem better. People stop trusting you. Changes to the framework require public discussion and agreement, not unilateral reinterpretation.

**Mistake 6 — Forgetting that virtue requires practice.**

You cannot announce "we are courageous" and be courageous. You must practice. Provide opportunities for people to practice virtues.
