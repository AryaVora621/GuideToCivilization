## Overview

A social contract is an agreement: "If you follow these rules and respect these rights, I will do the same. We agree to live together under these principles."

Early collapse settlements are held together by shared crisis. That dissolves. Once basic needs are met, conflicts arise: Who leads? What counts as fair? When should rules bend?

A written social contract answers these questions before they explode into conflict. It is a covenant everyone agrees to. When disputes arise, people can point to the contract. "We all agreed to this."

This chapter covers how to write one.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of ethical frameworks (Ch 1)
- Understanding of justice principles (Ch 2)
- Community leadership role
- Experience with community decisions

## Materials & Tools

- Writing materials (paper, ink, or stone)
- Community meeting space
- Time (writing a charter takes weeks or months)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Determine What Needs a Contract

Not everything needs a written contract. A contract is useful for:

- Core governance (who decides what, how are decisions made?)
- Rights and responsibilities (what are people entitled to? what are they obligated to do?)
- Property (how is land owned, transferred, and protected?)
- Succession (how does leadership change when a leader dies or steps down?)
- Amendment (how can the contract be changed?)

A contract is overkill for:

- Daily rules (what time work starts, what food to share)
- Temporary agreements (this season's harvest sharing)

These can be decided by leaders and changed as needed.

### Step 2: Convene a Founding Assembly

Do not write the contract alone. Convene a group of respected people from across the community.

- Who? Leaders, elders, people respected for wisdom or experience
- How many? 5-15 people (large enough for perspective, small enough to reach decisions)
- How long? Multiple meetings over weeks. One meeting is not enough.

Process:
1. First meeting: Discuss values. What do we believe about rights, justice, property, leadership?
2. Second meeting: Discuss structure. How should decisions be made? Who leads?
3. Third meeting: Draft the contract.
4. Fourth meeting: Community ratification (the whole community votes on it).

### Step 3: Define Foundational Principles

Start with values. Write down what your community believes:

**Example foundational principles:**

```
We believe:
- All people have the right to life and basic dignity
- Property rights must be balanced with community needs
- Decisions should be made fairly with input from those affected
- Leaders serve the community, not themselves
- Rules apply equally to all people
- Justice should repair harm and rehabilitate wrongdoers
```

These principles guide all later decisions. If there is conflict, return to them.

### Step 4: Define the Government Structure

Decide how decisions are made. Common models:

**Model 1 — Single Leader (Chief or Mayor)**

One person makes decisions, ideally advised by a council. Fast decision-making. Risk: authoritarian power concentration.

**Model 2 — Council**

A group of leaders (5-15 people) make decisions together. Slower but more diverse perspective. Risk: stagnation if leaders disagree.

**Model 3 — Direct Democracy**

All adults vote on major decisions. Very slow. Maximum legitimacy. Risk: majority tyranny (51% vote to oppress 49%).

**Model 4 — Representative Democracy**

People elect representatives who make decisions on their behalf. Moderate speed. Moderate legitimacy. Risk: representatives ignore the people who elected them.

**Hybrid (most practical):**

- A council makes daily decisions
- Major decisions (war, legal changes, leader succession) require community voting
- A leader (elected, not appointed) chairs the council and coordinates
- Term limits prevent power concentration

### Step 5: Define Rights and Responsibilities

List what people are entitled to and obligated to do.

**Example rights:**

```
Every community member has the right to:
- Life and basic safety
- Access to sufficient food and clean water
- Property ownership (personal goods, some may own land)
- Participation in community decisions (voting)
- Justice (fair trial if accused of wrongdoing)
- Freedom of belief and speech (limits: cannot incite violence)
```

**Example responsibilities:**

```
Every community member is obligated to:
- Contribute labor to community needs (minimum: 20% of time)
- Obey community laws
- Respect others' rights and property
- Assist in defense if community is attacked
- Participate in community governance (attend voting)
```

Make these concrete. "Contribute to community needs" is vague. "20% of time to community projects" is clear.

### Step 6: Define Property Rights

How is land owned? Can it be inherited? Bought and sold?

**Land ownership models:**

**Model 1 — Communal:**

Land is owned by the community collectively. Individuals have use-rights (you can farm this plot, but you cannot sell it). Works well for agricultural land.

**Model 2 — Private:**

Individuals own land outright. They can farm, build, sell, and pass it to heirs. Works well for establishing trade and incentive. Risk: wealth inequality.

**Model 3 — Hybrid:**

Some land is communal (forests, water, defense walls), some is private (homes, personal gardens).

Decide:
- How much land does each household get?
- Can they own more through purchase?
- Can they sell land? To whom?
- What happens to land if someone dies without heirs?

Write it clearly.

### Step 7: Define Leadership Succession

When the leader dies or steps down, what happens?

**Succession models:**

**Model 1 — Hereditary:**

The leader's child or relative becomes the next leader. Simple but risks placing unqualified people in power.

**Model 2 — Elected:**

The community votes for the next leader. Democratic but slower and sometimes contentious.

**Model 3 — Appointed:**

A council chooses the next leader from among candidates. Moderate speed and legitimacy.

**Model 4 — Rotational:**

Leadership rotates among a group of pre-agreed people. Fair distribution but may not put the best leader in charge.

**Example succession clause:**

```
When the leader steps down or dies, a council of elders 
nominates three candidates from the community. 
All adults vote. The candidate with the most votes becomes 
leader for a five-year term. No person can serve more than 
two consecutive terms.
```

### Step 8: Define Amendment Process

How can the contract be changed?

If it cannot change, it becomes rigid and harmful. If it changes too easily, it lacks stability.

**Example amendment clause:**

```
Amendment requires:
1. A proposal written by community members
2. Community discussion (at least one month)
3. Community vote (two-thirds majority required)
4. Ratification by the council

Minor changes (clarification, correcting errors) require 
only council approval. Major changes (rights, government 
structure, property rules) require community vote.
```

### Step 9: Write and Ratify the Contract

Once the founding assembly agrees on principles, structure, rights, property, and succession:

1. One person (skilled in writing) drafts the full contract
2. Assembly reviews and edits
3. Document is copied (multiple copies, given to multiple people)
4. Community meeting is held
5. Contract is read aloud in full (takes 1-2 hours)
6. Community discusses and votes
7. If approved (majority, or two-thirds, depending on your decision), it is ratified
8. Copies are posted in public places

### Step 10: Enforce and Interpret

The contract is only useful if people follow it and interpret it consistently.

- Leaders must model compliance (if the leader ignores the contract, why should anyone follow it?)
- When disputes arise about the contract's meaning, a court or council interprets it
- Changes to interpretation should be rare and documented

Example: "The contract says 'sufficient food.' What counts as sufficient?"
- Decision: A caloric minimum (2,000 kcal/day for adults)
- Document: Write down your interpretation
- Consistency: Apply it the same way each time

### Step 11: Plan for Future Change

A contract made in crisis is likely to be imperfect. Plan for review:

- Every 5 years, hold a community assembly to discuss the contract
- Ask: What is working? What is failing? What needs to change?
- Allow amendments through the process you defined
- Update the document

Evolution is not failure. A contract that adapts to new realities is stronger.

## Verification

**Successful social contract results in:**

- Clear governance (people know who decides what)
- Legitimacy (people believe the government is fair)
- Stability (people follow rules even when rules are inconvenient)
- Peaceful change (when leadership changes, it happens without conflict)

**If the contract fails:**

- Conflict over authority (people disagree about who should decide)
- Non-compliance (people ignore rules they do not believe are fair)
- Power consolidation (a leader ignores the contract and consolidates power)
- Oppression (a faction uses the contract to oppress others)

If failure appears, use the amendment process. If that is blocked, you may need a new founding assembly.

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Writing the contract alone.**

You write a perfect contract reflecting your values. The community does not accept it because they did not help create it. Write it together.

**Mistake 2 — Too much detail.**

The contract tries to answer every possible scenario. It becomes a massive document no one reads or remembers. Keep it concise: core governance, basic rights, property, succession.

**Mistake 3 — Ignoring practical constraints.**

You write "every adult votes on every decision." In practice, people have work to do. Direct democracy is exhausting and fails. Build in representatives or a council to handle daily decisions.

**Mistake 4 — Writing it in crisis.**

A contract written while people are dying of hunger is likely to be bad. Write or revise it once basic stability is achieved.

**Mistake 5 — Treating it as permanent.**

You write a contract and say "this is forever." Circumstances change. Make clear how it can be amended.

**Mistake 6 — Allowing the leader to interpret it unilaterally.**

A clause means whatever the leader says it means. This leads to tyranny. Assign interpretation to a council or court separate from the leader.
