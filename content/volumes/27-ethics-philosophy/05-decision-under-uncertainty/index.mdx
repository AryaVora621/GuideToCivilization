## Overview

You must decide whether to plant a crop that might fail. Whether to send scouts toward a threat or avoid it. Whether to accept refugees or turn them away. Every decision happens with incomplete information.

Perfect knowledge is impossible. The question is: how do you decide well with the knowledge you have?

This chapter covers two tools: Bayesian reasoning (updating beliefs as you learn) and expected value (comparing uncertain outcomes). Together, they let you make rational decisions even when you cannot know the future.

## Prerequisites

- Decision-making experience
- Willingness to think probabilistically (in percentages and odds, not certainties)
- Basic math (multiplication, percentages)

## Materials & Tools

- Paper and pen
- Ability to estimate odds (rough percentages are fine)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Accept Uncertainty

You cannot know the future. Accept this. Your goal is not certainty, but reasonable decision-making despite uncertainty.

Common mistakes:

- **Denial:** "This will work out" (it might not)
- **Paralysis:** "I do not know the future, so I cannot decide" (you must decide anyway)
- **False confidence:** "It will definitely work" (you are overconfident)

Better approach: "This might work. Let me estimate the odds and decide based on those odds."

### Step 2: Estimate Initial Odds (Priors)

Before deciding, estimate the probability of each outcome.

Example: "Should we accept these refugees?"

Possible outcomes:
- Refugees integrate, contribute to the community (probability: ???)
- Refugees drain resources and cause conflict (probability: ???)

Ask yourself: "Based on what I know, what percentage chance of each outcome?"

These are your "priors" (initial beliefs).

Example estimates:
- Integration and contribution: 60%
- Resource drain and conflict: 40%

These are rough guesses. Precision is not the point; reasonable estimation is.

### Step 3: Gather Evidence

New information should update your beliefs. If you learn the refugees are skilled farmers, your estimate of positive outcomes increases. If you learn they are fleeing because they started a conflict elsewhere, your estimate decreases.

**Bayesian updating:** When you get new evidence, update your odds.

Formula (simple version):

New belief = (Prior belief × Likelihood of evidence given that belief) / (Likelihood of all outcomes)

In practice, you do not need the formula. Just ask:

- "If outcome A is true, how likely is this evidence?" (e.g., if refugees will integrate, how likely is it they are skilled farmers? Very likely. So this evidence supports outcome A.)
- "If outcome B is true, how likely is this evidence?" (e.g., if refugees will drain resources, how likely is it they are skilled farmers? Less likely. So this evidence supports outcome A over B.)

**Example:** You learn the refugees include two experienced farmers and a healer.

Updated belief:
- Integration and contribution: 70% (up from 60%)
- Resource drain and conflict: 30% (down from 40%)

The new information shifted your odds.

### Step 4: Consider the Costs and Benefits

Even if an outcome is likely, it might not be worth the risk if the downside is severe.

Compare outcomes by "expected value." This is the average outcome you expect, weighted by probability.

**Example:** Accept refugees

Outcome A (60%): Integration, contribution. Benefit: +50 units of labor/skill, growth
Outcome B (40%): Resource drain, conflict. Cost: -100 units (lost resources, internal conflict)

Expected value = (0.60 × 50) + (0.40 × -100) = 30 - 40 = -10

Expected value is negative. Even though integration is more likely, the downside is large enough that this decision might not be good.

Compare this to the alternative: Refuse refugees.

Outcome A (80%): They leave peacefully. Neutral (no change)
Outcome B (20%): They linger nearby, gradually cause problems. Cost: -20 units

Expected value = (0.80 × 0) + (0.20 × -20) = -4

Expected value is less negative. Refusing is slightly better than accepting.

Decide: Refuse refugees, or accept with careful vetting to reduce the probability of outcome B.

### Step 5: Recognize Your Confidence Level

Your odds are estimates, not certainties. Recognize how confident you are.

- High confidence: Estimate based on similar past situations (e.g., you have accepted refugees before)
- Medium confidence: Estimate based on general knowledge (e.g., you know refugees sometimes integrate, sometimes do not)
- Low confidence: Estimate with almost no information (e.g., you know almost nothing about these people)

Low confidence means your odds are rough guesses. Plan accordingly:

- If low confidence, seek more information before deciding
- If low confidence and you must decide anyway, hedge your bets (e.g., accept refugees but put constraints on them; plan contingencies in case they cause problems)

### Step 6: Make the Decision

Calculate expected values for each option. Compare.

The option with the highest expected value is the rational choice (though not the only reasonable choice—other factors, like your values, matter too).

**Example decision tree:**

```
Decision: Accept refugees?

Option A: Accept (with vetting)
- 70% integration, contribution: +50 value
- 30% drain, conflict: -80 value
Expected value: (0.70 × 50) + (0.30 × -80) = 35 - 24 = +11

Option B: Refuse
- 80% leave peacefully: 0 value
- 20% linger, cause problems: -20 value
Expected value: (0.80 × 0) + (0.20 × -20) = -4

Option A has higher expected value. Accept.
```

### Step 7: Plan for Wrong Outcomes

Even if your decision is rational, it might fail. Plan contingencies.

"We will accept the refugees, with the expectation that 70% of the time this works out. But there is a 30% chance of problems. If problems arise, here is our contingency plan..."

Contingencies:
- Limit refugee numbers at first (so if problems arise, damage is contained)
- Monitor relationships (so you detect problems early)
- Have exit options (if conflict becomes intractable, arrange for refugees to leave)
- Protect vulnerable assets (keep food stores separate, so if refugees need to eat, they do not consume your whole supply)

### Step 8: Learn and Update

After you decide, observe outcomes. Did your odds prove accurate?

"We accepted refugees. 70% expected them to integrate. They did integrate (success). Next time we face a similar decision, we can update our confidence."

Or: "We accepted refugees. We estimated 30% chance of drain and conflict. That is exactly what happened. Our estimate was accurate. Next time we will revise the odds or impose more stringent vetting."

Over time, your estimates improve. Experienced leaders make better odds estimates than inexperienced ones.

### Step 9: Handle Multiple Decisions

Complex situations involve many decisions. Chain them together:

"Accept refugees? If yes, then assign them to what role? If they integrate, how do we handle their children? Each decision has odds and expected values."

Map out the decision tree:

```
Accept refugees?
├─ Yes
│  ├─ They integrate (70%)
│  │  └─ Assign to agriculture
│  │     ├─ They produce surplus (50%)
│  │     └─ They are mediocre (50%)
│  └─ They cause conflict (30%)
│     └─ Separate them, or try to reconcile
└─ No
   ├─ They leave (80%)
   └─ They linger nearby (20%)
```

Evaluate each path. The highest expected value path is the rational choice.

## Verification

**Successful decision-making results in:**

- Consistent outcomes (your odds estimates prove accurate over time)
- Good decisions (on average, your decisions produce better outcomes than not deciding)
- Learning (your odds improve as you gain experience)
- Community confidence (people see that your decisions are reasoned, not arbitrary)

**If decision-making fails:**

- Bad outcomes without learning (decisions fail, but you do not update your odds)
- Overconfidence (you underestimate risk, leading to repeated failure)
- Paralysis (you never commit to a decision, so nothing improves)

If failure appears, review your decision-making process. Are your odd estimates accurate? Are you missing information before deciding?

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Treating probability as certainty.**

You estimated 70% chance of success and it failed (30% chance). You conclude your odds estimate was wrong. But 70-30 is still a reasonable estimate even if the 30% outcome happens. Do not react to single failures.

**Mistake 2 — Refusing to estimate.**

"I do not know the odds, so I will not try to estimate." But you must decide anyway. Any estimate, even a rough guess, is better than deciding without reasoning.

**Mistake 3 — Ignoring low-probability, high-impact outcomes.**

You estimate a 5% chance of catastrophe (e.g., accepting refugees triggers a conflict that destroys the settlement). That is low probability, but if it happens, the impact is enormous. Expected value: (0.05 × -1000) = -50. This can outweigh positive outcomes.

**Mistake 4 — Not updating beliefs.**

You made an estimate five years ago. You have learned new information. But you are still using the old odds. Update your beliefs as you learn.

**Mistake 5 — Confusing confidence with accuracy.**

You are very confident in your odds ("I am sure these are 70-30"). But you are actually guessing (low confidence). Be honest about how much you know.

**Mistake 6 — Optimism bias.**

Humans naturally overestimate positive outcomes and underestimate negative ones. Consciously correct for this. If you think a task has 90% chance of success, increase the failure estimate to 15-20%.
