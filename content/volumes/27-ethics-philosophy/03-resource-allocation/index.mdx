## Overview

Within weeks of collapse, you will have more people than food, more sick than medicine, more work than workers. Every resource will be rationed. The way you distribute what you have—fairly or unfairly, transparently or secretly—will determine whether your community fragments into factions or holds together through the hardest years.

This chapter teaches you how to allocate scarce resources ethically and practically. There is no system that makes everyone happy when the pie is too small. But there are systems that prevent starvation of some while others feast, that stop corruption and hoarding, and that preserve enough trust that people will accept the hard choices. Your goal is simple: everyone knows the rationing system, understands why they get what they get, and trusts that the system is fair even when it hurts.

This requires written rules, transparent accounting, and rotating responsibility so no single person controls distribution. This chapter gives you the frameworks and procedures to do that.

## Materials & Tools

**For allocation decisions:**
- Written rationing rules (1–3 page document, drafted by a council, approved by the community)
- Accounting ledger (who receives what, when, how much; see Vol 8, Ch 5: Record-Keeping)
- Distribution schedule (daily, weekly, or monthly—see procedure below)
- Measurement tools (scales for weight, containers for volume, to ensure fairness)
- Public posting (rules displayed in public space so everyone sees them)

**For enforcement:**
- Rotation roster (different people administer each week to prevent corruption)
- Witness requirement (at least 2 people present at each distribution to prevent theft)
- Sealed containers (so people cannot tell who received what by container appearance)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Calculate What You Actually Have (Week 1)

You cannot allocate what you do not know you have. Before any distribution happens, take complete inventory.

**Food inventory:**
- Count total grain (wheat, rice, corn, oats), in kg or consistent volume
- Count preserved meat (dried, salted, smoked)
- Count root vegetables, dried fruit, legumes
- Count oil/fat stores
- Calculate weeks of supply: total kg ÷ (population × daily ration in kg) = weeks of supply

**Example:** 5,000 kg of grain + 2,000 kg preserved food ÷ 1,000 people ÷ 0.5 kg per person per day = 14 weeks of supply total. This is the hard limit. Do not promise more.

**Medicine inventory:**
- Count antibiotics/equivalent (if you have preserved antibiotics from before collapse; otherwise herbal antimicrobials)
- Count pain relief (willow bark, poppy, other analgesics; see Vol 4: Medicine)
- Count bandages, thread for sutures, splinting material
- Estimate treatment capacity: how many wounds can you treat completely per month?
- Be honest: if you can treat 50 wounds with available supplies and have 100 injuries, you have a 50-wound shortage.

**Labor supply:**
- Count able-bodied adults (ages 16–60, in reasonable health)
- List critical tasks: farming, water carrying, food preparation, sanitation, medical care, defense, construction, administration
- Estimate hours per week each task requires
- Calculate deficit: if farming requires 500 hours per week and you have capacity for 300, you have a 200-hour deficit

Do this honestly. Do not pretend you have more food than you do or that you can treat more patients than you can. Lying about resources is the fastest path to famine and collapse of trust.

### Step 2: Draft the Rationing Rules (Week 2, with community input)

Do not decide alone. Convene a council of 5–15 people: elders, respected community members, people with medical/agricultural expertise, and a few common workers. The council drafts the rules. The community votes to approve.

**Core principles to encode in writing:**
1. **Need-based adjustment:** Pregnant women, nursing mothers, children, and the sick receive more than healthy adults
2. **Transparency:** Every person knows the formula and can verify their own allocation
3. **Rotation of responsibility:** Different person/group administers distribution each week; no one person controls food forever
4. **Accountability:** Every distribution is recorded in a ledger that is publicly readable once per month
5. **Appeal process:** If someone believes they are allocated unfairly, they can appeal to a 3-person arbitration panel

**Example rationing formula (adjust to your context):**

```
FOOD DISTRIBUTION RULES — Community Council Approved

Base ration: 400g grain per person per day

Adjustments:
- Pregnant women: +100g per day (+25%)
- Nursing mothers: +150g per day (+37.5%)
- Children ages 4–12: +100g per day until adolescence
- Children under 4: +200g per day (higher calorie density needs)
- Elderly (over 70): -50g per day (lower calorie needs) UNLESS frail → see medical exemption
- Medical exemption (high fever, active wound care, post-surgery): +200g per day

Special provisions:
- Every person receives salt (5g per day), oil (10g per day), some vegetable matter (fresh or preserved)
- The sick receive medicine first; medicine is not rationed beyond medical need
- Nursing mothers and pregnant women receive first choice of higher-calorie foods (meat, oil) in preference to grain

Frequency: Daily distribution at central location
Administration: Rotating team of 2 people per day, overseen by weekly administrator
Record-keeping: Written log of name, amount, date, witness signature
Public review: Ledger posted monthly; any person can inspect it

Appeals: If a person believes their allocation is incorrect, they may appeal to a 3-person panel (one chosen by the claimant, one by council, one neutral). Panel reviews the ledger, confirms medical exemptions, and renders judgment within 3 days.
```

Write this out. Post it. Have everyone who can read sign a copy. For those who cannot read, have a leader or literate person explain it to them in person. Get explicit agreement that the rules are fair.

**Critical rules to prevent corruption:**
- No one except medical staff (1–2 people) may assess whether someone qualifies for "medical exemption"
- The medical staff person assessing exemptions is different from the person administering distribution
- Every distribution is witnessed by at least 2 independent people, at least one of whom is not related to the recipient
- Ledger is locked after distribution and may not be altered

### Step 3: Implement Fair Distribution (Starting Week 3)

**Daily distribution procedure (assuming daily ration):**

1. Recipient arrives at distribution point
2. Administrator checks ledger: does this person exist, are they entitled to their base ration, and any adjustments?
3. Second witness verifies the entry
4. Administrator measures out the grain using a standard container
5. If the person has medical exemption, medical staff verbally confirms the exemption (not in writing, to preserve privacy)
6. Grain is placed in an unmarked container (so no one watching can tell how much each person got)
7. Recipient's name and amount are logged: "2026-06-16, Maria Gonzalez, 500g (base 400 + mother 100), witness: Juan, Robert"
8. Recipient leaves

**For weekly or monthly distribution:**
- Same procedure, but larger amounts are handed out (e.g., 2.8 kg for week 1)
- Recipient brings container to hold their ration; you do not provide containers (saves resource)

**Handling disputes in real-time:**
- If a person argues about their allocation:
  - Do not argue back; maintain dignity
  - Say: "I will record your concern. The arbitration panel will review your case. Return tomorrow."
  - Log in ledger: "2026-06-16, Maria disputed allocation, claims she should qualify for medical exemption, panel review requested"
  - Panel reviews within 3 days, makes a determination, and logs the decision

### Step 4: Allocate Medicine by Triage (Immediate, Ongoing)

Unlike food, which is rationed equally (with adjustments), medicine is allocated by medical need.

**Triage protocol (from Vol 4: Medicine):**

**Red (life-threatening):** Treat immediately, use medicine without limit. This includes:
- Severe infection/sepsis (fever >39°C, confusion, rapid pulse)
- Severe wounds (deep lacerations, heavy bleeding)
- Acute respiratory distress
- Childbirth complications
- Poison/toxin ingestion

**Yellow (serious):** Treat within 24 hours, use medicine conservatively. This includes:
- Moderate wounds (infection risk, but not actively bleeding out)
- Moderate fever (38–39°C)
- Fractures (immobilization)
- Chronic illness flare-ups

**Green (minor):** Treat with home care or minimal medicine. This includes:
- Minor cuts, scrapes
- Mild fever (<38°C)
- Cough, sore throat
- Mild pain

**Principle:** Use your most powerful medicine (antibiotics, strong analgesics) on red cases first. Use weaker treatments on yellow and green cases. Teach people to do most of their own care (home remedies, rest, herbal teas) so medicine lasts longer.

**Recording medicine use:**
- Medical staff logs every dose given: date, patient, condition, medicine used, amount, result (did it work?)
- This creates a database of what works. After 6 months, you will know your treatments are effective.

### Step 5: Allocate Labor by Rotation and Necessity (Month 1+)

Labor cannot be rationed like food; people must work. But you can make it fair.

**Critical jobs (must be done daily or people die):**
- Water carrying and purification
- Food preparation
- Sanitation (latrines, waste management)
- Medical care
- Defense (if under threat)

**Important jobs (must be done weekly or the settlement degrades):**
- Farming/gardening
- Tool maintenance and repair
- Building and construction
- Record-keeping and administration

**Nice jobs (done when there is time):**
- Teaching and learning
- Art and entertainment
- Long-term infrastructure (roads, bridges)

**Allocation rules:**
- Everyone ages 16–60 who is healthy contributes 30–40 hours per week of labor (5–8 hours per day)
- Pregnant women, nursing mothers, and the sick contribute 10–20 hours per week (lighter tasks, flexible schedule)
- Elderly contribute 10–20 hours per week if they wish (no requirement, but many will want to)
- Children ages 10–15 contribute 5–10 hours per week (learn while working, light duties)

**Rotation to prevent resentment:**
- Every person rotates through 3–4 different tasks per month (2–3 weeks each)
- No one does the most unpleasant job (waste removal, latrine cleaning) for more than 1 week per quarter
- Leadership and administrators do 2 weeks per month of manual labor (keep them connected to reality)
- Specialists (healer, blacksmith, teacher) who cannot rotate keep their role, but contribute 1 week per quarter to common labor

**Enforcement:**
- Failure to show up for assigned labor has consequences:
  - First occurrence: warning and community discussion
  - Second occurrence: loss of 10% of food ration for one week
  - Third occurrence: loss of 25% of food ration for one week and public rebuke
  - Persistent non-compliance (4+ occurrences): community vote on exile or other punishment
- Exemptions for legitimate illness (documented by healer) or family emergency (approved by council)

**Example:** The settlement has 1,000 people. 600 are aged 16–60 and able-bodied. That is 18,000–24,000 labor-hours per week available. Critical jobs need 10,000 hours, important jobs need 8,000 hours, nice jobs need 2,000 hours. You have 4,000 hours of slack. Every person gets 40 hours per week, and there is room for some flexibility and some people to take a week off.

### Step 6: Document Decisions and Appeal (Ongoing)

Every allocation decision is recorded. This prevents corruption and provides proof if someone later claims they were treated unfairly.

**What to record:**
- Date
- Resource type (food, medicine, labor assignment)
- Who received it / was assigned it
- Quantity or description
- Justification if non-standard (e.g., "medical exemption," "punishment for non-compliance")
- Two witness names
- Any appeals or disputes filed

**Public access:**
- Once per month, the full ledger is read aloud publicly (or posted if people can read)
- Any person can request to see the ledger in detail (only their own entries, unless they request others')
- This creates accountability and makes cheating visible

**Dispute process:**
- If someone disputes an allocation, they file a complaint with the arbitration panel within 7 days
- Panel is 3 people: one chosen by the complainant, one by the council, one mutually agreed neutral party
- Panel reviews the ledger and interviews the administrator and witnesses
- Panel renders judgment within 5 days
- Decision is recorded and posted publicly

## Verification

**Success looks like:**
- After month 1: rules are written, posted, and the community has voted to accept them
- After month 2: distribution is happening daily/weekly without major disputes
- After month 3: ledger is public and auditable; people can see their own entries and understand them
- After month 6: one or two disputes have been resolved fairly; people trust the system

**Failure looks like:**
- Rationing rules are kept secret or change without explanation
- The same person administers distribution for months; people suspect theft
- Medicine is allocated secretly; sick people do not know why they are or are not receiving treatment
- The ledger is not public; accusations of favoritism grow
- Disputes are not resolved; people stop reporting inequity because no one listens

## Common Mistakes

1. **"We have enough, so we don't need rules"** — Scarcity always arrives faster than expected. When it does, if you have no rules in place, chaos happens immediately. Write rules now, even in abundance. Use them if abundance holds; they are ready if scarcity comes.

2. **"Let the leader decide allocation"** — This is the fastest path to authoritarianism and corruption. The leader gets hungry too, and suddenly their family eats more than others. Use the council and written rules. The leader enforces the rules; the leader does not make exceptions.

3. **"We will allocate by money"** — You have no money in the first months. And even if you did, money favors whoever had it before collapse. Use need and rotation instead.

4. **"Medicine should be rationed equally"** — Medicine is only valuable if it works. Using a powerful antibiotic on a minor infection when someone else is dying of a treatable infection is waste. Use triage. Allocate by medical need.

5. **"We don't need witnesses; everyone here is honest"** — Hunger makes people dishonest. Good people steal food for their children. Have witnesses. Write everything down. Trust but verify.

6. **"Let's not keep a ledger; it's too much work"** — A ledger is 10 minutes per day. It prevents 10 hours of disputes per week. Keep it.

7. **"If someone complains, we will just give them more"** — This creates incentive to complain. Give more only if the appeal panel rules in their favor. Otherwise, trust the system.

8. **"Old people and children should get less; they are less productive"** — Wrong. Children become your future. Elders have experience and wisdom. Allocate by need, not productivity. A person who is dying is not productive either, so you heal them.
