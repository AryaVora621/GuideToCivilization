## Overview

Leavened bread requires yeast or acid to rise. Post-collapse, fermented bread is staple carbohydrate.

This chapter covers:
1. **Sourdough starter:** Cultivating wild yeast and bacteria.
2. **Bread baking:** Using starter to leaven dough.
3. **Alternative leavening:** Baking soda, whey, fermentation without starter.
4. **Scaling:** Community bread baking.

Sourdough transforms grain into digestible, shelf-stable food. Essential skill post-collapse.

## Prerequisites

Understand yeast fermentation (Vol 12, Ch 2-3). Know grain production (Vol 10).

## Part 1: Sourdough Starter

### What Is Sourdough Starter?

Starter is a living culture of wild yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae) and lactic acid bacteria (Lactobacillus).

**Contents:**
- Wild yeast: Produces gas (CO2) that leavens bread.
- Lactic acid bacteria: Produce acid; flavor sour taste; preserve dough.

**Post-collapse advantage:** Self-perpetuating; no purchased yeast needed; improves with age.

### Cultivating Starter (From Scratch)

**Timeline:** 5-7 days to establish; 4-8 weeks to mature.

**Ingredients:**
- Flour (whole wheat best; contains more wild yeast than white).
- Water (non-chlorinated if possible; chlorine kills bacteria).
- Salt (optional; later additions).

**Process:**

**Day 1:**
- Mix 50g flour + 50g water in clean jar.
- Stir well; cover loosely with cloth (allows air; prevents dust).
- Leave at room temperature (18-25°C).

**Days 2-3:**
- Feed daily: Discard half the mixture (25g); add 25g flour + 25g water.
- Stir; cover.
- You will see bubbles starting (yeast/bacteria multiplying).

**Days 4-7:**
- Continue feeding daily (same 50-50 flour:water ratio).
- Starter will become increasingly active (bubbly; sour smell developing).
- By day 5-7, starter should double in size within 4-8 hours of feeding (sign of readiness).

**Week 2+:**
- Once active, can feed less frequently (every 2-3 days; or daily if using regularly).
- Starter continues improving in flavor (complexity develops over months/years).

### Maintaining Starter

**Feeding schedule:**
- Active use: Feed daily (before using in dough).
- Weekly baking: Feed 1-2 times daily (morning and night) the day before baking.
- Occasional use: Feed once weekly minimum (prevents starvation; maintains culture).

**Feeding ratio:**
- Simple: 1:1:1 by weight (starter:flour:water).
- Example: 50g starter + 50g flour + 50g water = 150g fed starter.
- Use what you need; discard excess to maintain manageable volume.

**Storage:**
- Room temperature if using regularly (daily feeding).
- Cold storage (refrigerator) if using weekly or less (slows activity; extends time between feedings to 1-2 weeks).
- Can dry starter on paper (emergency backup; rehydrate if needed).

### Starter readiness for baking

**Signs of readiness:**
- Doubles in size 4-8 hours after feeding.
- Bubbly throughout (not just surface).
- Sour smell (pleasant; not rotten).
- Passes the float test: Drop spoonful in water; floats = active.

**Using in dough:**
- Use starter at peak (when bubbly and maximum rise).
- Or use "fed but not yet peaked" starter for longer fermentation (slower rise; deeper flavor).

## Part 2: Bread Baking with Sourdough

### Simple Sourdough Recipe

**Ingredients (for 2 loaves):**
- 500g flour (all-purpose, whole wheat, or mix).
- 350g water.
- 100g active starter (fed 4-8 hours prior; bubbly).
- 10g salt (optional; enhances flavor; slows fermentation).

**Equipment:**
- Mixing bowl.
- Kitchen scale (helpful but not essential).
- Dutch oven or covered baking vessel (traps steam; improves crust).
- Thermometer (optional; monitors temperature).

### Baking Steps

**1. Mix dough (evening):**
- Combine water + starter; let sit 30-60 minutes.
- Add flour; mix until shaggy (wet dough is normal).
- Add salt; mix thoroughly.
- Cover; let rest overnight (8-12 hours) at room temperature.

**2. Bulk fermentation (overnight; 8-12 hours):**
- Dough ferments passively overnight.
- Yeast leavens slowly; bacteria develop flavor.
- Dough rises; becomes airier.
- Temperature affects speed (cool = slower; warm = faster).

**3. Shape (morning):**
- Turn dough onto floured surface.
- Pre-shape: Gently fold edges to center; let rest 20-30 minutes.
- Final shape: Fold into round or oval; place in floured basket or bowl.

**4. Final proof (2-4 hours):**
- Shaped dough rises further (30-50% volume increase).
- Poke test: Poke dough; if indent springs back slowly, ready. If springs back fast, not ready. If doesn't spring back, over-proofed.

**5. Bake:**
- Preheat oven to 450°C (very hot).
- Score dough (knife cuts on top; releases steam; controls expansion).
- Bake covered (Dutch oven or covered pan; traps steam) 20 minutes.
- Uncover; bake 25-35 minutes until deep brown.
- Total baking: 45-50 minutes.

**6. Cool:**
- Cool on rack before slicing (crumb sets as it cools).
- At least 1 hour of cooling before eating.

**Result:** Crusty exterior; open crumb (holey interior); sour flavor; digestible.

### Timeline and Temperature

**Room temperature (20-25°C):**
- Bulk fermentation: 8-12 hours.
- Final proof: 2-4 hours.
- Total time: 10-16 hours.

**Warm room (25-30°C):**
- Bulk fermentation: 4-8 hours.
- Final proof: 1-2 hours.
- Total time: 5-10 hours.

**Cool room (15-18°C):**
- Bulk fermentation: 12-16 hours.
- Final proof: 4-6 hours.
- Total time: 16-22 hours.

**Post-collapse:** Work with available temperature; plan baking schedule accordingly.

## Part 3: Alternative Leavening Methods

### Baking soda (chemical leavening)

**Requires acid source:**
- Whey (from cheese or yogurt making).
- Vinegar (homemade acceptable).
- Buttermilk (if dairy available).

**Recipe:** Flour + water + acid + baking soda → CO2 → bread rises.

**Timeline:** Minutes to hours (chemical reaction; much faster than fermentation).

**Advantage:** No starter needed; quick baking.

**Disadvantage:** No flavor development; less digestible than fermented bread.

**Use case:** Emergency baking; when time is critical.

### Self-rising by fermentation (without starter)

**Method:**
- Mix flour + water; let sit at room temperature 12-24 hours.
- Wild yeast naturally colonize and ferment dough.
- Dough rises; becomes sour.
- Bake normally.

**Advantage:** No starter maintenance; spontaneous fermentation.

**Disadvantage:** Less predictable; may fail if conditions poor; slower (requires 12-24 hours).

**Use case:** When starter is unavailable; experimental.

### Quick breads (no leavening)

**Method:**
- Flatten dough; bake immediately.
- No rise; dense bread (flatbread, crackers).

**Cooking methods:**
- Oven baking: 20-30 minutes at 200°C.
- Pan frying: Cast iron or griddle; 5-10 minutes per side.
- Buried coals: Wrap in leaves; bury in hot coals; 10-15 minutes.

**Post-collapse advantage:** Minimal fuel; quick food.

## Part 4: Scaling Bread Production

### Household (5-10 people)

**Weekly baking:**
- One batch per week (2 loaves).
- 1-2 hours active labor; 16+ hours passive fermentation.
- Starter maintenance: 5-10 minutes daily.

**Flour needed:** 25-50 kg per year.

**Starter:** One jar (maintained on kitchen counter).

### Settlement (50-100 people)

**Daily baking:**
- Multiple batches per day (10+ loaves daily).
- 3-4 hours active labor (mixing, shaping, baking).
- Dedicated baker (1 person full-time).

**Infrastructure:**
- Community oven (wood-fired; heats multiple loaves).
- Mixing equipment (large bowl or trough).
- Multiple starters (backup if one fails).

**Flour needed:** 500-1000 kg per year.

**Labor economics:**
- One baker produces 50+ loaves per week.
- Accounts for 3-7 hours labor per 50 loaves.
- Post-collapse, feeding settlement from single baker is feasible.

### Community oven benefits

**Efficiency:**
- One large fire heats oven to 400°C+.
- Multiple loaves bake simultaneously.
- Better fuel economy than individual household ovens.

**Social:**
- Baking day is community event.
- Maintains morale and cohesion.

## Part 5: Advanced Baking Techniques

### Gluten development

**What is gluten?** Protein network in flour that traps gas bubbles.

**Development methods:**
1. **Stretching and folding:** Every 30 minutes during bulk fermentation, wet hand grabs dough side, stretches up and folds over. Repeat 4-6 times. Develops gluten without traditional kneading.

2. **Autolyse:** Mix flour + water; rest 30-60 minutes before adding starter + salt. Flour hydrates; gluten develops passively. Reduces mixing time needed.

3. **High hydration dough:** Wetter dough (60%+ water) requires more handling to develop gluten. Post-collapse: use 50-60% hydration (manageable for hand mixing).

**Post-collapse:** Stretching and folding is accessible (no mixer needed); improves bread quality significantly.

### Hydration and dough consistency

**Percentage:** Water weight ÷ flour weight × 100.

**Examples:**
- 50% hydration: 500g flour + 250g water (stiff dough; easy to handle; dense bread).
- 65% hydration: 500g flour + 325g water (medium dough; balanced; good for beginners).
- 80%+ hydration: 500g flour + 400g water (sticky dough; difficult to handle; very open crumb if managed well).

**Post-collapse guideline:** Start at 65% hydration (350g water per 500g flour). Adjust based on flour type (whole wheat absorbs more; needs more water).

### Fermentation temperature control

**Desired temperature:** 20-25°C (room temperature).

**In summer (hot house):**
- Fermentation faster (may need shorter times; watch closely).
- Risk: Over-fermentation; sour flavor too intense.
- Solution: Ferment in coolest room; use cold water in dough.

**In winter (cold house):**
- Fermentation slower (extend times; be patient).
- Advantage: Deep flavor development; extended fermentation time.
- Solution: Ferment near heat source (not too close); insulate with blankets.

**No temperature control:**
- Accept variable fermentation times.
- Use visual/smell cues (dough risen and sour) rather than strict timing.

## Part 6: Troubleshooting Bread Baking

### Starter won't rise

**Cause:** Dormant (underfed) or dead (contaminated).

**Prevention:** Feed regularly; keep clean.

**Fix:** Feed twice daily for 3-5 days (may revive). If no bubbles after 1 week, start fresh batch.

### Bread doesn't rise

**Cause:** Weak starter, cold temperature, or over-salting.

**Prevention:** Use active starter; ferment at 20-25°C; limit salt to 10g per 500g flour.

**Fix:** Next batch: use freshly fed starter; warm fermentation location.

### Bread is dense (no holes)

**Cause:** Under-fermentation, weak starter, or poor gluten development.

**Prevention:** Longer bulk fermentation (12+ hours); stronger starter (regular feeding); mix and fold during fermentation.

**Fix:** Next batch: extend fermentation time; improve starter health.

### Bread spreads flat (no oven spring)

**Cause:** Over-proofed (fermented too long) or weak gluten.

**Prevention:** Use poke test; don't over-ferment; develop gluten by stretching and folding during bulk fermentation.

**Fix:** Shorter final proof time; adjust fermentation temperature.

## Verification

Your baking skill is adequate if:
1. **Starter:** You maintain active sourdough starter.
2. **Dough:** You can mix and ferment dough properly.
3. **Shaping:** You shape dough for final proof.
4. **Baking:** You bake in available oven (conventional, wood-fired, or alternative).
5. **Consistency:** You produce edible bread regularly (loaves are not dangerous; flavor and texture acceptable).
6. **Scaling:** You can produce 50+ loaves per week for settlement of 100.

## Common Mistakes

**Killing starter with chlorine.** Use filtered or boiled water for feeding.

**Over-fermentation.** Dough becomes over-sour; collapses during baking. Use poke test; bake when ready.

**Insufficient gluten development.** Bread is gluey; doesn't hold shape. Fold and stretch dough during bulk fermentation.

**No steam in oven.** Crust is pale and thick. Use Dutch oven or covered pan to trap steam.

**Baking in cold oven.** Bread doesn't rise. Preheat to 450°C+ before baking.

**Starter too salty.** Salt slows fermentation. Add salt to dough, not to starter feedings.

## Part 7: Bread Storage and Shelf Life

### Shelf life of sourdough

**Room temperature storage:**
- 3-5 days (crust softens; crumb dries slightly; mold risk increases after day 5).
- Keep in paper bag or cloth (allows air circulation; prevents moisture buildup that promotes mold).

**Longer storage:**
- Freeze slices (lasts months).
- Or dry for crackers/breadcrumbs (indefinite storage).

**Traditional practice:**
- Bake bread once per week.
- Fresh for first few days.
- Stale bread used for soups, breadcrumbs, or feed animals.
- No waste.

### Stale bread uses (post-collapse)

**Softening stale bread:**
- Sprinkle with water; cover; warm in oven (moisture restores texture temporarily).
- Toast stale bread (becomes crispy; acceptable texture).

**Conversion:**
- **Breadcrumbs:** Dry stale bread; crumble; use for breading or binding.
- **Soup bread:** Add stale bread chunks to soups/stews (absorbs liquid; thickens).
- **Croutons:** Cut into cubes; dry in oven; store for months.
- **Animal feed:** Pigs and chickens eat stale bread readily (supplement feed; not primary diet).

**Wastage prevention:** Community bread-making produces consistent supply; bread eaten before spoiling.
