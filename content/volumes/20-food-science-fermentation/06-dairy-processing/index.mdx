## Overview

Fresh milk spoils in days. Processed milk products last months/years. Post-collapse, dairy processing is critical for food preservation and nutrition security.

This chapter covers:
1. **Cheese-making:** Converting milk to stable, shelf-stable product.
2. **Butter:** Separating and preserving milk fat.
3. **Yogurt:** Fermented milk for digestion and storage.
4. **Whey:** Byproduct uses (baking, animal feed, fertilizer).

## Prerequisites

Understand fermentation (Vol 12, Ch 2). Know milk production (Vol 11, Ch 1).

## Part 1: Cheese-Making Fundamentals

### Why Cheese?

**Fresh milk:** 2-3 days refrigerated (post-collapse: 1 day unrefrigerated).

**Cheese:** 3-24 months at cool temperature (depends on type).

**Shelf life advantage:** 100× longer storage; concentrated nutrition; stable flavor.

### Cheese science

**Process:**
1. Acidification: Bacteria or acid lowers pH.
2. Coagulation: Milk protein (casein) forms curds.
3. Cutting: Curds are cut; whey drains.
4. Cooking: Temperature adjustment changes curd texture.
5. Draining: Whey separates; curds consolidated.
6. Salting: Salt added (preserves; flavors).
7. Aging: Time develops flavor.

**Chemistry:** Milk → curds (protein + fat) + whey (water + lactose).

### Types of cheese (simple post-collapse options)

**Soft cheese (1-14 days aging):**
- Ricotta: Whey-based; crumbly.
- Chevre: Fresh goat cheese; tangy.

**Hard cheese (3-12 months aging):**
- Cheddar: Cow milk; sharp; ages well.
- Parmesan: Cow milk; hard; granular texture; long aging.

**Post-collapse:** Start with soft cheese (easy; quick results); advance to hard cheese (requires patience and cool storage).

## Part 2: Hard Cheese Making (Cheddar-Style)

### Why hard cheese?

**Aging:** 3-12 months (vs. 1-14 days for soft cheese).

**Shelf life:** Very stable; flavor improves with age.

**Nutrition:** Concentrated protein and fat; calorie-dense.

### Hard cheese process (simplified)

**Ingredients:**
- 10 liters milk (larger batch required for hard cheese).
- Starter culture (or use whey from previous cheese).
- Rennet (enzyme that causes coagulation; if unavailable, substitute with acid + time).
- Salt.

**Equipment:**
- Large pot.
- Thermometer.
- Curd knife (or clean knife; cuts curds into small pieces).
- Cheesecloth.
- Cheese mold (wooden box with drainage holes; or improvise with cloth and basket).
- Weight (for pressing).
- Cool storage (10-15°C for aging).

### Simplified hard cheese steps

**Day 1:**
1. Heat milk to 32°C.
2. Add starter (or whey); let sit 30 minutes.
3. Add rennet (follow instructions); curds form within 30 minutes.
4. Cut curds into pea-sized pieces.
5. Heat slowly to 40°C over 30 minutes (stirring occasionally).
6. Drain whey; collect curds in cheesecloth.
7. Press curds overnight under weight.

**Day 2+:**
1. Remove from mold; cut into blocks.
2. Dry at room temperature for 1-2 weeks (turns slightly hard).
3. Salt the surface.
4. Age in cool place (10-15°C) for 3-12 months.
5. Turn cheese periodically (promotes even aging).

**Result:** Hard cheese with firm texture; sharp flavor after aging; shelf-stable indefinitely if kept cool.

**Post-collapse note:** Hard cheese-making is advanced; requires equipment and cool storage. Start with soft cheese; progress to hard cheese as confidence and facilities improve.

## Part 3: Simple Fresh Cheese (Ricotta)

### Ricotta recipe (starting point)

**Ingredients:**
- 1 liter milk (fresh is best).
- Acid: vinegar (or whey from previous cheese-making).
- Salt.

**Equipment:**
- Pot.
- Thermometer.
- Cheesecloth or fine mesh strainer.
- Colander.

### Steps

**1. Heat milk:**
- Heat milk to 80°C (hot but not boiling).
- Stir occasionally to heat evenly.

**2. Add acid:**
- Add 2-3 tablespoons vinegar (or whey) while stirring.
- Milk will immediately curdle (separate into white curds and yellowish whey).

**3. Let sit:**
- Stop stirring; let sit 10 minutes.
- Curds will firm up slightly.

**4. Strain:**
- Pour through cheesecloth into colander.
- Collect curds; whey drains.
- Whey can be saved (for baking, animal feed, or next batch).

**5. Press and salt:**
- Gather cheesecloth corners; hang to drain further (1-2 hours).
- Once firm enough, place curds in bowl.
- Add salt to taste.

**6. Use immediately or store:**
- Fresh ricotta: Best used same day (texture is creamy).
- Storage: Keep cool; use within 3-5 days.

**Result:** 100-150g ricotta from 1 liter milk. High protein; versatile (cooking, baking, spreading).

## Part 3: Butter

### Butter-making (simple)

**Method:** Separate cream from milk; churn cream to butter.

**Process:**

1. **Separate cream:** Let milk sit in cool place 12-24 hours; cream rises to top. Skim cream (or use centrifuge if available).

2. **Culture cream (optional):** Leave cream at room temperature 8-12 hours to develop tang. Skip if not desired.

3. **Churn:** Place cream in jar with tight lid; shake vigorously 10-15 minutes. Or use churn if available.
   - Cream thickens → whipped cream → flakes of butter appear.
   - Continue shaking until butter separates from buttermilk.

4. **Drain buttermilk:** Pour off liquid (buttermilk; save for baking).

5. **Wash butter:** Squeeze under cold water to remove residual milk (prevents spoilage).

6. **Salt (optional):** Work in salt to taste (preserves longer).

7. **Store:** Cool place; lasts 1-2 weeks unrefrigerated; months if salted and kept cool.

**Yield:** 1 liter cream → ~200-250g butter + buttermilk.

## Part 4: Yogurt

### Yogurt fermentation

**Method:** Milk + starter culture (Lactobacillus) → fermented yogurt.

**Starter:** Purchased yogurt with live cultures (if available); or wild fermentation.

**Science:** Lactobacillus consume lactose; produce lactic acid; yogurt sets as acid denatures milk protein.

**Process:**

1. **Heat milk:** 80°C for 10 minutes (kills competing bacteria; denatures whey protein; improves texture).
2. **Cool:** To 45°C (hot enough to kill pathogens; cool enough for starter bacteria).
3. **Add starter:** 2-3 tablespoons yogurt per liter milk (bacteria multiply in warm milk).
4. **Incubate:** Keep at 40-45°C for 4-8 hours (optimal temperature for Lactobacillus).
   - Without incubation box: Wrap bowl in blankets; place in warm spot; or sit in warm water bath.
5. **Cool:** Refrigerate when desired sourness reached (stops fermentation).

**Fermentation timeline:**
- 4 hours: Mild, creamy yogurt.
- 8 hours: Tangy, thicker yogurt.
- 12+ hours: Very sour, whey separation visible.

**Shelf life:** 1-2 weeks refrigerated; longer if kept very cold.

**Straining yogurt (Greek-style):**
- Strain yogurt in cheesecloth 4-8 hours (whey drains; yogurt becomes thick).
- Strained yogurt: Like cream cheese; lasts 3-4 weeks.
- Collected whey: Save for cooking/baking/cheese-making.

### Post-collapse yogurt making

**Maintaining culture:** 
- Save 2-3 tablespoons from each batch (use as starter for next batch).
- Culture can be reused indefinitely with regular feeding.
- If culture dies (no fermentation), start fresh with whey from previous cheese batch (natural cultures).

**Scaling for settlement:**
- Make one 10-liter batch daily (uses 2-3 hours).
- Provides probiotic source; extends milk shelf life; uses byproduct whey.
- Yogurt consumed immediately or strained for longer storage.

## Part 5: Whey Uses

### Byproducts of cheese and yogurt-making

**Whey composition:** Water, lactose (sugar), minerals, proteins.

**Uses:**

1. **Cooking and baking:**
   - Replace water in bread dough (adds nutrition).
   - Add to soups (nutrition).
   - Pickle vegetables (acid; preserves).

2. **Animal feed:**
   - Pigs and chickens consume whey readily.
   - Adds protein and minerals to diet.

3. **Fertilizer:**
   - Add to compost or dilute for garden fertilizer.
   - Minerals benefit plants.

4. **Bacterial cultures:**
   - Save whey from fresh cheese-making; use as starter for next batch.
   - Contains beneficial bacteria.

**No waste:** All dairy processing outputs have uses.

## Part 6: Milk Handling and Safety

### Raw vs. Pasteurized milk

**Raw milk (unpasteurized):**
- Direct from animal; may contain pathogens (Brucella, E. coli, Listeria).
- Post-collapse: No pasteurization equipment; must use raw milk.
- Advantage: Enzymes and cultures intact; better for fermentation.
- Risk: Foodborne illness if animal unhealthy or milk contaminated.

**Safety measures for raw milk:**
- Source milk only from healthy animals (see Vol 11, Ch 1 for health assessment).
- Use sanitary practices (clean udders; clean containers; cool immediately).
- Test milk safety visually (no discoloration, off-smell, clots).
- Ferment or heat before storage (fermentation and heat kill most pathogens).

### Heating milk (alternative to pasteurization)

**Low-temperature heating:**
- Heat milk to 65°C for 30 minutes (kills most pathogens; preserves cultures).
- Or: Heat to 80°C for 10 minutes (faster; kills more pathogens; safe for cheese/yogurt).

**Post-collapse:** Use available heat source (fire, stove). Monitor temperature with thermometer.

## Part 7: Cheese Aging and Storage

### Storage conditions by cheese type

**Soft cheese (ricotta, chevre):**
- Temperature: Cool (10-15°C) if possible; room temp acceptable.
- Duration: 1-14 days.
- Storage: Container or wrapped; keep cool.

**Hard cheese (cheddar, parmesan):**
- Temperature: Cool cellar (10-15°C) optimal; warmer is acceptable but shortens life.
- Duration: 3-24 months.
- Storage: Wooden shelf or cloth wrap (allows air circulation; prevents mold).
- Maintenance: Turn periodically (every 1-2 weeks) for even aging.
- Indicator: Cheese should dry slightly; small mold may develop on surface (normal; can be scrubbed off).

**Cheese with mold growth:**
- Surface mold (white/grey): Usually harmless; scrub or scrape off.
- Blue-green or pink mold: May indicate contamination; if limited to surface, can cut away; if throughout, discard.
- Smell test: Sour is normal; rotten smell indicates discard.

### Cheese preservation techniques

**Vacuum sealing (if possible):**
- Removes air; extends shelf life; prevents mold.

**Oil coating:**
- Coat hard cheese in food-grade oil; prevents drying.

**Cloth wrapping:**
- Wrap in cloth; allows breathing; prevents plastic moisture buildup.

**Caves/cellars:**
- Ideal storage is cool, humid cellar (10-15°C, 85%+ humidity).
- Post-collapse: Use natural caves, deep basements, or buried storage if available.

## Part 8: Scaling Dairy Processing

### Household (dairy cow or goats)

**Daily milk:** 10-20 liters (from 1-2 dairy animals).

**Processing options:**
- Daily: Make fresh cheese or yogurt.
- 2-3× weekly: Make butter.
- Weekly: Start hard cheese aging.

**Space needed:** Cool area (cellar, shed) with temperature 10-15°C for aging.

### Settlement (multiple dairy animals)

**Daily milk:** 50-100+ liters (from 5-10 dairy animals).

**Processing:** Dedicated dairy room; multiple cheese wheels aging; daily butter production, yogurt fermentation.

**Labor:** 2-3 hours daily for processing (one dedicated person).

**Output:** 5-10 kg cheese per week; 10-20 kg butter per week; yogurt as needed.

## Part 7: Troubleshooting Dairy Processing

### Cheese won't firm up

**Cause:** Temperature too low; acid insufficient; bad milk.

**Prevention:** Heat to exact temperature; use measured acid; use fresh milk only.

**Fix:** Cheese may still be usable (soft cheese acceptable); reduce liquid by letting sit longer.

### Cheese tastes off

**Cause:** Contamination; bad milk; wrong temperature.

**Prevention:** Sanitize equipment; use fresh milk; monitor temperature closely.

**Fix:** If mild flavor issue, may use anyway. If clearly bad, discard.

### Butter won't separate

**Cause:** Cream too warm or too cold; insufficient churning time.

**Prevention:** Keep cream at 15-20°C; churn longer if needed.

**Fix:** Continue churning (may take 20+ minutes in cold conditions).

### Yogurt is too thin (not setting)

**Cause:** Starter weak; temperature too low; not fermented long enough.

**Prevention:** Use fresh yogurt starter; maintain 40-45°C; ferment 6-8 hours.

**Fix:** Add more starter; extend fermentation time; or use as yogurt drink (thinner but drinkable).

## Verification

Your dairy processing is adequate if:
1. **Milk sourcing:** You have reliable milk supply (1+ dairy animal or access to milk).
2. **Cheese-making:** You can make simple cheese (ricotta or fresh cheese).
3. **Butter:** You can separate and churn butter.
4. **Yogurt:** You can ferment yogurt using starter.
5. **Storage:** You have cool area (10-15°C) for aging cheese.
6. **Scaling:** You can process daily milk production without waste.
7. **Byproducts:** You use whey and buttermilk (no waste).

## Common Mistakes

**Using unsanitized equipment.** Contamination ruins batches. Boil all equipment; dry thoroughly.

**Wrong milk temperature.** Too hot kills starter; too cold, bacteria don't ferment. Use thermometer; target exact temps.

**Over-aging soft cheese.** Soft cheese spoils if left too long. Use within specified timeframe.

**Not using salt in cheese.** Salt preserves and flavors. Even small amount extends shelf life.

**Ignoring whey.** Byproduct should be used; don't discard. Save for cooking, animals, or next batch.
