## Overview

Post-collapse, adequate nutrition will be harder to achieve. You will not have fortified cereals, multivitamins, or fresh produce year-round. Understanding nutritional requirements helps you plan food production and distribution.

This chapter covers:
1. **Caloric needs:** How many calories humans need per day.
2. **Macronutrients:** Carbohydrates, proteins, fats.
3. **Micronutrients:** Vitamins and minerals.
4. **Deficiency diseases:** What happens when nutrition is inadequate.

Poor nutrition is silent; malnutrition takes months to kill, but suffering is severe.

## Prerequisites

Understand body anatomy (Vol 4, Ch 1). Know food production (Vol 10).

## Part 1: Caloric Requirements

### Daily Caloric Needs

Humans need calories to fuel body functions and activity.

**Basal metabolic rate (BMR):** Calories needed for resting (breathing, heartbeat, digestion).
- Adult human: 1200-1800 kcal/day (at complete rest).
- Varies by age, sex, body size.

**Activity multiplier:**
- Sedentary (no physical work): BMR × 1.2-1.4 = daily need.
- Moderate activity (light work 1-2 hours/day): BMR × 1.4-1.6.
- Heavy activity (physical labor 6-8 hours/day): BMR × 1.8-2.0.

**Post-collapse estimates:**

| Activity Level | Daily Calories |
|---|---|
| Sedentary adult (child, elderly, limited mobility) | 1800-2200 |
| Moderate activity (some work, small farm) | 2200-2800 |
| Heavy labor (farming, construction, manual work) | 2800-3500 |
| Extreme labor (harvest, building, emergency) | 3500-4500 |

**Children:**
- Age 1-3: 1000-1400 kcal/day.
- Age 4-8: 1400-2000 kcal/day.
- Age 9-13: 1800-2600 kcal/day.
- Age 14+: 2200+ kcal/day (similar to adults).

**Pregnancy and lactation:**
- Pregnancy: Add 300-500 kcal/day.
- Lactation: Add 300-500 kcal/day.
- Combined (pregnant and nursing): Add 600-1000 kcal/day.

### Calculating Caloric Need for Settlement

**Example: 100-person settlement.**

Assume:
- 20 children (age 1-8): Avg 1200 kcal/day × 20 = 24,000 kcal.
- 10 elderly/limited mobility: 2000 kcal/day × 10 = 20,000 kcal.
- 70 adults, moderate activity: 2500 kcal/day × 70 = 175,000 kcal.

**Total daily:** 219,000 kcal/day.

**Total yearly:** 219,000 × 365 = 80,000,000 kcal = 80 million kcal.

**Production targets:**
- Grain (1800 kcal/kg): 44,000 kg grain per year (44 metric tons) for grain-only diet (not recommended).
- Plus vegetables, legumes, meat, dairy for balanced diet.

**Practical approach:** Plan 0.4-0.5 tons of grain per person per year for a 50% grain diet.

**Sample food pyramid (for 100 people, one year):**

| Food Group | % Calories | kg/year | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Grains | 50% | 40,000 | Wheat, barley, rice fields |
| Legumes | 15% | 6,000 | Beans, lentils, peas |
| Vegetables | 15% | 15,000 | Root crops, leafy greens, squash |
| Meat/Fish | 10% | 4,000 | Livestock, hunting, fishing |
| Dairy | 7% | 3,000 | Milk, cheese production |
| Fruit/Nuts | 3% | 2,000 | Orchard, preserved fruit |

**Land requirements (rough estimate):**
- Grains: 30-40 hectares (wheat yields 1-2 tons/hectare).
- Legumes: 10-15 hectares.
- Vegetables: 5-10 hectares (intensive; root crops lower yield).
- Livestock: 10-20 hectares (pasture for cattle, sheep, goats; also provides meat/dairy/eggs).
- Total: 55-85 hectares for 100-person settlement (0.55-0.85 ha per person).

**Reality check:** Small settlement (100 people) needs ~70 hectares of farmland (plus forest, buildings, water).

### Seasonal Variation

**Summer abundance:**
- Fresh vegetables, dairy (peak production), eggs, meat.
- Calorie density: 2200-2500 kcal/person/day (easier to meet).

**Winter scarcity:**
- Stored grains, preserved vegetables, minimal fresh produce.
- Limited dairy (animals not milking), limited eggs (reduced laying), limited fresh meat.
- Calorie density: 1800-2200 kcal/person/day (harder to meet).

**Implication:** Summer production must store enough calories for winter. 8 months of winter requires grain storage of 8/12 of yearly grain production.

### Tracking Nutritional Status

**For settlement leadership:**
- Monitor growth in children (height, weight).
- Monitor energy levels (are workers able to complete labor?).
- Monitor disease rates (deficiency diseases, recurrent infections).
- Monitor body condition in adults (visible ribs, hip bones = malnutrition warning sign).

**Action if inadequate nutrition detected:**
- Increase rations of most-deficient nutrients (grains if calorie-short; legumes if protein-short; leafy greens if vitamin-short).
- Reduce non-essential activities (minimize physical labor if calories are low).
- Improve food storage and preservation (reduce spoilage).
- Plan crop changes for next year (add legumes, vegetables if missing).

## Part 2: Macronutrients

### Carbohydrates (Starches and Sugars)

**Function:** Primary energy source.

**Daily need:** 50-65% of total calories.

**Example (2500 kcal diet):**
- 50%: 1250 kcal = 312 grams carbohydrate.
- 65%: 1625 kcal = 406 grams carbohydrate.

**Sources (post-collapse):**
- Grains: Wheat, barley, rice, corn (60-70% carbohydrate).
- Legumes: Beans, lentils (50-60% carbohydrate, also protein).
- Root crops: Potatoes, sweet potatoes, turnips (15-25% carbohydrate).
- Vegetables: Squash, carrots (5-15% carbohydrate).
- Fruits: Apples, berries (10-20% carbohydrate).

**Post-collapse reality:** You will eat more carbohydrates than pre-collapse (grains are staple; meat/dairy are limited). 60-70% carbohydrate diet is normal.

### Proteins

**Function:** Build and repair muscle, bone, enzymes, immune cells.

**Daily need:** 0.8-1.0 grams per kg body weight (average adult).

**Example (70 kg adult):**
- Minimum: 56 grams/day.
- Recommended: 70 grams/day.
- Heavy labor: 100+ grams/day.

**% of calories:** 10-15% of total.

**Sources (post-collapse):**
- Animal sources (complete protein, all amino acids):
  - Meat: 20-25% protein (beef, pork, poultry).
  - Dairy: 3-4% protein (milk), 25-30% (cheese).
  - Eggs: 13% protein.
- Plant sources (incomplete, lacking some amino acids):
  - Legumes: 20-25% protein (beans, lentils).
  - Grains: 8-15% protein (varies by type).
  - Nuts: 15-25% protein (if available).

**Amino acid concern:** Plant proteins lack some amino acids (e.g., legumes low in methionine; grains low in lysine). Combination (grain + legume) provides complete protein.

**Post-collapse protein diet:**
- Grain + legume combination (e.g., rice and beans): Provides all amino acids.
- Limited animal products (meat 1-2x/week, dairy occasional).
- 50-60 grams protein/day from mixed sources (acceptable).

### Fats

**Function:** Energy storage, vitamin absorption, hormone production.

**Daily need:** 20-35% of total calories.

**Example (2500 kcal diet):**
- 20%: 500 kcal = 55 grams fat.
- 35%: 875 kcal = 97 grams fat.

**Sources (post-collapse):**
- Animal: Butter, lard, milk (dairy), meat (contains fat).
- Plant: Nuts, seeds (if available), oils (walnut, sunflower).

**Deficiency risk:** Low in post-collapse if animal products are scarce (meat, dairy, oils limited). Inadequate fat = poor vitamin absorption = deficiency diseases.

**Strategy:** Prioritize fat sources (butter, lard, oils) for both nutrition and vitamin absorption.

## Part 3: Micronutrients (Vitamins and Minerals)

### Critical Vitamins Post-Collapse

**Vitamin A (retinol):**
- Function: Vision, immune function, skin health.
- Deficiency signs: Night blindness (early), dry eyes (severe), blindness (extreme).
- Sources: Animal liver, dairy, orange/yellow vegetables (beta-carotene converted to vitamin A).
- Post-collapse risk: Moderate (orange vegetables available; liver available).

**Vitamin C (ascorbic acid):**
- Function: Immune function, collagen synthesis, antioxidant.
- Deficiency signs: Scurvy (bleeding gums, poor wound healing, joint pain).
- Sources: Fresh fruits, vegetables. LOST in storage; preserved by fermentation (sauerkraut).
- Post-collapse risk: HIGH (winter scarcity of fresh produce).

**Vitamin D (cholecalciferol):**
- Function: Bone health, calcium absorption, immune function.
- Deficiency signs: Rickets (children; bone deformities), osteomalacia (adults; bone pain, weakness).
- Sources: Sunlight (skin synthesis), fatty fish, dairy, egg yolks.
- Post-collapse risk: Moderate (indoor work, limited sunlight in winter; fish availability varies).

**Vitamin B complex (thiamine, riboflavin, niacin, B6, B12, folate):**
- Function: Energy metabolism, nerve function.
- Deficiency signs: Beriberi (thiamine; nerve damage), pellagra (niacin; dermatitis, diarrhea, dementia).
- Sources: Whole grains (not refined), legumes, meat, dairy, vegetables.
- Post-collapse risk: Moderate (if refined grains are used; whole grains provide adequate B vitamins).

**Vitamin E:**
- Function: Antioxidant, protects cells.
- Deficiency signs: Rare; neurological damage in extreme deficiency.
- Sources: Nuts, seeds, plant oils, green vegetables.
- Post-collapse risk: Low (plant oils and nuts available).

### Critical Minerals Post-Collapse

**Calcium:**
- Function: Bone health, muscle contraction, nerve function.
- Deficiency signs: Osteoporosis (bones weaken), muscle cramps, poor wound healing.
- Sources: Dairy (primary source post-collapse), leafy greens, legumes, fish with bones.
- Post-collapse risk: HIGH if dairy is limited (no supplementation available).

**Iron:**
- Function: Oxygen transport in blood, energy production.
- Deficiency signs: Anemia (fatigue, weakness, pale skin), poor cognitive function.
- Sources: Meat (better absorbed), legumes, dark leafy greens.
- Post-collapse risk: Moderate (plant iron is poorly absorbed; need vitamin C for absorption).

**Zinc:**
- Function: Immune function, wound healing, protein synthesis.
- Deficiency signs: Poor immunity, slow wound healing, diarrhea, dermatitis.
- Sources: Meat, legumes, nuts, whole grains.
- Post-collapse risk: Moderate (available from diverse sources).

**Iodine:**
- Function: Thyroid function, metabolism.
- Deficiency signs: Goiter (enlarged thyroid), cretinism (children; intellectual disability, stunted growth).
- Sources: Sea vegetables (kelp), fish, iodized salt (if available).
- Post-collapse risk: CRITICAL (no iodized salt; sea vegetables required; region-dependent).

**Selenium:**
- Function: Immune function, thyroid function, antioxidant.
- Deficiency signs: Keshan disease (heart damage), Kashin-Beck disease (joint damage).
- Sources: Meat, fish, whole grains, nuts, vegetables (if soil is selenium-rich).
- Post-collapse risk: Moderate (region-dependent; selenium content in crops depends on soil).

## Part 4: Deficiency Diseases

### Scurvy (Vitamin C Deficiency)

**Cause:** No fresh fruit/vegetables for weeks/months (winter storage).

**Signs:**
- Bleeding gums (early).
- Poor wound healing.
- Joint pain (especially knees, ankles).
- Lethargy, depression.
- Death if severe (months of deficiency).

**Prevention:** Fermented vegetables (sauerkraut), pickled items, stored vegetables (potatoes, turnips contain some vitamin C).

**Treatment:** Fresh produce if available; fermented vegetables; herbal teas (pine needle tea has vitamin C).

### Goiter (Iodine Deficiency)

**Cause:** No iodized salt, no sea vegetables, no iodine-rich foods.

**Signs:**
- Enlarged thyroid (lump in neck).
- Fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance (hypothyroidism).
- Intellectual disability in children (cretinism).

**Prevention:** Iodized salt (if available), sea vegetables (kelp if accessible), fish (if region has coastline).

**Treatment:** Sea vegetables if accessible; iodized salt if available.

### Beriberi (Thiamine Deficiency)

**Cause:** Polished white rice diet (refined; lacks thiamine).

**Signs:**
- Peripheral nerve damage (tingling, weakness in extremities).
- Heart problems (wet beriberi; fluid retention, arrhythmias).
- Cognitive changes.

**Prevention:** Use whole grains (brown rice, whole wheat) not refined. Legumes provide thiamine.

**Treatment:** Whole grains, legumes, meat.

### Pellagra (Niacin Deficiency)

**Cause:** Corn-based diet without legumes (corn is low in bioavailable niacin).

**Signs:** The "4 Ds": Dermatitis (skin rashes), Diarrhea, Dementia, Death.

**Prevention:** Combine corn with legumes; use whole grains; adequate meat/dairy (niacin sources).

**Treatment:** Whole grains, legumes, meat, dairy.

### Rickets (Vitamin D Deficiency in Children)

**Cause:** Limited sunlight exposure, no dairy, no fatty fish.

**Signs:**
- Bone deformities (bowed legs, enlarged joints).
- Delayed growth, delayed tooth eruption.
- Muscle weakness.

**Prevention:** Sunlight exposure (30 minutes daily if possible); dairy/fish if available.

**Treatment:** Sunlight, dairy, fish (fatty fish best).

### Anemia (Iron Deficiency)

**Cause:** Limited meat, poor plant iron absorption.

**Signs:**
- Fatigue, weakness.
- Pale skin, pale mucous membranes.
- Poor cognitive function, difficulty concentrating.
- Shortness of breath with exertion.

**Prevention:** Meat 1-2x/week (adequate iron); legumes + vitamin C for plant iron absorption.

**Treatment:** Meat, legumes with vitamin C source (e.g., beans with sauerkraut).

## Verification

Your nutritional understanding is adequate if:
1. **Caloric planning:** You know your settlement's daily caloric needs.
2. **Food production:** You produce grains, legumes, vegetables, and some animal products to meet needs.
3. **Macronutrient balance:** Your diet is 50-70% carbohydrate, 10-15% protein, 15-30% fat.
4. **Micronutrient sources:** You have sources for critical vitamins/minerals (leafy greens, roots, meat, dairy, fermented vegetables).
5. **Deficiency prevention:** You understand which deficiencies are risks and how to prevent them.
6. **Storage:** You preserve vegetables and fruits to provide variety year-round.

## Common Mistakes

**Caloric starvation.** Inadequate food production leads to hunger and weak laborers. Plan for 2500+ kcal per person per day.

**Grain-only diet.** Grains alone cause malnutrition (missing amino acids, vitamins). Diversify with legumes, vegetables, meat.

**No fat.** Low-fat diet prevents vitamin absorption. Prioritize butter, lard, oils.

**Winter scurvy.** No fresh vegetables for months. Plan fermented/stored vegetables.

**Iodine deficiency.** Goiter and cretinism are preventable. Include sea vegetables or iodized salt.

**Refined grains.** White rice and white flour lack B vitamins. Use whole grains.

**No diversity.** Monotonous diet leads to deficiency. Grow multiple vegetables and fruits.
