## Overview

Food spoilage is microbial (bacteria, mold, yeast) or chemical (oxidation, enzymes). Understanding spoilage prevents foodborne illness and food waste.

This chapter covers:
1. **Bacterial spoilage:** Most common; causes off-flavors, gas, slime.
2. **Mold and yeast:** Visible; indicate moisture and contamination.
3. **Chemical spoilage:** Oxidation, enzyme action; less dramatic but degradation.
4. **Detection:** Signs of spoilage; when to discard.

## Prerequisites

Understand fermentation (Vol 12, Ch 1-7). Know sanitation (Vol 5, Ch 6).

## Part 1: Bacterial Spoilage

### Why bacteria spoil food

**Bacteria consume nutrients:** Food is substrate for microbial growth.

**Byproducts:**
- Gas (CO2, H2S): Causes swelling, off-smell.
- Acids: Cause sour taste, texture breakdown.
- Enzymes: Break down proteins, fats; cause slime, off-flavors.
- Toxins: Cause illness (see foodborne illness section below).

### Factors favoring bacterial growth

**Temperature:** 5-40°C is "danger zone" (bacteria multiply fastest 20-37°C).

**Moisture:** Bacteria need water (aw &gt;0.85).

**Oxygen:** Most spoilage bacteria are aerobic (need oxygen).

**Nutrients:** Proteins, carbohydrates, minerals; food contains all.

**Time:** Bacteria double every 20 minutes under optimal conditions.

### Common spoilage bacteria

**Bacillus:** Gas-producing; causes bloating in canned goods.

**Clostridium:** Anaerobic (no oxygen); produces gas; very dangerous if toxins present.

**Lactobacillus:** Acid-producing; causes sourness (useful in fermentation; spoilage in non-fermented food).

**Pseudomonas:** Produces slime; off-flavors; likes cold temperatures (refrigerator spoilage).

## Part 2: Mold and Yeast Spoilage

### Molds

**Growth conditions:**
- Temperature: 5-35°C (grows even in refrigerator).
- Moisture: Needs water (aw &gt;0.70, lower than bacteria).
- Oxygen: Aerobic (needs air).

**Appearance:**
- White, green, black, or pink colonies.
- Fuzzy or powdery surface.

**Signs of mold:**
- Visible growth (most obvious).
- Musty smell.
- Texture change (soft, crumbly).

**Toxins:** Some molds produce mycotoxins (dangerous; see foodborne illness section).

### Yeasts

**Growth conditions:**
- Similar to mold.
- Often visible as white film or haze.

**Spoilage signs:**
- Cloudiness (especially in liquids).
- Off-smell (fruity, fermented).
- Slight fizz (gas production).

### Mold/yeast management

**Prevention:**
- Keep food dry (moisture control).
- Keep food cool (&lt;10°C inhibits mold growth).
- Exclude oxygen (seal containers).

**When visible:**
- Soft cheese (white mold): Normal; scrape off; rest is safe.
- Hard cheese (white/grey mold): Scrape off; safe.
- Bread (mold): Discard entirely (mold roots penetrate bread).
- Vegetables (mold): Discard if soft/slimy; safe if just surface mold on hard vegetables (cut away).
- Liquids (cloudiness/film): Discard (mold or yeast pervasive).

## Part 3: Chemical Spoilage

### Oxidation (rancidity)

**Cause:** Fats exposed to oxygen; break down into off-flavors and odors.

**Signs:**
- Rancid smell (sharp, unpleasant).
- Bitter taste.
- Brown discoloration.

**Prevention:**
- Exclude oxygen (vacuum seal, refrigerate).
- Keep fat away from light and heat.
- Use within reasonable timeframe (months, not years).

**Foods affected:**
- Butter (rancid in 1-3 months at room temperature; months in cool storage).
- Nuts and seeds (rancid in weeks if not protected).
- Cured meats (fat goes rancid; trim excess fat).

### Enzyme action

**Cause:** Natural enzymes in food continue breaking down structure even without microbial growth.

**Examples:**
- Browning of fruits (polyphenol oxidase enzyme).
- Softening of vegetables (pectin breakdown).
- Protein breakdown in fish/meat (autolysis).

**Prevention:**
- Heat (blanching inactivates enzymes).
- Acid (inhibits enzyme action).
- Cold (slows enzyme action).
- Remove air (some oxidative enzymes need oxygen).

## Part 4: Detecting Spoilage

### Sensory tests (safe for most foods)

**Sight:**
- Mold (white, green, pink, black).
- Discoloration (brown, grey).
- Slime or liquid (not normal).

**Smell:**
- Off-odor (sour, musty, rotten, rancid).
- No smell is usually good sign.

**Taste (use caution; spit out if off):**
- Sour taste (fermentation or spoilage).
- Bitter taste (oxidation, enzyme action).
- Unpleasant aftertaste.

**Texture:**
- Slime or sticky surface.
- Mush or breakdown.
- Gas bubbles (fermentation or spoilage).

### Foods where sensory tests are NOT safe

**Canned goods (Clostridium botulinum risk):**
- Never taste if swollen or suspicious.
- Trust visual signs (bulging = discard).
- When in doubt, discard.

**Food with toxins (mycotoxins, shellfish toxins):**
- Cannot taste or see toxins.
- If source is questionable, discard.

**Highly perishable (fish, raw meat):**
- Trust sensory signs; if any doubt, discard.

## Part 4b: Specific Foods and Their Spoilage Patterns

### Vegetables

**Fresh vegetables (root crops: turnips, carrots, potatoes):**
- Spoilage: Softening, rotting (Botrytis or other fungi).
- Prevention: Store cool (5-10°C), dark, dry.
- Shelf-life: Weeks to months depending on crop and storage.
- Signs of spoilage: Soft spots, mold, rot (discard affected area if small; discard entirely if extensive).

**Leafy greens:**
- Spoilage: Wilting, slime (bacterial breakdown).
- Prevention: Keep cool, dry; use quickly (days, not weeks).
- Shelf-life: 3-7 days fresh.

### Grains and legumes

**Stored grain:**
- Spoilage: Mold (if moisture too high); insect infestation.
- Prevention: Keep dry (&lt;13% moisture); cool; sealed containers.
- Shelf-life: Years if properly stored.
- Signs of spoilage: Musty smell, mold visible, insect damage.

**Legumes (dried beans):**
- Spoilage: Similar to grains; also hardens (loses cooking ability).
- Prevention: Dry storage; sealed; cool.
- Shelf-life: 1-2 years (hardens with age).

### Fruits

**Fresh fruits:**
- Spoilage: Browning, softening, fermentation.
- Prevention: Cool storage; use within days.
- Shelf-life: Days to weeks depending on type.

**Preserved fruits (dried, jam):**
- Spoilage: Mold (if not sealed); crystallization (loss of texture, not spoilage).
- Prevention: Seal containers; keep dry.
- Shelf-life: Months to years.

### Oils

**Butter, lard, oils:**
- Spoilage: Rancidity (oxidation).
- Prevention: Keep cool, dark, sealed.
- Shelf-life: Weeks to months (depends on type and storage).
- Signs: Rancid smell, bitter taste.

### Water

**Fresh water storage:**
- Spoilage: Bacterial growth, algae, sediment.
- Prevention: Boil or use preserved water; keep sealed; keep cool.
- Shelf-life: Days if unpreserved; indefinite if boiled and sealed.
- Signs: Cloudiness, smell, discoloration.

## Part 5: Foodborne Illness Prevention

### Dangerous pathogens

**Botulism (Clostridium botulinum):**
- Anaerobic bacterium; produces lethal toxin.
- No smell, taste, or visible sign.
- Happens in improperly canned foods (low acid, unsealed).
- Prevention: Proper canning technique; discard any swollen cans.

**Salmonella:**
- Raw poultry, eggs; causes diarrhea, fever.
- Prevention: Cook to safe temperature; clean equipment.

**E. coli:**
- Raw beef, contaminated vegetables; causes severe diarrhea, kidney damage.
- Prevention: Cook meat thoroughly; wash vegetables.

**Listeria:**
- Cold-loving bacteria; grows in refrigerated food.
- Causes miscarriage in pregnant women; severe illness in elderly.
- Prevention: Don't store cooked food more than 3-4 days; don't rely on refrigeration for long-term safety.

### Prevention strategies

**Cleanliness:** Wash hands, utensils, surfaces (removes bacteria before multiplication).

**Temperature control:** Refrigerate (slows bacteria); cook (kills bacteria).

**Time limit:** Don't store suspect food more than 2-3 days unrefrigerated.

**Acid:** Fermented foods (pH &lt;4.6) inhibit many pathogens.

**Salt:** High salt (20%+) prevents bacterial growth.

**Heat:** Cooking &gt;65°C kills most pathogens; &gt;75°C kills nearly all.

## Part 6: Seasonal Spoilage Patterns

### Spring/Summer (warm; high spoilage risk)

**Temperature:** 20-30°C favors bacterial growth.

**Foods at risk:**
- Fresh vegetables (days of shelf-life).
- Meat/fish (hours to days without refrigeration).
- Dairy (hours without cold).
- Bread (mold in 2-3 days).

**Prevention strategy:**
- Ferment quickly (sauerkraut, kimchi).
- Salt-cure/smoke immediately (meat).
- Dry (vegetables, fruits).
- Consume fresh food daily (less reliance on storage).

### Fall/Winter (cool; low spoilage risk)

**Temperature:** 5-15°C slows bacterial growth significantly.

**Foods more stable:**
- Fresh vegetables in cool storage (weeks).
- Meat on cool ground or in cool cellar (weeks).
- Dairy in cool storage (weeks).
- Bread (weeks without mold).

**Prevention strategy:**
- Root cellaring (cool storage of vegetables, grains).
- Hanging meat in cool location (curing happens naturally).
- Minimal processing; rely on temperature.

## Part 7: Practical Testing Methods

### Float test (for botulism in canned goods)

**Why:** Clostridium botulinum produces gas; swollen cans indicate danger.

**Method:**
1. Look at can: Swollen/bulging = dangerous.
2. If bulging, do not open; discard safely (bury or burn).
3. Flat cans are acceptable.

**Limitation:** Early stage spoilage may not show swelling; trust smell when opening.

### Smell test (for most spoilage)

**Safe for:**
- Fermented foods (sour smell is normal).
- Preserved meat (salt/smoke smell is normal).
- Vegetables, grains, dried foods.

**Unsafe for:**
- Canned foods (toxins cannot be smelled).
- Raw meat/fish stored &gt;2 days (smell may not indicate early toxin production).

**Method:** Open or sniff food; if off-smell, discard immediately.

### Taste test (if absolutely necessary)

**Safe foods to taste (spit out if off):**
- Fermented vegetables (sour is normal; taste continues).
- Preserved meat (salty is normal; taste and check).

**Never taste:**
- Raw meat/fish (risk of pathogenic bacteria).
- Canned goods (toxin risk).
- Any food with visible mold (toxins inside).

## Part 8: Post-Collapse Food Safety Strategy

### Storage method determines safety

**Fermented (acid):** Safe indefinitely if stored cool; low illness risk.

**Cured/smoked (salt/smoke):** Safe for months if stored cool; low illness risk.

**Dried/dried:** Safe indefinitely if kept dry; low illness risk (spoilage is obvious before danger).

**Fresh/refrigerated:** Safe only days to weeks; high illness risk if stored improperly.

**Post-collapse:** Prioritize preservation methods (fermentation, curing, drying) over fresh storage.

### Decision tree for spoiled food

**Mold on surface (bread, vegetables, soft foods):**
- Discard entirely (mold roots throughout; invisible contamination).

**Mold on hard foods (cheese, cured meat, vegetables):**
- Cut away affected area (surrounding is safe).
- If &gt;25% affected or deep penetration, discard.

**Off-smell or off-taste:**
- Discard immediately.
- Trust your nose; if suspicious, do not risk illness.

**Slime, gas bubbles, discoloration:**
- Discard.

**Questionable appearance but no smell/taste issues:**
- If high-preservation method (fermented, salted, smoked) and stored properly, likely safe.
- If fresh/minimally preserved, discard if any doubt.

### Household food management practices

**FIFO (First In, First Out):**
- Label food with storage date.
- Use oldest food first.
- Prevents old food being lost in back of storage.
- Post-collapse: Critical for managing preservation without spoilage.

**Food rotation in cellar:**
- Organize by preservation method (fermented in one area; dried in another; fresh in cool area).
- Monitor regularly (monthly inspection for mold, pests, deterioration).
- Use root vegetables first (less shelf-stable); preserve fermented/salted foods longer.

**Pest prevention:**
- Seal all containers (prevents insect access and cross-contamination).
- Use quicklime or food-grade diatomaceous earth (controls insects without toxins).
- Keep cellar/storage clean (removes debris where pests hide).

**Family education:**
- Teach all settlement members safe practices (cooking temperature, handwashing, smell tests).
- Prevention through cleanliness is more effective than treatment after illness.
- Document what works (fermentation outcomes, storage conditions) for next year.

## Verification

Your spoilage knowledge is adequate if:
1. **Prevention:** You understand storage conditions (temperature, moisture, oxygen) that prevent spoilage.
2. **Detection:** You can identify signs of spoilage (mold, smell, slime, gas).
3. **Risk assessment:** You know when food is safe to eat vs. when to discard.
4. **Safety:** You follow practices that prevent foodborne illness (cooking temperature, cleanliness, time limits).
5. **Preservation:** You use methods (fermentation, curing, drying) that provide long-term food safety.

## Common Mistakes

**Ignoring smell.** If food smells wrong, do not eat it. Trust your nose.

**Trusting appearance alone.** Food can look good and be dangerous (bacterial toxins); food can look bad and be safe (fermentation).

**Leaving cooked food at room temperature too long.** Bacteria multiply rapidly; 2 hours maximum unrefrigerated.

**Not cooking meat to safe temperature.** Low temperatures allow pathogens to survive. Cook thoroughly.

**Relying on refrigeration for long-term storage.** Refrigeration slows spoilage but doesn't prevent it indefinitely. Use preservation methods.

**Eating from swollen cans.** Swelling indicates gas production from bacteria; discard immediately (botulism risk).
