## Overview

Lacto-fermentation is the oldest food preservation method. Salt, cabbage, and time produce sauerkraut that lasts months and tastes good.

Fermentation:
- Preserves vegetables for months without refrigeration.
- Preserves vitamin C (unlike cooking or drying).
- Creates beneficial bacteria (probiotics).
- Requires minimal equipment (jar, salt, vegetables).
- Requires minimal skill (hard to fail if you follow the rules).

This chapter teaches fermentation science and practical technique.

## Prerequisites

Understand nutritional needs (Vol 12, Ch 1). Know sanitation (Vol 5, Ch 6).

## Part 1: Fermentation Science

### What Is Lacto-Fermentation?

**Lacto = lactic acid (not dairy milk).**

Fermentation is:
1. Salt is added to vegetables.
2. Vegetables release liquid (osmotic pressure).
3. Salt + liquid create brine (salty environment).
4. Bacteria (Lactobacillus, naturally on vegetables) multiply in brine.
5. Lactobacillus produce lactic acid.
6. Lactic acid preserves vegetables (acidic environment kills harmful bacteria; preserves nutrients).

**Not mold, not yeast (those are different fermentation processes).**

Lacto-fermentation is specifically bacterial fermentation producing lactic acid.

### The Microbes: Lactobacillus

**Lactobacillus:** Naturally occurring bacteria on vegetables (especially cabbage).

**What it does:**
- Consumes sugars in vegetables.
- Produces lactic acid (sour taste).
- Produces vitamins (especially B vitamins and K2).
- Produces enzymes that aid digestion.

**It dies if:**
- Salt is too high (&gt;10% brine).
- Temperature is too hot (&gt;25°C; bacteria slow down; mold grows).
- Temperature is too cold (&lt;10°C; bacteria barely multiply; very slow fermentation).
- Oxygen is present (Lactobacillus is anaerobic; needs oxygen-free environment).

**Growth rate:**
- Optimal: 15-20°C, 2-5% salt brine, anaerobic (submerged in brine).
- Timeline: 3-7 days for mild fermentation; 2-4 weeks for full fermentation.

### Chemistry of Preservation

**Why fermentation preserves:**

1. **Lactic acid:** Creates acidic environment (pH &lt;4.6). Harmful bacteria (Salmonella, Botulism spores) cannot survive below pH 4.6. Fermented vegetables are safely acidic.

2. **Salt:** Creates osmotic pressure (water leaves cells). Microbes cannot grow in high-salt environment. Creates anaerobic brine (excludes oxygen-dependent pathogens).

3. **Anaerobic environment:** Lactobacillus produce acid in oxygen-free environment. Harmful aerobic bacteria cannot survive. Mold cannot grow (requires oxygen).

**Result:** Vegetables stay fresh for months at room temperature (no refrigeration needed).

### Comparison to Other Preservation Methods

| Method | Preserves Vitamin C | Preparation | Storage |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fermentation | Yes | Easy | Months (room temp) |
| Canning | No | Hot processing | Years (shelf stable) |
| Drying | Partial | Labor intensive | Months (dry storage) |
| Root cellar | Yes | Minimal | 3-6 months (cool, dark) |

**Post-collapse preference:** Fermentation (no heat, no special equipment, preserves nutrition, creates probiotics).

## Part 2: Practical Fermentation

### Ingredients

**Vegetables:**
- Cabbage (classic; easiest; best results).
- Other vegetables: Carrots, beets, radishes, turnips, beans, peppers.
- Leafy greens: Kale, chard (shorter fermentation; less stable).
- Root crops: Turnips, daikon (excellent; slow fermentation).

**Salt:**
- 2-5% by weight of vegetables.
- Sea salt or mineral salt (iodized salt interferes; avoid).
- Non-iodized salt only.

**Water (optional):**
- If vegetables do not release enough liquid, add non-chlorinated water (chlorine kills Lactobacillus).
- Boiled and cooled water is safest.

**Additions (optional):**
- Spices: Caraway seeds, juniper, dill (flavor; some have antimicrobial properties).
- Garlic: Antimicrobial (slows fermentation slightly; adds flavor).
- Chili pepper: Antimicrobial.
- Turmeric: Anti-inflammatory.

### Equipment

**Essential:**
- Glass jar (clay or glass; not metal or plastic).
- Salt (non-iodized).
- Vegetables.
- Cloth or paper (to cover jar; allows gas escape, prevents dust/flies).

**Helpful:**
- Scale (to measure salt accurately).
- Knife (to chop vegetables).
- Weight (glass weight, plate, or rock to keep vegetables submerged).

**Post-collapse:** Minimal equipment needed; rock or wooden weight works fine.

### Step-by-Step Fermentation

**1. Prepare vegetables:**
- Clean (rinse with water; do not scrub off natural bacteria).
- Chop (smaller pieces ferment faster; larger pieces ferment slower).
- Save outer leaves of cabbage (for packing on top).

**2. Calculate salt:**
- Weigh vegetables.
- Multiply by 2-5% (e.g., 1000g cabbage × 3% = 30g salt).
- Use 3% as starting point; adjust later if too salty or too bland.

**3. Mix vegetables and salt:**
- Place vegetables in bowl.
- Add salt.
- Massage vegetables for 5-10 minutes (releases liquid).
- Vegetables will release juices and become limp.

**4. Pack jar:**
- Transfer vegetables and liquid to jar.
- Press down firmly (pack tightly).
- Vegetables should be submerged in brine.
- Leave 2-3 cm headspace (fermentation produces gas).

**5. Keep submerged:**
- Use glass weight, plate, or rock to hold vegetables below brine.
- Reason: Vegetables exposed to air grow mold.
- Submerged vegetables ferment safely.

**6. Cover jar:**
- Cover with cloth or paper (allows CO2 to escape, prevents dust/flies).
- Do not seal tightly (gas needs to escape; tight seal may explode).
- Cloth secured with rubber band works well.

**7. Ferment at room temperature:**
- 15-20°C ideal (cool room, not cold, not hot).
- Room temperature (18-22°C) acceptable.
- Avoid direct sunlight.
- Avoid heat source (stove, radiator).

**8. Monitor fermentation:**
- Day 1-2: Vegetables release more liquid; brine forms.
- Day 3-5: Bubbles form (gas production; Lactobacillus multiplying).
- Day 7-14: Taste test (increasing sourness; fermentation accelerating).
- Day 14-21: Full fermentation (strong sour taste; stable preservation).

**9. Taste test:**
- Mild sourness: 3-7 days fermentation.
- Medium sourness: 1-2 weeks fermentation.
- Strong sourness: 3-4 weeks fermentation (more stable; lasts longer).

**10. Store:**
- Once fermented (taste is acceptable): Transfer to cool storage (root cellar, basement, cool room).
- If jar smells off (rotten, not sour): Discard (fermentation failed; mold or bad bacteria).
- If mold appears on top (white/fuzzy/colored): Skim off mold; fermentation underneath is usually safe (lactic acid prevents spread).

### Salt Levels

**2% brine:**
- Mild (less salty taste).
- Faster fermentation (3-7 days).
- Less stable (use within 1-2 months).
- Risk: Too little salt allows bad bacteria.

**3% brine (recommended):**
- Balanced (salty but not too salty).
- Moderate fermentation (1-2 weeks).
- Stable (lasts 2-4 months).
- Good starting point.

**5% brine:**
- Very salty taste.
- Slower fermentation (2-4 weeks).
- Very stable (lasts 4-6+ months).
- Risk: Too salty may kill Lactobacillus (requires extended time).

**Rule:** 3% is safe starting point; adjust to taste preference.

### Temperature Control

**15-18°C (cool room):**
- Slow fermentation (2-4 weeks for full fermentation).
- Very stable (longer shelf life).
- Preferred for long-term storage.

**18-22°C (room temperature):**
- Moderate fermentation (1-2 weeks).
- Acceptable for post-collapse.

**22-25°C (warm room):**
- Fast fermentation (3-7 days).
- Less stable (shorter shelf life; 1-2 months).
- Risk: Mold grows if warm and humid.

**&lt;15°C or &gt;25°C:**
- Too cold: Fermentation very slow; bacteria barely multiply; mold grows.
- Too hot: Fermentation very fast; risk of spoilage; mold risk.

**Rule:** Cooler is better for stability and shelf life.

## Part 3: Troubleshooting

### Mold on surface

**Cause:** Vegetables exposed to air.

**Prevention:** Keep vegetables submerged in brine.

**Treatment:** If white mold (likely safe; Lactobacillus mold), skim off; rest is fine. If colored mold (pink, green, black): Discard batch (dangerous mold).

### Slimy texture

**Cause:** Over-fermentation or contamination.

**Prevention:** Do not ferment too long (taste after 2-3 weeks).

**Treatment:** If freshly slimy (just appeared), may be salvageable (lactic acid bacteria); taste before using. If very slimy: Discard.

### Weak fermentation (no sourness)

**Cause:** Not enough salt, too cold, contamination.

**Prevention:** Use 3% salt; ferment at 15-20°C.

**Treatment:** Continue fermenting (extend fermentation another week or two). If after 4 weeks still not sour: Discard (bad batch).

### Fizzy/explosive jar

**Cause:** Jar was sealed tightly; gas pressure built up.

**Prevention:** Use cloth cover (allows gas escape); do not cap jar.

**Treatment:** Carefully open jar (gas escapes); re-cover with cloth; continue fermenting.

### Mushy vegetables

**Cause:** Over-fermentation, high temperature, or mold.

**Prevention:** Ferment at cool temperature (15-20°C); taste after 2 weeks.

**Treatment:** Use before mushiness advances (soft vegetables are still safe if no bad smell).

### Off-flavor or unpleasant smell

**Cause:** Contamination, wrong salt level, or fermentation gone bad.

**Prevention:** Use clean equipment, 3% salt, anaerobic conditions.

**Treatment:** If smell is sour (normal), taste may be acceptable. If smell is rotten/foul: Discard.

## Part 4: Scaling Fermentation for Settlements

### Small household (5-10 people)

**Weekly fermentation:**
- One 1-liter jar per week.
- Fermented for 2 weeks before use.
- Always 1-2 jars fermenting and ready.
- Provides 100-200g per person per week.

**Vegetables needed per year:**
- 50 kg cabbage/vegetables (+ salt: 1.5 kg at 3%).
- One-time harvest and ferment in fall (using stored vegetables).

### Medium settlement (20-50 people)

**Weekly fermentation:**
- One 5-10 liter crock or multiple jars per week.
- Staggered fermenting (one batch ready each week).
- Provides 100g per person per week.

**Infrastructure:**
- Cool storage room (cellar or shed; 10-15°C year-round).
- Multiple crocks or large jars.
- Simple pressure system (rock weights).

**Vegetables needed per year:**
- 200-400 kg vegetables + 6-12 kg salt.

### Large settlement (50-100 people)

**Weekly fermentation:**
- Multiple 10-20 liter crocks (one per week).
- Continuous fermentation schedule.

**Infrastructure:**
- Dedicated fermentation room (cool, dark, ventilated).
- Large ceramic or wooden crocks (easier to manage than many jars).
- Wooden weights and covers.

**Vegetables needed per year:**
- 500-1000 kg vegetables + 15-30 kg salt.

**Assignment:** One person 1-2 hours per week manages fermentation (mixing vegetables, checking progress, transferring to storage).

### Scaling math

**For N people:**
- Annual vegetables needed: (N × 5-10 kg)
- Annual salt needed: (N × 150-300 g)
- Weekly fermentation: (N × 100-200 g) ÷ 14 days = (N × 7-14 g) per day
- Jars/crocks needed: (N × 0.2-0.5 liters) × 3 staggered batches = (N × 0.6-1.5 liters) total crock capacity

**Example: 50-person settlement**
- Annual vegetables: 250-500 kg (doable from dedicated garden plot)
- Annual salt: 7.5-15 kg (from salt production or storage)
- Weekly fermentation: 350-700 g daily
- Crock capacity needed: 30-75 liters (three 10-25 liter crocks, staggered)

## Part 5: Advanced Fermentation Techniques

### Mixed vegetable fermentation

**Beyond cabbage:**
- Carrot + cabbage (sweeter, faster fermentation)
- Turnip + cabbage (earthier flavor, good for winter)
- Radish + cabbage (peppery, aids digestion)
- Beet + cabbage (colors pink; mild earthiness)
- Garlic + herb fermentation (flavor-forward; good starter for beginners)

**Ratio:** 3:1 or 4:1 cabbage to other vegetables (cabbage has best fermentation starter culture).

### Multi-stage fermentation

**For maximum nutrient development:**
1. Primary fermentation (2 weeks at room temperature): Lactobacillus rapidly multiply; lactic acid develops.
2. Secondary fermentation (2-4 weeks in cool storage): Slow fermentation continues; flavor deepens; stability increases.

**Advantage:** Greater nutrient development (more B vitamins, K2); longer shelf life; better flavor.

**Timeline:** 4-6 weeks total (longer than quick fermentation but stable for months).

### Whey starter (if dairy available)

**Optional (not essential; normal fermentation works without it):**
- Add 2-3 tablespoons whey (liquid from cheese-making) to brine.
- Provides additional Lactobacillus starter culture.
- Fermentation may be faster (3-5 days instead of 1-2 weeks).
- Not necessary post-collapse (natural Lactobacillus on vegetables is sufficient).

### Fermentation with minimal salt (lower-sodium)

**Technique:**
- Use 1-2% salt (very low).
- Ferment in very cool place (10-15°C).
- Ferment longer (3-4 weeks minimum).
- Watch closely for contamination (low salt means less preservation).

**Risk:** More prone to mold or spoilage. Use only if salt is scarce and you can monitor closely.

## Part 6: Using Fermented Vegetables

### Storage and shelf life

**Cool storage (15°C or below):**
- 3% salt fermentation: 2-4 months.
- 5% salt fermentation: 4-6 months.
- Can extend by keeping in cool place (root cellar, basement).

**Once opened:**
- Keep submerged in brine (prevents mold).
- Use clean spoon (prevents contamination).
- Will keep for weeks after opening.

### Serving

**As side dish:**
- Small portions (1/4 cup per meal) provides probiotics and vitamin C.
- Serve with warm food (hot food kills probiotics; serve alongside rather than mixed in).

**As base for soup:**
- Use fermented vegetable broth in soups (adds nutrients and flavor).

**In cooking:**
- Add to stews and braised dishes.
- Add to grain dishes (rice and sauerkraut).

### Nutritional benefits

- Vitamin C preserved (not destroyed by heat).
- Probiotics from Lactobacillus (aids digestion, immune function).
- Enhanced nutrient absorption (lactic acid aids mineral absorption).
- B vitamins produced during fermentation.

## Verification

Your fermentation skill is adequate if:
1. **Equipment:** You have jars, salt (non-iodized), and vegetables.
2. **Technique:** You can prepare, pack, and submerge vegetables properly.
3. **Monitoring:** You watch fermentation progress and taste-test at appropriate times.
4. **Troubleshooting:** You know how to identify and address common problems.
5. **Storage:** You store fermented vegetables in cool place; use within 2-4 months.
6. **Scale:** You can ferment enough for household consumption (one batch per week for family of 5; one batch per day for settlement of 100).

## Common Mistakes

**Using iodized salt.** Iodine kills Lactobacillus. Use only non-iodized salt.

**Sealing jar tightly.** Gas accumulates; pressure builds; jar explodes. Use cloth cover.

**Exposing vegetables to air.** Vegetables exposed to air grow mold. Keep submerged.

**Over-heating.** Hot temperature kills Lactobacillus, favors mold. Ferment at 15-20°C.

**Too much salt.** Above 5% salt kills Lactobacillus. Use 2-5% salt only.

**Not enough salt.** Below 2% allows harmful bacteria. Use minimum 2% salt.

**Ferment too long.** Beyond 4 weeks, over-fermentation causes mushiness. Ferment 1-4 weeks.

**Using chlorinated water.** Chlorine kills Lactobacillus. Boil and cool water if adding any.
