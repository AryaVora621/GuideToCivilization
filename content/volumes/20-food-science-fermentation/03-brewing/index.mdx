## Overview

Brewing produces alcohol from grains and honey. Post-collapse, fermented beverages serve multiple purposes:
- Safe liquid (water contamination was historically deadly; fermented beverages were safe).
- Preserved calories (grain converted to beer lasts months; grain alone spoils).
- Calories for festivals and morale (alcohol in small amounts lifts spirits).
- Medicinal uses (tinctures, pain relief).

This chapter teaches beer and mead brewing at household/settlement scale.

## Prerequisites

Understand fermentation (Vol 12, Ch 2). Know crop production (Vol 10).

## Part 1: Beer Brewing Fundamentals

### What Is Beer?

Beer is grain fermented to alcohol:
1. Grain (barley, wheat, rye) is soaked and sprouted (malting).
2. Grain is heated to halt sprouting (creates fermentable sugars).
3. Malt is steeped in hot water (sugars dissolve; creates wort).
4. Wort is boiled with hops (bitter herb; prevents spoilage; adds flavor).
5. Liquid is cooled and fermented with yeast (alcohol produced).
6. Final product: Beer (3-8% alcohol; lasts months).

### Beer ingredients

**Grains (provide sugar for fermentation):**
- Barley (best; traditional; well-suited to fermentation).
- Wheat (acceptable; lighter beer).
- Rye (acceptable; earthier flavor).
- Oats (acceptable; creamy texture).
- Corn (acceptable but requires careful handling; can produce off-flavors).

**Post-collapse:** Barley is ideal (grows in cool climates; stores well; ferments predictably).

**Hops (provide bitterness and preservation):**
- Bittering hops (boiled; bitter compounds preserve beer; prevent spoilage).
- Aromatic hops (late addition; provide flavor/aroma).

**Post-collapse:** Hops can be grown. Dried hops store for years. Without hops, other herbs can substitute (see Part 3).

**Yeast (ferments sugar to alcohol):**
- Ale yeast (warm fermentation; 15-20°C; faster; most common).
- Lager yeast (cold fermentation; 10-15°C; slower; requires cellar).

**Post-collapse:** Capture wild yeast from air (slower, less predictable) or cultivate baker's yeast if available.

**Water:**
- Clean water (boiled if contaminated).
- Different water minerals affect flavor (post-collapse: use available water).

## Part 2: Practical Beer Brewing

### Home-Scale Recipe (20 liters)

**Ingredients:**
- 4 kg malted barley (or grain).
- 30 g dried hops (or herbal bittering agent).
- 20 liters clean water.
- Yeast (wild yeast from environment or cultivated yeast if available).
- Optional: Honey (adds body and alcohol).

**Equipment:**
- Large pot (20+ liter capacity).
- Thermometer.
- Stirrer.
- Strainer/cheesecloth.
- Glass carboy or large jar (20+ liter).
- Airlock or cloth cover.
- Bottles (glass or ceramic for storage).

**Timeline:**
- Day 1: Mashing (grain + hot water extraction).
- Day 1: Boiling (1-2 hours; add hops).
- Day 1: Cooling and yeast pitch.
- Days 2-14: Primary fermentation.
- Days 14-30: Secondary fermentation.
- Day 30+: Bottling and storage.

### Detailed Steps

**1. Mashing (sugar extraction):**
- Crush malted grain (coarse; not powder).
- Heat 15 liters water to 65-68°C.
- Add grain to hot water (grain-to-water ratio 1:3 to 1:4).
- Stir well; let steep 1-2 hours at 65°C (hold temperature constant if possible).
- Temperature drops over time; stay between 60-70°C.

**2. Sparging (rinsing grain):**
- Strain liquid through cheesecloth (collect liquid; this is wort).
- Rinse grain with warm water (65°C) to extract remaining sugars.
- Collect all liquid; should have ~15-18 liters wort.

**3. Boiling:**
- Heat wort to rolling boil.
- Boil for 60-90 minutes.
- Add bittering hops at start of boil (they bitter the liquid).
- Add aromatic hops in last 15 minutes (for flavor/aroma).
- Boil time extracts bitter compounds from hops; long boil = more bitter.

**4. Cooling:**
- Cool wort to room temperature (18-20°C).
- Can chill by placing pot in cold water bath.
- Post-collapse: Use evening cool or well water.

**5. Yeast pitch:**
- Once cool, transfer wort to fermentation vessel (carboy or jar).
- Add yeast (wild yeast from environment or cultivated yeast).
- Wild yeast: Leave uncovered for 1-2 days; yeast from air will inoculate.
- Cultivated yeast: Add directly per instructions.

**6. Fermentation:**
- Cover with cloth (allows CO2 to escape; prevents contamination).
- Keep at 15-20°C for 7-14 days (ale fermentation).
- Fermentation active: Bubbles, foam on top.
- Fermentation slows: Bubbles diminish; beer clears.

**7. Secondary fermentation (optional but recommended):**
- After 7-14 days, transfer to secondary vessel (leave sediment behind).
- Ferment another 2-4 weeks in cool place.
- Allows more clarification; improves stability.

**8. Bottling:**
- Once fermentation complete (specific gravity stable for 3+ days), bottle.
- Add 5-10 g priming sugar per liter (provides carbonation).
- Bottle in glass or ceramic (not metal; carbonation creates pressure).
- Cap or cork bottles.

**9. Conditioning:**
- Let bottles sit for 1-2 weeks at room temperature (yeast carbonates beer).
- Observe: Bottles should feel firm (carbonation building pressure).
- After 1-2 weeks, move to cool storage.

**10. Storage:**
- Store in cool place (10-15°C if possible; stable longer).
- Protects from light (keeps quality longer).
- Shelf life: 3-6 months (unrefrigerated); longer in cool cellar.

### Troubleshooting Beer Brewing

**No fermentation (no bubbles after 3 days):**
- Cause: Dead yeast, too cold, or contamination.
- Prevention: Use fresh yeast; ferment at 15-20°C; clean equipment.
- Fix: Add fresh yeast; warm to 18°C; hope for recovery.

**Off-flavor (vinegar, sour, musty smell):**
- Cause: Bacterial contamination (acetic acid bacteria, Lactobacillus).
- Prevention: Clean equipment with boiling water; minimize air exposure.
- Fix: If mild, may drink despite flavor. If severe: Discard.

**Stuck fermentation (stops too early; too sweet):**
- Cause: Cold temperature, dead yeast.
- Prevention: Maintain 15-20°C; use healthy yeast.
- Fix: Warm to 18°C; add fresh yeast; may restart.

**Overcarbonation (bottles fizzy, overcarbonated, may explode):**
- Cause: Too much priming sugar.
- Prevention: Use 5-10 g priming sugar per liter (no more).
- Fix: Open bottles carefully; release pressure; serve flat.

## Part 3: Herbal Alternatives (If Hops Unavailable)

### Bittering agents (instead of hops)

**Traditional gruit (herbal beer):**
- Gruit was used before hops became standard.
- Mixture of herbs providing bitter flavor and preservation.

**Gruit recipes:**
- Juniper berries + mugwort: Medieval combination.
- Yarrow + mugwort + marjoram: Historic gruit mix.
- Bog myrtle + heather: Northern European combination.

**Proportions:**
- Use 15-30 g dried herbs per 20 liter batch.
- Boil in wort for 60 minutes (similar to hops).
- Results: Herbaceous, less bitter than hops; flavor varies.

**Post-collapse:** If hops are unavailable, growing gruit herbs (juniper, yarrow, mugwort) is feasible.

## Part 4: Mead Brewing

### What Is Mead?

Mead is honey fermented to alcohol. Historical honey wine.

**Advantages:**
- Honey is storable (years; no spoilage).
- Ferments reliably (high sugar; low risk of contamination).
- Alcohol content 10-15% (higher than beer; preserves longer).
- Simple ingredients (honey, water, yeast).

**Timeline:** 3-12 months (longer than beer; slower fermentation; extended aging improves flavor).

### Mead Recipe (20 liters)

**Ingredients:**
- 6 kg honey.
- 18 liters clean water.
- Yeast (wild or cultivated).
- Optional: Spices (cloves, cinnamon), citrus, herbs.

**Equipment:**
- Large pot (20+ liters).
- Thermometer.
- Glass carboy (20+ liters).
- Airlock or cloth cover.
- Bottles (glass, ceramic).

### Mead Steps

**1. Combine honey and water:**
- Heat water to 65°C (do not boil; kills yeast nutrients).
- Dissolve honey in hot water.
- Stir until uniform.
- Cool to 20-25°C.

**2. Pitch yeast:**
- Add yeast (wild from air or cultivated).
- Stir to aerate (yeast needs oxygen at start).

**3. Ferment (primary):**
- Cover with cloth (allows CO2 escape).
- Ferment at 15-20°C for 4-6 weeks.
- Fermentation slower than beer (high sugar content).
- Foam subsides after 2-3 weeks.

**4. Transfer (secondary):**
- After 4-6 weeks, transfer to secondary vessel (leaves sediment).
- Secondary fermentation: 2-6 months (mead improves with age).
- Can add spices/citrus in secondary if desired.

**5. Bottling:**
- Once clear and fermentation complete: Bottle.
- Mead may continue slow fermentation in bottle (carbonation develops slowly).
- Some prefer flat mead (do not add priming sugar).

**6. Aging:**
- Mead improves with age.
- 3-12 month aging period (longer = better flavor/smoothness).
- Shelf life: 1-2 years (higher alcohol preserves longer).

### Mead variations

**Cyser (mead + apple juice):**
- 50% honey + 50% apple juice or apple cider.
- Ferments faster than mead alone.
- Lighter flavor.

**Pyment (mead + grape juice):**
- 50% honey + 50% grape juice or wine.
- Grape yeast aids fermentation.
- More complex flavor.

**Metheglin (spiced mead):**
- Honey + spices (cloves, cinnamon, nutmeg).
- Add spices during secondary fermentation.
- Traditional medicinal drink.

## Part 5: Scaling Brewing for Settlements

### Household-scale brewing (5-10 people)

**Monthly production:**
- One 20-liter batch per month.
- ~600ml per person per month (modest consumption).
- Fermentation space: One carboy/vessel.
- Storage: 3-5 bottles per month (1-2 liters total).

**Labor:** 2-3 hours per batch (one person can manage easily).

**Grain/honey needs per year:**
- Beer: 48 kg malted barley + 360 g hops.
- Mead: 72 kg honey.

### Settlement-scale brewing (50-100 people)

**Weekly production:**
- One 100-200 liter batch per week.
- ~600ml per person per month (modest consumption for full settlement).
- Could increase to higher consumption if cellar space allows.

**Infrastructure:**
- Large brewing pot (50-100 liters).
- Multiple fermentation vessels (carboys or ceramic crocks).
- Cool storage cellar (10-15°C for stability).
- Bottling area (simple table/bench).

**Labor:** 4-6 hours per batch (one dedicated person per week).

**Grain/honey needs per year:**
- Beer: 240-480 kg malted barley + 1.8-3.6 kg hops per year.
- Mead: 360-720 kg honey per year (if pursuing mead at scale).

**Allocation of harvest:**
- For beer: Dedicate 1-2 hectares to barley production.
- Hops: 0.1-0.2 hectares (high density; 8-10 kg dried hops per hectare).
- For mead: Maintain 5-10 hives (120-150 kg honey per hive per year; 600-1500 kg total for mead).

### Settlement economics

**Production cost (per liter of beer):**
- Grain: ~0.2 kg barley = self-grown (minimal cost).
- Hops: ~1.5 g = self-grown (minimal cost).
- Water: Free.
- Yeast: One packet ferments 20 liters; cost negligible.
- **Total: Essentially free (uses farm surplus).**

**Shelf life:** 3-6 months unrefrigerated (longer in cool cellar).

**Comparison to grain storage:**
- 1 kg grain = 1800 kcal (stored as food).
- Same 1 kg grain fermented = 20 liters beer = 1000 kcal (alcohol) + 800 kcal (residual carbs) + social/morale benefit.
- Loss of ~20% calories in fermentation is acceptable for improved stability and social benefit.

## Part 6: Water Safety and Brewing

### Historical context

Pre-pasteurization, water contamination (cholera, dysentery, typhoid) killed millions. Fermented beverages (beer, cider, mead) were safer (alcohol kills pathogens).

**Boiling:** Killing pathogens in water was standard.
**Fermentation:** Acidic environment (alcohol + hops) preserves; kills pathogens.

**Post-collapse implication:** Fermented beverages serve dual purpose (nutrition + safe liquid storage).

### Safe water practices in brewing

**Boiling:** All water should be boiled before use (kills pathogens).
**Cooling:** Cool boiled water before fermenting (heat kills yeast).
**Sanitation:** Equipment must be clean (sterilize with boiling water).

## Verification

Your brewing skill is adequate if:
1. **Ingredients:** You can source or grow malted grain, hops (or hops alternatives).
2. **Equipment:** You have brewing pot, fermentation vessels, bottles.
3. **Process:** You can mash, boil, ferment beer following the recipe.
4. **Yeast:** You can cultivate wild yeast or source cultivated yeast.
5. **Troubleshooting:** You recognize and address common problems.
6. **Mead:** You can ferment honey into stable, drinkable mead.
7. **Scale:** You can brew for household or small settlement (20+ liters per batch).

## Common Mistakes

**Infected equipment.** Dirty pots/bottles introduce bacteria. Clean with boiling water.

**Contamination during fermentation.** Exposed wort grows mold. Keep covered (but allow CO2 escape).

**Temperature control.** Too cold: Slow fermentation. Too hot: Spoilage risk. Keep at 15-20°C.

**Over-carbonation.** Too much priming sugar causes explosions. Use 5-10 g per liter only.

**No sanitization.** Unsterilized equipment ruins batches. Boil all equipment.

**Bad water.** Contaminated water spoils beer. Boil all water before use.

**Expired yeast.** Old yeast does not ferment reliably. Use fresh yeast.
