## Overview

Meat is perishable (spoils in 1-2 days). Salt-cured and smoked meat lasts months/years. Post-collapse, meat preservation is critical.

This chapter covers:
1. **Salt-curing:** Removing water; inhibiting pathogens.
2. **Smoking:** Adding preservation compounds; improving flavor.
3. **Combined methods:** Salt + smoke for maximum stability.
4. **Scaling:** Community preservation.

## Prerequisites

Know meat production (Vol 10, Ch 8). Understand sanitation (Vol 5, Ch 6).

## Part 1: Salt Curing Fundamentals

### How salt preserves

**Mechanism:** Salt (NaCl) removes water through osmosis.

**Pathogens:**
- Most bacteria need water (aw &gt;0.85).
- Salt-cured meat has water activity &lt;0.75 (too dry for most bacteria).
- Result: Preserved indefinitely at cool temperature.

**Flavor:** Salt dehydrates; concentrates flavors; produces cured taste.

### Salt types, sources, and amounts

**Salt quality:** Non-iodized salt only (iodine interferes with curing; causes off-flavors).

**Salt sources post-collapse:**
- **Sea salt:** Harvested by evaporating seawater (if coastal).
- **Rock salt:** Mined deposits (if available locally).
- **Spring salt:** Evaporated from saline springs (if present).
- Post-collapse: Use whatever salt is available; avoid iodized table salt.

**Salt amounts:**
- 10-20% salt by weight of meat.
  - 10-12% salt: Milder cure; faster spoilage risk if warm; lasts 1-2 months.
  - 15% salt: Standard; good balance; lasts 3-6 months.
  - 20%+ salt: Hard preserve; very salty; lasts 1-2 years or longer.
- Example: 1 kg meat × 15% = 150g salt needed.

**Nitrates and nitrites (historical but post-collapse unavailable):**
- Pre-modern curing used saltpeter (potassium nitrate) or Prague Powder (nitrate blend).
- These give cured meat pink color; prevent botulism; improve flavor.
- Post-collapse: Unavailable (require chemical manufacturing).
- Alternative: Use high salt concentration (20%+) and keep cool; safe without nitrates.

**Types of cures:**
- **Dry cure:** Salt rubbed directly on meat (simple; traditional; works well post-collapse).
- **Wet cure:** Salt dissolved in water; meat submerged (faster; more uniform; requires more salt).

## Part 2: Dry Salt-Curing

### Process

**1. Prepare meat:**
- Clean and trim excess fat (fat goes rancid; remove most).
- Cut into manageable pieces (4-8 cm thickness; smaller cures faster).

**2. Apply salt:**
- Rub salt evenly over meat; covering all surfaces.
- Use 15% salt by weight (150g per kg meat).
- Press salt in; ensure coating.

**3. Pack:**
- Layer meat in ceramic or glass vessel.
- Meat and salt alternate; top layer is salt.
- Pack tightly (reduces air exposure).

**4. Cure time:**
- 1-2 weeks per 2.5 cm thickness (thicker pieces take longer).
- After 3-5 days, liquid appears (salt drawing out water); keep pressing down.
- At end of cure, meat is dried, darkened, and hardened.

**5. Rinse and dry:**
- Rinse off excess salt under cold water.
- Pat dry with cloth.
- Hang to air-dry (2-4 weeks) until surface is dry to touch.

**6. Store:**
- Cool location (10-15°C is ideal; room temperature acceptable).
- Wrapped in cloth (allows breathing; prevents moisture).
- Shelf life: Months to years if kept dry and cool.

### Post-collapse advantages

**Dry cure is simplest:** Requires only salt and patience. No equipment needed.

**Long shelf-life:** Properly cured meat is shelf-stable indefinitely.

**Flavor:** Concentrated, salty taste (use in soups, cooking; eat in small portions).

## Part 2b: Temperature Management During Curing

### Why temperature matters

**Curing speed:** Temperature affects osmosis rate and bacterial activity.

**Optimal curing temperature:**
- 5-10°C (slow curing; excellent flavor development).
- 10-15°C (moderate curing; good balance).
- &gt;15°C (fast curing; risk of spoilage if salt inadequate).

**Danger zone (&gt;20°C):**
- Bacteria multiply faster than salt can kill them.
- Risk of spoilage (sour smell; slime).
- Solution: Use higher salt concentration (20%+) or cure in cooler location.

**Cold curing (below 5°C):**
- Very slow fermentation of sugars (if present); adds complexity.
- Very safe; minimal spoilage risk.
- Post-collapse: Use winter naturally (outdoor/cellar curing).

### Seasonal curing

**Fall/winter (best):**
- Cool temperatures naturally slow curing.
- Risk of spoilage minimal.
- Cured meat ready by spring.

**Spring/summer (challenging):**
- Warm temperatures speed curing but increase spoilage risk.
- Solution: Increase salt to 20%+; cure in coolest available location (deep cellar, spring cave, buried).
- Monitor closely (spoilage can occur in days if warm).

## Part 3: Wet Salt-Curing

### Process (faster than dry)

**1. Prepare brine:**
- Salt + water (20-25% salt solution).
- Example: 250g salt + 1 liter water = 20% brine.
- Dissolve thoroughly.

**2. Submerge meat:**
- Place meat pieces in brine.
- Keep submerged (use weight or plate).
- Cure time: 5-7 days per 2.5 cm thickness (faster than dry cure).

**3. Remove and dry:**
- Remove meat from brine.
- Rinse lightly (remove excess salt if desired).
- Hang to air-dry 2-4 weeks (dries more slowly than dry-cured; smoke helps).

**4. Store:**
- Same as dry-cured meat.

### Advantage

**Faster:** Submerged meat cures in 5-7 days vs. 1-2 weeks for dry cure.

**Disadvantage**

**Surface may mold:** Exposed meat in air oxidizes and may mold. Solution: Smoke promptly after drying.

## Part 4: Smoking

### Why smoke?

**Flavor:** Smoky, complex taste.

**Preservation:** Smoke compounds (phenols, aldehydes) are antimicrobial. Adds years to shelf-life.

**Appearance:** Golden/brown color.

### Cold smoking vs. hot smoking

**Cold smoking (&lt;32°C):**
- Meat not cooked; stays raw.
- Slow process (days to weeks).
- Meat must be cured first (salt-cured then smoked).
- Result: Shelf-stable; cured bacon, jerky.

**Hot smoking (50-80°C):**
- Meat partially cooks; becomes cooked/firm.
- Faster (hours to days).
- Doesn't require pre-curing (smoke adds preservation).
- Result: Cooked, shelf-stable (weeks/months); not as long as cold-smoked.

**Post-collapse:** Cold smoking preferred (more preservation; works with cured meats).

### Smokehouse design (post-collapse)

**Simple structure:**
- Wooden shed or box structure (10-20 sq meters).
- Single door (controls smoke escape; allows access).
- Interior rods/racks for hanging meat.
- Smoke duct at bottom; exit hole at top (creates draft).

**DIY smokehouse:**
- Large wooden box or barrel (for small quantities).
- Buried pit with log structure above (traditional; very effective; cool smoking).
- Canvas or cloth structure over wooden frame (portable; simple).

**Smoke generation:**
- Fire pit or stove below smokehouse (generates smoke).
- Smoldering hardwood chips/logs (not flame; slow burn).
- Moisture (damp sawdust or wet wood) controls temperature (cools smoke).

**Temperature control:**
- Cold-smoking: Smoke cooled before reaching meat (&lt;32°C).
  - Use distance (smoke travels, cools).
  - Use water bath (smoke passes through water; cools and humidifies).
  - Use damp wood (produces cooler smoke).
- Hot-smoking: Warm smoke (50-80°C).
  - Direct fire below meat.
  - Hotter wood burn.

### Simple smoking process

**1. Prepare cured meat:**
- Salt-cured meat must be dry before smoking (pat dry).
- Fresh meat can be smoked without curing (but less preservation).

**2. Hang meat:**
- Suspend from rods in smokehouse (not touching).
- Arrange for air circulation.

**3. Generate smoke:**
- Start fire with hardwood (oak, hickory, apple).
- Smoke should be visible but not thick flame.
- Temperature: Monitor if possible (thermometer); adjust fuel.
- Duration: 
  - Cold-smoke: 2-4 weeks (daily smoke; long-term persistence).
  - Hot-smoke: 2-5 days (continuous).

**4. Monitor progress:**
- Color: Meat darkens from white/pink to golden/brown.
- Texture: Meat surface hardens; becomes dry.
- Smell: Smoky aroma develops.
- Taste: Small taste test after 1 week (smoke flavor increasing).

**5. Finish and store:**
- Remove when desired smokiness reached.
- Cool completely.
- Store in cool, dark, dry place (10-15°C ideal).
- Wrap in cloth or hang; ensures good air circulation.

## Part 5: Meat-Specific Curing Guidelines

### Beef (cattle)

**Cuts for curing:**
- Tough cuts (brisket, chuck) work better than tender cuts (steaks, tenderloin).
- Tougher meat withstands salt/smoke; develops good texture.

**Curing time:**
- Thick pieces (5+ cm): 2-3 weeks (dry cure).
- Thin pieces (3-4 cm): 1-2 weeks.

**Final product:**
- Very firm, concentrated flavor.
- Long shelf-life (months to years if stored cool/dry).
- Use in cooking (soups, stews); small portions (very salty).

### Pork (pig)

**Best cuts:**
- Belly (bacon): Fat and meat together; excellent for salt/smoke.
- Shoulder: Tough; good curing.
- Jowl, cheeks: Flavorful; less tender.

**Curing time:**
- Belly (bacon): 7-10 days (dry cure).
- Shoulder: 2-3 weeks.

**Final product:**
- Bacon: Versatile; good shelf-life (3-6 months).
- Smoked shoulder: Concentrated flavor; very salty.

### Fish (fresh catch)

**Best fish:**
- Oily fish (salmon, mackerel, herring): Fat preserves; develops good flavor.
- Lean fish (cod, pike): Need higher salt; drier texture.

**Curing time:**
- Small fish (whole): 3-5 days.
- Large fish (fillets): 5-10 days depending on thickness.

**Special consideration:**
- Gut immediately; chill before curing (fish spoils faster than meat).
- Salt percentage can be higher (20%+) to ensure preservation.

**Final product:**
- Shelf-stable 2-6 months.
- Use in soups, stews; eat sparingly (very salty).

## Part 6: Long-Term Storage of Cured/Smoked Meat

### Bacon (salt + smoke)

**Process:**
1. Salt-cure pork belly (15% salt; 7-10 days).
2. Rinse and dry.
3. Cold-smoke 2-3 weeks.
4. Result: Shelf-stable bacon; slices and cooks normally.

### Jerky (salt + smoke, dried)

**Process:**
1. Cut meat thin (4-6 mm).
2. Salt lightly (10% salt).
3. Dry in sun or smoke (2-7 days depending on method).
4. Result: Shelf-stable, chewy meat; high protein.

### Fish (salt + smoke)

**Process:**
1. Gut and clean fish.
2. Salt-cure (15-20% salt; 3-5 days depending on thickness).
3. Rinse and dry.
4. Cold-smoke 2-4 weeks.
5. Result: Shelf-stable; used in soups or eaten as-is.

## Part 6: Scaling Preservation

### Household (after hunt/slaughter)

**Meat amount:** 20-50 kg (from one animal).

**Preservation strategy:**
- Eat fresh: 2-5 days.
- Salt-cure: 10-20 kg (lasts months).
- Smoke: 5-10 kg (lasts longer; more labor-intensive).
- Other: Jerky, rendered fat, hide uses.

**Labor:** 2-3 hours curing/smoking setup; then passive process (days/weeks).

### Settlement (multiple slaughters/year)

**Preservation scale:**
- Community smokehouse or salt-curing area.
- Dedicated preservation season (fall slaughter → preserved through winter).
- Rotating batches (different curing stages).

**Labor:** 1-2 people managing preservation (intermittent; seasonal spike).

**Storage:** Cool cellar or root storage (10-15°C; dark; dry).

## Part 7: Troubleshooting

### Mold on cured meat

**Cause:** Humidity too high; insufficient salt; contamination.

**Prevention:** Keep dry; use 15%+ salt; clean equipment.

**Treatment:** Scrape off surface mold (usually harmless). If mold throughout or pink/green color, discard.

### Meat smells off (rotten, not salty)

**Cause:** Spoilage during curing (temperature too warm; inadequate salt).

**Prevention:** Use 15%+ salt; keep cool (&lt;15°C during curing).

**Treatment:** If smell is rotten, discard. If smell is just salty/off, may be acceptable; taste carefully first.

### Curing is too slow (meat not hardening)

**Cause:** Insufficient salt; temperature too warm; pieces too large.

**Prevention:** Use 15-20% salt; cure at cool temperature; cut meat smaller.

**Fix:** Continue curing longer (may take 3-4 weeks); or add more salt and re-cure.

### Smoke flavor too strong

**Cause:** Over-smoking; wrong wood.

**Prevention:** Limit smoking time; use mild hardwoods.

**Treatment:** Smoked meat can be used in cooking (flavor diluted); or eat in small portions.

## Verification

Your preservation capability is adequate if:
1. **Equipment:** You have salt and curing vessel.
2. **Curing:** You can dry-cure meat successfully.
3. **Smoking:** You can build/use simple smokehouse.
4. **Storage:** You have cool, dry storage (10-15°C).
5. **Scaling:** You can preserve 20+ kg meat per season.
6. **Products:** You can make bacon, jerky, or smoked fish.

## Common Mistakes

**Insufficient salt.** Below 15% allows spoilage. Use 15-20% minimum.

**Warm temperature.** &gt;15°C allows bacterial growth. Cure in cool location.

**Exposed to flies/insects.** Cover curing meat; ensure smokehouse is sealed.

**Softwood smoke.** Pine/fir creates off-flavors. Use hardwood only.

**Premature smoking.** Meat not adequately cured before smoking. Ensure meat is hardened first.
