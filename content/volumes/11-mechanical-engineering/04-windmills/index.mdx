## Overview

Wind is everywhere. Where water wheels are impossible (no suitable stream), windmills capture wind energy. A windmill consists of large sails (or blades) that rotate in the wind, driving machinery through gears.

Two main designs exist:
1. **Post mill:** The entire mill structure rotates on a central post to face the wind
2. **Tower mill:** The mill body is fixed; only the roof/cap rotates to aim the sails

Windmills work best in open areas with consistent wind. A good site produces power equivalent to a water wheel.

- Understanding of mechanical advantage (Vol 33, Ch 1)

- Access to an open, windy location

## Materials & Tools

### For Building a Windmill

- **Timber:** Strong wood for frame, sails, and structural support
- **Sail material:** Canvas, wood, or cloth for the rotating sails
- **Shaft:** Iron or steel for the main rotating shaft
- **Bearings:** Brass or bronze for shaft support
- **Gears:** To transmit power from the sail shaft to machinery shaft
- **Machinery:** Grain mill, pump, or other powered equipment

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Windmill Site Assessment

**Step 1: Measure wind speed and consistency.**

Wind power depends on wind speed (and wind speed raised to the third power).

**Wind speed measurement:**
1. Observe flags or smoke: Determine wind speed qualitatively
   - Smoke drifts slowly: 2–3 m/s (wind observable)
   - Leaves move, twigs bend: 5–8 m/s (good for windmills)
   - Branches sway: 10–15 m/s (very good)
   - Trees sway: over 15 m/s (strong, may damage mill)
2. Quantitative measurement (if available): Use an anemometer (wind speed meter)

**Wind consistency:**
1. Observe wind patterns over weeks or months
2. Record wind speed at different times of day
3. Wind is typically stronger in afternoon and weaker at dawn
4. Seasonal variation: Wind may be stronger or weaker in different seasons

**Rule of thumb:** A location with average wind speed of 7 m/s (about 15 knots) is suitable for a windmill.

**Step 2: Identify the best location.**

1. **High ground:** Wind is stronger on hilltops or open areas
2. **Unobstructed:** Avoid locations surrounded by trees or buildings (they block wind)
3. **Distance from obstacles:** The mill should be at least 10 times the height of nearby obstacles
4. **Space for rotation:** The mill (especially a post mill) must be able to rotate freely
5. **Level ground:** For ease of construction and operation

### Part 2: Post Mill Design (Simpler, Rotating Type)

A post mill is easier to build than a tower mill.

**Step 1: Build the main post structure.**

The central post is the pivot around which the entire mill rotates.

**Post design:**
1. Material: A large wooden timber (oak or other hardwood), roughly 0.3–0.5 meters in diameter
2. Height: 3–6 meters above ground (determines the height of the sail)
3. Foundation: Buried deep (1–2 meters) and anchored with stones or concrete to prevent tilting

**Bracing:**
1. Diagonal wooden beams ("stays") support the post against bending
2. Arrange stays at 90-degree angles (four directions)
3. Anchor the stays to the ground with rope and stakes

**Step 2: Build the mill house.**

This is the rotating structure that holds the sails, shaft, and machinery.

**Framework:**
1. A wooden frame (roughly cubic or rectangular) sits on top of the central post
2. The frame pivots on the post, allowing the entire structure to rotate
3. The interior holds the main shaft, gears, and grain mill or other machinery

**Pivot mechanism:**
1. The central post is connected to the rotating frame via a large wooden or metal pin
2. The rotating frame can spin freely around this pin
3. Typically, the bottom of the frame has a bearing (large brass ring) around the post

**Sail mounting:**
1. Two or four large sails (wooden frames covered with canvas) are mounted on the shaft
2. The shaft passes through one wall of the frame
3. The sails extend outward and upward

**Step 3: Design the sails.**

Sails capture wind energy and rotate the shaft.

**Sail design:**
- **Size:** Sails typically 5–10 meters per side (larger = more power)
- **Shape:** Rectangular or slightly trapezoidal
- **Material:** Canvas stretched over a wooden frame; can be wood or lattice (if no canvas available)
- **Number:** Typically four sails (like a cross), though two or three are possible
- **Angle:** Sails are angled relative to the wind to capture energy (not perpendicular; roughly 45-degree angle is optimal)

**Sail angle adjustment (for safety and control):**
1. Adjust the sail angle as wind changes
2. In strong wind, angle the sails more toward the vertical (to reduce force)
3. In weak wind, angle the sails more perpendicular to the wind (to capture more)
4. This manual adjustment prevents overload in gusts

**Step 4: Install the main shaft and gears.**

Power from the sails is transmitted via the shaft and gears to the machinery.

**Shaft design:**
1. A large wooden or iron shaft extends from the sail attachment
2. The shaft is supported by bearings inside the mill frame
3. The shaft rotates at the same speed as the sails (10–20 rotations per minute for most mills)

**Gear transmission:**
1. A large gear is attached to the main shaft
2. This gear drives smaller gears that turn the machinery shaft faster
3. Typical gear ratio: 5–20 (the machinery shaft rotates 5–20 times for each sail rotation)

**Step 5: Mount the machinery.**

The machinery (grain mill, pump, etc.) is mounted inside the rotating frame.

**Example: Grain mill**
1. A large stone wheel (the runner stone) rotates above a stationary stone (the bed stone)
2. Grain drops between the stones and is ground into flour
3. The gear shaft drives the runner stone

**Step 6: Build the tail vane.**

A tail vane keeps the sails facing the wind.

**Tail vane design:**
1. A large vertical sail or sheet at the rear of the mill
2. As wind direction changes, the tail vane pushes the entire mill to face the wind
3. The friction in the pivot allows the tail vane to gently rotate the mill without constant operator intervention
4. The operator may need to manually adjust the mill if wind shifts significantly

### Part 3: Tower Mill Design (Fixed, More Stable)

A tower mill has a fixed body (the tower) and only the cap (with sails) rotates.

**Step 1: Build the tower.**

The tower is a tall, sturdy structure (like a building).

**Tower design:**
1. Material: Brick, stone, or wood
2. Height: 6–12 meters (allows sails to extend well above ground obstacles)
3. Shape: Usually cylindrical (stronger and catches less wind than square towers)
4. Wall thickness: 0.5–1 meter (for strength)

**Foundation:**
1. Deep, solid foundation below the frost line (to prevent shifting)
2. The tower must be perfectly vertical (misalignment causes binding)

**Step 2: Build the rotating cap.**

Only the cap (the roof) rotates, not the entire mill.

**Cap design:**
1. A conical or domed roof containing the sails and gears
2. The cap sits on the top of the tower
3. A circular bearing allows the cap to rotate

**Sails and shaft:**
1. Sails are mounted on a shaft that rotates with the cap
2. The shaft drives gears inside the cap
3. Power is transmitted downward into the tower to the machinery

**Step 3: Implement the tail vane or wind sensor.**

Since only the cap rotates, a mechanism is needed to keep sails facing the wind.

**Option A: Tail vane**
1. A large vane at the rear of the cap
2. As wind direction changes, the vane pushes the cap to face the wind

**Option B: Automatic wind sensor (advanced)**
1. A sensor detects wind direction
2. Manual or mechanical controls rotate the cap to face the wind
3. More complex but allows constant optimal orientation

### Part 4: Power Output and Machinery Integration

**Step 1: Calculate available power.**

Power depends on wind speed and sail area.

**Simplified formula:**
Power (watts) ≈ 0.5 × wind speed³ × sail area (m²) × efficiency (0.25–0.35 for typical mills)

**Example:** Wind speed 8 m/s, sail area 50 m², efficiency 0.30:
Power ≈ 0.5 × 8³ × 50 × 0.30 ≈ 0.5 × 512 × 50 × 0.30 ≈ 3,840 watts ≈ 4 kW

This is comparable to a good water wheel and can power a grain mill continuously.

**Step 2: Match machinery to available power.**

Like water wheels, windmills must be sized appropriately:
- A 4 kW windmill can drive a large grain mill
- Multiple machinery (separate mills) can be driven in sequence or together if total power is sufficient
- Oversizing machinery beyond available wind power results in poor performance

**Step 3: Plan for variability.**

Wind is less consistent than water. Account for:
- Daily wind variation: Wind is stronger in afternoon, weaker at dawn
- Seasonal variation: Some seasons have more wind
- Gusts: Sudden strong winds can damage sails or machinery
- Calm periods: Some days have no wind; plan storage or alternative energy sources

### Part 5: Maintenance and Operation

**Step 1: Regular maintenance.**

- **Daily:** Check for sail damage; listen for unusual sounds
- **Weekly:** Oil bearings and gear pivots; check gearbox for leaks
- **Monthly:** Inspect sails for tears or separation; check tower structure
- **Seasonally:** Paint woodwork to prevent rot; inspect the bearing on which the cap rotates
- **Yearly:** Overhaul gears and bearings; replace worn parts

**Step 2: Sail adjustment (for post mills).**

1. As wind strength changes, adjust the angle of the sails
2. Strong wind: Angle sails more toward vertical (reduces force, prevents overload)
3. Weak wind: Angle sails more perpendicular to wind (captures more energy)
4. This adjustment balances power generation with safety

**Step 3: Brake system (for safety).**

1. A brake stops the mill in case of:
   - Emergency (dangerous situation)
   - High wind (to prevent damage)
   - Machinery jam (to prevent motor overload)
2. Simple brake: A wooden lever that applies friction to the main shaft
3. More complex: A geared brake system
4. The operator must be able to stop the mill quickly

## Verification

A properly functioning windmill shows:

- **Consistent rotation:** The sails turn smoothly without jerking
- **Expected power output:** Machinery operates at full capacity during typical wind
- **Sails face the wind:** The tail vane or cap keeps sails perpendicular to the wind direction
- **Cool bearings:** Bearings remain cool during operation
- **No noise:** Operation is relatively quiet (grinding sound is expected; grinding + squealing indicates bearing issues)

A poorly functioning windmill shows:

- **Jerky rotation:** The sails stop and start; indicates friction or misalignment
- **Low power output:** Machinery operates below capacity even in good wind
- **Sails not facing wind:** Wind is wasted because sails are not perpendicular to the wind
- **Hot bearings:** Bearings overheat during operation
- **Loud grinding or squealing:** Indicates bearing problems or gear misalignment
- **Vibration:** Excessive vibration indicates structural issues or unbalanced sails

## Common Mistakes

- **Poor site selection:** Building a windmill in a sheltered location (low wind) wastes effort. Choose high, open, unobstructed locations.
- **Unbalanced sails:** If sails are not the same weight or shape, the mill vibrates excessively. Make sails identical.
- **Oversized sails:** Very large sails may generate more power, but they're harder to control and prone to damage in gusts. Optimize sail size for the location and machinery.
- **Poor bearing support:** Misaligned or inadequately supported main shaft causes binding and friction. Use strong, well-lubricated bearings.
- **Inadequate tail vane (post mill):** Without a large, well-designed tail vane, the mill does not face the wind well. Size the vane appropriately.
- **Overloading in wind gusts:** Strong gusts can damage sails or machinery. Design a brake system to protect during high wind.
- **Neglecting maintenance:** Wind-powered equipment experiences constant stress. Regular maintenance prevents failures.
- **Structural instability:** A wobbly mill is dangerous and inefficient. Ensure the central post and foundation are solid and vertical.
