## Overview

A gear is a wheel with teeth. When two gears mesh, they transmit rotational motion from one shaft to another. The size and tooth count of gears determine the speed and force ratios.

Bearings support rotating shafts while minimizing friction. A shaft spinning on a poor bearing loses energy as heat. A shaft on a good bearing spins smoothly.

Together, gears and bearings are the foundation of all rotating machinery: mills, pumps, engines, and generators.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of mechanical advantage (Vol 33, Ch 1)
- Metalworking skills (drilling, filing, heat treatment)
- Understanding of friction and lubrication
- Access to metal stock and tools

## Materials & Tools

### For Building Gears

- **Metal stock:** Iron or steel (harder metals hold sharp teeth longer)
- **Cutting tools:** Files, chisels, or milling machine for cutting teeth
- **Measuring tools:** Calipers, ruler, protractor for precise tooth spacing
- **Lubricant:** Oil or grease (animal fat or plant oil works)

### For Building Bearings

- **Bearing material:** Brass, bronze, or wood (softer than the shaft) for sliding bearings
- **Rolling elements:** Balls or cylinders (if building ball/roller bearings) made of steel
- **Lubricant:** Oil or grease

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Understanding Gear Basics

**Step 1: Understand gear types.**

**Spur gears (straight teeth):**
- Teeth are parallel to the shaft axis
- Simplest to make and use
- Most common in post-collapse scenarios
- Efficient power transmission

**Bevel gears (angled teeth):**
- Teeth are angled (conical shape)
- Allows power transmission at an angle (perpendicular shafts)
- More complex to make
- Used when shafts intersect at an angle

**Helical gears (twisted teeth):**
- Teeth spiral around the gear
- Smoother, quieter operation than spur gears
- More efficient at high speeds
- Difficult to make without precision machinery

For post-collapse, focus on spur gears (simplest).

**Step 2: Calculate gear ratios.**

When two gears mesh, their teeth must match. The gear ratio determines how much speed is traded for torque (force).

**Formula:**
Gear ratio = number of teeth on driven gear / number of teeth on driving gear

**Example:** A driving gear with 20 teeth meshes with a driven gear with 60 teeth.
Gear ratio = 60 / 20 = 3

For every 1 rotation of the driving gear, the driven gear rotates 1/3 rotation. The driven gear rotates at 1/3 the speed but with 3 times the torque.

**Rule of thumb:** Large gear to small gear = speed reduction, torque increase. Small gear to large gear = speed increase, torque decrease.

**Step 3: Calculate tooth spacing.**

For gears to mesh properly, teeth must be the right size and spacing.

**Pitch (tooth spacing):**
Pitch = circumference of gear / number of teeth

**Example:** A gear 30 cm in circumference with 30 teeth:
Pitch = 30 cm / 30 = 1 cm (each tooth takes up 1 cm of the circumference)

Two gears must have the same pitch to mesh. A gear with 1 cm pitch teeth will not mesh smoothly with a gear that has 2 cm pitch teeth.

**Pressure angle:**
The angle of the tooth curve affects how smoothly power is transmitted. A 20-degree pressure angle is standard (tooth curves are designed for this angle).

**Step 4: Design a gear tooth.**

A gear tooth is not a simple triangle. It has a specific curve (involute curve) that ensures smooth transmission of power.

**Involute curve:** The curve you would trace if you wrapped a string around a circle and unwrapped it. This curve ensures that the contact point between two gears moves smoothly.

**For post-collapse (simplified):**
Rather than exact involute curves, approximate teeth as:
1. A rounded top (to avoid stress concentration)
2. An angled side (roughly matching the 20-degree pressure angle)
3. A wider base

This is not ideal but works well enough for low-speed applications.

**Tooth dimensions:**
- **Pitch (spacing):** Designed from the gear ratio and shaft size
- **Height:** Typically 1.25 times the pitch (tooth projects from the gear face)
- **Dedendum (root of tooth):** Slightly smaller than pitch (allows clearance for mating gear tooth)
- **Addendum (tip of tooth):** Extends above the pitch line

### Part 2: Cutting Gear Teeth

**Step 1: Prepare the gear blank.**

1. Cast or forge a metal disk (iron or steel) with a hole in the center for the shaft
2. Diameter should match the calculated gear size (from number of teeth and pitch)
3. The disk thickness (face width) should be roughly 0.5 to 1 times the pitch

**Step 2: Mark tooth positions.**

1. Mark the center of the gear (shaft hole)
2. Calculate the angular spacing: 360 degrees / number of teeth
3. Example: 30-tooth gear: 360 / 30 = 12 degrees per tooth
4. Mark positions around the gear circumference at these intervals
5. Use chalk or scribe to mark where each tooth should be

**Step 3: Cut the teeth.**

**Manual method (for small gears or post-collapse):**
1. Use a file to carefully cut each tooth space
2. Clamp the gear to a sturdy surface
3. File the space between tooth marks, angling slightly to match the desired pressure angle (roughly 20 degrees)
4. File from the center outward (not across the gear face, which might break teeth)
5. Repeat for all tooth spaces
6. This is slow (several hours per gear) but workable

**Machine method (if available):**
1. Use a milling machine with a gear cutter (a specialized cutting tool shaped to cut gear teeth)
2. The cutter is held on the milling machine spindle
3. The gear is rotated and fed under the cutter
4. Each pass cuts one tooth space
5. Much faster (under an hour per gear)

**Step 4: Finish the teeth.**

1. File any rough edges
2. Inspect for even spacing (all teeth should be approximately equal)
3. Test-fit with a mating gear (they should mesh smoothly with minimal backlash)

### Part 3: Bearing Types and Design

**Step 1: Understand bearing types.**

**Plain bearing (sliding):**
- A shaft rotates in a hole (usually lined with a softer metal)
- Friction is high (energy loss as heat)
- Simple to make; no rolling elements needed
- Effective if well-lubricated

**Roller bearing:**
- Cylindrical rollers sit between the shaft and the bearing housing
- Reduces friction compared to plain bearings
- More complex to make; rollers must be precision cylinders

**Ball bearing:**
- Steel balls sit between the shaft and housing
- Lower friction than roller bearings
- Most efficient; also most complex to manufacture

For post-collapse, focus on plain bearings (simplest) with good lubrication.

**Step 2: Design a plain bearing.**

**Simple plain bearing:**
1. A hole in a bearing block or housing
2. The hole is slightly larger than the shaft diameter
3. The hole is lined with brass or bronze (softer metal that wears instead of the shaft)
4. The liner is called a "bearing bush" or "bushing"

**Bushing construction:**
1. Machine a brass cylinder (hollow, like a tube) with:
   - Inner diameter slightly larger than the shaft (0.1–0.2 mm clearance)
   - Outer diameter fitting snugly into the bearing block hole
2. Press the bushing into the bearing block hole
3. The shaft rotates inside the bushing

**Lubrication:**
1. An oil groove (a small channel cut into the inner surface of the bushing) distributes oil
2. Oil is supplied through an oil hole (a small hole drilled through the bearing block)
3. As the shaft rotates, it carries oil around the bushing, reducing friction

**Step 3: Design a simple roller bearing.**

A homemade roller bearing is possible but challenging.

**Simple roller bearing:**
1. Two races (outer ring and inner ring): one stationary, one rotating
2. Rollers between the races
3. A cage that holds the rollers evenly spaced

**Simplified construction (post-collapse):**
1. Make two brass rings (inner ring on the shaft, outer ring in the housing)
2. Place steel or cast iron cylinders between them
3. The cylinders roll as the shaft rotates
4. Grease the gaps between cylinders to reduce friction

This is crude compared to modern ball bearings, but functional.

### Part 4: Assembling Gears and Bearings

**Step 1: Mount the gear on the shaft.**

1. The gear hole must be sized to the shaft (usually with a small clearance)
2. Press-fit the gear onto the shaft (apply force until it seats)
3. Or, fasten with a key (a small metal wedge that prevents the gear from rotating relative to the shaft)

**Key fastening:**
1. Cut a keyway (a rectangular slot) in both the shaft and the gear hole
2. Insert a rectangular key into the keyway
3. The key prevents the gear from slipping on the shaft

**Step 2: Align two meshing gears.**

1. Mount both shafts in bearings
2. Slide the gears onto their shafts
3. Adjust the distance between the shafts so the gear teeth just touch (mesh)
4. The ideal mesh has minimal backlash (clearance) but no binding (friction)
5. Tighten everything in place

**Backlash (clearance):**
- Essential (prevents binding when under load)
- Typically 0.1–0.5 mm between tooth faces
- Too much backlash causes noise and power loss
- Too little causes binding and tooth breakage

**Step 3: Lubricate the gears and bearings.**

1. Apply oil or grease to:
   - The mesh between gears
   - The bearings (inside the bushing or roller cage)
2. Frequency depends on speed and load:
   - Slow-speed: once per week
   - Medium-speed: every few days
   - High-speed: continuously (via an oil pump)

### Part 5: Efficiency and Power Loss

**Step 1: Understand power losses in gears and bearings.**

Power is lost to:
- **Friction between teeth:** Despite smooth meshing, teeth slide slightly, creating friction
- **Bearing friction:** The shaft-to-bearing interface dissipates energy
- **Churning (in oil):** High-speed gears churn oil, creating drag
- **Heat generation:** All friction losses become heat

**Typical efficiency:**
- Well-designed, well-lubricated spur gears: 95–98% efficient
- Plain bearings (good lubrication): 90–95% efficient
- Total system efficiency: product of individual efficiencies

**Example:** A gear train with two meshes and two bearings, each 95% efficient:
Total efficiency = 0.95 × 0.95 × 0.95 × 0.95 ≈ 81% (19% power loss as heat)

**Step 2: Reduce power losses.**

1. **Use low-speed applications:** Friction losses increase with speed. Keep speeds low if possible.
2. **Minimize backlash:** Backlash increases sliding and friction. Cut teeth carefully.
3. **Use good lubricants:** Clean oil reduces friction. Change oil if it becomes dirty or gritty.
4. **Reduce bearing friction:** Plain bearings with proper clearance and lubrication lose less power than plain bearings run dry.
5. **Keep gears clean:** Debris in the mesh causes increased friction and tooth damage.

## Verification

A properly functioning gear and bearing system shows:

- **Smooth rotation:** The shaft turns smoothly, without jerking or binding
- **Minimal noise:** Gears mesh quietly; grinding or squealing indicates problems
- **Proper speed ratio:** The driven gear rotates at the expected speed relative to the driving gear
- **No overheating:** Bearings and gears stay cool during normal operation
- **Longevity:** Gears and bearings do not wear excessively (lifespan measured in years or decades)

A poorly functioning system shows:

- **Binding or resistance:** Difficulty turning the shaft; high friction
- **Noise:** Grinding, squealing, or knocking sounds
- **Speed variation:** The driven gear does not rotate at constant speed
- **Overheating:** Bearings or gears become hot during normal operation
- **Rapid wear:** Teeth become damaged; bearing surfaces wear quickly
- **Oil breakdown:** Oil becomes dark and gritty (contamination or thermal breakdown)

## Common Mistakes

- **Mismatched pitch:** Gears with different tooth spacing will not mesh smoothly. Always ensure matching pitch.
- **Excessive backlash:** Too much clearance between meshing teeth causes noise and power loss. Adjust mesh until backlash is minimal but still present.
- **Inadequate lubrication:** Dry gears and bearings wear rapidly and generate excessive heat. Lubricate regularly.
- **Dirty lubricant:** Oil contaminated with metal particles or dirt acts like sandpaper, accelerating wear. Change oil if it becomes dark or gritty.
- **Bearing clearance too tight:** Zero clearance causes binding and rapid wear. Maintain 0.1–0.2 mm clearance.
- **Poor tooth surface finish:** Rough teeth increase friction and cause rapid wear. File smooth or finish carefully.
- **Misalignment:** If shafts are not parallel or gears are not centered, teeth will not mesh evenly. Check alignment carefully.
- **Overloading:** Applying excessive torque to a gear system can break teeth. Design for the expected load plus a safety margin.
