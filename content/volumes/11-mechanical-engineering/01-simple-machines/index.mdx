## Overview

A lever is a stick. A pulley is a wheel on a rope. An inclined plane is a ramp. These seem simple, but they are the building blocks of civilization. Combined, they move pyramids, hoist water, launch siege weapons, and power mills.

Each simple machine provides mechanical advantage: the ability to move a large load with a small force. Understanding the principles allows a post-collapse community to lift, move, and build without engines.

## Materials & Tools

### For Building Simple Machines

- **Wood:** Various hardwoods for sturdy frames and levers
- **Rope:** Strong rope (hemp, sisal, or plant-derived) for pulleys and support
- **Metal:** Iron or steel for pivot points, hinges, and reinforcement
- **Pulleys:** Wooden or metal wheels on axles (see Vol 33, Ch 2)
- **Fasteners:** Nails, bolts, or pins to hold structures together

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: The Lever

A lever is the simplest machine: a rigid bar pivoting around a point (fulcrum).

**Step 1: Understand lever classes.**

Levers are classified by the position of the fulcrum, load, and effort (force applied).

**Class 1 lever (fulcrum in the middle):**
- Fulcrum between the effort and the load
- Example: Seesaw, pry bar, scissors
- Mechanical advantage depends on the distance from fulcrum to each end
- Both effort and load move in opposite directions

**Diagram:**
```
Effort ← ─ ─ Fulcrum ─ ─ → Load
```

**Class 2 lever (load in the middle):**
- Load between the fulcrum and the effort
- Example: Wheelbarrow, nutcracker, door
- Always provides mechanical advantage (effort is always less than load)
- Load and effort move in the same direction (both up, or both down)

**Diagram:**
```
Fulcrum ← ─ ─ Load ─ ─ → Effort
```

**Class 3 lever (effort in the middle):**
- Effort between the fulcrum and the load
- Example: Tweezers, fishing rod, human arm
- Mechanical disadvantage (effort is greater than load)
- Used for precision and speed, not strength

**Diagram:**
```
Fulcrum ← ─ ─ Effort ─ ─ → Load
```

**Step 2: Calculate mechanical advantage.**

Mechanical advantage (MA) is the ratio of load to effort: how many times easier the machine makes the task.

**Formula:**
MA = distance from fulcrum to effort / distance from fulcrum to load

**Example:** A pry bar is 1 meter long. The fulcrum (pivot point) is 10 cm from the load end.
- Distance from fulcrum to load: 10 cm
- Distance from fulcrum to effort: 90 cm
- MA = 90 / 10 = 9

An effort of 100 kg (on the long end) can lift a load of 900 kg (on the short end). Note: The load end moves only 10 cm while the effort end moves 90 cm. You lift the load higher, but move further.

**Step 3: Build a practical lever.**

**Simple crowbar (pry bar):**
1. Find a strong wooden stick or iron rod (at least 1 meter long)
2. Identify where you will place the fulcrum (closer to the load than to your hand for maximum advantage)
3. Place a stone or wooden block as the fulcrum
4. Position the load on one end (the short end of the lever)
5. Apply force (effort) on the other end (the long end)
6. The load lifts

**Longer lever = more advantage:** A 2-meter lever provides twice the advantage of a 1-meter lever (for the same fulcrum position).

### Part 2: Pulleys

A pulley is a wheel with a rope threaded through it.

**Step 1: Understand pulley types.**

**Fixed pulley (overhead):**
- The pulley does not move; the rope moves
- Does not provide mechanical advantage (MA = 1)
- Changes the direction of force (pulling down is easier than pulling up)
- Useful for convenience, not power

**Movable pulley (attached to the load):**
- The pulley moves with the load
- Provides mechanical advantage (MA = 2 typically)
- One end of the rope is fixed, the other end is pulled
- The load is supported by two strands of rope (hence MA = 2)

**Pulley systems (multiple pulleys combined):**
- Multiple pulleys can be combined for greater advantage
- Examples: 2 fixed + 1 movable = MA of 2 or 3 (depending on configuration)
- 1 fixed + 2 movable = MA of 4

**Step 2: Calculate pulley mechanical advantage.**

For a system of pulleys, count the number of rope strands supporting the load.

**Rule:** MA = number of supporting rope strands

**Example 1: One fixed pulley**
- One strand of rope supports the load
- MA = 1 (no mechanical advantage, just direction change)

**Example 2: One movable pulley**
- Two strands of rope support the load (one from each side)
- MA = 2
- You must pull 2 meters of rope to lift the load 1 meter (trade-off: you lift less high but with half the force)

**Example 3: One fixed pulley with two movable pulleys**
- Four strands of rope support the load
- MA = 4
- You pull 4 meters of rope to lift the load 1 meter

**Step 3: Build a practical pulley system.**

**Simple mechanical advantage setup:**
1. Hang a fixed pulley overhead (from a beam or tree)
2. Thread a rope through a movable pulley (attached to the load)
3. Thread the same rope over the fixed pulley
4. Pull the free end of the rope
5. As you pull, the movable pulley (and the load attached to it) rises

**Mechanical advantage:** You lift 100 kg with 50 kg of effort (MA = 2).
**Trade-off:** You pull 2 meters of rope for every 1 meter the load rises.

**For greater advantage (MA = 4):**
1. Add a second fixed pulley and a second movable pulley
2. Thread the rope through all four in sequence
3. You now have four strands supporting the load
4. You lift 100 kg with 25 kg of effort

### Part 3: Inclined Plane

An inclined plane is a ramp. It allows heavy loads to be moved upward with less force (but over a longer distance).

**Step 1: Understand the physics.**

When you roll a barrel up a ramp instead of lifting it straight up, you use less force (but push over a longer distance).

**Mechanical advantage:**
MA = length of ramp / height of ramp

**Example:** A ramp 10 meters long, rising 2 meters high:
MA = 10 / 2 = 5

You can roll a 500 kg barrel up the ramp with 100 kg of effort (pulling or pushing horizontally). Without the ramp, you would need 500 kg of effort to lift it straight up 2 meters.

**Step 2: Calculate ramp angle.**

The angle of the ramp affects both MA and the stability of the load.

**Formula:**
angle = arctan(height / length)

**Safe angle:** Roughly 20 degrees (moderate slope) is practical for most loads. Steeper angles require more friction to prevent sliding. Shallower angles require less force but more distance.

**Step 3: Build a practical ramp.**

**Simple ramp for moving heavy stones or logs:**
1. Find a long wooden beam or logs
2. Support one end on the ground, the other end on a pile of stones (creating the slope)
3. For a 10-meter ramp rising 2 meters, stack stones about 2 meters high at one end
4. The beam becomes the ramp
5. Roll or slide heavy objects up the ramp
6. Add boards or wooden slats perpendicular to the slope for grip (prevents sliding)

**Anti-slip surface:**
- Rough wood grips better than smooth
- Rope wrapped around the ramp provides friction for objects
- Or nail wooden strips perpendicular to the slope

### Part 4: Wedge and Screw

A wedge is an inclined plane used to split or cut.

**Step 1: Understand wedges.**

A wedge concentrates force on a small area, allowing splitting.

**Examples:** An axe, a knife, a doorstop

**Mechanical advantage:**
MA = length of wedge / thickness of wedge edge

A wedge 10 cm long with a 1 cm thick edge: MA = 10

A small force on the back of the wedge is multiplied by 10 at the sharp edge.

**Step 2: Understand screws.**

A screw is an inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder (a thread).

**Mechanical advantage:**
MA = circumference of screw / pitch (distance thread advances per full turn)

A screw with a thread spacing (pitch) of 2 mm and a circumference (if you wrap the thread into a straight line) of 6 cm:
MA = 60 mm / 2 mm = 30

One full turn of the screw (with your hand) moves the load vertically 2 mm, but you push much harder than if you just pressed down. This is useful for precise, strong fastening.

### Part 5: Wheel and Axle

A wheel and axle is a lever, with the axle as the fulcrum.

**Step 1: Understand the principle.**

A large wheel on a small axle provides mechanical advantage (like a lever with different arm lengths).

**Examples:** Door knob, steering wheel, cart wheel

**Mechanical advantage:**
MA = radius of wheel / radius of axle

A wheel with a 30 cm radius on a 3 cm radius axle:
MA = 30 / 3 = 10

A small force on the wheel's rim (30 cm from the center) is multiplied 10 times at the axle.

**Step 2: Calculate rotation ratios.**

When a wheel and axle are geared together (with ropes or in contact), the rotation relationship is:
rotations of axle = (radius of wheel / radius of axle) × rotations of wheel

A small wheel driving a large axle moves the axle slowly but powerfully.

## Verification

A properly functioning simple machine shows:

- **Mechanical advantage:** Less force is required to move the load (observable by the effort required)
- **Direction change (if applicable):** Pulleys redirect force; levers change the angle of application
- **Stability:** The structure holds the load without collapsing or slipping
- **Predictable motion:** The load moves consistently (not jerky or unstable)

A poorly functioning simple machine shows:

- **Friction losses:** Excess friction dissipates energy (rope, wood, or rope rubs against something)
- **Structural failure:** The machine breaks or bends under load
- **Unexpected advantage loss:** The actual force required is higher than calculated (usually due to friction or misalignment)
- **Instability:** The load swings, slides, or the machine rocks

## Common Mistakes

- **Incorrect fulcrum placement:** Placing the fulcrum in the wrong spot reduces mechanical advantage. Calculate or measure the distance ratios before building.
- **Poor rope quality:** Weak rope frays, breaks, or slips under load. Use strong, fresh rope in good condition.
- **Insufficient friction on ramps:** A smooth ramp allows loads to slide backward. Add rough surfaces or angle the ramp less steeply.
- **Neglecting friction in all calculations:** Real machines have friction losses. The actual mechanical advantage is always less than the theoretical value. Account for this when planning heavy lifts.
- **Overloading a pulley system:** Rope can break; pulleys can fail. Test with gradually increasing loads before trusting a system with critical loads.
- **Misaligning pulleys:** If pulleys are not aligned, the rope twists, frays, and jams. Ensure all pulleys are in line.
- **Using weak wood for levers:** A weak lever breaks when loaded. Use dense, strong wood (oak, hickory) without large knots or cracks.
