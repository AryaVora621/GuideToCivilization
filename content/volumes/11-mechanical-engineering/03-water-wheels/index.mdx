## Overview

A river or stream provides energy. A water wheel captures this energy and converts it to rotational motion that can power mills, pumps, and machinery. Unlike manual labor or animals, a water wheel runs continuously without fatigue.

Three main designs exist:
1. **Undershot:** The wheel hangs in the current; water hits the bottom buckets
2. **Overshot:** Water falls onto the top of the wheel; gravity pulls the buckets down
3. **Breast:** Water hits the middle of the wheel; combines features of both

The choice depends on water available (speed and height).

## Materials & Tools

### For Building a Water Wheel

- **Wooden timber:** Oak or other hardwood for the frame
- **Wooden buckets or paddles:** Attached to the wheel rim
- **Shaft:** Iron or steel rod to support the wheel and transmit power
- **Bearings:** Brass or bronze bushings to support the shaft
- **Gears:** To reduce speed and increase torque (optional, if geared to machinery)
- **Water channel:** Constructed to direct water to the wheel (or use natural stream)

### Tools

- Saw, chisel, hammer, drill
- Measuring tools (rope for span, measuring stick for height)
- Spade (for digging water channel)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Choosing the Right Design

**Step 1: Assess your water source.**

Measure two things:
1. **Water height (head):** How far the water falls vertically (in meters)
2. **Water flow rate:** How much water moves past per unit time (cubic meters per second)

**Head measurement:**
1. Identify the top of the water source (where the wheel will receive water)
2. Identify the bottom of the water source (where water leaves the wheel area)
3. Measure the vertical distance between these points
4. This is the "head" available

**Flow rate measurement:**
1. Identify a uniform section of the stream
2. Measure the width and depth of the water
3. Measure how fast the water moves (throw a stick and time how far it travels)
4. Flow rate (approximately) = width × depth × speed

**Example:** A stream 0.5 m wide, 0.3 m deep, flowing at 1 m/s:
Flow rate ≈ 0.5 × 0.3 × 1 = 0.15 cubic meters per second

**Step 2: Choose the wheel design.**

**Undershot wheel:**
- Requires: High flow rate, low head (under 1 meter)
- Efficiency: 25–40% (water washes away quickly)
- Advantage: Easy to build; works in low-head situations
- Disadvantage: Inefficient; can freeze in winter if water is shallow

**Overshot wheel:**
- Requires: High head (2–20 meters), moderate flow
- Efficiency: 60–85% (gravity assists the buckets)
- Advantage: Very efficient; works with limited flow if head is available
- Disadvantage: Requires building an elevated water channel (labor-intensive)

**Breast wheel:**
- Requires: Moderate head (1–3 meters), good flow
- Efficiency: 50–70% (middle ground between overshot and undershot)
- Advantage: Flexible; works with variable conditions
- Disadvantage: More complex design than undershot

**Rule of thumb:**
- High head + low flow → Overshot wheel
- Low head + high flow → Undershot wheel
- Moderate head + moderate flow → Breast wheel

### Part 2: Building an Overshot Water Wheel (Most Efficient)

Overshot wheels are most efficient and work well for post-collapse scenarios with limited fuel.

**Step 1: Construct the wheel frame.**

The wheel is a wooden structure with a shaft running through its center.

**Wheel dimensions:**
- Diameter: 2–6 meters (larger wheels move more water)
- Width (axial length): 0.5–2 meters (the distance between the support bearings)
- Buckets: Arranged around the rim

**Frame construction:**
1. Create two large wooden circles (the front and back of the wheel)
   - Diameter: determined by the head available and desired power
   - Made from wooden segments bolted together (a large wooden wheel is too heavy to carve from one piece)
2. Connect the two circles with wooden spokes or curved pieces (the wheel rim)
3. Attach a central shaft (iron or steel rod) through the center
4. The shaft rests in bearings mounted on the wheel frame or the building structure

**Spoke construction (for strength):**
1. Arrange spokes radially from the hub (the center)
2. Spokes support the weight of the wheel and the water
3. Typically 8–12 spokes for a wheel 3 meters in diameter
4. Each spoke is a wooden plank bolted to the hub and the rim

**Step 2: Attach the buckets.**

The buckets catch the falling water and transfer energy to the wheel.

**Bucket design:**
1. Width: Same as the wheel axial width (fits the full width of the wheel)
2. Depth: 30–60 cm (determines water capacity)
3. Shape: Curved to shed water as the bucket exits the water

**Bucket attachment:**
1. Mount buckets around the wheel rim
2. Buckets are typically spaced at 30–45 degree intervals (8–12 buckets around the wheel)
3. Each bucket is bolted to the rim
4. As water falls into a bucket, its weight pulls that side of the wheel down, rotating the wheel

**Step 3: Build the water channel.**

Water must be directed to the top of the wheel.

**Channel construction:**
1. Dig or build a channel from the water source to the top of the wheel
2. The channel should slope gently toward the wheel (about 0.5–1 meter drop for every 10 meters of length)
3. At the wheel, the channel should be at the height where water will drop into the buckets
4. The channel width should match the wheel width (no spillage)

**Water control:**
1. Include a sluice gate (a movable board) to control water flow
2. Opening the gate increases water flow to the wheel
3. Closing the gate reduces or stops the wheel
4. The operator can adjust power by adjusting the gate

**Step 4: Support the shaft and bearings.**

The shaft must be supported at each end (where it exits the wheel).

**Bearing support:**
1. Mount bearings on strong wooden beams or masonry structures
2. The bearings (brass or bronze bushings) allow the shaft to rotate freely
3. The shaft rotates inside the bearings, so the wheel is free to spin
4. The bearings must be well-lubricated (oil-soaked cloth or grease)

**Step 5: Engage the wheel with machinery.**

The rotating shaft can power gears that drive machinery.

**Direct connection (simple):**
1. Attach a large gear to the shaft
2. This gear drives a smaller gear on the machinery's shaft
3. The gear ratio reduces speed and increases torque (see Vol 33, Ch 2)

**Example:** The water wheel shaft has a 40-tooth gear. A mill (like a grain mill) has a 10-tooth gear on its shaft.
Gear ratio = 40 / 10 = 4
The mill shaft rotates 4 times faster than the wheel shaft.

### Part 3: Building an Undershot Water Wheel (Simpler)

Undershot wheels are easier to build and suit high-flow, low-head situations.

**Step 1: Construct the wheel frame.**

Similar to the overshot wheel, but the buckets are replaced by flat paddles.

**Frame design:**
1. Two wooden circles (front and back)
2. Connected by spokes and a rim
3. Central shaft
4. Mounted in bearings

**Paddle design:**
1. Paddles are flat boards attached radially to the rim
2. The paddles are perpendicular to the flow direction
3. As water hits the paddles, they are pushed, rotating the wheel
4. Paddles are typically spaced around the rim (8–16 paddles for a 2-meter wheel)

**Step 2: Position the wheel in the water.**

The wheel hangs partially submerged in the current.

**Positioning:**
1. The wheel's bottom should touch the water surface (or slightly below)
2. The current pushes the paddles, rotating the wheel
3. The wheel should be perpendicular to the water flow direction

**Channel design:**
1. The water channel should direct the stream smoothly to the wheel
2. No sharp turns (causes turbulence and energy loss)
3. The channel width should match the wheel width

### Part 4: Power Output Calculation

**Step 1: Calculate available power.**

Power = (water density × flow rate × gravity × head) / time

**Simplified formula:**
Power (watts) ≈ 10 × flow rate (cubic meters/second) × head (meters)

**Example:** A stream with 0.1 m³/s flow and a 3-meter head:
Power ≈ 10 × 0.1 × 3 = 3 kilowatts

This is the theoretical power. Actual power depends on wheel efficiency (60–85% for overshot wheels).

**Practical power:** 3 kW × 0.70 efficiency = 2.1 kW

**Perspective:** 2 kilowatts can power:
- A large grain mill (continuously)
- Multiple sawmills (alternating)
- Several water pumps
- Basic industrial machinery

**Step 2: Design machinery scaled to available power.**

1. Do not over-design machinery beyond the available power
2. A 2 kW wheel cannot drive a 5 kW mill efficiently
3. Match the machinery to the available power for maximum efficiency

### Part 5: Maintenance and Operation

**Step 1: Regular maintenance schedule.**

- **Daily:** Check water level and channel for debris
- **Weekly:** Inspect bearings for smooth rotation; oil if needed
- **Monthly:** Inspect paddles or buckets for cracks or separation
- **Seasonally:** Clean debris from the channel (leaves, branches, silt)
- **Yearly:** Inspect the shaft for rust or cracks; replace wooden components if rotted

**Step 2: Seasonal considerations.**

**Winter:**
- Ice buildup can stop the wheel
- Lower water levels may reduce power
- Channel may need clearing of ice
- Solution: Design channels to prevent ice formation or include heating from waste heat

**Spring (flood season):**
- High water can damage the wheel
- Build a spillway or bypass channel to handle excess water
- Solution: Include a spillway gate that diverts excess water around the wheel

**Summer (low water):**
- Low flow may reduce power
- Plan for reduced productivity or store energy (if the system is damned)
- Solution: Consider a reservoir upstream to maintain flow during dry periods

**Step 3: Safety.**

1. The rotating wheel and shaft are dangerous
2. Keep people away from moving parts
3. Use guards or barriers around the wheel and gears
4. Ensure the operator can stop the wheel quickly (sluice gate control)

## Verification

A properly functioning water wheel shows:

- **Smooth rotation:** The wheel turns continuously without binding
- **Expected power output:** Machinery operates at full speed with water flowing
- **No vibration:** The rotating wheel does not shake or rattle
- **Proper water flow:** Water flows smoothly onto the wheel; no splashing or waste
- **Cool bearings:** Bearings stay cool during operation (indicates low friction)

A poorly functioning wheel shows:

- **Jerky rotation:** The wheel stops and starts; indicates binding or misalignment
- **Low power output:** Machinery operates slowly or inconsistently
- **Vibration:** The wheel shakes or the whole structure vibrates
- **Water waste:** Water misses the wheel or splashes away
- **Hot bearings:** Bearings become hot during operation (indicates excessive friction or misalignment)

## Common Mistakes

- **Wrong wheel design for the situation:** Choosing undershot for high head (inefficient) or overshot for low head (impractical). Assess head and flow carefully before choosing.
- **Undersized buckets or paddles:** Small buckets waste water energy. Make buckets as large as practical.
- **Misaligned water channel:** If the channel is not centered on the wheel, water misses the buckets. Align carefully.
- **Poor bearing support:** If the shaft is not well-supported, it bends and binds. Use strong bearings at both ends.
- **Inadequate lubrication:** Dry bearings generate friction and heat, reducing efficiency. Oil bearings regularly.
- **Bucket spillage:** If buckets spill water before reaching the bottom, energy is wasted. Design buckets to hold water until the bottom.
- **Overloading the wheel:** A wheel rated for 2 kW cannot drive a 5 kW load. Match machinery to available power.
- **Ignoring seasonal changes:** Water levels vary with season. Design channels and spillways to handle both high and low water.
