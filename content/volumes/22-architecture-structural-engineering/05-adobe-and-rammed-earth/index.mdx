## Overview

Adobe and rammed earth are ancient earthen construction techniques that remain practical post-collapse. Adobe bricks are dried (not fired), making them quick and cheap to produce. Rammed earth is compacted earth in wooden forms, creating solid walls without cutting bricks. Both techniques use clay soil found nearly everywhere, require no kiln or specialized equipment, and produce strong, durable structures with excellent thermal properties.

Earthen buildings are common in dry climates (the American Southwest, North Africa, parts of the Middle East) where they stand for centuries. Your settlement can be building permanent adobe houses within weeks of discovering suitable soil.

## Materials & Tools

**For adobe bricks:**
- Clay soil (find via Vol 14, Ch 6: excavate and test)
- Sand (if soil is too clayey; see Step 1)
- Straw or fiber (optional but improves strength)
- Wooden brick molds (roughly 20 cm × 10 cm × 5 cm)
- Water
- Mixing tools: hoe, shovel, or human feet (mixing by treading)

**For rammed earth:**
- Clay soil (same as adobe)
- Sand (to adjust clay content)
- Wooden formwork (boards and posts to contain the earth)
- Rammer or tamper (heavy wooden tool or stone)
- Water
- Leveling tools: straightedge, level

**For both:**
- Manpower (earthen construction is labor-intensive but requires no specialized skills)
- Basic tools: shovel, wheelbarrow, hose or water barrel

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Assess and Prepare Soil

Before making adobe or rammed earth, test your soil to ensure it has the right clay-to-sand ratio.

**Soil composition test:**

1. **Gather soil:** Collect soil from the proposed building site (use top 30 to 50 cm; topsoil is good)
2. **Remove large rocks and organic matter:** Screen out debris
3. **Jar test (see Vol 14, Ch 6):**
   - Fill a clear jar with soil and water (roughly 50/50 mix)
   - Shake vigorously for 2 to 3 minutes
   - Let settle for 24 hours
   - Observe layers: sand sinks first (bottom), silt in middle, clay rises to top
   - Ideal ratio: 15 to 30 percent clay, 70 to 85 percent sand/silt
   - If too clayey (under 60 percent sand), add coarse sand
   - If too sandy (under 15 percent clay), find clay-rich soil elsewhere

**Optimal soil properties:**

- **Clay content:** 15 to 30 percent (too much clay cracks; too little doesn't bind)
- **Sand content:** 70 to 85 percent (coarse sand is preferred; fine sand is okay)
- **Organic matter:** Less than 5 percent (decays and weakens the structure)
- **pH:** Neutral or slightly alkaline (acidic soils can be treated with lime)

**Soil improvement:**

If your soil is too clayey (over 30 percent clay):
1. Add coarse sand: roughly 1 part sand per 3 parts soil by volume
2. Mix thoroughly (mechanical mixer, by hoe, or by treading)
3. Test again; adjust until 15 to 30 percent clay

If your soil is too sandy (under 15 percent clay):
1. Excavate from a different location or source clay separately
2. Mix clay-rich soil with sandy soil until balance is achieved

### Step 2: Make Adobe Bricks

Adobe bricks are hand-formed, dried (not kiln-fired), and then stacked into walls.

**Adobe brick dimensions:**

- **Standard size:** 20 cm long × 10 cm wide × 5 cm thick (similar to fired bricks, but thicker for rammed earth effect)
- **Alternative size:** 25 cm × 12 cm × 7 cm (larger bricks are faster to lay)
- **Wall thickness:** Three bricks wide (roughly 30 cm) for single-story; thicker for multi-story

**Making adobe bricks:**

1. **Mix soil and straw:**
   - Spread clay soil in a pile
   - Add water gradually (hose or buckets) while mixing with a hoe
   - Target consistency: like stiff mud (just wet enough to mix, not soupy)
   - Add straw: roughly 10 to 20 percent by volume of the wet soil (about 5 cm of loose straw per 20-liter bucket of mud)
   - Mix until straw is evenly distributed
   - Treading with human feet is an effective traditional mixing method

2. **Fill brick molds:**
   - Wooden mold: 4-sided frame with bottom (open top)
   - Sprinkle sand on the ground (prevents sticking)
   - Fill mold with mixed soil, press firmly
   - Strike off excess with a straightedge (level with mold top)
   - Lift mold vertically; brick remains on the ground

3. **Drying:**
   - Lay bricks in the sun with space between them for air circulation (at least 10 cm)
   - Dry for 3 to 4 weeks in warm, dry weather
   - Turn bricks over halfway through to ensure even drying
   - Once dry, bricks are rock-hard and can be stacked or stored

**Brick quality:**

- Well-dried bricks should not dent when pressed hard with thumb
- Bricks made with adequate straw should not crack excessively (hairline cracks are normal)
- Bricks should ring (not thud) when struck
- Yield: One person can make 50 to 100 bricks per day, depending on efficiency

### Step 3: Make Mortar for Adobe Bricks

Adobe mortar is simply clay soil and sand with straw, similar to the bricks themselves.

**Mortar recipe:**

```
Clay soil (with high clay content):  50 percent
Coarse sand:                         40 percent
Straw (chopped fine):                10 percent
Water:                               as needed
```

**Preparation:**

1. Mix soil, sand, and straw thoroughly
2. Add water slowly until mortar is creamy (thick enough to stick to a trowel but spreadable)
3. Use within hours of mixing; do not let mortar dry out

### Step 4: Build Adobe Brick Walls

Adobe walls are laid like conventional brick, but with thicker mortar joints.

**Construction procedure:**

1. **Foundation:**
   - Adobe requires a strong, dry foundation (1 to 1.5 meters high minimum; see Vol 22, Ch 1)
   - Foundation should be stone or fired brick (not adobe; ground moisture rots the adobe)
   - The foundation must be above the water table and above flood level

2. **Lay first course:**
   - Butter the foundation top with mortar (1 to 2 cm thick)
   - Lay bricks in a running bond (alternating pattern; not like fired bricks)
   - Press each brick down firmly into mortar
   - Ensure joints are solid (no gaps)
   - Mortar joints: 2 to 3 cm thick (thicker than fired brick walls, for better adhesion with sun-dried units)

3. **Build up the wall:**
   - Each course is laid horizontally, offset by half a brick from the one below
   - Build walls evenly on all sides to avoid stress concentrations
   - Rest between courses (let mortar dry partially) every 3 to 4 courses
   - Overall pace: one course every 1 to 2 days (faster in dry climate, slower in humid)

4. **Openings (doors, windows):**
   - Plan openings before building; leave space in the wall as you lay bricks
   - Install wooden headers (horizontal beams) above openings to support the weight above
   - Header should extend at least 50 cm beyond the opening on each side

5. **Roof connection:**
   - The top of the wall should be level
   - Install a wooden plate (beam running along the wall top)
   - Roof rafters rest on and are tied to this plate
   - Without the plate, roof weight concentrates on the highest bricks, causing cracking

### Step 5: Build Rammed Earth Walls

Rammed earth is more labor-intensive but uses less brick-making. Earth is compacted directly into forms.

**Rammed earth advantages:**

- Uses raw earth; no brick-making time
- Denser than adobe; slightly stronger
- Faster for large walls (single continuous wall vs. stacking thousands of bricks)
- Requires more initial formwork but less total work per volume

**Rammed earth process:**

1. **Design formwork:**
   - Wooden boards (2 to 5 cm thick) form both sides of the wall
   - Internal bracing (cross-timbers) holds the boards apart at a fixed distance (typically 30 to 50 cm for wall thickness)
   - Forms must be level, plumb (vertical), and strong enough to resist earth pressure

2. **Prepare earth:**
   - Same soil mixture as adobe (15 to 30 percent clay)
   - Do not add straw (ramming compacts it anyway)
   - Earth should be damp but not wet (just enough water so it compacts)

3. **Ram the earth:**
   - Fill forms in 15 to 20 cm lifts (layers)
   - Each lift is compacted with a wooden rammer (roughly 10 kg weight on a long handle)
   - Ram until the earth is hard and compact (roughly 30 to 50 rammer strikes per lift)
   - Add next lift and repeat
   - Continue until the form is full (wall height reached)

4. **Remove formwork:**
   - After 3 to 7 days (earth has partially dried and gained strength)
   - Remove side boards carefully
   - Move formwork to the next section of wall
   - Allow wall to continue curing for 2 to 4 weeks before roof loading

5. **Advantages of rammed earth:**
   - Monolithic (no joints to fail)
   - Very good compression strength (stronger than adobe)
   - Fast wall construction (move formwork along the wall)

### Step 6: Finish and Protect Earthen Walls

Earth is vulnerable to water damage. Proper finishing protects the building.

**Plaster:**

1. **Mud plaster (simplest):**
   - Mix clay soil with sand and straw (similar to mortar)
   - Apply 2 to 3 cm thick over the wall surface
   - Smooth with a trowel
   - Dries hard; provides weather protection

2. **Lime plaster (more durable):**
   - Mix lime (from Vol 14, Ch 6) with sand and straw
   - Better weather resistance than mud plaster
   - More expensive (requires making lime)
   - Lasts longer (10 to 20 years before needing renewal)

**Roof overhang:**

- Large overhangs (1 to 2 meters) protect walls from rain
- Walls cannot dry if constantly wet
- Gutters or roof drains prevent water running down the face

**Base protection:**

- Avoid planting vines or trees directly against walls (roots damage walls)
- Keep ground slope away from the building (water should drain away)
- Maintain the foundation damp-proof barrier (see Vol 22, Ch 1)

### Step 7: Thermal Properties and Maintenance

Earth buildings have excellent thermal properties. They stay cool in summer and warm in winter if designed properly.

**Thermal mass benefit:**

- Thick earth walls (30 to 50 cm) absorb heat during the day and release it at night
- Walls slow heat transfer; interior remains more stable than exterior temperature
- In hot, dry climates, adobe buildings are naturally cool without air conditioning
- In cold climates, the thermal mass stores solar heat and releases it slowly

**Maintenance:**

- Earth buildings require maintenance every 10 to 20 years
- Plaster cracks and needs renewal
- Small patches of erosion should be resealed
- Mud plaster is easily repaired (apply new plaster over damaged areas)
- Large cracks in the wall itself indicate structural problems (usually settlement or water damage)

**Longevity:**

- Well-built, well-maintained adobe buildings last 300+ years
- Examples exist in the American Southwest (Pueblo cultures) and North Africa
- Failure usually comes from water damage or poor foundation, not material weakness

## Verification

**Success looks like:**

- Dried adobe bricks are hard, do not crumble, and ring when struck
- Adobe mortar adheres to bricks; joints are solid (no gaps)
- Rammed earth wall is dense, hard, and monolithic
- Walls are plumb (vertical) and level (horizontal)
- Roof and plaster protect the walls from water damage

**Failure looks like:**

- Adobe bricks crumble or are weak (too much clay or not enough straw)
- Mortar does not adhere; bricks shift during drying
- Wall leans or is out of plumb
- Water damage or erosion from poor roof/plaster design
- Large cracks indicating settlement or structural problems

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll use clay-rich soil to make stronger bricks"** — Too much clay causes excessive cracking as bricks dry. Stay in the 15 to 30 percent clay range.

2. **"I'll make adobe bricks without straw"** — Straw dramatically increases strength and reduces cracking. Always include 10 to 20 percent straw.

3. **"I'll fire adobe bricks in a kiln"** — This makes them regular brick, expensive in fuel and labor. Adobe is valuable precisely because it is sun-dried. Use the sun.

4. **"I'll skip the foundation"** — Adobe fails from water damage. A strong, dry foundation 1 to 1.5 meters high is essential.

5. **"I'll lay bricks with thin mortar like fired brick walls"** — Adobe mortar should be thicker (2 to 3 cm) to accommodate drying differences between bricks and mortar.

6. **"I'll build the wall quickly without letting mortar dry"** — Adobe walls need time for mortar to set before supporting weight. Build slowly; allow rest days between courses.

7. **"I don't need a roof overhang"** — Water is adobe's enemy. Large overhangs (1 to 2 meters) are essential for protection.

8. **"I'll use rammed earth without formwork bracing"** — The earth pressure will collapse weak forms. Brace forms strongly at the correct distance apart.
