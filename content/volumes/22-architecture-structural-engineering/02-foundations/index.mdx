## Overview

Foundations transfer the weight of a building safely into the earth. Poor foundations cause buildings to crack, settle unevenly, and fail. Good foundations last centuries.

This chapter covers foundation types, soil assessment, construction methods, and avoiding common failures.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of loads and forces (Vol 11)
- Knowledge of materials (stone, brick, concrete)
- Ability to assess soil conditions

## Materials & Tools

### For assessment
- Shovel (for test holes)
- Measuring tools
- Water (for soil moisture and drainage tests)

### For construction
- Materials: stone, brick, mortar, timber (depending on type)
- Tools: shovels, picks, levels, squares, compaction tools

## Part 1: Soil Assessment

Before building, understand the soil.

### Soil types

**Clay:** Expands when wet, shrinks when dry. Moves easily. Poor bearing capacity.

**Silt:** Fine particles. Easily eroded. Can compact well but loses strength when wet.

**Sand:** Large particles. Drains well. Stable. Good bearing capacity.

**Rock:** Excellent bearing capacity. Difficult to excavate.

**Loam:** Mix of clay, silt, and sand. Often reasonable bearing capacity.

### Bearing capacity

Soil's ability to support weight. Measured in pounds per square foot.

- **Clay:** 1,500–3,000 psf (poor to fair)
- **Sand:** 2,000–6,000 psf (fair to good)
- **Rock:** 5,000 psf and higher (excellent)

A 2-story house with 100 sq ft foundation distributes its weight over this area. If the soil can only support 2,000 psf, the foundation cannot exceed 2,000 × 100 = 200,000 pounds (100 tons).

### Frost action

In cold climates, soil freezes and expands (frost heave), pushing buildings up. When thaw occurs, buildings settle. Solution: build below the frost line (depth where ground does not freeze, typically 2–4 feet in cold regions).

### Drainage

Poor drainage keeps soil wet, reducing bearing capacity and causing rot. Good drainage prevents water from pooling around foundations.

## Part 2: Foundation Types

### Type 1: Spread footing (strip foundation)

**Use:** Small buildings, good soil, simple construction.

**Construction:**

1. Excavate trenches to below frost line (3–4 feet in cold regions) or to stable soil.
2. Create a base of gravel or sand for drainage (3–6 inches).
3. Build foundation walls on this base using stone, brick, or concrete.
4. Walls are typically 1–2 feet thick, transferring the building's weight into the soil below.

**Advantages:** Simple, cheap, uses local materials.

**Disadvantages:** Limited load capacity, sensitive to soil conditions.

### Type 2: Rubble trench (modified spread footing)

**Use:** Good soils, small-medium buildings, energy-efficient.

**Construction:**

1. Dig trenches to below frost line.
2. Fill partway with rubble (broken rock, brick, gravel) for drainage and thermal mass.
3. Top with concrete or stone foundation wall (12–18 inches).
4. Rubble inside acts as a drainage layer and provides some insulation.

**Advantages:** Lower material cost, good drainage, some insulation benefit.

**Disadvantages:** Requires stable rock/gravel rubble, less suitable for clay soils.

### Type 3: Slab on grade

**Use:** Warm climates, flat terrain, simple construction.

**Construction:**

1. Prepare a gravel or sand base for drainage (4–6 inches).
2. Add a moisture barrier (plastic sheet) to prevent water seeping up.
3. Pour a concrete slab (4–6 inches thick) directly on this base.
4. Reinforce with stone or steel to prevent cracking.

**Advantages:** Cheap, fast, no excavation needed.

**Disadvantages:** Subject to frost heave in cold climates, moisture problems if drainage fails, difficult to repair.

### Type 4: Pile foundation

**Use:** Poor soil, sloped terrain, areas subject to flooding.

**Construction:**

1. Drive or bore piles (timber posts or concrete columns) deep into soil until they reach stable layer.
2. Piles can be 30–50 feet deep if needed.
3. Build the building's structure on top of these piles.

**Advantages:** Works in poor soil, raises building above flood zones, adaptable to slopes.

**Disadvantages:** Expensive, requires specialized equipment, difficult to construct without machinery.

### Type 5: Grade beam

**Use:** Large buildings, uneven terrain, when different parts need different depths.

**Construction:**

1. Build columns or piers that rest on stable soil at varying depths.
2. Connect the tops of these piers with a reinforced beam (grade beam).
3. The building structure sits on the grade beam.

**Advantages:** Adapts to uneven terrain, distributes loads, flexible design.

**Disadvantages:** Complex construction, requires careful engineering.

## Part 3: Construction Details

### Depth

Foundation must reach below frost line or to stable soil, whichever is deeper.

**Rule of thumb:** In cold climates, excavate 1 foot deeper than the average frost depth.

### Width

Foundation must be wide enough to distribute loads safely. Wider foundations spread weight over more area, reducing pressure on soil.

For a 2-story building on sand (2,500 psf capacity), walls carrying 50 pounds per linear foot of height need a foundation at least 2 feet wide.

### Drainage

- **Perimeter drain:** A gravel or pipe layer around the outside to carry water away.
- **Interior drain:** A layer of gravel inside the foundation to prevent water pooling below the slab.
- **Slope:** Grade should slope away from the building, carrying water toward storm drains or away from the structure.

### Waterproofing

- **Exterior:** Coat foundation walls with waterproof material (clay-based or tar coating historically).
- **Interior:** Sump pump (in modern systems) or good drainage layer.

### Cracks

Small cracks in foundations are normal and usually harmless. Larger cracks (over 1/4 inch wide) suggest movement. Monitor them; if they grow, investigate the cause (settlement, heave, poor drainage, structural overload).

## Part 4: Common Failures

### Settlement

The building sinks uniformly or (worse) unevenly. Causes:

- **Soil compression:** Bearing capacity exceeded. Solution: wider foundation, stronger soil preparation, or deeper piles.
- **Wet soil:** Clay expands and contracts. Solution: improve drainage, build below frost line.
- **Fill soil:** Previously disturbed soil compresses over time. Solution: fully compact fill before building.

### Frost heave

Soil freezes, expands, pushes building up. Solution: build below frost line.

### Sloping floors

Uneven settlement causes floors to slope. Indicates ongoing movement. Investigate and correct the cause (drainage, structural overload, deteriorating foundation).

### Rot

Wood in contact with soil or moisture rots. Solution: use stone or concrete for below-grade work, keep wood separated from soil.

### Cracks radiating from corners

Indicates uneven settlement or excessive loads at corners. Investigate and redistribute load.

## Part 5: Historic Methods

### Stone foundations

Medieval and ancient buildings used stone set in mortar.

- **Rubble stone:** Irregular stone and mortar. Cheap, serviceable, long-lasting if mortar stays intact.
- **Cut stone:** Fitted carefully. More expensive, longer-lasting, more beautiful.

These buildings are still standing after centuries, proving that good foundations endure.

### Brick

Brick on stone or earth. Common in 18th–19th centuries.

### Concrete

Modern concrete foundations (20th century on) use reinforced concrete for strength and durability.

## Verification

A good foundation:

1. **Does not settle:** Building remains level.
2. **Stays dry:** No water seeping into the basement.
3. **Is level:** Floors are not sloping.
4. **Has no major cracks:** Small cracks are normal; large growing cracks indicate problems.
5. **Supports the building:** No signs of structural distress (cracking walls, sticking doors/windows, bowing walls).

## Common Mistakes

1. **Building on fill:** Disturbed soil compresses, causing settling.
2. **Inadequate depth:** Building above frost line heaves in winter.
3. **Insufficient width:** Foundation too narrow causes settlement.
4. **Poor drainage:** Water around foundation causes rot and weakening.
5. **Ignoring soil:** Not assessing soil before building leads to failures.
6. **Building on slopes:** Uneven bearing capacity causes differential settlement.
