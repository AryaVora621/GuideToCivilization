## Overview

An arch is a curved structure that spans an opening by transmitting weight outward and downward along the curve. Arches allow large open spaces (aqueducts, bridges, covered halls) without internal columns. Vaults are extended arches; domes are arches rotated in three dimensions. These structures are among the most important architectural innovations—they enable permanent settlements to have large public spaces and functional infrastructure.

This chapter teaches you the engineering principles of arches and domes, how to calculate dimensions, and how to build them. By year 3, your settlement can build domed structures and arched bridges that last centuries.

## Materials & Tools

**For arch construction:**
- Stone blocks (cut or roughly rectangular, matched in size)
- Mortar (lime mortar; see Vol 14, Ch 6: Clay and Limestone)
- Temporary wooden centering (formwork to support arch during construction)
- Tools: plumb bob, straightedge, measuring cord

**For dome construction:**
- Stone blocks (same as arches, but smaller and more numerous)
- Mortar
- Wooden formwork (dome-shaped mold)
- Curved templates (to match stone to dome curve)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Understand Arch Mechanics

An arch works by compression: weight pushes the curved stones together, and forces travel down the curve to the supports (abutments).

**Key principle:** In an arch, loads transfer as compression along the curve, rather than as bending (which would snap a straight beam).

**Parts of an arch:**

- **Voussoirs:** the wedge-shaped stones that form the arch
- **Keystone:** the central voussoir at the top (pushes the others into place)
- **Abutments:** the supports at each end (must be very strong to resist outward force)
- **Span:** the horizontal distance the arch covers
- **Rise:** the vertical height of the arch above the supports

**Load path:**

1. Weight on the arch (like traffic or water) pushes downward
2. The curved shape transfers this weight along the curve
3. Forces travel down and outward to the abutments
4. Abutments resist outward force (called "thrust")

**Why arches are strong:**
- The curved shape is inherently stable (compression is the strongest force for stone)
- Larger arches with deeper curves are stronger (steeper curve distributes force better)
- Multiple layers of stone increase strength (thicker arches are stronger)

### Step 2: Calculate Arch Dimensions

Arch dimensions depend on the span (distance to cover) and the material (stone, brick, or timber).

**Simple arch rule (rule of thumb for stone arches):**

```
Rise (height) = Span / 4

Example:
- Span: 8 meters
- Rise: 8 / 4 = 2 meters
- Result: arch is 8 m wide, 2 m tall
```

**More detailed calculation (for accuracy):**

For a semicircular arch (most common):
- Span = 8 m
- Radius = Span / 2 = 4 m
- The arch is half a circle with radius 4 m

For a pointed arch (Gothic style, stronger):
- Span = 8 m
- Rise = 2 × Span / 3 = 5.3 m (taller than semicircular)
- More pointed arches distribute load better; stronger for long spans

**Thickness of arch:**
- Rule of thumb: Arch thickness = Span / 12 to Span / 15
- Example: 8 m span → thickness 0.5 to 0.7 m (roughly 0.6 m)
- Thicker arches are stronger but more expensive

**Abutment size:**
- Abutments must resist outward thrust
- Abutment thickness: roughly 1/3 of the arch span
- Example: 8 m arch → 2.5 m thick abutments
- If abutments are too thin, the arch collapses outward

### Step 3: Design the Voussoirs (Wedge Stones)

Voussoirs are the individual stones that form the arch curve.

**Voussoir sizing:**
- **Radial design:** Each voussoir is shaped so its sides are perpendicular to the arch radius (wedge shape)
- **Typical size:** 20 to 40 cm long, 15 to 25 cm deep, varying height (inner voussoirs are shorter, outer are taller)
- **Number of voussoirs:** For an 8 m span arch, roughly 16 to 24 voussoirs

**Cutting voussoirs:**

1. Draw the arch profile (curved line showing the arch shape)
2. Divide the profile into equal angles (e.g., 15° angles for 24 voussoirs = 360°)
3. Draw radii from the center through each angle division
4. Mark the stone at the inner and outer radius
5. Cut the stone to match the wedge shape (sides parallel to the radii)

**Quality check:**
- Each voussoir should fit snugly against its neighbors
- Radial joints (between voussoirs) should point toward the arch center
- Mortar bed should be uniform (under 1 cm thick)

### Step 4: Build Temporary Centering (Support Structure)

An arch cannot support itself during construction. You must build a wooden form to support the voussoirs until the keystone is in place and mortar sets.

**Centering design:**

1. **Curved wooden form (rib):**
   - Build a wooden arc matching the arch profile
   - Use straight timbers (pine, fir) bent into the curve, or laminated wood
   - Support the form with vertical props underneath

2. **Props (temporary support posts):**
   - Wooden posts every 1 to 2 meters along the span
   - Support the curved form; transfer weight to the ground
   - Must be strong enough to support the full weight of voussoirs and mortar

3. **Shoring beams:**
   - Horizontal timbers distributing the load from props to the ground
   - Base must be wide and stable

**Centering removal:**
- After mortar sets (2 to 4 weeks), carefully lower the props
- Lower all props equally (slightly at a time, over several days)
- As props lower, the arch settles slightly (mortar compresses)
- Once the keystone is engaged (locked in place), the arch is self-supporting

### Step 5: Build the Arch

**Procedure:**

1. **Build the abutments first:**
   - Construct solid stone/brick walls at each end of the span
   - Make them very thick (1/3 of span)
   - Abutments must be complete and strong before arch loading

2. **Erect the centering:**
   - Position the wooden form between abutments
   - Level it carefully (critical for uniform arch)
   - Brace it against movement

3. **Lay voussoirs:**
   - Start laying voussoirs from each abutment, working toward the center
   - Apply mortar to each joint (uniform bed, under 1 cm thick)
   - Press voussoirs firmly against the form
   - Work both sides equally (balance the load on the centering)

4. **Set the keystone:**
   - As you approach the center, the opening narrows
   - The last stone (keystone) is wedged at the top
   - Setting the keystone locks the arch into place
   - After the keystone is set, the arch begins to be self-supporting

5. **Allow mortar to cure:**
   - Leave centering in place for 2 to 4 weeks
   - Mortar must set before removing support
   - Cold weather requires longer curing time

6. **Remove centering:**
   - Lower props gradually and evenly
   - Arch settles slightly as it fully engages
   - Once keystone is fully locked, remove centering completely

### Step 6: Build a Barrel Vault (Extended Arch)

A barrel vault is an arch extended along its length, creating a tunnel-like structure.

**Design:**

- Width: 4 to 10 meters (same as arch span)
- Length: 5 to 20 meters (or more)
- Height: Rise follows the same proportion as a simple arch

**Construction:**

1. **Build two arch ribs (curved supports) at each end**
   - These are like regular arches
   - Space them across the width (one on each side, or multiple across)

2. **Span between the ribs with voussoirs**
   - Lay curved layers of voussoirs between the ribs
   - Each layer is like a single arch
   - Stack layers along the length

3. **Alternative method (simpler):**
   - Use barrel-shaped centering (like a half-cylinder wooden form)
   - Lay voussoirs in curves along the form
   - Each course (layer) is perpendicular to the vault length

4. **Removal of centering:**
   - Same as arch: lower gradually, allow mortar to set first

**Advantages of barrel vaults:**
- Covers large spaces (10 m × 20 m covered with one vault)
- Distributes loads evenly along length
- Can support heavy loads (floors, roofs)

### Step 7: Build a Dome

A dome is an arch rotated in three dimensions, creating a spherical roof.

**Design:**

- Diameter: 6 to 20 meters (dome span)
- Height: varies; semicircular dome has height = radius
- Strength: domes are very efficient structures (distribute load radially)

**Construction:**

1. **Build a circular wall (drum) at the base**
   - Must be very strong and perfectly circular
   - Supports the entire dome weight

2. **Create dome-shaped centering:**
   - Build a hemispherical wooden form
   - This is complex but critical for dome accuracy

3. **Lay voussoirs in horizontal rings:**
   - Start at the base, work upward
   - Each ring is a complete circle of voussoirs
   - Each ring is smaller (narrower radius) as you go up
   - Voussoirs are wedge-shaped, narrower at the center

4. **Close at the top with a capstone:**
   - The final ring has only one or a few voussoirs
   - A single stone (oculus) or small opening closes the top

5. **Remove centering:**
   - After mortar sets, lower the form carefully
   - Dome settles as it fully engages
   - Very slow removal (over weeks) is safest

**Dome engineering:**

- The dome transmits weight vertically down and slightly outward
- The circular base (drum) resists the outward thrust
- Larger domes require stronger supporting walls
- Domes are one of the most elegant and efficient structural forms

### Step 8: Inspect and Maintain

After construction, monitor the arch or dome for problems.

**Inspection:**

1. **Check mortar joints:**
   - Any gaps or cracking in mortar indicate settling or movement
   - Repoint (refill) cracked joints with new mortar

2. **Check for cracks in voussoirs:**
   - Small radial cracks (pointing toward center) are normal
   - Large cracks or horizontal cracks indicate problems

3. **Check the abutments:**
   - Look for cracks or outward movement
   - If abutments are spreading, the arch is failing

4. **Monitor over time:**
   - Arches settle and crack slightly in the first year
   - This is normal; monitor that it stabilizes
   - If cracking continues to grow, investigate

## Verification

**Success looks like:**
- Completed arch/vault/dome stands without internal supports
- Mortar joints are tight (under 1 cm thick)
- No excessive cracking or settling
- Abutments show no signs of spreading or failure
- Structure stands for decades without repair

**Failure looks like:**
- Arch/vault collapses during or shortly after construction
- Abutments crack and spread outward
- Excessive cracking in voussoirs
- Visible movement or settling continuing beyond first year

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll make abutments as thin as the arch"** — Abutments must be very thick (1/3 of span). Thin abutments fail as the arch thrusts outward.

2. **"Semicircular arches are strongest; I'll always use them"** — Pointed arches are stronger for long spans. Choose based on your span and material.

3. **"I'll remove centering as soon as mortar feels dry"** — Mortar continues curing for weeks. Leave centering for at least 2 to 4 weeks, longer in cold weather.

4. **"The centering can be temporary and weak"** — Centering must support the full weight of stones and mortar. Use strong timbers and brace well.

5. **"I'll use small, thin stones to save money"** — Small stones require more mortar joints, and thin stones may crack. Use larger, thicker voussoirs.

6. **"The keystone is just another stone"** — The keystone is critical; it locks the arch. Place it with care; ensure it is fully seated and level.

7. **"Domes are the same as arches extended"** — Domes are more complex; they distribute load differently. Study dome design carefully before attempting.

8. **"Arches need perfect geometry"** — Slight variations are acceptable. But radial joints must point toward the center; this is critical.
