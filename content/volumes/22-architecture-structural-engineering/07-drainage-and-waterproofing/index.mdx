## Overview

Water is a building's primary enemy. Moisture causes rot, mold, structural failure, and material degradation. Proper drainage and waterproofing ensure buildings last centuries instead of decades.

This chapter covers three scales of water protection:

1. **Site drainage:** Managing water on the land around the building
2. **Foundation protection:** Preventing water from entering below the building
3. **Surface sealing:** Protecting exposed materials (wood, adobe, brick)

By mastering these techniques, you prevent the majority of post-collapse building failures. A well-drained, well-sealed building in even a wet climate will outlast neglected buildings in dry climates.

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Understand Water Movement

Water moves three ways: from above (rain), from the side (groundwater), and through capillary action (rising from soil).

**Rainfall:**

- Runs downslope; concentrates in low areas
- Soaks into permeable soil
- Runs off hard surfaces

**Groundwater:**

- Moves through soil toward lower elevations
- Accumulates above impermeable layers (clay, rock)
- Rises via capillary action (water is drawn upward through soil pores, especially in clay)

**Capillary action:**

- Water can rise 30 to 60 cm above the water table in fine-grained soil
- A foundation sitting in moist soil can draw water upward into the building
- This is why damp-proof barriers (between foundation and superstructure) are essential

**Site assessment:**

Before building:

1. Dig a test hole 1 to 2 meters deep near the proposed building site
2. Leave it overnight or after rain
3. If water collects in the hole, groundwater is present at that depth
4. Plan foundations to be 30 to 60 cm above the highest water table (seasonal high point)

### Step 2: Design Site Drainage

Proper site grading directs water away from the building.

**Grading principle: Slope the ground away from the building.**

**Procedure:**

1. **Establish the grade (slope):**
   - Ground should slope away from building at a minimum of 2 percent (2 cm drop per meter of distance)
   - Example: at 5 meters from the building, ground is 10 cm lower than ground at the building base
   - This ensures water does not collect against the foundation

2. **Create swales (drainage channels):**
   - Swales are shallow, broad channels that guide water away
   - Placed uphill of the building or along its sides
   - Slope: 2 to 5 percent (similar to ground grading)
   - Vegetation can line swales to slow water and improve filtration

3. **Install perimeter drainage:**
   - A gravel-filled trench dug around the building perimeter, 1 to 2 meters away
   - Filled with coarse gravel (drains quickly)
   - Perforated pipe or open channel along the bottom (directs water downslope)
   - This intercepts water before it reaches the foundation

4. **Manage roof runoff:**
   - Downspouts from gutters direct water to the drainage system
   - Direct water at least 2 to 3 meters away from the building (splash-back distance)
   - Lead water to a swale or perimeter drainage trench

**Example drainage layout:**

```
Building (raised slightly above surrounding ground)

Slope away from building:
    High point (at building)
         |  \  (slope 2%)
         |    \___
         |        \___
        5m          \___  (10 cm lower)

Swale or gravel trench intercepts water uphill.
Water flows downslope to drainage outlet.
```

### Step 3: Design and Build Damp-Proof Course (DPC)

A damp-proof course is a barrier between the foundation and the superstructure that prevents capillary water from rising into the building.

**DPC materials:**

1. **Stone or brick layer:**
   - A single course (layer) of stone or fired brick
   - Non-porous materials resist water penetration
   - Typically 10 to 15 cm above grade (ground level)

2. **Lime or clay mortar:**
   - Lime mortar is less permeable than clay mortar
   - Applied as a thick, sealed layer (2 to 3 cm)
   - Acts as a water barrier

3. **Bitumen or pitch (if available):**
   - Very effective waterproofing
   - Applied as a hot layer over stone
   - Lasts decades

**Installation procedure:**

1. **Build foundation:**
   - Construct stone or brick foundation to about 30 to 60 cm above grade (above seasonal water table and above splash-back level)
   - Foundation should be level and even

2. **Apply DPC layer:**
   - Option A (Fired brick): Lay a course of fired brick in lime mortar
   - Option B (Lime plaster): Apply a 3 to 5 cm thick layer of lime mortar, well-smoothed
   - Ensure the layer is continuous (no gaps; all edges sealed)

3. **Wait for curing:**
   - DPC must cure completely before building on top (2 to 4 weeks)
   - During this time, protect from rain

4. **Build superstructure:**
   - Once DPC is cured, build adobe, brick, or stone walls on top
   - The DPC prevents water from rising up the walls

**DPC placement:**

- Must be above the highest water table
- Must be above splash-back (roof runoff splashing on the base of the wall)
- Typically 30 to 60 cm above final grade
- Must be continuous (wraps around the entire building perimeter)

### Step 4: Seal Masonry Walls (Adobe, Stone, Brick)

Exposed masonry walls are porous and absorb water. Sealing protects them.

**Sealing options:**

**Option 1: Plaster coat (simplest)**

- Mud plaster: Clay, sand, and straw (see Vol 22, Ch 5: Adobe and Rammed Earth)
- Lime plaster: Lime, sand, and straw (more durable)
- Application: 2 to 3 cm thick coat, smoothed
- Frequency: Renewed every 10 to 20 years as it wears

**Option 2: Paint or lime wash (protective)**

- Lime wash: Slaked lime (from Vol 14, Ch 6: Clay and Limestone) mixed with water and pigment
- Applied annually or every few years
- Protects plaster and provides color
- Breathable (allows the wall to dry if moisture does penetrate)

**Option 3: Oil or natural finish (durable)**

- Linseed oil or tung oil soaked into wood or porous stone
- Water-resistant
- Lasts longer than plaster alone
- More labor-intensive to maintain

**Sealing procedure:**

1. Inspect wall: remove loose plaster or paint
2. Fill cracks (use plaster or mortar)
3. Apply plaster coat: 2 to 3 cm, smoothed smooth
4. Allow to cure (2 to 4 weeks)
5. Apply finish (lime wash, paint, or oil) once plaster is dry
6. Maintain: repaint/reapply finish annually; replaster every 10 to 20 years

### Step 5: Protect Roof and Prevent Leaks

The roof is the primary defense against water entry.

**Roof design principles:**

1. **Slope for drainage:**
   - Minimum slope: 2 percent (2 cm drop per meter of horizontal distance)
   - Steeper slopes (5 to 10 percent) drain faster; flatter roofs retain water
   - Water must not pool on the roof

2. **Material choice:**
   - Thatch: Excellent drainage if properly installed; slopes 4 to 7 percent
   - Tile: Excellent; slopes 3 to 5 percent (see Vol 15, Ch 4: Kiln Design)
   - Wood shingles: Good; slopes 3 to 5 percent
   - Flat roof (rare post-collapse): Requires waterproof membrane (bitumen, clay seal)

3. **Overhang for wall protection:**
   - Roof should extend 1 to 2 meters beyond walls
   - Prevents water from running down the exterior walls
   - Reduces water exposure to plaster and sealants

**Roof maintenance:**

- Inspect twice a year (spring and fall)
- Clear gutters of debris (leaves, moss)
- Repair damaged tiles, thatch, or shingles immediately
- Replace worn roofing material every 30 to 50 years (varies by material)

### Step 6: Install Gutters and Downspouts

Gutters and downspouts direct roof water away from the building.

**Gutter design:**

1. **Shape and slope:**
   - Gutters should slope toward downspouts (1 to 2 percent slope)
   - Shape: channel or half-round (collects water efficiently)
   - Material: wood, ceramic (see Vol 15: Ceramics for production), or metal

2. **Size:**
   - Based on roof area and rainfall intensity
   - Rough estimate: 1 cm of gutter width per 10 m² of roof area
   - Example: 100 m² roof → 10 cm wide gutter

3. **Installation:**
   - Attached to the roof edge with brackets or nails
   - Must be tight (no gaps where water leaks)
   - Slope ensures water flows to downspouts

**Downspouts:**

1. **Material:**
   - Ceramic (terra cotta) tubes: see Vol 15, Ch 7: Ceramic Applications
   - Wood: less durable (rots over time)
   - Metal: excellent but difficult to produce post-collapse

2. **Placement:**
   - Directs water from gutters to the ground
   - Should reach 2 to 3 meters away from the building
   - Can drain into a swale, perimeter drainage trench, or storm drain

3. **Maintenance:**
   - Clear downspout blockages (leaf debris)
   - Inspect for damage or separation

### Step 7: Control Interior Moisture

Even with perfect exterior protection, some moisture enters buildings. Managing interior moisture prevents mold and rot.

**Ventilation:**

- Bathrooms and kitchens: open windows or vents to expel moisture-laden air
- Whole building: cross-ventilation (windows on opposite sides) allows air circulation
- This prevents moisture from accumulating

**Moisture control:**

1. **Cooking and bathing:**
   - Produce moisture (steam)
   - Direct outside via windows or vents (see Vol 22, Ch 6: Passive Solar Design, stack ventilation)

2. **Thermal mass:**
   - Exposed adobe or stone walls absorb and release moisture
   - Helps moderate indoor humidity

3. **Avoid condensation:**
   - Condensation forms when warm, moist air meets cold surfaces
   - Prevented by ventilation and maintaining reasonable temperature gradients

### Step 8: Maintain Drains and Waterproofing

Ongoing maintenance extends the life of drainage and waterproofing systems.

**Annual inspection:**

- Check that site grades slope away from building (erosion may change slope)
- Verify gutters are clear and water flows freely
- Inspect downspouts for blockage
- Check plaster/sealants for cracks
- Look for signs of water entry (stains, mold, soft spots in walls)

**Repairs:**

- Replaster or reseal any cracks in plaster or sealant immediately (prevents water penetration)
- Re-grade areas that have eroded or settled
- Replace damaged or deteriorated roofing material
- Clean gutters quarterly or after storms

**Long-term maintenance:**

- Re-plaster exterior every 10 to 20 years (depending on climate and plaster type)
- Repaint or lime wash every 2 to 5 years
- Replace roofing material every 30 to 50 years
- Rebuild perimeter drainage if it clogs or fails

## Verification

**Success looks like:**

- Building stays dry in heavy rain and seasonal flooding
- No mold, mildew, or musty smells inside
- Plaster is sound; no cracks leaking water
- Roof is intact; no water stains on interior ceilings
- Exterior walls show no signs of rising damp (efflorescence, discoloration)

**Failure looks like:**

- Water enters during heavy rain or flooding
- Mold or mildew visible on interior walls or ceilings
- Plaster cracks and water seeps through
- Roof leaks (water stains, damp spots)
- Rising damp: white powder or discoloration on walls 30 to 60 cm above grade

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll build on low ground where water naturally collects"** — Low areas accumulate groundwater. Build on elevated ground; water flows downhill.

2. **"Damp-proof course is optional; plaster alone protects the building"** — DPC is essential. Capillary water rises through soil and masonry; DPC is the only barrier that stops this.

3. **"I don't need a damp-proof course if the foundation is above grade"** — Even 30 to 60 cm above grade is not high enough if the water table is close. Capillary action rises water further.

4. **"I'll skip gutters and downspouts to save time"** — Water running down exterior walls causes the most damage. Gutters are cheap relative to the protection they provide.

5. **"Flat roofs are fine; I'll just seal the surface"** — Flat roofs require perfect waterproofing and drain slowly. Sloped roofs drain naturally and are far more reliable.

6. **"One layer of plaster protects adobe forever"** — Plaster wears and cracks. Plan to replaster every 10 to 20 years and maintain it actively.

7. **"I'll grade the land toward the building to keep it wetter"** — This is backwards. Grade AWAY from the building; water should flee, not congregate.

8. **"Interior cracks don't matter if the exterior is sealed"** — Cracks allow capillary water and rain infiltration. Seal both interior and exterior.
