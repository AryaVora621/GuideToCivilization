## Overview

Timber-frame buildings have stood for centuries because the joints are strong and flexible. A well-made mortise-and-tenon joint (a tenon from one beam fits into a mortise or hole in another) is stronger than a bolted connection and works without metal. Traditional joinery uses hand tools—chisel, saw, drill, auger—and wood pegs to lock joints together.

This chapter teaches you how to make the five fundamental joints that hold timber-frame buildings together. By year 1, your settlement can be building permanent timber-frame structures that last centuries.

## Materials & Tools

**For joinery:**
- Timber (straight-grained hardwood preferred: oak, ash, maple)
- Hand tools:
  - Saw (hand saw, crosscut or ripsaw)
  - Chisels (1 cm to 5 cm widths) for cutting mortises
  - Mallet (wooden hammer) for striking chisels
  - Auger or hand-drill for holes and mortises
  - Square (carpenter's square for marking right angles)
  - Marking gauge (for consistent measurements)
  - Pencil or chalk for marking
- Wooden pegs or dowels (0.5 to 2 cm diameter) made from hardwood branches

**For assembly:**
- Clamps (to hold joints while glue sets; can be wooden wedges or rope tourniquets)
- Wood glue (hide glue, made from animal collagen; see Vol 5: Chemistry for production)
- Measuring tape or rule

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Master the Mortise-and-Tenon Joint

A mortise is a hole; a tenon is a tongue. The tenon fits into the mortise, creating a strong joint.

**Dimensions (for a typical frame joint):**

- **Tenon length:** 5 to 10 cm (extends through or nearly through the mortised piece)
- **Tenon width:** 5 to 8 cm (roughly 1/3 the width of the beam)
- **Tenon thickness:** 2 to 4 cm (roughly 1/2 the thickness of the beam)
- **Mortise:** matches the tenon's dimensions

**Cutting a tenon:**

1. **Mark the beam:**
   - Mark the shoulder (where the tenon begins) with a marking gauge set to the desired length from the end
   - Draw a line completely around the beam face
   - Mark the cheeks (sides) of the tenon with a marking gauge set to the tenon thickness

2. **Saw the tenon:**
   - Cut down along the marks with a saw, creating the sides of the tenon
   - Make four cuts: two for each cheek (both faces of the beam)
   - Saw should stop at the shoulder line
   - The waste wood falls away, leaving the tenon protruding

3. **Pare the tenon:**
   - Use a chisel to trim and square the tenon edges
   - Ensure the tenon is a snug fit in a test hole

**Cutting a mortise:**

1. **Mark the mortise:**
   - Mark the location on the receiving beam
   - Use a marking gauge to mark the exact edges of the mortise hole
   - Mortise should be centered on the beam thickness

2. **Drill the hole:**
   - Use an auger (hand drill) to bore holes within the mortise area
   - Drill holes about 2 to 3 cm apart, as deep as the mortise needs to be
   - The holes should be inside the mortise perimeter

3. **Chop out the waste:**
   - Use a chisel and mallet to chop out the remaining wood between the drilled holes
   - Work from both sides if possible (prevents splitting)
   - Pare the mortise smooth with careful chiseling
   - Test fit the tenon; adjust if needed

4. **Final fit:**
   - The tenon should fit snugly but not so tight it cannot be driven in
   - A slight tap with a mallet should fully seat the joint

**Assembly:**

1. Apply wood glue to the tenon and inside the mortise
2. Drive the tenon into the mortise with a mallet
3. Clamp or wedge to hold the joint while glue dries
4. Drill a hole through the joint and drive a wooden peg through to lock it permanently

### Step 2: Master the Lap Joint

A lap joint is simpler than a mortise-and-tenon; two beams overlap and are pegged together.

**Types of lap joints:**

**Half-lap (full-lap):**
- Two beams of equal thickness cross at right angles
- Each beam is cut halfway through its thickness
- The beams interlock; the joint is flush (no protrusion)
- Used for: crossing beams, roof trusses

**Cutting a half-lap:**

1. **Mark the overlap:**
   - Mark the area where the two beams overlap (typically 10 to 20 cm)
   - Mark the midline of the beam thickness with a marking gauge

2. **Saw the half-lap:**
   - Saw down along the edge of the overlap area to the midline
   - Then saw horizontally along the midline to remove the waste
   - Alternatively, use a chisel and mallet to chop out the waste

3. **Test fit:**
   - The two half-laps should interlock with a snug fit
   - Adjust if one half-lap is too thick or thin

4. **Assembly:**
   - Apply glue to both half-laps
   - Interlock the beams
   - Clamp or wedge to hold
   - Drill and peg through the joint to lock it

**Advantages of lap joints:**
- Simpler to make than mortise-and-tenon
- No protruding tenons (good for hidden joints)
- Adequate strength for most timber-frame applications

### Step 3: Master the Pegged Joint

Wooden pegs driven through a joint lock it permanently and increase strength.

**Peg design:**

- **Diameter:** 1 to 2 cm (sized to fit the hole drilled through the joint)
- **Length:** 5 to 8 cm (extends through the joint and slightly protrudes)
- **Material:** hardwood (oak, ash preferred; avoid soft wood that splinters)

**Making pegs:**

1. Split branches or thin logs lengthwise to create square dowels
2. Round the dowels slightly with a chisel or by hand (so they seat in round holes)
3. Cut to length with a saw
4. Test fit: should be slightly larger than the hole (will compress and wedge tightly)

**Driving pegs:**

1. **Drill the hole:**
   - After assembling the joint (mortise-and-tenon or lap), drill a hole through both pieces
   - Drill hole should be slightly smaller than the peg diameter

2. **Insert the peg:**
   - Apply a light coat of glue to the peg
   - Drive the peg through the hole with a mallet
   - Tap until it is fully seated and slightly protrudes through the other side

3. **Cut off excess:**
   - Saw the protruding peg flush with the surface (or leave it slightly proud for visual effect)
   - Sand smooth

### Step 4: Master the Dovetail Joint

Dovetails are complex but create extremely strong joints used for boxes and fine woodwork.

**Dovetail design:**

- Two beams interlock with alternating rectangular and tapered fingers
- The tapered shape locks the joint together mechanically (cannot pull apart)

**Cutting dovetails (simplified method):**

1. **Mark the dovetail pattern:**
   - Draw lines showing where the tails (protruding fingers) and pins (indentations) will be
   - Typical spacing: 3 to 5 cm wide for each tail and pin

2. **Saw the tails:**
   - Saw along the angled lines to create the tapered tails
   - Undercut slightly (lean inward) so the joint locks when driven together

3. **Use the tails as a template:**
   - Place the completed tail board on the pin board
   - Mark around the tails to outline the pins
   - Saw or chop out the pins to match

4. **Assemble:**
   - The tails should slide into the pins with a snug fit
   - Glue and clamp
   - Pegging is optional (glue is sufficient for dovetails)

### Step 5: Design a Timber-Frame Structure

Timber frames rely on properly designed joinery and good timber selection.

**Typical timber-frame building:**

```
Post-and-beam frame:
- Posts: 20 cm × 20 cm timbers, spaced 3 to 5 meters apart
- Beams: 15 cm × 30 cm timbers connecting posts
- Joinery: mortise-and-tenon at all connections

Example frame:
- Main posts at corners: 8m tall (double-story)
- Posts at 4m intervals along walls
- Horizontal beams (plates) across the top of posts
- Roof trusses (triangular frames) supported on beams
- Floor joists (smaller beams) for upper floors
```

**Critical joints:**

1. **Post-to-beam:** Mortise-and-tenon (post has mortise, beam has tenon)
2. **Beam-to-beam:** Lap joint or mortise-and-tenon (where beams cross or meet)
3. **Truss-to-beam:** Mortise-and-tenon or lap joint (roof trusses connect to main beams)
4. **All joints:** Locked with wooden pegs

### Step 6: Assemble a Timber Frame

Building a timber frame is a major undertaking involving multiple workers.

**Assembly process:**

1. **Build on the ground:**
   - Assemble frame sections on the ground to test fit all joints
   - Make adjustments before raising
   - Ensure all joints fit snugly

2. **Raise the frame:**
   - Use ropes and levers to lift completed sections
   - Raise one section at a time
   - Brace it with temporary props while connecting to the next section

3. **Connect sections:**
   - Glue and clamp joints as each new section is raised
   - Peg joints once glue is dry
   - Temporary props keep the frame stable

4. **Add roof and infill:**
   - Once main frame is up and braced, add roof trusses
   - Install secondary framing (rafters, floor joists)
   - Add infill (stone, brick, wattle-and-daub) between frame members

### Step 7: Finishing and Maintenance

After assembly, finish the frame to protect it from weather.

**Protection:**

1. **Seal exposed timber:**
   - Bark must be stripped (prevents beetle damage)
   - Apply a weather-resistant finish (lime wash, oil, or pitch)

2. **Provide good drainage:**
   - Ensure water runs off the frame
   - Keep the base of posts clear of standing water
   - A stone foundation keeps timber off wet ground

3. **Allow movement:**
   - Timber shrinks and swells with moisture
   - Pegged joints allow some movement without breaking
   - Monitor joints for checking (seasonal cracking) but this is normal

**Inspection:**

- Annually check for signs of decay or insect damage
- Replace damaged members promptly
- Well-maintained timber frames last 300+ years

## Verification

**Success looks like:**
- All joints fit snugly without gaps
- Pegs are driven and locked; joints do not shift
- Frame stands square and plumb (vertical, not leaning)
- No visible splitting or damage at joints

**Failure looks like:**
- Gaps in joints (poor fitting)
- Pegs pull out or joints shift under load
- Timbers split along the grain
- Frame racks (becomes parallelogram-shaped instead of square)

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll make tenons too long to save time"** — Over-long tenons split the receiving timber. Keep tenons under 10 cm even for large beams.

2. **"I'll glue joints without pegging"** — Hide glue loses strength over time. Always peg critical joints with wooden dowels.

3. **"I'll use any wood for joinery"** — Softwood is too weak; use hardwood (oak, ash). Avoid pine or fir for load-bearing joints.

4. **"I'll make mortises too wide to fit tenons loosely"** — Loose joints fail. Tenons should be tight; they compress into the mortise when driven.

5. **"I'll assemble the frame standing up"** — Test-fit on the ground first. Mistakes discovered standing up are expensive.

6. **"Pegs should be oak and very hard"** — Pegs should be slightly softer than the beam wood so they compress and lock. Ash or hickory is better than oak.

7. **"I'll use cut nails instead of wooden pegs"** — Iron nails rust and stain wood. Use wooden pegs; they last as long as the frame.

8. **"Dovetails are the strongest joint"** — Mortise-and-tenon is simpler and adequate for most frames. Reserve dovetails for fine joinery.
