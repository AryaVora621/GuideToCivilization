## Overview

Passive solar design harnesses the sun's energy to heat or cool buildings without mechanical systems, burning fuel, or active technology. A well-designed passive solar building stays warm in winter and cool in summer using only the sun, thermal mass (earth or stone), and natural ventilation.

Passive solar design is especially valuable post-collapse: it requires no ongoing fuel, requires no equipment maintenance, and works reliably for centuries. A properly designed adobe or stone building in a temperate climate can maintain comfortable temperatures year-round with minimal energy input.

This chapter teaches the principles: building orientation, window sizing, thermal mass placement, and ventilation strategies. By understanding these concepts, you can design buildings for your climate that naturally moderate temperature.

- Knowledge of your location's latitude (determines sun angles)
- Local climate data: average temperatures by season, prevailing winds, rainfall, cloud cover
- Building materials: high thermal mass (adobe, stone, water), insulation (straw, earth), glazing (glass or clear plastic)
- Designing tools: compass (for orientation), protractor or angle guide (for calculating solar angles)
- Knowledge of your building site: shading from nearby trees/hills, slope of land, soil

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Understand Sun Path and Solar Angles

The sun's position in the sky changes seasonally. Understanding this is the foundation of passive solar design.

**Key concepts:**

- **Winter:** Sun is low in the sky; rays strike at a shallow angle
- **Summer:** Sun is high in the sky; rays strike at a steep angle
- **Latitude:** Determines sun angles (higher latitudes have larger seasonal variation)
- **Solar noon:** The sun reaches its highest point; exact time depends on longitude and time zone

**Calculate solar angles for your latitude:**

For a given latitude (example: 40°N):

**Winter solstice (December 21):**
- Sun altitude: 90° - latitude + 23.5° = 90° - 40° + 23.5° = 73.5° - Wait, this is wrong. Let me recalculate.
- Sun altitude at noon: 90° - (latitude - 23.5°) = 90° - (40° - 23.5°) = 90° - 16.5° = 73.5° — This is still wrong.
- Correct formula: Altitude = 90° - latitude - declination
- Winter: Altitude = 90° - 40° - 23.5° = 26.5° (low in the sky)

**Summer solstice (June 21):**
- Summer: Altitude = 90° - 40° + 23.5° = 73.5° (high in the sky)

**Practical implication:**

At 40°N latitude:
- Winter: Sun is 26.5° above horizon at solar noon; rays come from the south at a steep downward angle
- Summer: Sun is 73.5° above horizon; rays come nearly straight down
- This means a south-facing window allows winter sun in (heating) but can shade summer sun (cooling)

**For your location:**

Use the formula or measure directly:
1. At solar noon (roughly 1 PM standard time), observe where the sun is in the sky
2. Measure the angle above the horizon (use a protractor and a stick, or visual estimation)
3. Note the direction (south, southeast, etc.)
4. Repeat on winter solstice and summer solstice to understand the range

### Step 2: Design Building Orientation

Correct orientation is the first and most important passive solar design decision.

**Primary rule: Face the sun, avoid afternoon heat.**

**Northern Hemisphere:**

- **Long axis of building:** Run east-west (so north and south faces are large)
- **South-facing wall:** This wall receives maximum winter sun and is the primary heating surface
- **North-facing wall:** This wall receives minimal direct sun; minimize windows here
- **East and west:** Minimize window area (morning and evening sun cause overheating; harder to control)

**Southern Hemisphere:**

- Reverse all directions: north-facing wall receives maximum winter sun

**Actual building placement:**

1. Face the building south (north in Southern Hemisphere) within about 15° of true south
2. Use a compass to find true south (magnetic south is offset; true south is direction toward the pole)
3. If trees or hills shade one side, orient to face the unshaded side
4. Consider prevailing winds for ventilation (see Step 5)

**Example building:**

```
North
 |
 +------ (north-facing wall — minimize windows)
 |
 | Building (50 m × 20 m)
 |
 +------ (south-facing wall — maximize south-facing windows in winter)
 |

East-West orientation means the long axis runs east-west.
North-south wall (perpendicular to sun's summer path) minimizes daily sun variation.
```

### Step 3: Size and Place Windows for Solar Gain

Windows are the mechanism of heat transfer. Proper sizing controls heating and cooling.

**Winter heating (maximize south-facing windows):**

1. **Calculate window area needed:**
   - Typical rule: south-facing window area should be 20 to 40 percent of the building's floor area
   - Example: 100 m² floor area → 20 to 40 m² of south-facing windows
   - This assumes thermal mass to absorb the heat (see Step 4)

2. **Place windows high on south wall:**
   - High windows allow winter sun to penetrate deep into the building
   - Winter sun is low; high windows receive it directly
   - Allows the sun to heat the interior thermal mass

3. **Minimize north, east, and west windows:**
   - North windows lose heat (no direct sun) and provide no heating
   - East and west windows cause afternoon overheating in summer
   - Limit to 10 to 15 percent of wall area (needed for light and ventilation)

**Summer cooling (block afternoon sun):**

1. **External shading devices:**
   - Overhangs: Block high summer sun while allowing low winter sun through
   - Calculate overhang depth based on solar angles (see Step 2)
   - Example at 40°N: Winter sun at 26.5°, summer sun at 73.5°
   - A 1-meter overhang at a south-facing window blocks 73.5° sun but allows 26.5° sun

2. **Louvered shutters or screens:**
   - On east and west windows, use adjustable shading
   - Close in morning (east) and afternoon (west) to block heat
   - Open at night for ventilation

3. **Internal heat control:**
   - Thick thermal mass (see Step 4) absorbs summer heat
   - At night, open windows to ventilate and cool the mass
   - Daytime, close windows to prevent outdoor heat from entering

### Step 4: Design Thermal Mass

Thermal mass absorbs heat during the day and releases it at night, moderating temperature swings.

**Materials with high thermal mass:**

- **Adobe or rammed earth:** Excellent; 30 to 50 cm thick walls store significant heat
- **Stone:** Excellent; thermal mass per meter is similar to adobe
- **Water:** Excellent; can store large amounts of heat in tanks or a pool
- **Masonry:** Good; fired bricks store less heat than adobe but are durable
- **Wood:** Poor; low thermal mass (valuable for structure, not heat storage)

**Thermal mass sizing:**

- Rule of thumb: 30 to 40 kg of thermal mass per square meter of south-facing window area
- Example: 30 m² of south-facing windows → need 900 to 1200 kg of thermal mass
- This can be 30 to 50 cm thick adobe walls (30 to 40 kg per m² of wall per cm of thickness)

**Placement of thermal mass:**

1. **On the south side of windows:**
   - Place thermal mass directly behind (inside) south-facing windows
   - Winter sun heats the mass directly
   - At night, the mass releases heat slowly, warming the interior

2. **On interior floors and walls:**
   - Interior thermal mass absorbs heat from south windows and releases it at night
   - Stone or tile floors are excellent; wooden floors are not

3. **In contact with conditioned space:**
   - The thermal mass must be in the air-conditioned room (exposed to indoor air)
   - If thermal mass is outside the heated space, it cannot help
   - Example: a massive south wall inside the building heats the interior; the same wall outside contributes nothing

### Step 5: Design Natural Ventilation for Cooling

In summer, natural ventilation removes heat and cools the building. This is called free cooling or convective cooling.

**Ventilation principle: Use temperature difference and wind to create airflow.**

**Stack ventilation (temperature-driven):**

1. **Cool air enters low; hot air exits high:**
   - Openings (windows, doors) near ground level allow cool air in
   - Openings high on the opposite wall allow hot air to exit
   - The temperature difference creates a draft (buoyancy)

2. **Design procedure:**
   - Put windows on the north side near the ground (receives cool shade)
   - Put exhaust openings on the south side high on the wall or on the roof
   - Height difference between inlets and outlets drives ventilation
   - Greater height difference = stronger draft

3. **Example:**
   - Ground-floor window (north side, ground level) is inlet
   - Roof vent (south side, peak of roof) is outlet
   - Height difference: 3 to 4 meters
   - Result: strong draft even without wind

**Wind-driven ventilation:**

1. **Use prevailing wind:**
   - Determine prevailing wind direction (typically from climate data or observation)
   - Design one set of windows to face the wind (receives wind pressure)
   - Design opposite side to open (allows wind to exit)

2. **Cross ventilation:**
   - Windows on opposite sides of the building
   - Wind enters one side, exits the other
   - Works best if building is narrow (no more than 15 m, so wind penetrates the full depth)

3. **Pressure difference:**
   - Windward side experiences positive pressure (wind pushes air in)
   - Leeward side experiences negative pressure (wind pulls air out)
   - This pressure difference drives ventilation

**Combined (stack + wind):**

- Most effective: combine stack and wind ventilation
- Day: open south-facing windows for ventilation (wind)
- Night: open north-facing windows to allow cool night air in and cool the thermal mass

### Step 6: Design for Your Climate

Passive solar design varies by climate. Tailor the design to your specific conditions.

**Hot-arid climate (desert, dry season):**

- Maximize thermal mass to moderate daily temperature swings
- Minimize window area (too much direct sun)
- Design for evaporative cooling (water features, damp cloths)
- Night ventilation is critical (remove daytime heat)
- Interior courtyards trap cool night air

**Temperate climate (moderate seasons):**

- Balance heating (winter) and cooling (summer)
- South-facing windows for winter heat
- Overhangs and shading for summer cooling
- Stack and wind ventilation for summer
- Thermal mass to smooth temperature swings

**Cold climate (harsh winters):**

- Maximize south-facing windows for heating
- Maximize thermal mass (stores winter sun heat)
- Minimize north, east, west windows (heat loss)
- Insulation is critical (thick walls, multiple glazing if available)
- Tight construction (minimal air leaks)

**Humid-tropical climate (warm year-round):**

- Cooling is the priority; heating is rarely needed
- Maximize cross ventilation (wind-driven ventilation is most effective)
- Minimize direct sun (shade overhangs, dense landscaping)
- Elevate building off ground (allows air circulation underneath)
- Large overhangs and deep shade all year

### Step 7: Build a Passive Solar Building

Integrating all the previous steps into actual construction.

**Design checklist:**

```
Orientation:
[ ] Building aligned north-south (within 15° of true south)
[ ] Site is unshaded by trees/hills (south-facing direction)
[ ] Prevailing winds considered in design

Windows:
[ ] South-facing windows: 20-40% of floor area
[ ] North, east, west windows: limited to 10-15%
[ ] Windows placed high on south wall for winter sun penetration
[ ] Overhangs calculated to shade summer sun but allow winter sun

Thermal Mass:
[ ] 30-40 kg/m² of thermal mass (30-50 cm thick adobe or stone walls)
[ ] Thermal mass inside (south side of windows, on interior surfaces)
[ ] Direct solar access to thermal mass (no shading)

Ventilation:
[ ] North-facing openings (inlet) near ground level
[ ] South-facing or roof openings (outlet) high on wall
[ ] Cross-ventilation pathway allows wind to enter and exit
[ ] Operable windows allow control (open night, close day in summer)

Insulation:
[ ] Walls thick enough to store heat
[ ] Roof insulated (prevents summer heat gain and winter heat loss)
[ ] Gaps sealed to minimize air leaks
```

**Construction steps:**

1. **Establish foundation and floor:**
   - Insulated from ground (prevents heat loss)
   - Floor material: stone, tile, or high-mass earth (thermal mass)

2. **Build walls:**
   - South wall: massive adobe or stone (30 to 50 cm thick)
   - North, east, west: adequate thickness for insulation and strength

3. **Install windows:**
   - South-facing: large, unobstructed by trees or buildings
   - Overhangs: sized for local sun angles
   - Other sides: small windows, high on wall

4. **Roof:**
   - Insulated or designed to reflect heat (light color in hot climates)
   - Overhangs extend 1 to 2 meters to shade walls

5. **Interior design:**
   - Exposed thermal mass (adobe/stone walls, stone floors)
   - Furniture and partitions allow heat to move around the interior
   - Living spaces on south side (receive heat)
   - Sleeping areas can be smaller or north-facing (cooler)

### Step 8: Monitor and Adjust

After construction, observe the building's performance and make adjustments.

**Winter heating:**

- Monitor indoor temperature
- If too cold, you may have insufficient thermal mass or windows
- If overheating at midday, add internal shading (curtains, shutters)

**Summer cooling:**

- Monitor peak temperatures
- If overheating, increase ventilation (open more windows at night)
- If cooling is slow, ensure thermal mass is exposed and overnight ventilation is working

**Seasonal adjustment:**

- Winter: close east/west windows, maximize south window exposure
- Summer: close south windows or use external shading; maximize ventilation

## Verification

**Success looks like:**

- Winter: building stays warm (18 to 20°C) even on cold nights, warmer during sunny days
- Summer: building stays cool (under 28°C) through night ventilation and thermal mass
- No active heating or cooling systems needed
- Seasonal comfort with only window and door adjustments

**Failure looks like:**

- Winter: building is cold throughout the day, even with south-facing windows
- Summer: building overheats during the day and stays hot at night
- Persistent need for auxiliary heating or cooling
- Lack of wind protection or uncontrolled heat loss

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll face the building east-west instead of north-south"** — This reduces thermal benefit. North-south orientation (long axis east-west) maximizes sun exposure and wind ventilation.

2. **"Large windows in every direction let in light and heat"** — Wrong; north and east/west windows lose heat (winter) and cause overheating (summer). Concentrate windows on the south.

3. **"I don't need thermal mass; windows and ventilation are enough"** — Thermal mass is essential for storing heat during the day and releasing it at night. Without it, temperatures swing wildly.

4. **"I'll build thick walls but then insulate the outside"** — This prevents the thermal mass from absorbing solar heat. Thermal mass must be inside (in contact with the interior).

5. **"Large overhangs block too much light in winter"** — Overhang depth should be calculated for your latitude to allow low winter sun through while blocking high summer sun. At 40°N, a 1-meter overhang works well.

6. **"Stack ventilation doesn't work; I need fans"** — Passive stack ventilation (temperature difference between high and low openings) is very effective and requires no power. Trust the physics.

7. **"I'll seal the building tight to keep heat in"** — In summer, you need to open windows for night cooling. Design for controllable ventilation, not constant sealing.

8. **"Passive solar design is complex; I'll just add more insulation and heating fuel"** — Passive solar design is elegant and simpler in practice; design principles are straightforward.
