## Overview

You look at the sky and see white, puffy clouds. A friend sees the same sky and says, "Storm coming." Who is right?

The friend is reading the clouds—their shape, color, altitude, and rate of change. This knowledge lets you predict weather before it arrives.

This chapter teaches:

1. **Cloud types:** Recognize the major cloud families
2. **What clouds reveal:** Atmospheric conditions indicated by each type
3. **Weather predictions:** What each cloud type suggests is coming
4. **Altitude observation:** How high clouds tell you about pressure and stability
5. **Practical forecasting:** Predict weather for the next 6–24 hours

Clouds are the atmosphere's visible signal. Learn to read them.

## Prerequisites

- Ability to observe the sky carefully
- Understanding of altitude (high, middle, low)
- Understanding of weather patterns from Chapter 1
- Willingness to observe patterns (some learning requires repeated observation)

## Materials & Tools

**For observation:**
- Eyes and clear sky
- Notebook (to record observations and patterns)
- No instruments needed (though a barometer helps)

**For prediction:**
- Understanding of local weather patterns (what direction do storms come from?)
- Historical knowledge of cloud-to-weather correlations in your region

## Part 1: Cloud Families

Clouds are classified into three altitude categories (families):

### High-Altitude Clouds (above 6 km / 20,000 ft)

**Characteristic:** Made of ice crystals (it is too cold for water droplets)

**Types:**

**Cirrus:**
- Appearance: Thin, wispy, feathery strands; isolated or in streaks
- Texture: Delicate, transparent (sun visible through them)
- Height: 5–13 km

**Meaning:** Ice crystals in the upper atmosphere. Often signals weather change within 24 hours (warm front approaching, bringing moisture aloft).

**Weather prediction:** If cirrus appears and thickens, rain likely within 6–12 hours.

**Cirrocumulus:**
- Appearance: Small, white puffs in regular patterns (like fish scales or a rippled surface)
- Height: 5–13 km

**Meaning:** Indicates instability aloft and moisture. Often precedes thunderstorms (but not immediately; usually 12+ hours).

**Saying:** "Mackerel sky, not 24 hours dry" (referring to the fish-scale pattern).

**Cirrostratus:**
- Appearance: Thin, uniform layer; sun is visible but diffuse (no sharp shadow); may produce halo around sun or moon
- Height: 5–13 km

**Meaning:** Indicates moisture aloft and lifting. Often precedes a warm front and rain within 12 hours.

### Middle-Altitude Clouds (2–6 km / 6,500–20,000 ft)

**Characteristic:** Mix of water droplets and ice crystals

**Types:**

**Altocumulus:**
- Appearance: White or gray puffs in layers or groups; larger than cirrocumulus
- Height: 2–6 km

**Meaning:** Convection at middle altitude. Can indicate developing instability.

**Weather prediction:** If altocumulus builds during afternoon, thunderstorms possible by evening.

**Altostratus:**
- Appearance: Uniform gray or blue-gray layer; sun is visible but outline is fuzzy (like looking through frosted glass)
- Height: 2–6 km

**Meaning:** Moisture at middle altitude; often precedes rain or snow.

**Weather prediction:** Rain likely within 6–12 hours.

**Nimbostratus:**
- Appearance: Dark, shapeless gray layer; blocks sun completely (no sun outline visible)
- Height: 2–6 km

**Meaning:** Thick clouds producing steady rain or snow.

**Weather prediction:** Precipitation happening or imminent. Rain will be continuous.

### Low-Altitude Clouds (below 2 km / 6,500 ft)

**Characteristic:** Made of water droplets

**Types:**

**Cumulus:**
- Appearance: Puffy, white clouds with flat base and rounded top; well-defined edges
- Height: 1–2 km

**Meaning:** Fair-weather convection. Formed by surface heating. Usually indicate fair weather.

**Weather prediction:** If cumulus clouds dissipate in evening, fair weather continues. If they grow taller and darken, thunderstorms may develop.

**Common saying:** "Fair-weather clouds."

**Cumulonimbus:**
- Appearance: Massive, towering clouds; anvil-shaped top (flattened by upper wind shear); dark base
- Height: 0.5–15 km (extends high into atmosphere)

**Meaning:** Strong convection and instability. Produces thunderstorms, heavy rain, hail, and tornadoes.

**Weather prediction:** Severe weather imminent. Seek shelter.

**Stratus:**
- Appearance: Uniform, featureless gray layer; like fog above ground
- Height: 0–2 km

**Meaning:** Low moisture and weak convection. Usually overcast but no precipitation.

**Weather prediction:** Overcast but dry. If stratus thickens, light rain or drizzle possible.

**Stratocumulus:**
- Appearance: Low layer of rounded masses or rolls; white and gray patches
- Height: 1–2 km

**Meaning:** Weak convection at low altitude. Often indicates fair weather or light precipitation.

**Weather prediction:** Partly cloudy. Usually fair.

## Part 2: What Clouds Reveal

### Altitude and Pressure

**High clouds (cirrus, cirrocumulus):** 
- Ice crystals form only where it is very cold (high altitude)
- Indicate moisture is rising high into the atmosphere
- Suggest changing pressure (warm air is rising, pushing colder air upward)

**Middle clouds (altocumulus, altostratus):**
- Indicate convection at middle altitude
- Suggest instability (rising air from below is strong enough to reach middle altitude)

**Low clouds (cumulus, stratus):**
- Most abundant
- Indicate convection is weak or lacking
- Fair-weather clouds if scattered; overcast if continuous

### Vertical Development

**Shallow cumulus:** Small, puffy clouds with little vertical extent. Weak convection. Fair weather.

**Towering cumulus:** Clouds with significant vertical extent, growing upward. Moderate convection. Possible showers by afternoon.

**Cumulonimbus:** Massive, towering clouds extending from low to high altitude. Strong convection. Severe weather.

**Observation:** Watch how clouds grow during the day. Growing clouds indicate building instability.

### Color

**White clouds:** Water droplets scatter all wavelengths equally. Indicate fair weather or moderate moisture.

**Dark clouds (gray or black base):** Thick clouds with little light penetration. Indicate heavy moisture and precipitation.

**Note:** The darkening is relative to the background. A cloud appears darker against bright blue sky, lighter against overcast.

## Part 3: Cloud Sequences and Weather Prediction

### Sequence 1: Approaching Warm Front (Rain Within 12 Hours)

**Timeline:**

**Evening before:** Clear sky, fair weather. Temperature moderate.

**Morning:** Cirrus appears (thin, wispy). Temperature still moderate.

**Late morning:** Cirrostratus layer develops. Sun visible but diffuse. Temperature rising.

**Afternoon:** Altostratus layer moves in. Sky darkens. Wind picks up from southeast (in Northern Hemisphere).

**Evening:** Stratus or nimbostratus. Light rain begins. Temperature warm.

**Next morning:** Rain continues. Temperature warm.

**Interpretation:** Warm, moist air is moving in from the south. High clouds signal the front's approach 12+ hours in advance.

### Sequence 2: Approaching Cold Front (Thunderstorms Possible)

**Timeline:**

**Afternoon:** Warm, humid. Cumulus clouds grow taller (towering cumulus).

**Late afternoon:** Cumulus clouds grow into cumulonimbus. Sky darkens. Wind picks up.

**Early evening:** Thunderstorm arrives. Heavy rain, lightning, hail possible. Temperature drops.

**Night:** Cold front passes. Clear, cool. Pressure rising. Winds from northwest.

**Interpretation:** Cold front moves fast. High clouds appear briefly or not at all. Towering cumulus signals imminent severe weather.

### Sequence 3: Fair Weather Continues

**Timeline:**

**All day:** Scattered cumulus clouds. Flat base, puffy top. Cloud size stable (not growing).

**Evening:** Clouds dissipate as surface heating stops. Sky clears. Temperature drops gradually.

**Night:** Clear sky. Radiative cooling (surface loses heat to space).

**Interpretation:** Stable air. No approaching weather system. Fair weather continues.

## Part 4: Regional Cloud Patterns

Cloud patterns vary by geography. Learn your local patterns.

### Coastal Regions

**Daytime:** Sea breeze brings ocean air onshore. Cumulus clouds form over land (land heats faster than water).

**Nighttime:** Land cools; land breeze flows seaward. Stratus forms over cool water.

**Pattern:** Cumulus during day, clear at night (if no weather system is passing).

### Mountain Regions

**Windward side (facing wind):** Air is forced upward. Clouds form and thicken. Rain or snow likely.

**Leeward side (downwind): Air descends. Clouds evaporate. Dry, clear weather.

**Pattern:** Rain or snow on windward side; dry on leeward side (regardless of time of day).

### Desert Regions

**Few clouds year-round.** When clouds appear, they are usually high (cirrus) and do not produce rain (dry air evaporates moisture before it reaches ground).

**Pattern:** Clear skies, dramatic temperature swings (hot day, cold night without cloud cover).

## Part 5: Worked Examples

### Example 1: Predicting a Thunderstorm

**Observation (morning):** Clear sky, warm, humid.

**Observation (mid-afternoon):** Cumulus clouds appearing, growing taller. Sky brightness decreasing.

**Observation (late afternoon):** Cumulus clouds become cumulonimbus. Dark gray base. Anvil shape visible. Wind strengthens.

**Prediction:** Severe thunderstorm within 1–2 hours. Seek shelter, secure loose objects.

**Outcome:** Thunderstorm arrives at 6 PM. Heavy rain, lightning, wind. Lasts 30 minutes. Temperature drops 10°C.

**Lesson:** Cumulonimbus development is rapid. Once you observe towering clouds, severe weather is imminent.

### Example 2: Reading a Changing Sky

**Morning:** Clear. Visibility excellent. Fair weather.

**Noon:** Cirrus clouds appear overhead (thin strands). Visibility still good.

**Afternoon:** Cirrostratus layer thickens. Sun becomes diffuse (halo visible). Wind from south. Temperature rising.

**Prediction:** Warm front approaching. Rain within 12 hours. Prepare for rain tonight or tomorrow morning.

**Outcome:** Light rain starts at midnight. Continues through morning.

**Lesson:** High clouds signal approaching systems. Change happens gradually (12–24 hours), giving time to prepare.

### Example 3: Stable Fair Weather

**All day:** Scattered cumulus clouds. Cloud size stable or shrinking in evening. Clear between clouds.

**Color:** Clouds white, not gray.

**Wind:** Light and variable.

**Pressure:** Stable or rising slowly.

**Prediction:** Fair weather continues. No precipitation expected. Safe to travel or work outdoors.

**Outcome:** Fair weather continues for 2–3 days.

**Lesson:** Scattered, stable cumulus indicates fair weather. Change takes days to develop.

## Part 6: Developing Observational Skill

### Daily Observation Practice

1. **Morning:** Observe cloud types, altitude, color, organization. Record in notebook.
2. **Midday:** Repeat observation. Note changes.
3. **Evening:** Repeat observation. Predict tomorrow's weather.
4. **Next morning:** Check if prediction was correct.

### Building a Personal Data Set

After weeks of observation, patterns emerge:
- Which cloud types lead to rain in your region?
- How much time between cloud appearance and weather change?
- Which direction do weather systems approach from?
- What is the typical seasonal pattern?

Local knowledge is more useful than general rules.

### Cloud Photography

If possible, photograph clouds (same location, same time daily) to track changes. Over weeks, you will see patterns.

## Part 7: Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Confusing Cloud Types

Cumulus and cumulonimbus both have puffy tops. Difference: Cumulonimbus is much larger (extends high into atmosphere) and has a dark base. Study examples until you can distinguish them.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Wind Direction

Wind direction reveals where air is coming from, which reveals weather approaching. Southeast wind usually brings warm, moist air. Northwest wind brings cool, dry air. Pay attention.

### Mistake 3: Assuming Red Sky Always Means Fair Weather

"Red sky at night, sailor's delight" is true in many regions but not universal. Red sky means moisture in air to the west. In your region, does this lead to fair weather or rain? Observe your patterns.

### Mistake 4: Not Accounting for Season

Summer afternoon convection is common. Winter high clouds might be more significant. Seasonal context matters.

### Mistake 5: Predicting Too Far in Advance

Accurate prediction is possible 6–12 hours out. Beyond 24 hours, accuracy drops. Cirrus clouds 24 hours before rain is useful, but not certain.

## Verification

You understand cloud identification when:

1. You can identify cumulus, cumulonimbus, stratus, and altostratus clouds
2. You understand what each cloud type indicates about atmospheric conditions
3. You can predict weather 6–12 hours in advance by observing clouds
4. You recognize the cloud sequence preceding a warm or cold front
5. You know how to adjust predictions for your local geography and season
6. You understand why high clouds often precede weather changes
