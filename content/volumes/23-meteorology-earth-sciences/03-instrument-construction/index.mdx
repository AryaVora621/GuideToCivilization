## Overview

Weather forecasting begins with measurement. Before you can predict whether a storm is coming, you must be able to track temperature, air pressure, humidity, wind, and rainfall. Each of these is measured by a specific instrument, and remarkably, every one of these instruments can be built from simple materials: glass, water, metal, and careful observation. The thermometer, barometer, hygrometer, wind vane, anemometer, and rain gauge together form a basic weather station, and from their readings — especially the trend in air pressure — you can forecast the weather a day or two ahead.

For a rebuilding civilization, weather instruments are valuable far beyond curiosity. Knowing a storm is approaching protects crops, ships, and lives. Tracking temperature and rainfall guides planting and harvest. Recording pressure trends gives early warning of dangerous weather. The instruments are not difficult to make, but they must be built and calibrated carefully, because a wrong reading is worse than none.

This chapter explains how each core instrument works and how to construct, calibrate, and read it.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding that air has weight and pressure, and that warm air holds more moisture (Vol 26)
- Basic ability to work glass, metal, and wood (Vol 10)
- Access to a sealed glass tube, a liquid (water, alcohol, or mercury if available), and simple hand tools
- Patience for calibration

## Materials & Tools

**Common materials:**

- Glass tubing (ideally thin-bore for thermometers and barometers)
- A liquid: colored water or alcohol for thermometers, mercury if you have it for barometers
- A sealed reservoir or bulb
- Wood and metal for frames, vanes, and mounts
- A scale marked in consistent units
- A reference for calibration (freezing and boiling water; a known good instrument if available)

## Part 1: The Thermometer (Temperature)

### How It Works

Liquids expand when heated and contract when cooled. A thermometer traps a liquid in a bulb connected to a thin tube; as temperature rises, the liquid expands and climbs the tube. The narrow bore magnifies the small volume change into a visible movement.

### Construction

1. Take a small glass bulb (a reservoir) joined to a long, thin-bore glass tube.
2. Fill the bulb and part of the tube with colored alcohol (alcohol works over a wide range and does not freeze easily) or colored water for mild climates.
3. Seal the top, leaving the liquid free to rise and fall in the bore.
4. Mount it against a board where you will mark the scale.

### Calibration

Use two fixed points everyone can reproduce:

- Place the bulb in melting ice (a mix of ice and water): mark the liquid level. This is 0 degrees Celsius (the freezing point of water).
- Place the bulb in boiling water at sea level: mark that level. This is 100 degrees Celsius.
- Divide the distance between the two marks into 100 equal parts. Each part is one degree.

This two-point calibration is the heart of all thermometry. Always recheck against melting ice periodically.

## Part 2: The Barometer (Air Pressure)

The barometer is the single most important forecasting instrument, because falling pressure warns of approaching storms and rising pressure signals fair weather.

### How It Works

Air has weight and presses down on everything. A barometer balances that air pressure against a column of liquid. When air pressure rises, it pushes the liquid higher; when pressure falls, the liquid drops. The trend matters more than the absolute reading.

### The Mercury Barometer

If you have mercury:

1. Fill a glass tube, closed at one end and about a meter long, completely with mercury.
2. Invert it into a small open dish of mercury, keeping the open end submerged.
3. The mercury falls until the column's weight balances the air pressure, leaving a vacuum at the top. At sea level the column stabilizes near 760 millimeters.
4. Mark a scale alongside the tube and record the level daily.

### The Water / Aneroid Alternative

Without mercury, a water barometer works on the same principle but needs a much taller column (water is far lighter than mercury, so the column is over 10 meters — impractical for most). A more practical fuel-less option is a simple "storm glass" or a sealed-chamber (aneroid) design: a sealed, partly evacuated metal chamber flexes as pressure changes, and a lever amplifies the motion onto a dial. The aneroid is harder to build but far more portable.

### Reading for Forecasts

- **Steady high pressure:** fair, stable weather.
- **Falling pressure:** a storm or rain is likely approaching; the faster the fall, the stronger the system.
- **Rising pressure:** improving, clearing weather.

Record pressure at the same times each day and watch the trend.

## Part 3: The Hygrometer (Humidity)

Humidity — the amount of water vapor in the air — affects comfort, drying, and the chance of rain or fog.

### The Hair Hygrometer

Human or animal hair lengthens slightly as humidity rises and shortens as it falls. Stretch a clean hair between a fixed point and a lightweight lever or pointer; as the hair changes length, the pointer moves across a scale. Calibrate the extremes against very dry and very humid conditions.

### The Wet-and-Dry-Bulb Hygrometer (Psychrometer)

More accurate and easy to build: mount two identical thermometers side by side. Wrap the bulb of one in a wet cloth wick dipped in water. Evaporation cools the wet bulb; the drier the air, the faster the evaporation and the bigger the temperature gap between the two thermometers. The difference between the wet and dry readings tells you the humidity (read from a reference table). When the air is saturated (about to produce dew or rain), both read the same.

## Part 4: Wind Instruments

### The Wind Vane (Direction)

A wind vane is a balanced pointer that pivots freely on a vertical axis. Make one arm catch the wind (a broad tail) and the other a narrow point; the wind pushes the broad end downwind, so the point indicates the direction the wind is coming from. Mount it high and clear of obstructions, with fixed markers for the compass directions below it. Wind direction is a strong clue to coming weather: a shift in wind often precedes a change in conditions.

### The Anemometer (Speed)

The classic design uses cups (often three or four) mounted on arms that spin around a vertical axis. Wind catches the cups and spins the assembly; the faster the wind, the faster the spin. Count the rotations over a fixed time to gauge speed, and calibrate by comparing against known wind speeds (for example, by walking or riding at a known pace in still air). Even a simple swinging-plate design — a hinged flap that the wind lifts against a scale — gives a usable estimate.

## Part 5: The Rain Gauge (Precipitation)

The simplest accurate instrument. A rain gauge is just a straight-sided container that collects rain, with a measured scale for depth.

1. Use a cylinder with vertical sides (so depth is uniform regardless of how full it is).
2. Place it in the open, away from buildings and trees that block or funnel rain, with the rim well above the ground to avoid splash.
3. After rain, measure the depth of water collected — this is the rainfall in millimeters.
4. For more precision, use a wide funnel feeding a narrow measuring tube, which magnifies small amounts for easier reading.

Empty and record after each event, and keep a running log.

## Part 6: Building a Weather Station

Mount your instruments together in a sensible arrangement:

- Thermometers and the hygrometer go in a shaded, ventilated screen (a louvered box) so the sun does not heat them directly — you want the air temperature, not the sun's.
- The barometer stays indoors in a stable spot, read at fixed times.
- The wind vane and anemometer go high and exposed.
- The rain gauge sits in the open, clear of obstructions.

Read all instruments at the same times each day (morning and evening at minimum), and write every reading in a log. The log is what turns isolated measurements into forecasting power, because trends — not single readings — predict the weather.

## Worked Example: A First Forecast

Over three days you record:

- Day 1 morning: pressure steady high, wind from the west, clear.
- Day 2 morning: pressure has fallen noticeably, wind has shifted, high thin clouds appearing.
- Day 2 evening: pressure falling faster, humidity rising (wet and dry bulbs converging).

The falling pressure, shifting wind, and rising humidity together strongly suggest an approaching storm. You warn the community to secure crops and shelter. By Day 3, rain arrives — your instruments gave a full day's warning. This is the practical payoff of a weather station.

## Verification Criteria

- **The thermometer tracks known points.** It reads 0 in melting ice and 100 in boiling water (at sea level); intermediate readings change smoothly with temperature.
- **The barometer responds to weather.** Its level falls before storms and rises in fair weather, tracking real changes rather than sitting still.
- **The hygrometer distinguishes dry from humid air.** The wet/dry gap widens in dry air and narrows toward zero when saturated.
- **Wind instruments respond correctly.** The vane points into the wind; the anemometer spins faster in stronger wind.
- **The rain gauge gives consistent depths.** Two gauges side by side should read nearly the same after the same rain.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thermometer in direct sun.** Measuring sun-heated glass gives a false high reading. Shade and ventilate temperature instruments.
- **Skipping calibration.** An uncalibrated instrument gives numbers without meaning. Always fix at least two known points.
- **Reading absolute pressure instead of the trend.** A single barometer reading tells little; the direction and speed of change is what forecasts weather.
- **Rain gauge poorly sited.** Placed under trees or beside buildings, it under- or over-collects. Site it in the open.
- **Irregular reading times.** Comparing readings taken at random times hides trends. Read at fixed times daily.
- **Tapered rain gauge.** A container with sloped sides gives wrong depths. Use straight vertical sides.

A handful of simple instruments, carefully built and faithfully read, transform the weather from a mystery into a readable pattern. Build your thermometer, barometer, and hygrometer first; add wind and rain instruments as you can; and above all, keep a daily log. The trends in those numbers are what let you see weather coming before it arrives.
