## Overview

A glaze is a glass-like coating on pottery that serves two purposes: waterproofing (so pots hold water without seeping) and decoration (color, shine, surface texture). Without glaze, a fired pot is porous—water slowly seeps through the clay walls. Glaze seals the surface, making the pot functional for storage and cooking.

Glazes are made from minerals: silica (glass-forming), alumina (stabilizer), and flux (melting agent). These melt together at high temperature, forming glass. Your settlement can make serviceable glazes from wood ash (flux source), clay or feldspar (alumina), and sand (silica). This chapter teaches you the chemistry, how to formulate glazes, and how to apply them to pots.

## Materials & Tools

**For glaze materials:**
- Silica source: sand (SiO₂) or quartz pebbles
- Alumina source: clay, feldspar (K-feldspar, soda feldspar), or pure alumina
- Flux source: wood ash (potassium carbonate), soda ash (sodium carbonate), or lime (calcium carbonate)
- Additional minerals (optional): iron oxide (red/brown color), copper oxide (green/blue color), cobalt oxide (blue color), manganese oxide (brown color)

**For glaze preparation:**
- Mortar and pestle (or stone tools) for grinding minerals
- Containers: clay pots, wooden bowls
- Measuring tools: cups, spoons (consistency matters more than precision)
- Sieve or fine screen (for milling glaze to fine powder)
- Water supply
- Brushes or sponges (for applying glaze)
- Spray equipment (optional; for uniform glaze application)

**For testing:**
- Test kiln or access to production kiln
- Test tiles (small clay pieces to test glaze color/texture before applying to pots)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Understand Glaze Chemistry (The Science)

A glaze is a mixture of three components that melt together at high temperature, forming glass.

**Silica (SiO₂) — the glass-former:**
- **Role:** Forms glass when melted
- **Source:** Sand, quartz pebbles, flint, crushed quartz rock
- **Melting point (alone):** 1710°C (too high to use without flux)
- **In glazes:** Makes up 50 to 70 percent of glaze by weight

**Alumina (Al₂O₃) — the stabilizer:**
- **Role:** Stiffens the glass, prevents flowing; raises melting point
- **Source:** Clay (contains 10 to 40 percent alumina), feldspar (contains 15 to 20 percent alumina)
- **Melting point (alone):** 2050°C (very high)
- **In glazes:** Makes up 10 to 30 percent of glaze by weight

**Flux — the melting agent:**
- **Role:** Lowers the melting point of silica so it melts at kiln temperature (900 to 1200°C)
- **Source:** Wood ash (potassium carbonate, potassium oxide), soda ash, lime
- **Melting point (alone):** Varies; fluxes melt at 600 to 1000°C
- **In glazes:** Makes up 5 to 30 percent of glaze by weight

**The chemistry:**
When heated together in the kiln:
1. Flux melts first (lowest melting point)
2. Molten flux dissolves silica
3. Alumina (dissolved in the melt) stiffens it
4. Above the melting point, the mixture flows like glass
5. Below the melting point, it solidifies as a glassy coating

**Why wood ash works as flux:**
- Wood ash contains potassium carbonate (K₂CO₃) and calcium carbonate (CaCO₃)
- When heated, carbonates decompose, releasing CO₂ gas and leaving potassium oxide (K₂O) and calcium oxide (CaO)
- These oxides are powerful fluxes, melting silica at 900 to 1100°C

### Step 2: Formulate a Simple Ash Glaze

The simplest post-collapse glaze uses three ingredients: wood ash (flux), clay (alumina), and sand (silica).

**Basic ash glaze recipe:**

```
Wood ash:        50 percent (flux source)
Clay:            30 percent (alumina source)
Sand:            20 percent (silica source)
```

**Scaling for production:**
- 5 kg wood ash + 3 kg clay + 2 kg sand = 10 kg glaze (sufficient for 50 to 100 medium pots)

**Variations (for different kiln temperatures):**

**For lower-temperature kilns (900 to 950°C) — add more flux:**
```
Wood ash:        60 percent
Clay:            25 percent
Sand:            15 percent
```

**For higher-temperature kilns (1050 to 1150°C) — reduce flux:**
```
Wood ash:        40 percent
Clay:            35 percent
Sand:            25 percent
```

**For stoneware (1150 to 1250°C) — minimal flux:**
```
Wood ash:        30 percent
Feldspar:        30 percent
Clay:            20 percent
Sand:            20 percent
```

### Step 3: Collect and Prepare Glaze Materials

**Collecting wood ash:**

1. Collect ash from wood fires (not charcoal, not coal ash)
2. Sieve to remove large charcoal pieces and debris
3. Store dry (wet ash loses potassium to leaching)
4. Yield: a typical cooking fire produces 0.5 to 1 kg ash per day

**Preparing sand and clay:**

1. **Sand:** Use clean, fine sand (beach sand or riverbed sand, free of salt)
   - Rinse in water to remove salt and dust
   - Dry completely
2. **Clay:** Use potter's clay (from Vol 15, Ch 1) or any dense clay
   - Dry completely
   - Pound into powder (mortar and pestle, or stone on stone)

**Preparing feldspar (if available):**

1. Feldspar is a mineral found in granite
2. Crush granite or feldspar-rich rock to powder
3. Sieve to a fine powder
4. More powerful flux than clay; use in higher-fire glazes

### Step 4: Mill the Glaze to Fine Powder

Glaze must be ground to a fine, uniform powder for even melting.

**Milling procedure:**

1. **Mix the ingredients:**
   - Combine wood ash, clay, and sand in the correct proportions
   - Stir thoroughly to distribute evenly
   - Result: dry mixture ready for water

2. **Wet-mill (optional but improves results):**
   - Add water to the dry glaze mixture (roughly equal volume)
   - Grind with mortar and pestle or stone tools for 20 to 30 minutes
   - Goal: eliminate lumps, create uniform paste
   - Pass through a sieve to remove any remaining grit
   - Result: smooth glaze slip

3. **Dry-mill (faster, simpler):**
   - Grind the dry mixture with stone tools until powder-fine
   - Sieve to remove any grit
   - Store dry until use

### Step 5: Apply Glaze to Pots

Glaze must be applied evenly and thin (under 1 mm thickness) to fire properly.

**Application methods:**

**Method 1: Dipping (most reliable)**

1. **Prepare glaze slip:** Add water to dry glaze powder until it resembles milk (thick liquid)
2. **Dip the pot:** Quickly submerge the pot in glaze slip for 1 to 3 seconds
3. **Remove and drain:** Lift out and allow excess to drip off
4. **Inspect:** Glaze should coat evenly, no drips or bare spots
5. **Dry:** Set on kiln shelf to dry completely before firing

**Method 2: Brushing**

1. **Prepare glaze slip:** Same as dipping
2. **Brush onto pot:** Use a natural-hair brush to apply glaze
3. **Multiple coats:** Apply two to three thin coats, allowing drying between coats
4. **Smooth:** Use a damp sponge to even out brush marks
5. **Dry:** Fully dry before firing

**Method 3: Spraying (if you have a spray device)**

1. **Thin glaze slip:** Adjust water ratio until it sprays evenly
2. **Spray evenly:** Multiple light passes create even coverage
3. **Dry:** Fully dry before firing

**Glaze thickness (critical):**
- Too thin (under 0.5 mm): glaze is pale, incomplete coverage
- Optimal (0.5 to 1 mm): rich color, smooth finish
- Too thick (over 1.5 mm): glaze runs, drips, pools (especially near the rim)

### Step 6: Color Glazes with Oxides

Plain ash glazes are gray or tan. Add metal oxides for color.

**Metal oxides and their effects (percentages by weight of total glaze):**

**Iron oxide (Fe₂O₃) — reds, browns, blacks:**
- 5 to 10 percent: red-brown
- 10 to 15 percent: dark brown
- Over 15 percent: almost black
- Effect: darkens glaze; runs at high temperatures

**Copper oxide (CuO) — greens, turquoises:**
- 3 to 8 percent: pale green
- 8 to 12 percent: blue-green
- Over 12 percent: dark blue-green
- Effect: toxic fumes when fired (ventilate kiln); very powerful

**Cobalt oxide (CoO) — deep blues:**
- 1 to 3 percent: light blue
- 3 to 5 percent: medium blue
- Over 5 percent: very dark blue
- Effect: extremely powerful; start with under 2 percent

**Manganese oxide (MnO₂) — browns, blacks:**
- 5 to 10 percent: brown
- Over 10 percent: nearly black
- Effect: good for stoneware; less common than iron

**Rutile (TiO₂) — yellows, creams:**
- 10 to 20 percent: cream to pale yellow
- Effect: opacifies glaze (makes it less shiny)

**Adding color:**

1. Add oxide powder to your base glaze formula
2. Example: Base ash glaze (10 kg) + iron oxide (0.5 kg = 5 percent) = rusty-brown glaze
3. Mix thoroughly (uneven color will result in mottled pots)
4. Test on a test tile before applying to finished pots

### Step 7: Fire Glazed Pots

Glazed pots fire at the same temperature as unglazed pottery, but glaze changes dramatically.

**Firing considerations:**

1. **Glaze melting point:** Most ash glazes melt between 1000 and 1150°C
2. **Temperature is critical:** Under-fired = glaze remains dull, chalky, not waterproof
3. **Over-fired:** Glaze runs, flows, may damage kiln shelf
4. **Oxidation:** Glazes are affected by kiln atmosphere (oxygen-rich or reduction)
   - **Oxidation (oxygen-rich):** Colors are bright (reds, blues, greens)
   - **Reduction (oxygen-poor):** Colors shift (copper becomes red, iron becomes dark)

**Loading glazed pots in kiln:**

1. **Wipe the base:** Remove glaze from the pot's foot (bottom) so it does not stick to the kiln shelf
2. **Space pots:** Leave at least 2 cm between pots; glazed surfaces must not touch
3. **Stack carefully:** If stacking, place clay stilts between layers
4. **Position:** Place glazed pots on kiln shelf with the glaze visible (heat must reach glaze to melt it)

**Kiln shelf protection:**

- If glaze runs (over-fired), it sticks to the shelf
- Prevent this by:
  - Coating the shelf with kiln wash (clay and sand slurry)
  - Checking glaze thickness before firing (prevent thick glaze near rims)
  - Testing glaze on a sacrificial test tile first

### Step 8: Test Glazes Before Production

Never glaze all your pots with a new glaze formula. Always test.

**Testing procedure:**

1. **Make test tiles:** Small ceramic tiles (5 cm by 5 cm by 1 cm thick)
2. **Apply test glaze:** Coat test tiles with the new glaze formula
3. **Fire in production kiln:** Load test tiles alongside regular pots
4. **Evaluate results:**
   - Color (expected or off?)
   - Texture (smooth, matte, crystalline?)
   - Durability (water-resistant?)
   - Runoff (did glaze flow too much?)
5. **Adjust formula if needed:** Add more flux if under-fired, reduce flux if over-fired
6. **Retest:** Fire another batch of test tiles with the adjusted formula
7. **Once satisfied:** Glaze production pots

### Step 9: Troubleshoot Glaze Failures

**Problem: Glaze is chalky, matte, not shiny**

- Cause: Under-fired (temperature below glaze melting point)
- Solution: Increase kiln temperature 25 to 50°C in next firing, or add more flux to glaze

**Problem: Glaze runs, flows, sticks to shelf**

- Cause: Over-fired (temperature above glaze melting point) or too much glaze applied
- Solution: Reduce kiln temperature or apply thinner glaze coats

**Problem: Glaze is uneven (thin in spots, thick in others)**

- Cause: Uneven application
- Solution: Use dipping (most even) instead of brushing; apply two coats if brushing

**Problem: Glaze has tiny bubbles or pinhole texture**

- Cause: Air trapped in glaze; glaze is releasing gases during firing
- Solution: Fire more slowly (slower heating allows gases to escape); add a small amount of clay to the glaze (stabilizes the melt)

**Problem: Glazed pot is still porous (leaks water)**

- Cause: Glaze melted incompletely; not waterproof
- Solution: Same as chalky glaze—increase temperature or adjust flux

## Verification

**Success looks like:**
- By week 2: You have tested a simple ash glaze on test tiles and achieved a shiny, waterproof surface
- By week 4: You have applied glaze to production pots and fired them
- By month 2: 80 percent or more of glazed pots are waterproof and durable

**Failure looks like:**
- Glaze remains chalky and porous after firing
- Glaze runs and sticks to kiln shelf
- Glazed pots are visually appealing but leak water (not truly waterproof)

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll use coal ash instead of wood ash"** — Coal ash lacks the potassium oxide needed as flux. Use only wood ash.

2. **"I'll add lots of oxide color (over 20 percent)"** — Too much oxide overpowers the glaze, makes it muddy or stiff. Stay under 15 percent for most oxides.

3. **"I'll glaze the bottom of the pot"** — Glaze on the bottom sticks to the kiln shelf. Always wipe glaze off the foot.

4. **"I'll apply thick glaze (over 2 mm) for richer color"** — Thick glaze runs and pools at the bottom of the pot. Apply thin, even coats under 1 mm.

5. **"Ash glaze is done; I don't need to test it"** — Even minor changes (ash source, wood type, kiln temperature) affect results. Always test.

6. **"I'll use salt ash (from salt-water evaporation)"** — Salt interferes with glaze chemistry. Use only wood ash from burned wood.

7. **"Glaze is too runny; I'll add sand to thicken it"** — Sand does not dissolve in water; it settles. Instead, reduce water or add clay to thicken.

8. **"Copper glaze is beautiful; I'll fire every pot with it"** — Copper oxide releases toxic fumes. Limit use; ensure kiln is well-ventilated.
