## Overview

Beyond tableware and storage pots, ceramics serve critical infrastructure purposes: water pipes distribute water from springs to settlements, crucibles allow metalworking by containing molten metal, and refractory bricks line furnaces and kilns to withstand extreme heat. These specialized applications require different clay bodies, firing temperatures, and forming techniques than ordinary pottery.

This chapter teaches you how to produce three ceramic products essential for advancing civilization. By year 3, your settlement can be producing its own water-pipe system, its own crucibles for metalworking, and its own refractory bricks for kiln upgrades and industrial furnaces.

## Materials & Tools

**For water pipes:**
- Stoneware clay (dense, vitrified, waterproof)
- Pipe-forming tools: wooden dowels (for rolling coils), wedges (for joining)
- Trimming tools: knives, wires
- Kiln access (for firing to stoneware temperature, 1100 to 1150°C)

**For crucibles:**
- Fire clay or high-alumina clay (refractory clay, heat-resistant)
- Hand-forming tools: coiling tools, smoothing tools
- Internal mold (ceramic or graphite) to shape the crucible
- Kiln access (high-fire stoneware or specialty kiln for 1200°C+)

**For refractory bricks:**
- Fire clay or chamotte clay (has grog, coarse particles, for strength)
- Brick molds (wooden frames matching brick dimensions)
- Cutting tools (wire for cutting unfired bricks)
- Kiln (for full vitrification at 1200 to 1250°C)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Produce Water Pipes

Water pipes are hollow cylinders that carry water without leaking.

**Clay selection:**
- Stoneware clay (vitrified, waterproof when fired)
- Must be plastic enough to form, but not so plastic that it warps
- Firing temperature: 1100 to 1150°C (fully vitrified)

**Pipe dimensions (standard):**
- **Inner diameter:** 5 to 10 cm (larger pipes for main distribution, smaller for branches)
- **Wall thickness:** 1.5 to 2.5 cm (thick enough to withstand pressure and breakage)
- **Length:** 30 to 50 cm (longer pipes reduce number of joints; shorter is easier to handle)

**Forming procedure:**

1. **Prepare clay:** Wedge thoroughly to remove air bubbles
2. **Roll a coil:** Roll clay into a uniform coil matching the desired inner diameter
3. **Form the cylinder:**
   - Wrap the coil around a wooden dowel (sized to the inner diameter)
   - Coil should spiral around the dowel, each loop overlapping slightly
   - Smooth and weld the overlapping coils together (blend the seams)
   - Result: a hollow cylinder

4. **Smooth the interior and exterior:**
   - Use a damp sponge to smooth surfaces
   - Interior: smooth for water flow
   - Exterior: smooth to prevent catching on other pipes

5. **Create joints (coupling):**
   - At one end, create a slightly wider lip (4 to 5 cm diameter, extends outward)
   - At the other end, keep the pipe at normal diameter
   - The lip on one pipe slips over the normal end of the next pipe (telescoping joint)
   - Seal the joint with clay mortar (clay + water mixture) when pipes are installed

6. **Dry slowly:** Water pipes are thick and prone to cracking
   - Cover with cloth to slow drying
   - Drying time: 3 to 4 weeks
   - Cracks in pipes can be repaired with clay slip before firing, or tolerated if small

7. **Fire in kiln:** Stoneware firing (1100 to 1150°C, 16 to 20 hour cycle)

**Yield:** A skilled potter can form 20 to 40 pipes per week (depending on size and experience)

**Installation:**
- Lay pipes along ground or slightly buried
- Joint the pipes using clay mortar (waterproof at stoneware firing)
- Slope slightly for gravity-fed water flow
- Support on stones or wooden props every 1 to 2 meters

### Step 2: Produce Crucibles for Metalworking

Crucibles are containers for melting metal in furnaces. They must withstand extreme heat and molten metal.

**Clay selection:**
- Fire clay or high-alumina clay (refractory clay, highly heat-resistant)
- Must not contain mica, iron oxide, or other contaminants that weaken at high temperature
- Firing temperature: 1200 to 1300°C (extreme heat tolerance)

**Crucible design:**

- **Shape:** Roughly cylindrical or slightly tapered (wider at top, narrower at bottom)
- **Wall thickness:** 2 to 4 cm (must withstand repeated heating/cooling cycles)
- **Size:** 
  - Small crucible: 5 to 10 cm inner diameter, 10 to 15 cm tall (holds 0.5 to 1 kg molten metal)
  - Large crucible: 10 to 15 cm inner diameter, 15 to 20 cm tall (holds 2 to 5 kg)
- **Capacity:** Sized to your furnace and metalworking needs

**Forming procedure:**

1. **Use a mold (easiest method):**
   - Create an outer mold from ceramic (or make a wooden mold lined with cloth)
   - Create an inner mold (ceramic form or wooden dowel)
   - Wrap clay coils around the inner mold, pressing against the outer mold
   - Remove inner mold; the outer mold holds the crucible shape
   - Allow to firm up before removing from outer mold

2. **Hand-form method (alternative):**
   - Coil-build a pot using fire clay
   - Shape as a tapered cylinder (narrower at base, wider at rim)
   - Smooth all surfaces thoroughly
   - Trim the rim flat and even
   - Dry very slowly (crucibles are thick and crack easily)

3. **Strengthen the base:**
   - The base experiences the highest stress (sitting in the furnace)
   - Make it slightly thicker (4 to 5 cm) than walls (3 to 4 cm)
   - Smooth any seams or rough areas that could crack

4. **Drying:** Crucibles must dry very slowly
   - Cover completely with cloth or burlap
   - Drying time: 4 to 6 weeks (very slow)
   - Cracks in unfired crucibles must be filled and smoothed

5. **Fire in kiln:** High-fire stoneware (1200 to 1300°C, 20 to 24 hour cycle)
   - Support crucible on kiln shelf with props (must not touch other pots)
   - Fire slowly (rapid heating cracks crucibles)
   - Cooling should be very slow (over 24 hours minimum)

**Testing:**
- After firing, test crucible with water before using with molten metal
- It should not leak
- Gentle tap test: should ring with a clear tone (indicates good firing)

**Yield:** 5 to 10 crucibles per week (slow production due to long drying time)

**Lifespan:** With careful use, a crucible lasts 20 to 50 melting cycles before cracking or wearing through

### Step 3: Produce Refractory Bricks

Refractory bricks withstand extreme heat and form the interior of furnaces, kilns, and other high-temperature equipment.

**Clay selection:**
- Fire clay (high-alumina, heat-resistant) or chamotte clay (fire clay mixed with grog, coarse particles)
- Grog improves durability and reduces warping
- Firing temperature: 1200 to 1300°C (full vitrification)

**Brick specifications:**

- **Standard size:** 20 cm long × 10 cm wide × 5 cm thick (similar to building brick, but heat-resistant)
- **Material:** Dense, hard clay with minimal porosity
- **Properties:** Should ring when struck; should not absorb water

**Production procedure:**

1. **Prepare clay:**
   - Wedge fire clay thoroughly
   - If making chamotte: add grog (coarse clay particles) at 10 to 20 percent by weight
   - Grog improves strength and reduces warping

2. **Mold the bricks:**
   - Create a wooden brick mold (20 cm × 10 cm × 5 cm)
   - Press clay into the mold firmly
   - Strike off the top level with a straightedge
   - Remove from mold and place on drying rack

3. **Dry:**
   - Dry at room temperature for 2 to 3 weeks
   - Do not cover (slow air circulation prevents cracking)
   - Stack with spacers (thin wood sticks) between bricks so air circulates

4. **Cut and trim:**
   - After drying to leather-hard, trim any rough edges with a knife or wire
   - Square up the corners for tight fitting

5. **Fire in kiln:**
   - Stoneware or high-fire kiln (1200 to 1250°C)
   - Stack bricks in kiln in a stable configuration
   - Fire for 20 to 24 hours
   - Cool very slowly (24 to 48 hours)

**Quality control:**
- Weight: refractory bricks are heavier than regular bricks (denser clay)
- Hardness: should not scratch easily with a fingernail (unlike earthenware)
- Sound: should ring with a clear tone when struck
- Water absorption: should absorb minimal water (less than 5 percent)

**Yield:** 50 to 100 bricks per week (faster than crucibles because they are smaller and dry faster)

**Cost and efficiency:**
- Refractory bricks from your kiln are cheaper than trading for them
- Makes business sense to produce for yourself and trade excess
- High demand in any settlement with furnaces

### Step 4: Design and Plan Ceramic Production Schedule

Plan when to produce each specialty ceramic to balance fuel consumption and labor.

**Production calendar (example):**

```
Month 1–3:  Standard tableware and storage pots (build production capacity)
Month 4:    Water pipes (30 to 40 pipes for 500 m of piping system)
Month 5:    Refractory bricks (100 to 200 bricks for furnace linings)
Month 6:    Crucibles (10 to 20 for metalworking)
Month 7–9:  Mixed production (some of each as demand dictates)
Month 10–12: Focus on tableware and storage pots (high seasonal demand for winter storage)
```

**Capacity planning:**
- One kiln can fire: 200 pieces of tableware OR 50 pipes OR 150 bricks OR 15 crucibles
- Assume one major firing per week
- Rotate production to balance demand with kiln capacity

### Step 5: Integrate Ceramic Production with Other Infrastructure

**Water system integration:**
- Pipes from ceramic source (spring) to settlement
- Crucibles support metallurgical production (better tools, weapons)
- Refractory bricks improve furnace efficiency (better metal quality)

**Timeline:**
- Year 1: Establish tableware production
- Year 2: Begin water-pipe production (build water distribution)
- Year 3: Expand to refractory bricks (support metallurgy)
- Year 4+: Full production of all three (specialized ceramics support advanced technology)

## Verification

**Success looks like:**
- By month 4: First water pipes fire successfully; initial piping system is installed
- By month 6: Furnaces/kilns are lined with refractory bricks; performance improves
- By month 8: First crucibles are in use; metalworking quality improves
- By year 2: Ceramic water system carries water; reduces labor for water transport
- By year 3: Metalworking furnaces are efficient (good crucibles, refractory linings); tool production accelerates

**Failure looks like:**
- Water pipes crack in the kiln or leak when installed
- Crucibles fail (crack or melt) during first use
- Refractory bricks are too porous; furnaces lose heat efficiency
- Production is too slow to meet demand

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll use regular clay for water pipes"** — Regular clay remains porous. Use stoneware clay, fired to full vitrification.

2. **"Thick-walled pipes are stronger"** — Over-thick walls crack during drying and cooling. Keep walls at 2 to 3 cm.

3. **"Crucibles are just pots; I'll make them like tableware"** — Crucibles must withstand extreme heat and thermal shock. Use fire clay and fire to high temperature.

4. **"I'll cool crucibles quickly to use them sooner"** — Fast cooling cracks crucibles. Cool over 24 to 48 hours.

5. **"Refractory bricks can be fired at earthenware temperature"** — They won't be refractory. Fire to stoneware or high-fire temperature (1200°C+).

6. **"One crucible is enough"** — One crucible fails under repeated use. Have at least 3 to 5 crucibles so you can continue working if one breaks.

7. **"Pipe joints don't need sealant"** — Without clay mortar sealant at joints, water leaks. Seal every joint.

8. **"Refractory bricks are cheaper than regular bricks"** — They are not. But they save fuel long-term by improving furnace efficiency.
