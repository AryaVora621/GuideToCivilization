## Overview

A kiln is an oven that reaches 900–1200°C, transforming clay into durable pottery. Without a kiln, your hand-built or wheel-thrown pots are just dried clay—they will dissolve in water and crumble easily. A kiln transforms them into permanent ceramic vessels that last centuries.

Kilns are complex but buildable with post-collapse materials: brick, clay mortar, and stone. This chapter teaches you three kiln designs—updraft, downdraft, and cross-draft—each with different advantages. You will learn how to manage temperature, fuel your kiln, and fire pots to completion. By year 2, your settlement can have a functioning kiln producing hundreds of fired pots per week.

## Materials & Tools

**For kiln construction:**
- Bricks (500–2,000 bricks depending on kiln size)
- Mortar (clay/lime mixture; see Vol 14, Ch 6)
- Stone (optional, for structural support)
- Timber (for temporary structural support during construction; removed after completion)
- Refractory materials (optional but useful):
  - High-alumina clay (more heat-resistant than regular pottery clay)
  - Fire brick (if available; much more durable than common brick)
  - Ash (wood ash can be used as a low-temperature refractory material)

**For firing:**
- Fuel: wood, coal, or dried dung (see Step 5)
- Thermometer or pyrometric cones (to measure temperature; optional but useful)
- Tongs or long-handled tools (for loading and removing pots)
- Kiln shelf (ceramic or clay tile to support pots during firing)

**For daily operation:**
- Fire-starting materials (kindling, starter fuel)
- Ash shovel or rake (for managing fuel in the kiln)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Choose Your Kiln Type

Three primary designs exist, each with different fuel efficiency, temperature range, and complexity.

**Type 1: Updraft kiln (simplest, most fuel-efficient)**

**Design principle:**
- Single chamber with a firebox at the bottom
- Flames and hot gases rise through the chamber
- Pots sit in the chamber as gases pass upward
- Smoke and gases exit through a chimney at the top

**Advantages:**
- Easiest to build
- Most fuel-efficient (90%+ of heat goes into pots)
- Works well for pottery (earthenware 900–1000°C, stoneware 1000–1200°C)
- Moderate temperature control

**Disadvantages:**
- Temperature variations top to bottom (bottom of chamber is hotter)
- Slower heat transfer (cooler upper chamber)
- Requires careful stacking (pots in lower chamber experience highest temperatures)

**Best for:**
- First kiln (if you have no experience)
- Fuel-limited settlements
- Pottery that fires at 900–1050°C

**Type 2: Downdraft kiln (complex, best temperature uniformity)**

**Design principle:**
- Double chamber (firebox + main chamber)
- Flames rise through a fire-box, then reverse direction
- Hot gases pushed down through the pots
- Gases exit at the bottom (flue opening)

**Advantages:**
- Most uniform temperature throughout the chamber
- Excellent results for fine pottery
- Professional-quality results

**Disadvantages:**
- Complex to build (requires careful flue design)
- More energy to build (larger structure)
- Temperature control is difficult (small mistakes cause huge variations)

**Best for:**
- Second or third kiln (after gaining experience)
- Settlements that can support skilled kiln operators
- High-quality pottery production

**Type 3: Cross-draft kiln (moderate complexity, good results)**

**Design principle:**
- Single chamber with a firebox on one side
- Flames enter the chamber horizontally
- Gases exit through a flue on the opposite side
- Heat transfers sideways across the pots

**Advantages:**
- Moderate temperature uniformity
- Simpler than downdraft but more uniform than updraft
- Versatile (works well for 900–1150°C)

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires careful kiln shelf arrangement
- Moderately complex flue design

**Best for:**
- Second kiln (simpler than downdraft, better than updraft)
- General-purpose pottery production

### Step 2: Design Your Updraft Kiln (Recommended for first kiln)

An updraft kiln for a medium settlement requires ~1000 bricks and 2–3 weeks of construction.

**Kiln dimensions:**

- **Chamber height:** 1.5 meters
- **Chamber width/depth:** 1 meter × 1 meter (square footprint)
- **Chamber volume:** 1.5 m³ (holds 50–100 pots per firing)
- **Firebox height:** 0.5 meters
- **Chimney height:** 2 meters (tall chimney creates better draft)

**Construction layout:**

```
Top view:
[1m × 1m chamber]
[firebox on one side]

Side view:
Chimney (2m tall)
|
[Main chamber (1.5m tall)]
_________________________
[Firebox (0.5m)]
```

**Wall construction:**

1. **Build the base (firebox):**
   - Dig a shallow pit (~0.5m × 1.5m × 0.5m deep)
   - Line with bricks mortared together (firebox must be strong; it experiences highest temperatures)
   - Create an opening at the front (30 cm × 30 cm) for fuel loading

2. **Build the chamber floor:**
   - Layer of bricks with mortar
   - Ensure it is level and strong (it supports pots)

3. **Build walls (up 1.5 meters):**
   - Stack bricks in standard running bond (alternating rows offset by half a brick)
   - Use clay/lime mortar between bricks
   - Walls should be 1 brick thick (10 cm) minimum
   - Build all four walls equally to height of 1.5 meters

4. **Build the roof:**
   - Option A: Corbel the bricks inward (each row extends slightly less than the row below) until a keystone closes the center opening
   - Option B: Install a clay barrel vault (curved roof) made from voussoir-shaped bricks
   - Option C: Use a wooden frame with clay covering (simpler; remove wood after clay sets)
   - The roof must be strong (supports its own weight + internal pressure)

5. **Install the chimney:**
   - A vertical flue (20 cm × 20 cm) from the top of the chamber
   - Built from bricks, rising 2 meters above the roof
   - Must be vertical and stable (internal guy-wires or external bracing)

6. **Install the damper (optional but useful):**
   - A movable brick or plug that closes part of the flue
   - Controls draft (less draft = slower heat rise, better temperature control)
   - Operate by trial and error

**Curing the kiln (before first use):**

After construction, allow the kiln to dry and harden:

1. **Week 1:** Leave the kiln open, protected from rain. Mortar sets.
2. **Week 2:** Light a small fire in the firebox (no pots). Heat to ~300°C for 12 hours. Cool slowly.
3. **Week 3:** Light a medium fire, heat to ~600°C for 12 hours. Cool slowly.
4. **Week 4:** Ready for production firing (900–1000°C).

Rapid heating in the first firings can cause cracking. Slow, gradual temperature rise strengthens the kiln.

### Step 3: Prepare the Kiln Interior

Before loading pots, prepare the firing surface.

**Steps:**

1. **Create a kiln shelf base:**
   - Layer bricks or clay tiles on the chamber floor
   - Creates a firing surface for pots
   - Shelves can be ceramic tiles (if available) or clay tiles that you make yourself

2. **Optional—install additional shelves:**
   - If you need to fire multiple layers of pots, install a secondary shelf mid-chamber
   - Use ceramic posts or clay supports to hold the upper shelf
   - Higher shelves experience lower temperatures; reserve them for lower-temperature pottery or test pieces

3. **Prepare pots for stacking:**
   - Place largest, strongest pots on lower shelf (highest temperature)
   - Stack smaller pots and bowls on shelves above
   - Leave space between pots (1–2 cm) for heat circulation
   - Do not stack pots on top of each other (heat cannot reach inner pots)

### Step 4: Load Pots and Manage the Firing

Kiln loading is an art. How you arrange pots determines firing success.

**Loading procedure:**

1. **Inspect all pots:**
   - Remove any with large cracks (they will shatter and damage neighbors)
   - Ensure all pots are bone-dry (dried for at least 2 weeks)

2. **Arrange pots strategically:**
   - **Bottom shelf:** Large, sturdy vessels (water jars, storage pots). These tolerate heat variations.
   - **Middle shelf:** Standard pottery (bowls, plates, smaller jars). Most stable here.
   - **Top shelf:** Fine ware, small pieces, test pieces. Most insulated from direct heat.
   - **Spacing:** 2–3 cm between pots, minimum. Heat must circulate.

3. **Stack carefully:**
   - Do not nest small bowls inside large pots (heat cannot reach inner surfaces)
   - Do not stack pots directly on top of each other
   - If stacking, place clay stilts or supports between pots

4. **Load gradually:**
   - Load pots one or two at a time, allowing access to the chamber
   - Close the chamber opening partially as you load (heat escape = slow warming)

### Step 5: Fire the Kiln (12–24 hours)

Firing is a skilled process. Temperature management determines success.

**Firing schedule:**

**Hours 0–2 (Slow start, 0–200°C):**
- Light a small fire in the firebox
- Use dry kindling and small wood pieces
- Goal: gentle heat rise, no rapid temperature change
- Monitor: chamber should feel warm but not hot

**Hours 2–6 (Medium rise, 200–600°C):**
- Gradually increase fuel (add wood to the fire as existing wood burns)
- Maintain steady flames
- Do not overfuel (rapid temperature rise = thermal shock = cracked kiln)
- Monitor: chimney smoke color changes from white (steam/moisture) to gray/black (hot gases)

**Hours 6–10 (Rapid rise, 600–900°C):**
- Increase fuel steadily
- Maintain consistent flames
- Kiln should be visibly hot (inside glows red at high temperatures)
- Monitor: pyrometric cones (if available) indicate temperature; visual assessment suffices otherwise

**Hours 10–12 (Peak, 900–1000°C):**
- Continue fuel, maintaining temperature
- This is "soak time" where pots continue to mature at peak temperature
- Duration: 2–4 hours at peak temperature (longer = more thorough firing)
- At 1000°C+, pots begin to vitrify (glaze melts if applied)

**Hours 12–24 (Cool-down, 1000°C → 200°C):**
- Stop adding fuel
- Allow kiln to cool naturally
- Cooling takes 12–24 hours (do not force cooling; it causes cracking)
- Do not open the kiln until it is cool enough to touch the outside walls
- As it cools, pots gradually set in their final form

**Temperature assessment (without a thermometer):**

- **500°C:** Kiln glows dull red inside
- **700°C:** Glows bright orange
- **900°C:** Glows yellow-orange (pottery matures here)
- **1100°C:** Glows nearly white
- **Peak achieved when:** Pyrometric cone (if present) bends completely, or inside is yellow-white glowing

### Step 6: Unload and Assess Results

After cooling, carefully unload pots and assess the firing results.

**Unload procedure:**

1. **Ensure the kiln is cool:**
   - Outside walls should be cool enough to touch
   - Inside may still be warm; use caution

2. **Unload carefully:**
   - Remove pots one at a time
   - Inspect each pot as it comes out
   - Set aside any that are cracked or failed

3. **Assess quality:**
   - **Successful:** Hard, dense, uniform color, rings when struck, no cracks
   - **Partial failure:** Cracked but still usable (repaired or used for non-critical purposes)
   - **Complete failure:** Shattered, extremely weak, unusable

**Yield expectations:**

- **First firing:** 70–80% success (learning experience)
- **Early firings (1–5):** 75–85% success
- **Routine firings (10+):** 85–95% success (as you master temperature control)
- **Professional potter:** 95%+ success

### Step 7: Fuel Management and Long-Term Operation

Efficient fuel use determines whether your kiln operation is sustainable.

**Fuel options:**

**Wood:**
- **Advantages:** Easy to source, easy to control burns, good heat
- **Disadvantages:** Fast-burning, requires large quantities (~500 kg per firing), smoke creates atmospheric losses
- **Yield:** 1 firing per 2–3 tons of wood per year (depends on kiln size and year-round operation)

**Coal or coke:**
- **Advantages:** Higher heat content, burns longer, less air pollution
- **Disadvantages:** Requires coal deposits nearby (see Vol 14, Ch 4: Coal and Peat)
- **Yield:** 1 firing per 200–300 kg of coal (excellent efficiency)

**Dried dung:**
- **Advantages:** Renewable, free (livestock waste), good for initial heating
- **Disadvantages:** Lower heat output, must dry for weeks, smoky
- **Yield:** 1 firing per 600–800 kg of dried dung

**Mixed fuel approach (most practical):**
- Start with wood (fast burning, easy control)
- Add coal/coke at peak temperature (high heat, efficient)
- Use dung for pre-heating or supplemental fuel

**Production timeline and efficiency:**

- **Weekly firing:** 1 kiln, 1 firing per week = ~5,000 pots/year
- **Fuel demand:** 200–500 kg per firing × 52 firings = 10–26 tons fuel/year
- **Labor:** 1 skilled operator can manage loading, firing, unloading (full-time work)

### Step 8: Troubleshoot Common Firing Problems

**Problem: Uneven firing (top too light, bottom too dark)**

- **Cause:** Temperature gradient (bottom hotter than top)
- **Solution:** Adjust flue damper to slow draft; pots at top will receive more sustained heat. Alternatively, move pots to different shelf positions in next firing.

**Problem: Pots crack or shatter in the kiln**

- **Cause:** Thermal shock (rapid temperature change), clay defects, or overfiring
- **Solution:** Slow the heating rate early; ensure pots are bone-dry before loading; reduce peak temperature slightly in next firing.

**Problem: Kiln cracks or fails**

- **Cause:** Thermal stress, poor construction, or defective bricks
- **Solution:** Repair damaged areas with clay mortar; slow the next firing; consider reinforcing kiln walls with external metal bands or stone supports.

**Problem: Kiln takes too long to reach peak temperature (>18 hours)**

- **Cause:** Poor draft (chimney clogged or too short), insufficient fuel, or insulation too thick
- **Solution:** Clean chimney; ensure 2 m+ height; increase fuel rate; reduce kiln wall thickness in next kiln.

**Problem: Temperature stays below 900°C**

- **Cause:** Insufficient fuel, poor kiln design, or chimney issue
- **Solution:** Use coal/coke instead of wood (higher heat); check that chimney is patent (not clogged); verify kiln walls are intact.

## Verification

**Success looks like:**
- By month 1: Kiln is built, cured, and ready for first firing
- By month 2: First firing is complete; 70%+ of pots successfully fired
- By month 3: Second and third firings are successful; quality is improving
- By month 6: Routine firings (weekly) are successful; 85%+ success rate
- By year 1: Kiln is reliable and efficient; thousands of pots fired annually

**Failure looks like:**
- Kiln walls crack extensively during first heating (poor construction or curing)
- Peak temperature never exceeds 800°C (kiln design flaw, poor draft, or fuel issues)
- Majority of pots are cracked or shattered (thermal shock, poor kiln design, or defective clay)
- Kiln requires rebuild within first year (design or construction error)

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll build a huge kiln to fire many pots at once"** — Large kilns are hard to control and waste fuel. Start small (1 m³ chamber); scale up after success.

2. **"I'll fire as hot as possible (1200°C+) for strongest pots"** — Overfiring weakens pottery. 900–1050°C is optimal for earthenware; 1000–1150°C for stoneware.

3. **"I'll cool the kiln as fast as possible to get results faster"** — Rapid cooling cracks pots and damages the kiln. Cool slowly over 12–24 hours.

4. **"I'll use poor-quality bricks for kiln construction"** — Low-grade bricks fail quickly. Use the hardest, densest bricks available.

5. **"I won't bother with curing the kiln; I'll jump straight to production firing"** — Uncured kilns crack. Cure with three progressively hotter fires (300°C, 600°C, then production).

6. **"I'll stack pots tightly to maximize space"** — Tight stacking blocks heat circulation. Allow 2–3 cm between all pots.

7. **"The kiln doesn't need a tall chimney; short is fine"** — Short chimneys create poor draft, preventing temperature rise. Chimney must be 2m+ above the chamber.

8. **"I'll add more fuel if the kiln is not reaching temperature"** — Excess fuel creates thermal shock. Increase fuel gradually; fix draft issues if needed.
