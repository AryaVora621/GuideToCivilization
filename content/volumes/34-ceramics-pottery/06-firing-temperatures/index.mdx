## Overview

Firing temperature determines the properties of pottery: strength, porosity, durability, and cost. Earthenware fires at 900 to 1050°C and is soft but easy to produce. Stoneware fires at 1050 to 1200°C and is strong and waterproof. Porcelain fires at 1200 to 1300°C and is extremely hard and white but requires pure clay and high fuel consumption.

Your settlement will likely focus on earthenware (easiest, lowest fuel cost) for the first few years. As fuel supply stabilizes and clay quality improves, you can experiment with stoneware for specialized vessels. Porcelain is beyond the scope of most post-collapse communities, requiring resources you do not have.

This chapter teaches you what happens to clay at different temperatures, how to recognize when your kiln reaches each temperature, and how to optimize fuel use while maintaining quality.

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Understand Pyrometric Cones (Temperature Markers)

Pyrometric cones are small clay pyramids that bend at specific temperatures, indicating when the kiln has reached the desired firing point.

**What cones are:**
- Small triangular pyramids made from specially formulated clay mixtures
- Each cone bends completely (fuses to the kiln shelf) at a specific temperature
- Used as visual kiln thermometer without electrical equipment

**Cone numbering system:**
- Cone 01: bends at 1023°C (earthenware, low-fire)
- Cone 1: bends at 1046°C
- Cone 2–4: bends at 1060–1086°C (high earthenware / low stoneware)
- Cone 6: bends at 1222°C (mid-fire stoneware)
- Cone 8–10: bends at 1260–1305°C (high stoneware / porcelain)

**Using cones in your kiln:**

1. Place 2 to 3 cones inside the kiln where you can observe them
2. As the kiln heats, watch the cones
3. When the target cone bends (bows forward), you have reached that temperature
4. For safety and reliability, use three cones: one target, one below (tells you you're close), one above (tells you not to over-fire)

**Example setup for earthenware firing:**
- Place Cone 01 (target), Cone 1 (indicator), Cone 2 (safety) on the kiln shelf
- When Cone 01 bends completely, you have reached 1023°C (full earthenware maturation)
- When Cone 1 bends, you are at 1046°C (start of stoneware range)
- When Cone 2 bends, you have over-fired (above 1060°C)

**Note:** If cones are unavailable (difficult to make post-collapse), rely on visual assessment (see Step 2).

### Step 2: Visual Temperature Assessment (Without Cones)

If you cannot make pyrometric cones, learn to estimate temperature by kiln interior color and glaze behavior.

**Temperature indicators (visual):**

**Below 600°C:**
- Kiln interior is dark (no visible glow)
- Glazes show no change

**600 to 800°C:**
- Kiln interior glows dull red
- No glaze melting

**800 to 900°C:**
- Kiln glows bright red
- Glazes begin to dull (lose shine)

**900 to 1000°C (Low-fire/Earthenware range):**
- Kiln glows yellow-orange
- Glazes begin to melt (look glossy)
- Clay is stiff but not fully mature

**1000 to 1100°C (High-fire Earthenware / Low Stoneware range):**
- Kiln glows orange-yellow
- Glazes are fully melted (glossy and smooth)
- Clay has matured; pots are waterproof

**1100 to 1200°C (Stoneware range):**
- Kiln glows yellow-white
- Glazes may show slight pooling (running slightly)
- Clay is very hard and fully waterproof

**1200 to 1300°C (High Stoneware / Porcelain range):**
- Kiln glows nearly white
- Glazes flow noticeably
- Clay may warp if not supported

**Assessment method:**
1. Look into the kiln through the peephole (small opening in kiln wall)
2. Compare the interior glow to the color descriptions above
3. Estimate temperature based on color
4. Accuracy: within ±50°C

### Step 3: Understand Three Pottery Types and Their Properties

**Type 1: Earthenware (900 to 1050°C)**

**What it is:**
- Most common pottery type post-collapse
- Fired at relatively low temperature
- Made from common clays (any plastic clay works)

**Properties:**
- **Strength:** Moderate (pots break if dropped)
- **Porosity:** Porous (water seeps through if unglazed; glazing makes it waterproof)
- **Color:** Red, brown, orange, buff (varies by clay)
- **Texture:** Fine and smooth (if clay is good) to gritty (if clay is sandy)
- **Durability:** Good for storage and cooking if glazed; survives centuries if kept dry

**Advantages:**
- Easiest to fire (lowest kiln temperature)
- Works with common clay
- Fast firing (8 to 16 hours)
- Lowest fuel consumption

**Disadvantages:**
- Porous without glaze (not functional for liquid storage)
- Weaker than stoneware (more likely to break)
- Glazes can chip off if thin

**Best for:**
- Water storage jars (if glazed)
- Cooking pots (if glazed)
- Everyday tableware
- Most early post-collapse pottery

**Type 2: Stoneware (1050 to 1250°C)**

**What it is:**
- Fired at higher temperature than earthenware
- Made from clays with higher alumina content (not all clays work)
- Rarely fully vitrified (completely glassified) below 1100°C

**Properties:**
- **Strength:** High (pots resist breaking)
- **Porosity:** Non-porous (water cannot seep through, even unglazed)
- **Color:** Gray, tan, brown, buff (depends on clay and firing atmosphere)
- **Texture:** Smooth and glass-like (properly fired)
- **Durability:** Extremely durable; lasts centuries

**Advantages:**
- Very strong (survives heavy use)
- Waterproof even unglazed
- Glazes adhere very well
- Works well for durable vessels

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires higher kiln temperature (more fuel)
- Not all clays work (need testing)
- Longer firing time (16 to 24 hours)
- More fuel consumption (20 to 30 percent more than earthenware)
- Kiln must reach over 1100°C consistently

**Best for:**
- Long-term storage vessels
- Specialized pottery (tiles, crucibles for metallurgy)
- Items that need extreme durability

**Type 3: Porcelain (1200 to 1300°C)**

**What it is:**
- Fired at very high temperature
- Requires pure kaolin (China clay) or equivalent
- Fully vitrified (completely glassified)

**Properties:**
- **Strength:** Extreme (hard, rings when struck)
- **Porosity:** Non-porous
- **Color:** White or off-white (very pure)
- **Texture:** Glass-like and smooth
- **Durability:** Lasts indefinitely

**Advantages:**
- Extremely strong and beautiful
- Pure white color
- Finest porcelain is prized

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires very pure clay (rare post-collapse)
- Very high kiln temperature (difficult to control)
- Massive fuel consumption (50 percent more than stoneware)
- Difficult to glaze (glazes must match clay expansion)
- Little practical advantage over stoneware for survival

**Best for:**
- Not practical for post-collapse communities (focus on earthenware and stoneware)

### Step 4: Assess Your Clay's Firing Range

Different clays mature (become fully waterproof and strong) at different temperatures.

**Testing procedure:**

1. **Make test tiles:** Small ceramic pieces from your clay
2. **Fire at low temperature (900°C):** Bisque-fire to check if clay is viable
3. **Fire at medium temperature (1000°C):** Check maturation
4. **Fire at high temperature (1100°C):** Check for over-firing
5. **Assess results:**
   - At 900°C: porous, soft, water seeps through
   - At 1000°C: waterproof or nearly so, clay has matured
   - At 1100°C: very strong, may show color change
   - At 1200°C+: may warp, glaze may run excessively

**Optimal firing temperature for your clay:**
- The temperature at which the clay becomes waterproof without over-firing
- For most earthenware clays: 1000 to 1050°C
- For stoneware clays: 1100 to 1150°C
- Example: If your clay is waterproof at 1000°C but starts to warp at 1150°C, your optimal range is 1000 to 1100°C

### Step 5: Plan Firing Schedules by Pottery Type

**Earthenware firing schedule (900 to 1050°C):**

**Total time:** 8 to 14 hours

**Heating curve:**
- 0 to 2 hours: Slow rise 0 to 300°C (remove moisture)
- 2 to 6 hours: Rapid rise 300 to 900°C
- 6 to 10 hours: Hold at peak (900 to 1050°C) for 2 to 4 hours (soak time)
- 10 to 14 hours: Cool naturally

**Stoneware firing schedule (1100 to 1200°C):**

**Total time:** 16 to 24 hours

**Heating curve:**
- 0 to 2 hours: Slow rise 0 to 300°C
- 2 to 8 hours: Rapid rise 300 to 1000°C
- 8 to 14 hours: Slow rise 1000 to 1100°C (maintain even temperature)
- 14 to 18 hours: Hold at peak (1100 to 1200°C) for 2 to 4 hours (soak time)
- 18 to 24 hours: Cool naturally

**Fuel consumption comparison:**

Assume a 1 m³ kiln firing pottery:
- **Earthenware (1000°C):** 300 to 400 kg wood or 100 to 150 kg coal
- **Stoneware (1150°C):** 400 to 500 kg wood or 140 to 200 kg coal
- **Fuel increase:** Stoneware requires about 30 percent more fuel than earthenware

### Step 6: Optimize Fuel Consumption

**Strategies to reduce fuel consumption per firing:**

1. **Fire multiple batches in sequence:**
   - Cool kiln to 200°C
   - Load new pots while kiln is still warm
   - Reheat to peak temperature
   - Saves 20 to 30 percent fuel compared to starting cold each time

2. **Insulate the kiln:**
   - Add clay layers around the kiln exterior (increases insulation)
   - Reduces heat loss; retains more heat
   - Savings: 10 to 15 percent fuel

3. **Preheat kiln fuel:**
   - For coal or dried dung, stack fuel inside the firebox before lighting
   - Pre-drying wood for 6 months improves burn efficiency
   - Savings: 5 to 10 percent fuel

4. **Use coal or coke for high-temperature firings:**
   - Coal burns hotter and longer than wood
   - Allows you to reach high temperatures with less total fuel
   - Stoneware firing: coal costs 1/3 as much as wood to reach same temperature

5. **Increase firing frequency:**
   - Firing weekly is more efficient than firing monthly
   - Kiln retains some heat; sequential firings build heat efficiently
   - Annual fuel savings: 15 to 20 percent

### Step 7: Recognize Over-Firing and Under-Firing

**Under-firing (kiln too cool):**

**Signs:**
- Glazes are dull, matte, chalky
- Pots remain porous (water seeps through)
- Clay color is pale
- Glaze has a rough texture

**Problems:**
- Pottery is not waterproof
- Glazes are not durable

**Solution:**
- Increase kiln temperature 25 to 50°C next firing
- Use more fuel; extend heating time
- Ensure kiln is well-sealed (no air leaks)

**Over-firing (kiln too hot):**

**Signs:**
- Glazes are glossy, overly smooth (may run)
- Glazes have started to flow (pooling near rims or base)
- Clay is very dark (oxidized)
- Pots may warp slightly

**Problems:**
- Glaze may run onto kiln shelf
- Pots may stick together if too close
- Excessive glaze runoff wastes glaze

**Solution:**
- Reduce kiln temperature 25 to 50°C next firing
- Shorten the soak time (peak temperature duration)
- Use less fuel; stop adding fuel earlier

## Verification

**Success looks like:**
- By firing 3: You can reach consistent earthenware temperature (1000 to 1050°C) and 90 percent of pots fire successfully
- By firing 10: You can predict temperature without cones; 95 percent success rate
- By firing 20: You are experimenting with stoneware; fuel consumption is optimized
- By year 2: You fire weekly; production is consistent; pottery is reliable for storage and cooking

**Failure looks like:**
- Majority of firings under-fire; pottery remains porous
- Kiln over-fires repeatedly; glazes run, pots stick
- Unable to reach target temperature; kiln design flawed
- Fuel consumption is excessive; kiln is poorly insulated or leaks heat

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll fire as hot as possible for strongest pottery"** — Over-firing weakens pottery and wastes fuel. Fire to the clay's optimal temperature, no higher.

2. **"Earthenware is weak; I'll fire all pottery as stoneware"** — Stoneware requires 30 percent more fuel. Earthenware is adequate for most uses. Use stoneware selectively.

3. **"I'll skip the slow heating phase to save time"** — Fast heating cracks pots and damages the kiln. Slow heating (especially the first 2 hours) is essential.

4. **"Cones are not important; I'll just look at the kiln"** — Visual assessment is imprecise (±50°C error). Cones (if available) are more reliable.

5. **"I'll fire every day to maximize production"** — Continuous firing burns out the kiln; refractory materials fail faster. Fire weekly, not daily.

6. **"Water seeping through pots is normal"** — It is not. If glazed earthenware seeps, it is under-fired. Increase temperature next firing.

7. **"I'll cool the kiln in 2 hours to get results faster"** — Fast cooling cracks pots and damages the kiln. Cool for 12 to 24 hours.

8. **"All clays fire the same"** — Different clays mature at different temperatures. Test your specific clay to find its optimal range.
