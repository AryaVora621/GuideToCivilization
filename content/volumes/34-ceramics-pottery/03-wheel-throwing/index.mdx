## Overview

The pottery wheel is a fundamental industrial technology. A potter on a wheel can produce 5–10 vessels per hour; the same potter hand-building can produce 1–2 per hour. For a settlement that needs thousands of water storage pots, cooking pots, and trade goods, the wheel is transformational. A single skilled wheel-thrower can supply a settlement of 1,000 people with pottery.

This chapter teaches you to build a kick wheel (human-powered using foot), the simplest wheel suitable for post-collapse use. You will learn the mechanical principles, construction details, and the throwing techniques of centering, opening, and pulling walls. By year 2, your settlement can have a functioning pottery operation producing hundreds of pots per week.

## Prerequisites

- Vol 15, Ch 1: Clay Identification (clay quality assessment)
- Vol 15, Ch 2: Hand-Building (clay handling and form-making)
- Vol 11: Mechanical Engineering (wheel mechanics, axles, bearings, balance)
- Ability to construct wooden structures and maintain mechanical devices
- Physical capacity: throwing is exhausting work; requires strong legs and core

## Materials & Tools

**For wheel construction:**
- Wood (hardwood preferred: oak, maple; softwood: pine acceptable):
  - Flywheel disc: 60 cm diameter, 5 cm thick (can be laminated from planks)
  - Axle: 3–4 cm diameter, ~1 meter long (hardwood dowel or sapling)
  - Wheel shaft support frame: sturdy timbers for bearing support
  - Head assembly (rotating platform where clay sits): 30 cm diameter disc, 2–3 cm thick
  - Kick bar (footrest): wooden beam attached to flywheel
- Metal (if available):
  - Bearings or bearing blocks to reduce friction (bronze or iron bushings are ideal; wood-on-wood bearings are acceptable but require lubrication)
  - Nails, bolts, dowels (for assembly)
- Fasteners: rope, twine (for binding)
- Tool materials: stone, metal edge tools (files, chisels)

**For throwing:**
- Clay (prepared, plastic, consistent quality)
- Water (for dampening hands and clay)
- Tools:
  - Natural sponge (for smoothing)
  - Wooden calipers (for measuring diameters)
  - Wire or string tool (for cutting clay from wheel)
  - Throwing sticks or wooden tools (for shaping)
- Buckets for water supply

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Build the Kick Wheel (3–4 weeks of construction)

A kick wheel is a mechanical device: a flywheel turned by foot, connected to a rotating head where clay is thrown.

**Design parameters:**

**Flywheel:**
- **Diameter:** 60 cm (smaller is hard to control, larger is hard to kick)
- **Thickness:** 5 cm (mass provides momentum; thicker = more momentum, easier throwing, harder to start)
- **Weight:** 30–50 kg (provides inertia; heavier wheel spins longer between kicks)
- **Material:** Hardwood is ideal (denser, stronger); softwood is acceptable if thick enough

**Axle:**
- **Diameter:** 3–4 cm (strong enough not to bend under load)
- **Length:** ~1 meter (extends through flywheel and head)
- **Material:** Hardwood dowel or straight sapling (straight is critical; wobbles cause problems)
- **Bearings:** Wood-on-wood bearings (bushing of hardwood with greased hole) are acceptable; metal bearings are better (less friction)

**Head assembly:**
- **Diameter:** 25–30 cm (large enough for pots but small enough to control)
- **Thickness:** 2–3 cm
- **Material:** Hardwood (hardness prevents clay from sticking)
- **Attachment:** Bolted or keyed to the axle (must not slip)

**Construction steps:**

**Step 1A: Create the flywheel**

1. **Option A—Solid disc:** If you have a large piece of wood, cut a 60 cm diameter disc from hardwood. Ensure it is flat and well-balanced.

2. **Option B—Laminated disc (if solid wood is not available):** Glue planks together (using lime mortar or wooden dowels) to create a 60 cm disc. Sand or plane the surface smooth and flat.

3. **Balance the flywheel:**
   - Mount the wheel on the axle
   - Spin it
   - Mark the heavy spot (it will rotate to the bottom)
   - Remove weight from the heavy spot (carve away clay) or add weight to the light spot
   - Test again; iterate until balanced

4. **Attach the kick bar:**
   - Bolt or nail a wooden beam (5 cm × 10 cm × 50 cm) tangent to the wheel's edge
   - This is where your foot pushes to spin the wheel
   - Height: position it so your foot is comfortable (usually 20–30 cm above ground when seated)

**Step 1B: Create the axle and bearings**

1. **Choose and prepare the axle:**
   - Select a straight hardwood dowel (3–4 cm diameter, ~1 meter long)
   - Sand it smooth
   - Ensure it is straight (place it on a flat surface and spin it; any wobble is visible)

2. **Create bearing blocks:**
   - Hardwood blocks (10 cm × 10 cm × 5 cm) with a hole drilled through the center
   - Hole diameter: slightly larger than axle (e.g., if axle is 3 cm, drill a 3.2 cm hole)
   - Insert the axle through the hole
   - Add lubrication: animal fat, plant oil, or wax reduces friction
   - If metal bearings are available, use them instead (lower friction = easier spinning)

3. **Support the axle:**
   - Build a frame from sturdy timber (8 cm × 8 cm beams)
   - Frame height: ~1 meter (so the head is at comfortable working height)
   - Frame width: ~1 meter (to span the bearing blocks)
   - Mount bearing blocks on the frame so the axle is level and centered

**Step 1C: Create the head assembly**

1. **Cut the head:**
   - Hardwood disc, 25–30 cm diameter, 2–3 cm thick
   - Surface must be smooth and flat
   - Sand or plane if necessary

2. **Attach to the axle:**
   - Drill a centered hole (matches axle diameter)
   - Insert the axle through the head
   - Secure with a wooden key (a wedge that prevents slipping) or metal pin
   - Ensure the head does not wobble on the axle

3. **Optional—add a bat (removable clay-working surface):**
   - Create a removable disc (clay bat) that sits on the head
   - Allows potters to remove finished pots without stopping the wheel
   - New bat is placed for the next pot
   - Bats are simple discs of hardwood or plaster

**Step 1D: Test the wheel**

1. Mount the completed wheel (frame + axle + flywheel + head)
2. Spin it by hand; it should rotate smoothly for 10–20 seconds without friction
3. Spin it with your foot on the kick bar; it should accelerate easily and coast for several seconds
4. Make adjustments:
   - If it is hard to turn: reduce friction in bearings (add lubrication or adjust bearing tightness)
   - If it wobbles: balance the flywheel or check that the axle is straight
   - If it stops quickly: increase the flywheel weight or check for bearing drag

### Step 2: Prepare Your Wheel-Throwing Space

A wheel-throwing station needs good light, ventilation, and drainage.

**Setup requirements:**

1. **Work surface:** Place the wheel on a stable foundation (packed earth, stone, or wood platform)
2. **Seating:** A bench positioned so your feet reach the kick bar comfortably
3. **Clay supply:** Buckets of prepared clay nearby (arm's reach from the wheel)
4. **Water supply:** Bucket of water for dampening hands and clay
5. **Tool rack:** Hooks or shelf for throwing tools within reach
6. **Light:** Position so you can see the pot as it rotates (side light is best; direct overhead light creates shadow)
7. **Drainage:** The area around the wheel becomes wet and muddy; slope the ground away from the wheel or provide a channel for water runoff

### Step 3: Master Centering (Foundation Technique)

Centering is the critical first step. If clay is not centered, the pot will be lopsided or collapse.

**Centering procedure:**

1. **Prepare the clay:**
   - Take a fist-sized ball of clay (~500 g)
   - Wedge it thoroughly by hand to remove air bubbles (knead for 2–3 minutes)
   - Compress it into a ball shape

2. **Mount on the wheel:**
   - Place the ball of clay on the center of the wheel head
   - Spin the wheel by hand or foot at slow to medium speed (~30 RPM to start)

3. **Find the center with hands:**
   - Place both hands on the clay, one on each side
   - Apply firm, steady pressure inward
   - The clay will wobble initially; your hands will follow the motion
   - Gradually reduce the wobble by increasing inward pressure
   - The goal: clay rotates without any visible eccentricity (off-center motion)

4. **Position your hands for centering:**
   - **Outside hand:** Press from the side, steadying the clay
   - **Inside hand (if the clay has a hole):** Insert thumb/fingers into any opening and apply outward pressure to counter the outside hand's inward pressure
   - **Thumb:** Use your thumb to feel the clay's rotation and guide it to center

5. **Increase speed gradually:**
   - Once centered at slow speed, gradually increase wheel speed (~60 RPM for throwing)
   - Centering becomes easier at faster speeds due to centrifugal force

6. **Test for center:**
   - Centering is successful when:
     - The clay spins smoothly with no visible wobble
     - You can touch the spinning clay gently with a finger and feel it rotating around a fixed center
     - The surface is smooth and uniform

**Common centering errors:**

1. **"I'll use too much pressure"** — Excessive pressure distorts the clay or stalls the wheel. Use steady, moderate pressure.
2. **"I'll center too fast"** — Slow to moderate speed is easier. Once centered, increase speed for throwing.
3. **"The clay is centered when it feels centered"** — Spin the wheel and touch the clay lightly; if it pushes your finger outward and inward as it spins, it is not centered.

### Step 4: Open and Shape the Pot

Once clay is centered, you open it and shape it into a vessel.

**Opening procedure:**

1. **Create an opening:**
   - Position your thumb at the center of the spinning clay
   - Slowly lower your thumb into the clay, creating a hole
   - Leave ~1 cm of clay at the bottom (do not pierce through)
   - As the hole opens, insert your fingers to widen it

2. **Control the opening:**
   - **Outside hand:** Stabilizes the pot, applies light pressure
   - **Inside hand (fingers in the hole):** Gradually widens the opening by pulling outward gently
   - The walls will thin as you pull outward

3. **Pull the walls upward:**
   - Once the opening is ~3–5 cm diameter, begin pulling the walls upward
   - **Technique:** Fingers inside the pot, thumb and finger outside, gently squeeze and lift
   - **Motion:** Pull slowly and steadily, working from the base upward
   - **Repetition:** Complete 3–5 pulls; each pull thins and raises the walls

4. **Shape and refine:**
   - **Wider opening:** Angle your hands outward; walls flare
   - **Narrower opening:** Angle inward; walls taper
   - **Smooth surface:** Use a damp sponge to smooth any striations or roughness

5. **Finishing:**
   - **Rim:** Thicken the rim by pressing thumb and finger around the edge (safety: rim is strongest when reinforced)
   - **Symmetry:** Check that the pot is balanced and even
   - **Wall thickness:** Estimate ~5 mm (consistent thickness is critical)

**Common throwing errors:**

1. **"I'll pull too hard and too fast"** — Walls collapse. Pull slowly and steadily, with light pressure.
2. **"The pot is collapsing; I'll keep throwing"** — If walls collapse, the pot is lost. Stop the wheel, remove the pot, and start over.
3. **"I'll make pots with thin walls (2 mm) for beauty"** — Too thin. Pots crack during drying and break in use. 4–7 mm is standard.
4. **"I'll throw without centering first"** — Uncentered clay throws lopsided, impossible to correct. Always center before opening.

### Step 5: Remove the Pot from the Wheel

Once the pot is complete, you must remove it cleanly.

**Removal procedure:**

1. **Stop the wheel:** Gradually slow and stop by dragging a stick or your foot (do not stop suddenly; momentum can cause accidents)

2. **Cut the pot from the wheel:**
   - Use a thin wire or string tool
   - Position it under the pot's base
   - Draw it across smoothly, cutting the pot free
   - Or: use a wooden bat (removable platform) under the pot to lift it away

3. **Support the pot:**
   - Support the pot from beneath as you lift it
   - If the pot is large, use both hands
   - Place it gently on a drying rack or shelf

4. **Inspect for cracks:**
   - Check for cracks or weaknesses that may have appeared during throwing
   - Small cracks can be repaired; large cracks mean the pot is unusable

### Step 6: Manage Production and Quality

As you master wheel-throwing, increase production systematically.

**Production timeline:**

- **Week 1–2:** Master centering. Practice on 5–10 clay balls, throwing until you center consistently.
- **Week 3–4:** Throw complete pots. Make 10–20 simple bowls, focusing on smooth walls and even rims.
- **Week 5–6:** Increase speed. Throw 5–10 pots per day, gradually adding complexity (taller pots, narrower necks).
- **Month 2–3:** Specialize. Make specific pot types (water jars, cooking pots, serving bowls) repeatedly to develop speed and consistency.
- **Month 3+:** Production at scale. A skilled thrower produces 30–50 pots per day.

**Quality control:**

- **Wall thickness:** Measure with calipers or estimate by feel. Aim for 4–7 mm consistently.
- **Rim strength:** Thicker rims (6–8 mm) survive use better than thin rims.
- **Symmetry:** Pots should be balanced; wobbling when spinning indicates off-center throwing.
- **Drying without cracking:** Follow drying procedures from Vol 15, Ch 2 (cover with cloth initially to slow drying).

**Output targets:**

- **New thrower:** 5–10 pots per day (weeks 1–4)
- **Developing thrower:** 15–25 pots per day (weeks 5–12)
- **Skilled thrower:** 30–50 pots per day (month 3+)

## Verification

**Success looks like:**
- By week 2: Clay centers consistently; you can feel the balanced rotation
- By week 4: You have thrown 20+ complete pots; most are usable after firing
- By month 2: You are producing 15–20 pots per day
- By month 3: You are producing 30+ pots per day; 90%+ firing success rate
- By month 6: You have thrown 5,000+ pots; production is reliable and fast

**Failure looks like:**
- Clay will not center; wobbles persist
- Pots collapse during throwing; walls thin too much
- Walls crack during drying (poor clay quality or throwing issues)
- Wheel stops constantly or is hard to control (bearing friction issues, imbalance, or axle wobble)

## Common Mistakes

1. **"A smaller flywheel is easier to control"** — Smaller wheels have less momentum and are harder to throw on. 60 cm diameter is optimal.

2. **"I'll use softwood for the flywheel to save weight"** — Softwood is too weak or requires excessive thickness. Use hardwood.

3. **"I'll start throwing immediately without practicing centering"** — Centering is the foundation. Practice until you center consistently before throwing complete pots.

4. **"The wheel is hard to spin; that's normal"** — No. Friction in bearings should be minimal. Fix bearing lubrication or adjustment.

5. **"I'll throw tall pots (30+ cm) on my first tries"** — Tall pots are difficult. Master small bowls (5–10 cm tall) first; advance to taller forms gradually.

6. **"I'll throw pots with thick walls (10+ mm) for strength"** — Too thick. They crack during drying and are heavy. 4–7 mm is standard.

7. **"The wheel vibrates and wobbles; it's fine"** — Wobbling indicates imbalance or axle issues. Fix the balance or straightness before continuing; wobbles make throwing impossible.

8. **"I don't need a bat (removable platform); I'll throw directly on the head"** — Bats allow removal without stopping the wheel. They increase production speed significantly.
