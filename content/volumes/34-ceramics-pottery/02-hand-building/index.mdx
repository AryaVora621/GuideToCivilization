## Overview

Hand-building pottery is the foundation of ceramic craft. Before the pottery wheel (invented ~3000 BCE in the Middle East), all pots were hand-built. You do not need a wheel to produce functional, beautiful pottery. Your settlement can supply vessels for water, food storage, cooking, and trade using hand-building alone.

This chapter teaches three primary hand-building methods: pinch pots (simplest, for small vessels), coil pots (medium-sized vessels, flexible form), and slab pots (flat plates and large vessels). You will learn the physics of how clay thickens and thins, how to avoid cracking during drying, and how to make vessels that are both functional and durable. By week 2 post-collapse, you can be making usable pots. By month 1, you can be producing 100+ vessels per week.

## Prerequisites

- Vol 15, Ch 1: Clay Identification (understanding clay properties and plasticity)
- Vol 14, Ch 6: Clay and Limestone (clay extraction and properties)
- Ability to work with your hands, observing what happens as you shape clay
- Patience: drying takes days; firing takes hours

## Materials & Tools

**For hand-building:**
- Clay (prepared and plastic; see Vol 15, Ch 1)
- Work surface: smooth board, ceramic table, or packed earth (flour on the surface prevents sticking)
- Water (to dampen hands and clay when sticking pieces together)
- Tools (optional but helpful):
  - Sponge (for smoothing and moistening)
  - Wooden modeling tools or sticks (for detail work)
  - Wire (thin string or plant fiber) for cutting clay
  - Ruler (for measuring wall thickness)
  - Calipers (for measuring diameters)

**For drying:**
- Drying racks or shelves (allow air circulation beneath the pot)
- Cloth (lightweight, breathable—muslin or linen—for covering partially dry pots)
- Shade location (protected from rain, sun, and wind)
- Thermometer (optional, to monitor drying conditions)

**For firing:**
- Kiln (see Vol 15, Ch 4: Kiln Design)
- Kiln shelf or clay tile (to place pots on during firing)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Master Pinch Pots (Foundation Technique)

Pinch pots teach you how clay behaves as you thin and shape it.

**Pinch pot procedure:**

1. **Start with a ball:** Roll a fist-sized ball of prepared clay between your palms, compressing out air bubbles. Diameter: ~5 cm.

2. **Create the opening:** Push your thumb into the center of the ball, leaving ~1 cm of clay at the bottom. Do not push all the way through.

3. **Thin the walls:**
   - Pinch the clay gently between thumb (inside) and fingers (outside)
   - Rotate the pot continuously, pinching evenly all around
   - Gradually lift and widen the opening as you thin
   - Target wall thickness: 3–5 mm (roughly pencil-width)
   - Do not thin below 3 mm or the pot is fragile

4. **Shape the rim:**
   - Smooth and even the top edge with your thumb
   - You can fold the rim inward (for a lip) or outward (flare it)
   - Make the rim slightly thicker (5–7 mm) for strength

5. **Final form:**
   - Round pots: maintain spherical shape
   - Oval pots: pinch one side more to elongate
   - Tapered pots: pinch more at the base, less at the opening
   - Target: functional form, balanced weight distribution

**Physics of pinch pots:**

- **Uniform thickness is critical:** If one wall is much thicker, it dries faster, causing uneven stress and cracking.
- **Small diameter is safer:** Pots >10 cm diameter are prone to cracking; &lt;7 cm diameter dries more uniformly.
- **Rim is strongest when reinforced:** Making the rim slightly thicker creates a stronger edge that resists chipping.

**Common errors (pinch pots):**

1. **"The pot cracked because it's air-trapped inside"** — False. Cracks from drying unevenly, not from air. But you can vent air: poke a tiny hole in the base with a stick, then close it.
2. **"I'll make thin walls (2 mm) for delicate pots"** — Too thin. Pots crack during drying and break easily in use. 3–5 mm is minimum.
3. **"I'll make pots with thick walls (10+ mm)"** — Too thick. They take weeks to dry, crack from the inside out, and are heavy and wasteful of clay.

### Step 2: Build Coil Pots (Medium to Large Vessels)

Coil pots allow you to build larger, taller vessels than pinch pots. Coiling is the most versatile hand-building method.

**Coil pot procedure:**

1. **Create coils:**
   - Roll clay between your palms, rolling outward until it is even in diameter (~1 cm)
   - Length: ~30–50 cm per coil (longer coils are easier to work with)
   - Keep coils uniform: they should be the same diameter along their entire length
   - Make many coils; you will use ~20–40 coils for a medium pot

2. **Build the base:**
   - Flatten a ball of clay into a disc ~2 cm thick, diameter 10–20 cm (this is your pot's bottom)
   - Use your hands or a board to smooth the bottom surface
   - Score (roughen) the top surface with a stick or knife to help the first coil adhere

3. **Attach the first coil:**
   - Dampen the scored surface with a wet sponge
   - Attach a coil around the perimeter of the disc
   - Press the coil gently into the groove created by damping
   - Smooth the connection (inside and outside) with your thumb or tool

4. **Stack additional coils:**
   - For each new coil:
     - Score the top surface of the previous coil
     - Dampen it with a sponge
     - Attach the next coil, pressing gently
     - Smooth the interior and exterior junctions
   - Continue adding coils until the pot reaches your desired height

5. **Shape as you build:**
   - After every 3–5 coils, gently shape the pot:
     - Press outward for a wider body
     - Press inward for a narrower neck
     - Smooth the surface with a damp sponge
     - Remove and smooth any visible coil seams if desired (for a unified appearance)

6. **Finish the rim:**
   - Reinforce the rim: coil a thin coil around the top
   - Shape the rim: smooth it, thicken it to 5–7 mm
   - Create a lip by turning the rim inward or outward

7. **Final smoothing:**
   - Use a damp sponge to smooth the entire pot
   - Remove visible coil marks if desired (for a finished appearance)
   - Paddle the pot gently with a flat tool to strengthen walls and smooth surface

**Coil pot sizes:**

- **Small:** 5–10 coils, height 5–10 cm, diameter 5–8 cm
- **Medium:** 15–25 coils, height 15–25 cm, diameter 10–15 cm
- **Large:** 30–50 coils, height 30–50 cm, diameter 15–25 cm

**Drying considerations for coil pots:**

- **Larger pots take longer:** A 30 cm tall pot may take 2–3 weeks to dry completely
- **Coil-built pots are prone to cracking along coil lines:** If coils are not well-smoothed together, they can separate during drying
- **Even drying is critical:** Cover a partially-dry pot with cloth to slow the top from drying faster than the interior

### Step 3: Build Slab Pots (Flat Forms and Large Vessels)

Slab pots are made from flat sheets of clay, like building with clay "boards." They are useful for plates, platters, and square or rectangular vessels.

**Slab pot procedure:**

1. **Create slab:**
   - Roll out clay to even thickness (~5 mm) using your hands or a roller (wooden pin or dowel)
   - Slab dimensions: 20 cm × 30 cm (adjustable based on your desired pot)
   - Ensure even thickness: clay should be uniform throughout

2. **Cut shapes:**
   - Decide your form: square box, rectangular tray, flat plate, or complex shapes
   - Cut slabs to size using a knife or wire
   - For a simple box: 1 base + 4 walls (each same dimensions)
   - For a plate: 1 large circular slab

3. **Assemble (for box-shaped pots):**
   - Stand the base slab flat
   - Score the edges (roughen them with a knife or stick)
   - Dampen the scored edges
   - Attach side walls: stand them upright, press them against the scored base
   - Support walls with props (small stones or wadded clay) to prevent collapse while joining

4. **Reinforce seams:**
   - Apply a thin coil of clay along each interior seam where walls meet the base
   - Smooth the coil into the seam, welding walls and base together
   - Repeat on exterior seams if desired (for extra strength)
   - Do the same at corners where walls meet each other

5. **Reinforce the rim:**
   - Add a thin coil around the top edge
   - Smooth and thicken to 5–7 mm for strength

6. **Finish:**
   - Smooth all surfaces with a damp sponge
   - Create a lip or decorative edge if desired
   - Check for uniformity and balance

**Slab pot form variations:**

- **Flat plate:** Single large circular slab, ~20 cm diameter, slightly raised rim for functional edge
- **Serving tray:** Rectangular slab, 30 cm × 20 cm, low raised edges (2–3 cm tall)
- **Box or storage vessel:** Four walls + base, square or rectangular, with lid (optional)
- **Tilework:** Individual tiles (15 cm × 15 cm, 1 cm thick) that can be assembled into larger structures

**Drying considerations for slab pots:**

- **Slab-built pots are prone to warping:** Flat surfaces can bow during drying. Turn plates or trays occasionally to even out moisture loss.
- **Uneven drying causes cracking:** Cover partially dry pots with cloth to prevent the top from drying too fast.
- **Thick seams crack:** If coils used to reinforce seams are too thick, they crack during drying. Use thin coils (0.5 cm diameter) for seams.

### Step 4: Manage Drying to Prevent Cracking

Cracking is the most common failure of hand-built pottery. Understanding drying is essential.

**Why pottery cracks during drying:**

1. **Uneven moisture loss:** If the pot's surface dries faster than the interior, the surface shrinks faster, pulling inward and creating stress. The interior shrinks later, but the surface is already rigid, causing cracks.

2. **Rigid seams:** Where two pieces are joined (coil to base, or slab to slab), the seam shrinks differently from the walls. If the seam is too thick or has thick coil reinforcement, it cracks as it dries faster than the walls.

3. **Trapped air:** If air pockets are trapped in clay (not fully wedged out during preparation), they collapse during drying, creating voids and cracks.

**Drying timeline and management:**

**Days 1–2 (soft leather-hard stage):**
- Pots are still somewhat flexible but hold shape
- Smooth coil lines if desired (clay is still plastic enough to reshape)
- Cover with cloth to slow initial drying
- Do not move the pot much; it is fragile

**Days 3–5 (leather-hard stage):**
- Pots are firm but not fully rigid
- Surface is dry to the touch, but interior is still damp
- Remove cloth covering to allow continued, gradual drying
- Turn large pots occasionally (every 12 hours) to even out moisture loss
- Risk of cracking is high; do not expose to direct sun or heat

**Days 6–10 (stiff leather-hard to bone-dry stage):**
- Pot is nearly fully dry
- Shrinkage has mostly occurred
- Keep in shade, protected from wind
- Slow drying is better than fast

**Days 11–14 (bone-dry stage):**
- Pot feels light and sounds hollow when tapped
- No visible moisture; clay is pale in color
- Pot is ready for firing

**Drying optimization strategies:**

1. **Cover with cloth initially:** Wet cloth (or thin muslin) slows evaporation, allowing moisture to move from interior to surface evenly. As drying progresses (days 3–5), gradually expose the pot.

2. **Rotate large pots:** Every 12 hours, rotate a large pot 180° to even out moisture loss from different sides.

3. **Vent the interior (optional):** If you are worried about trapped air, poke a very small hole (2–3 mm) in the pot's base or sides with a stick, then smooth it closed. This allows air inside to escape slowly during drying.

4. **Avoid drafts and direct sun:** Wind and direct sun cause rapid surface drying. Dry in shade, sheltered from wind.

5. **Group pots for humidity:** Potters dry pots close together because they transpire moisture, creating a humid microclimate that slows drying evenly.

6. **Test for dryness:** A bone-dry pot is light, pale in color, and sounds hollow. If you break a test piece (same clay, same wall thickness), the interior should show no discoloration or damp spots.

### Step 5: Repair Cracks Before Firing

Small cracks that appear during drying can often be repaired.

**Repair procedure:**

1. **Identify the crack:** Look for any visible separation in the clay.

2. **Widen the crack slightly:** Use a knife to carefully open the crack a bit (easier to fill than a hairline crack).

3. **Fill with slip:** Prepare a thick slurry (liquid clay) by mixing clay powder with a little water. Fill the crack with this slip.

4. **Smooth and compress:** Use a tool or your finger to smooth the slip into the crack, compressing it so it is well-integrated with the clay on either side.

5. **Allow to dry:** The crack should be nearly invisible after drying if done well.

**Prevention is better than repair:** Follow the drying strategies above to minimize cracking in the first place.

### Step 6: Understand Wall Thickness and Firing

The thickness of your pot's walls affects how it fires and how durable it is.

**Wall thickness guidelines:**

- **Too thin (&lt;3 mm):** Pot is fragile, breaks easily, may shatter in the kiln
- **Optimal (3–8 mm):** Strong enough to use, dries evenly, fires well
- **Too thick (>10 mm):** Takes weeks to dry, prone to cracking, heavy, wastes clay

**Thickness variations:**

- **Base:** Slightly thicker (6–8 mm) for strength
- **Walls:** Medium (4–6 mm) for balance
- **Rim:** Slightly thicker (5–7 mm) for resistance to chipping

**Checking wall thickness:**

- Visual: Hold the pot to light; thin walls appear translucent
- Physical: Use calipers or a ruler to measure through a small hole (if you can, or on test pieces)
- Feel: Run your thumb inside and outside; you should feel thick clay, not thin

## Verification

**Success looks like:**
- By day 3: You have made your first pinch pot and begun drying
- By week 1: You have made 5+ pinch pots and 1+ coil pots; several are drying
- By week 2: First pots are bone-dry and ready for firing
- By week 4: You have fired your first batch (10+ pots); several are successfully fired and functional
- By month 3: You are producing 50+ pots per week; most are usable for storage and cooking

**Failure looks like:**
- Pots crack extensively during drying (poor drying management)
- Pots shatter in the kiln (too thin, or trapped air)
- Pots are weak and break easily after firing (poor clay quality or too thin)

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll make thin walls (2 mm) for delicate pottery"** — Too thin. Pots crack during drying and break in use. 3–5 mm is minimum.

2. **"I'll dry pots in the sun to speed drying"** — Direct sun causes uneven drying and cracking. Dry in shade.

3. **"I'll not smooth coils together; the pot looks more rustic"** — Unsmoothed coils separate during drying. Smooth them or accept cracking.

4. **"I'll attach coils without dampening the surface"** — Coils will not adhere and will separate during drying. Always dampen and score before joining.

5. **"The pot is done drying after 5 days"** — Too fast. Pots take 1–2 weeks to fully dry. Check by weight (heavy = still damp) and sound (dull = still damp).

6. **"Cracks in dried pots will disappear in the kiln"** — Cracks are permanent after drying. Repair before firing or accept the crack.

7. **"I'll make large pots (50 cm tall) with hand-building"** — Large pots are prone to warping and cracking. For large vessels, use coil-building with thick coils and take weeks to dry carefully.

8. **"I don't need to vent air from inside the pot"** — Trapped air can cause the pot to explode in the kiln. Poke small vent holes (close them before firing if visible).
