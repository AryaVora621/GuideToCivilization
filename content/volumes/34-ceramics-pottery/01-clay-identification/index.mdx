## Overview

Not all clay is equally suitable for pottery. The same clay that makes excellent bricks may be too coarse and gritty for fine pots. The clay that throws beautifully on a wheel may be too plastic for hand-building without warping. Your settlement will benefit from pottery—vessels for water storage, food preservation, cooking, medicine—but only if you find and use clay suitable for the purpose.

This chapter teaches you three practical field tests that require no equipment and tell you whether a clay deposit is suitable for pottery. You will learn to assess plasticity (how easily a clay can be molded), shrinkage (how much it shrinks during drying), and firing behavior (what happens when you fire it in a kiln). By applying these tests to multiple clay sources, you can build a mental map of your region's clay quality and choose the best source for different pottery purposes.

## Prerequisites

- Vol 14, Ch 1: Geology Basics (rock types, mineral composition, soil and sediment)
- Vol 14, Ch 6: Clay and Limestone (clay deposits, weathering, plasticity basics)
- Ability to observe physical properties (color, texture, stickiness)
- Willingness to conduct hands-on experiments with clay samples

## Materials & Tools

**For testing clay samples:**
- Water (for adding to clay, testing plasticity)
- Containers for samples (cloth bags, ceramic pots, or baskets)
- Work surface (smooth board, ceramic plate, or clean ground)
- Measuring tools: ruler (for measuring coil diameter), calipers or string (for measuring shrinkage)
- Drying area: shaded, flat, protected from rain and direct sun
- Firing facility: access to a kiln capable of 900–1100°C (see Vol 15, Ch 4: Kiln Design)
- Notebook and pencil for recording observations

**Optional but useful:**
- Magnifying glass (10x) for examining clay particle size
- Scale (for measuring weight changes during drying and firing)
- Thermometer (for noting air temperature and kiln temperature)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Collect Clay Samples from Multiple Sources

Do not test only one clay source. Collect samples from at least 5–10 locations in your settlement area and surrounding region. You are building a resource map.

**Sampling protocol:**

1. **Identify potential clay sources** (see Vol 14, Ch 6 for location clues)
2. **For each location, dig down** to expose pure clay (beneath topsoil)
3. **Collect a sample** at depth: at least 1–2 kg per source, taken from 0.5–2 meters below surface
4. **Label each sample** clearly: location name, depth, color, date

**Example sampling map (for a typical settlement region):**

```
Clay Source 1: Stream bottom (wet)
Clay Source 2: Hilltop weathered granite (residual)
Clay Source 3: Valley clay pit (old brickyard)
Clay Source 4: Lakebed deposit (fine texture)
Clay Source 5: Alluvial plain (mixed sand and clay)
Clay Source 6: Clay layer in road cut (uniform color)
```

Collect at least 2 kg from each source. You will use these for testing.

### Step 2: Assess Plasticity (How Easily Clay Molds)

Plasticity is the clay's ability to be shaped without breaking. High-plasticity clay is ideal for throwing on a wheel; medium plasticity is suitable for hand-building; low plasticity is only usable for bricks or coarse pottery.

**Test procedure:**

**Part A: Coil test (quick plasticity assessment)**

1. Take a sample of clay (~100 g)
2. Add water slowly while kneading until the clay is plastic (becomes soft and moldable)
3. Form the clay into a ball
4. Roll the ball on a flat surface using your hands to form a coil ~1 cm diameter and ~30 cm long
5. Gently bend the coil into a complete circle (loops back to touch itself)
6. Observe the result:
   - **No cracking at the bend:** Excellent plasticity (ideal for wheel throwing)
   - **Small hairline cracks at the bend:** Good plasticity (suitable for hand-building and wheel throwing)
   - **Visible cracks that propagate:** Fair plasticity (hand-building possible, wheel throwing difficult)
   - **Coil breaks before you can bend it; too gritty to form properly:** Poor plasticity (unsuitable for fine pottery; only for bricks)

**Plasticity scale (0–10):**
- **9–10:** Excellent (high-quality wheel clay)
- **7–8:** Good (versatile for most pottery)
- **5–6:** Fair (hand-building possible, wheel difficult)
- **3–4:** Poor (bricks only)
- **0–2:** Very poor (unusable for pottery)

**Part B: Thin wall test (advanced plasticity assessment)**

For clays that pass the coil test:

1. Form a small pinch pot (thumb-sized, roughly spherical) from the clay sample
2. Thin the walls by pinching and stretching until the walls are ~5 mm thick
3. Observe:
   - **Walls thin without breaking or cracking:** Excellent plasticity
   - **Walls thin but show some fine cracks:** Good plasticity
   - **Walls break or tear when thinned:** Fair to poor plasticity

**Recording plasticity:**

For each clay sample, record:
- Location name
- Coil test result (excellent / good / fair / poor)
- Thin-wall test result (if conducted)
- Note any visual characteristics that indicate plasticity level

### Step 3: Assess Shrinkage (How Much Clay Shrinks When Dry)

Clay shrinks as water evaporates during drying. Excessive shrinkage causes cracking and warping; insufficient shrinkage indicates clay is too sandy.

**Test procedure:**

**Part A: Brick shrinkage test (simplest method)**

1. Form a brick-shaped sample: ~15 cm × 10 cm × 5 cm
2. Mark the dimensions with a stick or pencil:
   - Mark a line along the length (side-to-side)
   - Mark a line along the width (end-to-end)
   - Mark corners for reference
3. Allow the brick to dry slowly in shade for 5–7 days (not in direct sun or near heat, which causes rapid drying and cracking)
4. Measure the dried brick's dimensions:
   - Compare to original marks
   - Measure length, width, and height
5. Calculate shrinkage percentage:
   - Shrinkage % = ((original dimension - final dimension) / original dimension) × 100
   - Example: original length 15 cm, final length 14.4 cm = (0.6 / 15) × 100 = 4% shrinkage

**Shrinkage evaluation:**

- **Drying shrinkage &lt;5%:** Excellent (minimal cracking risk)
- **Drying shrinkage 5–10%:** Good (acceptable, monitor for cracks)
- **Drying shrinkage 10–15%:** Fair (expect some cracks, especially in thick pieces)
- **Drying shrinkage >15%:** Poor (severe cracking, not suitable for pottery without modifications)

**Part B: Extended drying test (thorough method)**

For clay that shows good or better plasticity:

1. Form several small test pieces (cylinders ~5 cm tall, 3 cm diameter)
2. Dry for 2 weeks (instead of just 5–7 days), allowing complete water evaporation
3. Measure again (final drying shrinkage)
4. Note the degree of cracking (none, hairline, significant)

This extended test reveals whether hidden shrinkage occurs after initial drying.

**Part C: Weight loss during drying (quantitative test)**

For more precise assessment:

1. Form a sample and weigh it (wet weight)
2. Dry for 7 days and weigh again (dry weight)
3. Calculate water loss: (wet weight - dry weight) / wet weight × 100
4. Water loss percentage correlates with shrinkage: higher water loss = higher shrinkage

Example: wet weight 200 g, dry weight 140 g = 60 g water loss = 30% water loss = expect ~10–12% linear shrinkage (shrinkage scales roughly with water loss, but is less dramatic).

**Recording shrinkage:**

For each clay sample, record:
- Drying shrinkage percentage (measured after 7 days)
- Cracks observed (none / minor / significant)
- Final drying shrinkage percentage (if conducted at 2 weeks)
- Note color changes during drying

### Step 4: Assess Firing Behavior (What Happens in the Kiln)

Firing behavior is the most important test because it reveals whether the clay is suitable for pottery that must last. Poor firing behavior makes unusable pottery.

**Test procedure:**

**Part A: Small-scale firing test (most practical)**

1. Create 5–10 small test pieces:
   - Pinch pots (~5 cm diameter, 5 mm wall thickness)
   - Coils (~1 cm diameter, 10 cm long, coiled into a spiral)
   - Flat tiles (5 cm × 5 cm × 5 mm)
2. Dry all pieces completely (7–14 days in shade)
3. Fire in a kiln at 900–1000°C (see Vol 15, Ch 4: Kiln Design for kiln construction):
   - Place pieces on a kiln shelf
   - Bring kiln temperature to 900°C over 2–4 hours (gradual heating prevents cracking)
   - Hold at 900°C for 2–4 hours (allows mineral transformation)
   - Cool for 12–24 hours
4. Remove fired pieces and examine:
   - **Hardness:** Strike with a metal object. Good pottery rings; poor pottery is soft or crumbly
   - **Color:** Note color change (indicates iron content and firing success)
   - **Warping:** Note if pieces twisted, bent, or distorted during firing
   - **Cracking:** Note if pieces cracked or shattered
   - **Porosity:** Break a piece. Examine the cross-section. Dense and uniform is good; porous or uneven is poor

**Firing behavior evaluation:**

- **Hard, dense, ring sound, minimal cracking, slight color change:** Excellent (ideal pottery clay)
- **Moderately hard, minor warping or cracking:** Good (acceptable for non-critical pottery)
- **Soft, crumbly, significant warping:** Poor (unsuitable for lasting pottery)
- **Shattered or exploded in kiln:** Very poor (unusable; contains problematic impurities or trapped air)

**Part B: High-fire test (advanced)**

If you will build a higher-temperature kiln (1000–1200°C for stoneware or tile production), repeat the firing test at higher temperature:

1. Fire test pieces at 1100°C instead of 900°C
2. Assess results the same way
3. Note whether clay becomes more or less dense at higher temperature

Some clays improve at higher temperatures; others become brittle or glassy.

**Part C: Color and texture comparison**

For each fired piece:

1. **Color:** Record the fired color (red, orange, tan, buff, brown, gray, black, etc.). Color indicates iron content and firing success.
2. **Surface texture:** Smooth or rough? Vitrified (glassy) or matte (dull)?
3. **Weight:** Higher-fired clay is denser and heavier

**Recording firing behavior:**

For each clay sample, record:
- Firing temperature (900°C, 1000°C, etc.)
- Result (excellent / good / fair / poor / unusable)
- Color and texture
- Any cracking, warping, or shattering
- Suitability for different uses (wheel pottery, hand-built pots, tiles, bricks)

### Step 5: Identify Contaminants and Assess Purity

Some clays contain impurities that ruin pottery.

**Contaminants to test for:**

**Mica (shiny flakes, visible in samples):**
- Effect: Can reduce strength; may cause lamination during firing
- Test: Look under magnifying glass. Mica appears as shiny flakes.
- Acceptance: Small amounts (&lt;10% visible) are acceptable; high amounts (>20%) are problematic

**Iron oxides (clay is reddish or orange-colored):**
- Effect: Clay fires darker (red, brown, black), is harder, but may become brittle if iron content is very high
- Test: Examine fired pieces. Red or brown color = present; buff or white = absent
- Acceptance: Iron oxide is generally acceptable; moderate amounts are fine. Extreme iron content (very dark red) may cause brittleness.

**Calcium carbonate (limestone; visible white grains):**
- Effect: Reacts during firing, can cause spalling (pieces flaking off the surface) if not fully decomposed
- Test: Acid test—drop dilute HCl on sample. If it fizzes, calcium carbonate is present.
- Acceptance: Small amounts acceptable; large amounts problematic. If fizzing is visible, blend with acid-free clay.

**Organic matter (visible plant roots, wood, decomposing material):**
- Effect: Burns out during firing, leaving holes and weakening the pottery
- Test: Observe visually. Look for fibers, roots, decomposing plant matter.
- Acceptance: None. Use only clay that is clean.

**Salt (rare in inland areas, but check if near coast or in arid regions):**
- Effect: Causes white crust on surface (efflorescence) and may attract moisture
- Test: Taste a wet sample or dissolve some dried clay in water and taste. Salty taste = present.
- Acceptance: Unacceptable for fine pottery; acceptable for bricks.

**Fine-grained sand (clay feels gritty when rubbed between teeth—very fine test):**
- Effect: Reduces plasticity, makes clay coarse
- Test: Rub dried clay powder between your teeth. Gritty sensation = sand present.
- Acceptance: &lt;10% sand is acceptable; >20% sand makes clay unsuitable for fine pottery.

### Step 6: Compile Your Clay Resource Map

After testing 5–10 clay sources, summarize your findings in a reference document.

**Example clay resource map:**

```
CLAY DEPOSITS — SETTLEMENT REGION

Source 1 — Stream Bottom (2 km east)
- Plasticity: Good (coil bends with minor cracking)
- Shrinkage: 8% (acceptable)
- Firing: Excellent (hard, dense, buff color)
- Contaminants: None visible
- Quality: EXCELLENT for wheel pottery
- Quantity: Large (unlimited due to ongoing river deposition)
- Accessibility: Easy (exposed on bank)
- RECOMMENDATION: Primary source for fine pottery

Source 2 — Valley Clay Pit (1 km south)
- Plasticity: Medium (coil breaks slightly)
- Shrinkage: 12% (fair; expect minor cracking in thick pieces)
- Firing: Good (moderately hard, reddish-tan color)
- Contaminants: Visible iron oxide (red color), minor mica
- Quality: GOOD for hand-building and bricks
- Quantity: Large (old brickyard, substantial clay layer)
- Accessibility: Moderate (requires some digging to reach good clay)
- RECOMMENDATION: Secondary source; good for building pots, good for bricks

Source 3 — Hilltop Residual Clay (3 km north)
- Plasticity: Poor (coil too gritty, breaks easily)
- Shrinkage: 4% (low; indicates high sand content)
- Firing: Fair (somewhat soft after firing)
- Contaminants: High sand content (visibly gritty)
- Quality: POOR for fine pottery; acceptable for coarse handmade vessels or bricks
- Quantity: Medium
- Accessibility: Easy (surface exposure)
- RECOMMENDATION: Tertiary source; use for bricks or coarse ware only

Source 4 — Alluvial Plain (5 km northeast)
- Plasticity: Excellent (coil bends without cracking)
- Shrinkage: 6% (excellent)
- Firing: Excellent (hard, dense, gray color, rings when struck)
- Contaminants: None visible
- Quality: EXCELLENT for wheel pottery and fine hand-building
- Quantity: Very large (extensive deposit)
- Accessibility: Easy (old clay pit, well-exposed)
- RECOMMENDATION: Primary source; ideal for all pottery purposes

[Continue for sources 5–10...]

PRIORITY RANKING:
1. Alluvial Plain (Source 4) — use for finest pottery, wheels, complex vessels
2. Stream Bottom (Source 1) — use for fine pottery, secondary source
3. Valley Pit (Source 2) — use for hand-building, bricks, practical vessels
4. Hilltop Residual (Source 3) — use only for coarse ware or bricks if others are unavailable
```

This map becomes a reference for your pottery-making decisions.

### Step 7: Optimize Clay Use by Blending (Advanced)

If no single clay source is ideal, blend clays to create an optimized mixture.

**Blending strategy:**

**Problem:** Your best clay (Source A) has 12% shrinkage, causing cracking in thick pieces.
**Solution:** Blend with a lower-shrinkage clay (Source B, 6% shrinkage) in a 1:1 ratio.
**Result:** 1 part Source A + 1 part Source B = blended clay with ~9% shrinkage, acceptable for most pottery.

**Problem:** Your clay (Source C) lacks plasticity for wheel throwing.
**Solution:** Blend with a high-plasticity clay (Source D) in a 2:1 ratio.
**Result:** 2 parts Source C + 1 part Source D = blended clay with improved plasticity for wheels.

**Blending procedure:**

1. Prepare clay samples from both sources (fist-sized each)
2. Mix in your intended ratio (e.g., 1:1 means equal amounts)
3. Knead thoroughly (10–15 minutes) to ensure uniform mixing
4. Test the blended clay using the three main tests (plasticity, shrinkage, firing)
5. Adjust ratio if needed and retest
6. Once optimized, use the ratio consistently for production

## Verification

**Success looks like:**
- By week 2: You have collected and tested clay from 5–10 local sources
- By week 4: You have a written clay resource map with firing test results
- By week 6: You are making pottery from your best clay source
- By month 3: You are producing functional pots that survive repeated use without cracking

**Failure looks like:**
- Clay samples show poor or fair firing results
- Pottery cracks or shatters after a few weeks of use
- Clay shrinkage is excessive (>15%), causing firing failures
- No suitable clay sources are accessible without extensive mining

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll use any clay without testing"** — You will fail. Test first; produce later.

2. **"The coil test alone is sufficient"** — Coil test shows plasticity but not firing behavior. You need all three tests (plasticity, shrinkage, firing).

3. **"I'll test only one clay source"** — Different sources have different properties. Test multiple sources; you will find variation.

4. **"I'll dry clay quickly in the sun"** — Rapid drying causes cracking. Dry in shade; slow drying is better.

5. **"I can ignore shrinkage; it's not important"** — High shrinkage causes cracking during drying, before the kiln. Assess it carefully.

6. **"If it fires hard, it's good pottery"** — Good pottery is hard, dense, and does not crack or warp. Just hard is not enough.

7. **"I'll use clay from the top layer of the soil"** — Top layer is topsoil (organic matter, roots). Dig down to pure clay layers.

8. **"I tested at 900°C; it will be fine at 1000°C"** — Different firing temperatures produce different results. Test at the temperature you actually use.
