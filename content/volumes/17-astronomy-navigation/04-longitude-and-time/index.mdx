## Overview

Latitude (north-south) can be found from star observations alone. Longitude (east-west) requires knowing the time precisely—a difficult problem that stumped navigators for centuries.

Without electric clocks or radio time signals, post-collapse navigators must choose between: (1) keeping a precise mechanical chronometer, (2) using lunar distance observations, or (3) accepting modest longitude uncertainty.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of right ascension and sidereal time (Vol 34, Ch 1)
- Understanding of the moon's orbital motion
- Ability to measure angles precisely
- Access to astronomical tables
- Mathematical skill (trigonometry and logarithms)

## Materials & Tools

- Chronometer or precise clock (for method 1)
- Sextant for measuring angles (for method 2)
- Star catalogs with right ascensions
- Lunar distance tables
- Astronomical almanac or ephemeris
- Mathematical tables or calculator
- Notebook for calculations

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Understanding the Problem

**Step 1: Why longitude is hard.**

Longitude is defined by time:
- Earth rotates 360 degrees in 24 hours
- Earth rotates 15 degrees per hour
- Earth rotates 0.25 degrees per minute
- A 1-minute error in timekeeping = 0.25-degree longitude error (about 28 km at the equator)

**For accurate longitude, you need accurate time.**

**Step 2: Know your reference meridian.**

Longitude is measured from a reference (0-degree meridian). Modern convention uses Greenwich, England.

**Post-collapse, any reference works:**
- Choose the meridian of your home port or city
- Measure all longitudes relative to that meridian
- When meeting other navigators, agree on a common reference

### Part 2: The Chronometer Method

**Step 1: Keep track of Greenwich Mean Time (or your reference time).**

A chronometer is a precise mechanical clock kept on shipboard.

**Before departing:**
1. Set the chronometer to Greenwich time (or agreed reference time)
2. Keep it in a temperature-stable location
3. Wind it regularly (some chronometers need daily winding)
4. Protect it from vibrations

**Step 2: Observe local noon (sun at highest point).**

1. Watch the sun's position throughout the day
2. When the sun reaches its highest point, record the chronometer time
3. The chronometer time is "Greenwich time" (if it's accurate)
4. The time difference = local hour angle = longitude

**Conversion:**
- Longitude (degrees) = (chronometer time - local time) × 15 degrees per hour

**Example:**
- Local noon occurs when the chronometer reads 3:15 PM (Greenwich time)
- Time difference = 3 hours 15 minutes = 3.25 hours
- Longitude = 3.25 × 15 = 48.75° West (behind Greenwich)

**The observer is about 48.75° west of the Greenwich meridian.**

**Step 3: Maintain chronometer accuracy.**

Chronometers drift over time. A good chronometer drifts less than 1 second per day.

**Monitoring drift:**
1. Observe local noon daily
2. Calculate the chronometer error each day
3. Plot the error over time
4. If the error grows steadily, note the rate (e.g., "losing 1 second per day")

**Correcting for drift:**
1. Apply a correction factor to the chronometer reading
2. Example: If losing 1 second per day, after 30 days the chronometer is 30 seconds slow; add 30 seconds to the reading

### Part 3: The Lunar Distance Method

**Step 1: Understand the principle.**

The moon moves about 13 degrees eastward per day, passing near different stars.

**The procedure:**
1. Measure the angle between the moon and a known star
2. Compare to predicted values in astronomical tables
3. Find the time when that angle occurs
4. Knowing the time = knowing the longitude

**Why this works:**
- The moon's position changes predictably with time
- If you observe the moon's position relative to stars, you can look up the exact time
- Knowing the time tells you the longitude

**Step 2: Prepare lunar distance tables.**

Lunar distance is the angle between the moon and a reference star.

**Reference stars for lunar distances:**
- Spica (brightest in tables)
- Aldebaran
- Antares
- Polaris
- Regulus
- Others (any bright star)

**Tables show:**
- For each hour of Greenwich time, the predicted lunar distance
- Tables span 6-12 months and must be updated regularly (from an ephemeris)

**Step 3: Observe the lunar distance.**

**Requirements:**
- Moon is visible (not too close to new moon)
- Reference star is visible
- Sextant for measuring the angle

**Procedure:**
1. At least 2-3 hours before or after local noon (when the moon and star aren't too close)
2. Measure the angle between the moon's center and the star's center using a sextant
3. Record the angle to the nearest arcminute (0.017 degrees)
4. Note the chronometer time when the measurement was taken

**Step 4: Correct the observed distance.**

Raw sextant measurements include errors:

**Corrections needed:**
1. **Sextant error** (typically 0-2 arcminutes): Subtract from reading
2. **Refraction** (about 0.5 degrees near horizon, less at altitude): Subtract from reading
3. **Parallax** (the moon's position differs slightly depending on observer location on Earth): Depends on latitude and moon altitude
4. **Semidiameter** (the moon and star have angular size): Subtract half-sizes

**Correcting for refraction and parallax requires:**
- Moon's altitude (measured with sextant)
- Observer's latitude
- Mathematical tables or calculation

**Simplified approach:**
- For rough navigation, ignore parallax and semidiameter corrections
- Accuracy is reduced to about 1-2 degrees longitude, but the method still works

**Step 5: Look up the time in lunar tables.**

**Using pre-computed tables:**
1. Calculate the corrected lunar distance
2. Look up this distance in the lunar distance tables
3. Find the Greenwich time when this distance occurred
4. This is the Greenwich time at the moment of observation

**Example:**
- Observed lunar distance (corrected): 62°15'
- Tables show this distance at 14:30 Greenwich time
- Local chronometer time was 10:30 (the time observed, if no chronometer)
- Time difference = 4 hours
- Longitude = 4 × 15 = 60° West

### Part 4: Lunar Distance Procedure with Worked Example

**Step 1: Prepare materials.**

Assuming a working lunar distance table (from an ephemeris):
- Date: June 15, 2026
- Reference star: Spica
- Observer's chronometer: Available (but may be inaccurate)

**Step 2: Observe the lunar distance.**

- Moon altitude: 45°
- Star altitude: 30°
- Sextant reading (moon to star): 68°22'
- Chronometer time: 18:45 (6:45 PM on June 15)

**Step 3: Apply corrections.**

Corrections:
- Sextant error: -1' (subtract 1 arcminute)
- Refraction (average of moon and star altitudes, ~37.5°): -1' (very small at this altitude)
- Parallax and semidiameter: Ignored (for rough navigation)

Corrected distance: 68°22' - 1' - 1' = 68°20'

**Step 4: Look up in tables.**

Lunar distance tables for June 15 (Spica reference):
- 18:00 GMT: Distance = 68°05'
- 18:30 GMT: Distance = 68°18'
- 19:00 GMT: Distance = 68°31'

Interpolating: The corrected distance of 68°20' corresponds to approximately 18:33 GMT.

**Step 5: Calculate longitude.**

- Greenwich time (from tables): 18:33
- Chronometer time (observed): 18:45
- Difference: 18:45 - 18:33 = 12 minutes = 0.2 hours
- Longitude = 0.2 × 15 = 3° East

**The observer is about 3° east of the reference meridian.**

### Part 5: Building a Lunar Distance Table

**Step 1: Understand what's needed.**

If no published lunar distance table is available, one can be constructed from an ephemeris (a reference giving moon and star positions).

**For each day:**
1. Calculate the moon's RA and Dec (from ephemeris)
2. Calculate the reference star's RA and Dec (constant, from star catalog)
3. Calculate the angle between them using spherical trigonometry
4. Record this angle against Greenwich time

**Step 2: Use spherical trigonometry.**

The angle between two celestial objects on the celestial sphere:

cos(distance) = sin(Dec₁) × sin(Dec₂) + cos(Dec₁) × cos(Dec₂) × cos(RA₂ - RA₁)

Where:
- RA, Dec are right ascension and declination
- distance is the angular separation

**This calculation requires:**
- Mathematical tables or calculator
- Understanding of spherical trigonometry

**Simplified alternative:**
- Use an ephemeris that already lists lunar distances (easier)

### Part 6: Combining Methods

**Step 1: Use both methods together.**

A navigator with both a chronometer and lunar distance observations can:

**Method 1: Chronometer, if accurate:**
- Provides longitude directly
- Faster than lunar distances
- Requires good timekeeping

**Method 2: Lunar distances, if chronometer is unreliable:**
- Provides time (hence longitude)
- More work but doesn't require an accurate chronometer
- Reduces accumulating chronometer error

**Step 2: Cross-check and average.**

If both methods are used:
1. Calculate longitude from chronometer method
2. Calculate longitude from lunar distance method
3. If results differ by over 2-3 degrees, recheck observations
4. Average the results if both are reliable

## Verification

A successful longitude measurement results in:

- **Chronometer method:** Uncertainty of 1-2 degrees (assuming chronometer drifts predictably)
- **Lunar distance method:** Uncertainty of 1-2 degrees (without parallax correction); under 1 degree (with parallax correction)
- **Combined method:** Uncertainty under 1 degree

A failed measurement shows:

- **Implausible drift:** Chronometer loses or gains over 30 seconds per day (indicates problem)
- **Inconsistent lunar distances:** Repeated observations differ by over 1 degree (indicates poor measurement or calculation)
- **Large discrepancy:** Chronometer and lunar distance methods disagree by over 5 degrees (one method has an error)

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing sidereal time and solar time:** Sidereal time (based on stars) differs from solar time (based on sun) by about 4 minutes per day
- **Wrong reference star:** Using the wrong star makes the lunar distance table lookup impossible
- **Forgetting to apply corrections:** Sextant, refraction, and parallax corrections are essential for accuracy
- **Chronometer drift:** Assuming the chronometer is perfect when it drifts 1+ second per day
- **Reading tables incorrectly:** Lunar distance tables require careful interpolation
- **Mixed unit errors:** Confusing hours with degrees when converting time to longitude
