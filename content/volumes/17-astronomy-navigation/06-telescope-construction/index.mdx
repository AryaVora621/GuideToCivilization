## Overview

A telescope is a tool for collecting and focusing light from distant objects. By gathering more light than your eye alone, telescopes make faint stars visible and reveal details on the Moon and planets. Understanding how telescopes work, and being able to build one, transforms your astronomical capabilities.

This chapter teaches two fundamental telescope designs:

1. **Refractor telescopes:** Use two lenses (objective and eyepiece) to magnify distant objects. Simpler to build, but large objectives become heavy and expensive to grind.
2. **Reflector telescopes:** Use a curved mirror (objective) and a smaller mirror or lens to direct light to the eye. Lighter, more stable, and easier to achieve large apertures.

Both designs follow the same optical principle: the objective collects light and brings it to a focus, and the eyepiece magnifies that focused image. Your ability to build a telescope depends on three things: obtaining or grinding quality optics, achieving mechanical precision, and understanding how to align the components.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of lens properties: focal length, curvature, refraction (Vol 29)
- Experience grinding and polishing glass (Vol 32, glass-grinding)
- Ability to work with metal and wood tools (measuring, filing, drilling, sawing)
- Understanding of angular magnification and field of view
- Patience with mechanical alignment—precision matters, and small errors compound

## Materials & Tools

### For a Refractor Telescope (Doublet Design)

**Optical components:**
- Objective lens (hand-ground or salvaged): minimum 2 inches (50 mm) aperture, focal length under 100 cm (easier to manage)
- Eyepiece lens: a simpler plano-convex lens with focal length 1-3 cm
- Secondary lens or prism (optional): for erecting the image (inverted is acceptable for astronomy)

**Structural components:**
- Brass tubing: 1-2 inches outer diameter, wall thickness 1-2 mm (length = objective focal length + 10-20 cm)
- Wood blocks or machined aluminum: for mounting the objective and eyepiece
- Threaded rods (M8 or M10): for focusing mechanism and altitude/azimuth adjustments
- Metal straps or bands: to hold the tube in position

**Mechanical tools:**
- Lathe or drilling machine (for precision holes and threads)
- Files, saw, sandpaper
- Calipers and ruler (for measuring and alignment)
- Wrench set (for assembling mechanical components)

### For a Reflector Telescope (Primary Mirror Design)

**Optical components:**
- Primary mirror: concave, hand-polished to a paraboloid shape; typical aperture 6-12 inches (15-30 cm) for hand-made telescopes
- Secondary mirror (flat): smaller mirror (1-1.5 inches) to direct light to the eyepiece
- Eyepiece: same as refractor, 1-3 cm focal length plano-convex lens

**Structural components:**
- Thick glass blank for primary mirror (borosilicate recommended for thermal stability)
- Aluminum or steel tubing: for the telescope tube (diameter = primary mirror diameter + a few inches for structural strength)
- Wooden or metal rings: to hold the primary mirror (must support weight while allowing slight tilts for collimation)
- Metal struts or spider: to hold the secondary mirror in optical alignment
- Threaded rods: for focus adjustment and mount movements

**Mechanical tools:**
- All tools from the refractor list, plus:
- Mirror polishing kit (fine abrasives, polishing compound)
- Mirror mount with adjustable screws (at least three points of support, typically three screws on the back)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Refractor Telescope (Simpler Design)

**Step 1: Select and test your lenses.**

A refractor's performance depends on lens quality. Before investing in a tube, test your optics.

1. Place the objective lens at one end of a long table (or hall)
2. Place a small, bright light source (candle, lantern) 10-20 meters away
3. Move the eyepiece toward the objective until you see a sharp image of the light source
4. The distance at which this sharp image forms is the focal length of the objective
5. Record this distance—it determines your telescope's tube length

**Why this matters:** If your objective has a focal length of 80 cm, your tube must be at least 80 cm long. If it's too short, you cannot focus at infinity, and the telescope won't work for stars.

**Step 2: Build the main tube.**

1. Cut the brass tubing to length: approximately focal length of objective + 15 cm (extra space for mechanical linkages)
2. At one end, drill a hole large enough for the objective lens to fit
3. Machine or file a seat (a cylindrical step) inside the tube so the objective lens sits flush
4. At the other end (eyepiece end), machine threads or a socket to hold the eyepiece assembly

**Critical measurement:** The objective must be at exactly the right distance from the eyepiece. Too close, and you cannot focus on distant objects. Too far, and the eyepiece has no way to reach the focal plane. Measure three times; drill once.

**Step 3: Build the focusing mechanism.**

The eyepiece must move slightly along the optical axis to focus on objects at different distances.

1. Machine a cylindrical barrel that slides inside the main tube
2. Attach the eyepiece to this barrel
3. Add threads on the outer surface of the barrel
4. Mount a fixed nut on the main tube so turning a knob (attached to the nut) moves the barrel in and out
5. Alternatively, use a simple rack-and-pinion mechanism: a gear on the knob meshes with teeth cut into the barrel

**Typical focus range:** 2-3 cm of movement is usually sufficient. Mark the "best focus" position when you test the telescope.

**Step 4: Build the mount.**

The tube must be held stable and moveable in altitude (up-down) and azimuth (left-right).

Simple altazimuth mount:
1. Attach the tube to two vertical wooden or metal rings using straps
2. Mount these rings on a horizontal base
3. Add a horizontal axis through the center of the rings (threaded rod works) so the tube pivots up and down
4. Mount the rings on a rotating base (wooden turntable or a bearing) so the whole assembly swivels left and right
5. Add friction or lock screws so the telescope stays pointed at your target

**Step 5: Test and collimate.**

Collimation is the process of aligning the objective and eyepiece along the same optical axis.

1. Point the telescope at a bright, distant object (a lantern or bright star)
2. Move the focus knob until the image is sharpest
3. Check: is the image centered in the eyepiece, or off to one side?
4. If off-center, loosen the objective mounting and tap it gently to center it
5. Retighten and test again
6. The objective should be perfectly centered in the tube opening

### Part 2: Reflector Telescope (Larger Aperture)

**Step 1: Prepare the primary mirror.**

A reflector's primary mirror must have a precise parabolic shape to focus light correctly.

1. Begin with a thick glass blank (1-2 inches thick) with diameter matching your desired aperture
2. If you have an existing flat mirror, it can be used for testing but won't focus well; a parabolic mirror is essential for good results
3. If hand-polishing, follow the procedure from Vol 32 (glass-grinding): rough grinding with coarse abrasive, then finer abrasives, testing with a Ronchi grating or simple focus test

**Testing the mirror:**
1. Mount the mirror at one end of a bench
2. Place a light source at the center of curvature (distance = 2 × radius of curvature)
3. Move a screen toward the mirror until you see a sharp image
4. The mirror's focal length is the distance from mirror to screen divided by two (roughly)

**Step 2: Coat the mirror.**

To reflect light effectively, the front surface of the mirror must be silvered (coated with reflective material).

1. Clean the glass thoroughly with distilled water and let it dry completely
2. Apply a reflective coating:
   - **Aluminum coating:** Professional vacuum silvering if available; gives 90-95% reflectivity
   - **Silver coating:** Chemical silvering process using silver nitrate and reducer (detailed in specialized optics texts); gives 95-98% reflectivity but is fragile
3. Protect the reflective coating with a thin clear lacquer to prevent oxidation

**Step 3: Build the mirror cell (support structure).**

The mirror must be held securely but with the ability to tilt slightly for alignment.

1. Create a rigid backing structure (plywood or aluminum plate) larger than the mirror by several inches
2. Mount the mirror on three points (typically three adjustable screws or cork pads on the back)
3. These three points should form an equilateral triangle roughly 80% of the mirror's diameter
4. **Critical:** The mirror must rest on the support points, not be glued or rigidly clamped; this allows thermal expansion and collimation adjustments

**Step 4: Build the telescope tube.**

1. Cut metal or PVC tubing to length: roughly 1.5–2 × the focal length of the primary mirror
2. Attach the mirror cell (with the primary mirror) to the bottom (back) of the tube
3. At the top (front) of the tube, machine a secondary mirror holder (spider)
4. The secondary mirror (a small flat mirror, 1-1.5 inches) must be positioned to intercept the light cone from the primary and direct it to the eyepiece location
5. Position the secondary mirror at a distance equal to the primary's focal length minus the eyepiece's focal length (approximately)

**Step 5: Build the secondary mirror mount (spider).**

The secondary mirror is held in place by thin metal struts (the spider) that allow light to pass around them.

1. Create a secondary mirror cell: mount the small flat mirror on a backing plate with adjustable screws (two-axis tilt adjustment)
2. Attach four thin metal struts (or rods) from the secondary cell to the main tube's inner walls
3. Position these struts at 45-degree angles (or at the cardinal directions) to minimize obstruction
4. The secondary mirror's tilt must be adjustable so it directs the primary's focal point to the eyepiece

**Step 6: Build the eyepiece focus mount.**

1. Create a focuser tube: a small cylindrical component that holds the eyepiece and slides along the optical axis
2. Mount this focuser at the side of the main tube (Newtonian reflector design) or at the bottom (Cassegrain variant)
3. Add a focusing screw mechanism (rack-and-pinion or threaded barrel) for smooth, precise focus
4. The eyepiece should move under 5 cm of travel range

**Step 7: Collimate the reflector.**

Collimation ensures all optical surfaces are aligned on the same axis.

1. Remove the eyepiece and look down the focuser hole
2. You should see the secondary mirror, then the primary mirror, centered
3. If the primary is off-center, adjust the three support screws on the mirror cell (turn them gently; small adjustments go a long way)
4. Once the mirrors are centered, install the eyepiece and focus on a star
5. Use a collimation eyepiece (or a simple crosshair) to fine-tune alignment by ensuring the star's diffraction rings are concentric

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Calculating Magnification for a Refractor

You have built a refractor with an objective focal length of 100 cm and an eyepiece focal length of 2 cm.

Magnification = Objective focal length / Eyepiece focal length = 100 / 2 = 50×

This means objects appear 50 times closer. A crater on the Moon 50 km wide would appear to subtend the same angle as a crater 1 km wide to the naked eye.

**Practical note:** Higher magnification requires better optics and more stable mount (wind and vibrations are magnified too). For hand-built telescopes, 30-50× is reasonable; under 100× requires very fine craftsmanship.

### Example 2: Mirror Focal Length Testing

You have a primary mirror blank 8 inches (20 cm) in diameter and you want to determine its focal length.

1. Mount the mirror vertically (front facing you)
2. Place a bright light source 100 cm away
3. Move a white screen toward the mirror until the light forms a sharp, small image on the screen
4. Measure the distance from the mirror to the screen: let's say 110 cm
5. Focal length is approximately half this distance: f = 110 / 2 = 55 cm

This is a medium focal length—suitable for a reflector scope with good magnification and a reasonable field of view.

## Verification Criteria

Your telescope is ready for use when:

1. **Optical test:** Point at a known distant object (a building 500 meters away, a bright star). The image is sharp and detail is visible that you cannot see with the naked eye.
2. **Collimation check:** Stars show concentric diffraction rings and are centered in the eyepiece.
3. **Stability:** The mount holds the telescope steady without vibration when you touch the tube.
4. **Focus range:** The focusing mechanism has smooth travel across at least 2-3 cm without binding.
5. **Light transmission:** The view is bright (no obvious dark spots from misalignment or dust).

## Common Mistakes

1. **Insufficient focal length:** Grinding a mirror or objective that is too short (focal length under 30 cm) for the aperture. This causes extreme spherical aberration and a blurry image. **Fix:** Use simpler, longer focal lengths (f/6 to f/10 is safer than f/4).

2. **Poor collimation before testing:** Installing the eyepiece before ensuring the objective and primary mirror are centered. **Fix:** Always collimate with the eyepiece removed first.

3. **Dirty optics:** Dust, fingerprints, or condensation on the objective or mirrors severely reduce light transmission. **Fix:** Cover the telescope when not in use; clean optics only when absolutely necessary, using only lens paper and distilled water.

4. **Unstable mount:** A wobbly tube makes high magnification unusable. **Fix:** Ensure all connections are tight; add weights to the base for stability.

5. **Secondary mirror obstruction too large:** A secondary mirror much larger than necessary wastes light and creates diffraction effects. **Fix:** Use the smallest secondary mirror that intercepts the full light cone from the primary.

6. **Eyepiece in wrong position:** Mounting the eyepiece at the wrong distance from the primary focal plane. The eyepiece must be precisely positioned. **Fix:** Test with a lamp before finalizing the position; mark the correct focus position for future reference.

7. **Thermal effects:** The telescope changes focal length as temperature changes (glass expands or contracts). **Fix:** Allow the telescope 30 minutes to thermally stabilize before serious observations.
