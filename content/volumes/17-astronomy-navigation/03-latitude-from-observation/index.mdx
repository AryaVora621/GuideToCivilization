## Overview

Your latitude on Earth controls which stars you can see and their positions in the sky. By observing celestial objects, you can determine your latitude. Understanding this helps you:

1. **Find your location:** Know your approximate latitude without maps
2. **Navigate long distances:** Maintain constant latitude while traveling east-west
3. **Verify position:** Cross-check other navigation methods
4. **Explore confidently:** Travel to known latitudes
5. **Share position:** Communicate your location to others

This chapter teaches practical latitude measurement.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of altitude and declination
- Ability to identify bright stars
- Ability to estimate angles by hand
- Basic geometry and simple math
- Ability to observe sun position at noon

## Materials & Tools

**No-equipment method:**
- Eyes and hand for angle measurement
- Notebook for recording
- Basic understanding of star positions

**Simple instruments:**
- Straight stick (1-2 meters)
- Stone or stick marker
- Water level or plumb bob

**Better instruments:**
- Quadrant (quarter-circle with angle markings)
- Astrolabe (rotating disk with sights)
- Sextant (can measure angles directly)

## Part 1: Using Polaris (Northern Hemisphere)

### The Key Relationship

**Fundamental rule:** The altitude of Polaris above the horizon equals your latitude.

**Why?** Polaris marks the north celestial pole. Your latitude determines how high that pole is in your sky.

**Examples:**
- At equator (latitude 0°): Polaris on the horizon (altitude 0°)
- At 45°N: Polaris 45° above horizon
- At 60°N: Polaris 60° above horizon
- At north pole (90°N): Polaris directly overhead (altitude 90°)

**Accuracy:** Within about 1 degree due to Polaris not being exactly at the pole.

### Measuring Altitude by Hand

**Fist method (rough):**
- Extend arm with fist toward horizon
- One fist width = approximately 10 degrees
- Stack fists from horizon to Polaris
- Count fists = approximate latitude

**Accuracy:** ±5 degrees typical

**Finger method (better):**
- Extend arm with index and middle fingers together
- Width between fingertips = approximately 1 degree
- Count fingers from horizon to Polaris
- More precise but slow for large angles

**Accuracy:** ±1-2 degrees

**Angle-measuring tool (best):**
Create a simple quadrant (see Part 3 below)
- Accuracy: ±0.5 degrees with care

### Time Considerations

**Important fact:** Polaris's altitude depends on where you measure it from (always due north), but that altitude is constant throughout the night.

**Best time to measure:** When Polaris is due north (crosses the meridian).

**Determining meridian crossing:** 
- Polaris appears highest when due south (wait, this is wrong for Polaris)
- Actually, Polaris never sets; it rotates around the north celestial pole
- Measure Polaris's altitude when it's directly above/below the pole (on the meridian)

**Practical advice:** Measure Polaris altitude multiple times, average the results.

### Accounting for Polaris's Offset

Polaris is not exactly at the north celestial pole (about 0.75 degrees away).

**Effect:** Polaris's altitude varies slightly as it rotates around the true pole.

**Correction:**
- Maximum altitude: Your latitude + 0.75 degrees
- Minimum altitude: Your latitude - 0.75 degrees
- Average: Your latitude (approximately)

**For navigation:** The small 1.5-degree total variation is acceptable for most purposes. If precision needed, measure multiple times and average.

## Part 2: Using the Southern Cross (Southern Hemisphere)

### Finding the South Celestial Pole

The Southern Cross points toward the south celestial pole, but you must calculate its position.

**Method:**
1. Identify Southern Cross (Crux) constellation
2. Find the long axis (two brightest stars)
3. Extend this line 4.5× the length of the cross
4. Note: There's no bright star at the pole like Polaris in the north

**Finding the pole more precisely:**
1. Locate Southern Cross
2. Locate nearby bright star Achernar
3. The south celestial pole lies roughly in the area between these
4. Use both as references to triangulate

### Latitude from South Pole Altitude

**Same rule as north:** Altitude of south celestial pole = Your latitude (southern hemisphere).

**Measurement:**
1. Determine where the pole is (using cross and Achernar)
2. Measure altitude to that point
3. That altitude = Your latitude

**Accuracy:** ±2 degrees typical (less precise than Polaris method due to fainter reference points).

## Part 3: Using the Sun's Altitude

### Method Overview

The Sun's altitude at solar noon depends on latitude and time of year.

**Relationship:**
- Altitude = 90 degrees - |latitude - sun's declination|
- Therefore: Latitude = 90 degrees - altitude + declination

**Advantages:**
- Easy to measure (sun is bright)
- Works without knowing specific stars
- Works anywhere on Earth

**Disadvantages:**
- Need to know sun's declination (varies with date)
- Need to find solar noon accurately
- Can't observe at night

### Finding Solar Noon

Solar noon is when the sun reaches its highest point in the sky, crossing the meridian (due south in northern hemisphere, due north in southern).

**Method 1: Watch the sun**
1. Observe sun's position throughout the day
2. Note when it appears highest (smallest shadow cast by stick)
3. That moment is solar noon

**Method 2: Use a gnomon (stick)**
1. Place vertical stick in ground
2. Mark shadow tip hourly
3. Shadow is shortest at solar noon
4. This method also gives direction (true south/north)

**Accuracy:** ±15 minutes typical

### Sun's Declination

The sun's declination changes throughout the year, from -23.5° (winter solstice) to +23.5° (summer solstice).

**Key dates:**
- March 20 (vernal equinox): Declination = 0°
- June 20 (summer solstice): Declination = +23.5°
- September 22 (autumnal equinox): Declination = 0°
- December 21 (winter solstice): Declination = -23.5°

**Finding current declination:**
- If you know the date, use a table or calculate
- Alternative: Use sun's actual position to infer approximate declination

**Table of approximate solar declination:**
- January: -20°
- February: -11°
- March: 0°
- April: +12°
- May: +20°
- June: +23°
- July: +21°
- August: +13°
- September: 0°
- October: -10°
- November: -19°
- December: -23°

### Calculation Example

**Scenario:** You measure sun's altitude at solar noon as 60 degrees. It's June 20 (summer solstice, declination +23.5°).

**Calculation:**
1. Altitude = 60°
2. Sun's declination = +23.5°
3. Latitude = 90° - (altitude - declination)
4. Latitude = 90° - (60° - 23.5°)
5. Latitude = 90° - 36.5°
6. Latitude = 53.5°N

**Interpretation:** You're at approximately 53.5°N latitude (similar to Edinburgh, Scotland or central Canada).

### Finding Altitude of the Sun

**Stick shadow method:**
1. Place stick vertically in level ground
2. Measure length of shadow
3. At solar noon: Angle = arctangent(stick height / shadow length)
4. Use right triangle: altitude angle is complement of shadow angle

**Hand measurement:**
- Extend arm at 90 degrees to stick
- Hold thumb and fingers apart
- Adjust until span equals angle to sun
- Measure that angle in hand-widths

**Water level method:**
1. Fill container with water until surface is level
2. Place at an angle
3. The water's surface shows the horizontal
4. Measure angle between horizontal and sun's reflection

**Quadrant (see Part 4):**
- Direct measurement tool designed for this

## Part 4: Building Simple Instruments

### The Cross-Staff (Simple)

**Materials:**
- Straight stick (1 meter long)
- Smaller cross-stick (30 cm long)
- Knife or file to notch the main stick

**Construction:**
1. Drill or notch a hole through the main stick at its midpoint
2. Insert the cross-stick through the hole so it can slide
3. Notches or marks on the main stick mark angle increments

**Using a cross-staff:**
1. Hold the main stick horizontal
2. Sight along it toward Polaris
3. Slide cross-stick until top aligns with Polaris
4. Position determines the angle

**Calibration:**
- Test against known angles (using hand measurements)
- Mark the stick with angle values

### The Quadrant

A quadrant is a quarter-circle marked with angles 0-90 degrees.

**Materials:**
- Wood or cardboard
- Two straight edges (one for baseline, one for sighting)
- String with plumb bob
- Protractor or angle markings

**Construction:**
1. Cut a quarter-circle from wood (radius 30-50 cm)
2. Mark degree markings around the curved edge
3. Attach one straight edge along the vertical side
4. Attach another straight edge (sighting arm) at the corner
5. Hang a plumb bob from the corner
6. Sighting arm and plumb bob point toward the object being measured

**Using a quadrant:**
1. Hold vertically
2. Look along the sighting arm at the target (e.g., Polaris)
3. Plumb bob hangs freely
4. Read the angle where the string crosses the marked arc

**Accuracy:** ±0.5-1 degree with careful construction

### The Astrolabe (Advanced)

An astrolabe is a rotating disk with sights and a reference circle. It's more complex but very accurate.

**Basic components:**
- Circular disk marked with 360 degrees
- Rotating sighting arm (alidade)
- Plumb bob reference
- Star positions marked on disk (for advanced use)

**Using an astrolabe for latitude:**
1. Align disk so 0° faces down
2. Sight toward Polaris using rotating arm
3. Plumb bob indicates angle on the disk
4. That angle = Your latitude

**Construction:** Requires precision metalwork or careful woodworking. Often used as a decorative tool as much as functional.

### The Sextant (Professional)

A sextant is a precision instrument that measures angles up to 120 degrees.

**Key features:**
- Mirrors that double the measured angle for better precision
- Graduated arc showing 0-120 degrees
- Eyepiece with telescope option
- Adjustment screws for fine-tuning

**Using a sextant:**
1. Look through the eyepiece
2. Position the sun, star, or horizon in the mirror
3. Adjust the arm until the object reflects into view
4. Read the angle from the graduations

**Accuracy:** ±0.1 degree with proper technique

**Construction:** Requires skilled craftsmanship or salvage from existing instruments. Not practical to build from scratch post-collapse.

## Part 5: Methods Without Instruments

### Noon Sun Position Method

**Concept:** The sun's position at noon (time relative to true noon, shadow length) reveals latitude.

**Requirements:**
- Know approximate date
- Measure sun's shadow length at local noon
- Determine direction of shadow (true south/north)

**Execution:**
1. Place vertical stick in ground
2. Track shadow throughout day
3. When shadow is shortest, it's solar noon
4. At that moment, measure shadow angle
5. Use sun declination table and calculation (see Part 3)

**Accuracy:** ±2-3 degrees without instruments

### Circumpolar Star Method

**Concept:** The height of circumpolar stars (stars that never set) varies with latitude.

**How it works:**
1. Watch a bright circumpolar star (like Big Dipper)
2. Measure its altitude when at its lowest point (due north, near horizon)
3. That altitude ≈ Your latitude - 45 degrees
4. Therefore: Latitude ≈ Altitude + 45 degrees

**Requires:** Multiple observations and careful hand measurement.

**Accuracy:** ±3-5 degrees

## Part 6: Improving Accuracy

### Multiple Measurements

**Best practice:** Take multiple measurements over several nights.

**Procedure:**
1. Measure latitude using primary method
2. Repeat the next clear night
3. Average the results
4. Estimate uncertainty (variation between measurements)

**Benefit:** Reduces random errors from wind, ground unevenness, eye strain.

### Cross-Checking Methods

Use two different methods, compare results.

**Example:**
- Method 1: Polaris altitude = 50.2°
- Method 2: Sun's altitude at noon = 50.1°
- Conclusion: Latitude ≈ 50°N, uncertainty ±0.5°

**Benefit:** Identifies systematic errors in single method.

### Accounting for Refraction

Atmospheric refraction makes objects appear slightly higher than they actually are, especially near the horizon.

**Effect:** About 0.5 degrees near horizon, negligible at high angles.

**Correction:** For Polaris (which is high in the sky), ignore refraction.

**For sun near horizon:** Subtract 0.5 degrees from measured altitude.

## Verification

**Understanding latitude measurement means:**

1. You can estimate latitude using Polaris or sun altitude
2. You can build a simple angle-measuring tool (quadrant or cross-staff)
3. You understand the relationship between celestial altitude and latitude
4. You can calculate latitude from sun's altitude and declination
5. You can determine solar noon using a gnomon
6. You can verify your latitude using multiple methods

## Common Mistakes

1. **Confusing altitude with azimuth:** Altitude is angle above horizon; azimuth is compass direction. You need altitude for latitude.

2. **Measuring Polaris's altitude when it's not due north:** Always measure Polaris when it's on the meridian (highest or lowest point) for most accurate result.

3. **Forgetting to account for sun's declination:** Sun's altitude depends on both latitude AND date. Must include declination in calculation.

4. **Assuming shadow angle equals altitude angle:** Shadow angle and sun altitude angle are complementary (add to 90°). Don't confuse them.

5. **Not verifying solar noon:** Using clock time instead of actual solar noon can introduce large errors (up to 15 degrees depending on longitude).

6. **Ignoring hand-measurement limitations:** Hand measurements have ±5 degree errors. Use them for rough checks, not precise navigation.

7. **Not repeating measurements:** Single measurements have random errors. Always repeat on multiple nights and average.

8. **Forgetting about light pollution:** Dim stars fade in bright locations. Move to darker sites for better observations.

9. **Expecting perfect accuracy without instruments:** Even careful hand measurements give ±1-2 degree accuracy. That's good enough for navigation but not surveying.
