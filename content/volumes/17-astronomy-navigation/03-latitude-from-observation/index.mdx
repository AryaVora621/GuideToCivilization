## Overview

Latitude is your position north or south of the equator, ranging from 0 degrees (equator) to 90 degrees (poles).

Unlike longitude (which requires precise timekeeping), latitude can be determined from the night sky using simple geometry. This made latitude-finding the foundation of pre-modern navigation.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of altitude (angle above the horizon)
- Understanding of declination (star positions on the celestial sphere)
- Ability to measure angles to at least 1-degree precision
- Knowledge of basic geometry and trigonometry
- Clear skies for observation

## Materials & Tools

- Measuring device:
  - Sextant (most precise, ~1 arcminute)
  - Astrolabe (medieval instrument, ~1 degree)
  - Cross-staff (simple, ~1 degree)
  - Improvised: hand-span method (rough, ~5-10 degrees)
- Reference materials: star catalogs with declinations
- Notebook for calculations
- Mathematical tables or calculator

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: The Polaris Method (Northern Hemisphere)

**Step 1: Understand the principle.**

Polaris is very close to the north celestial pole. Its altitude above the horizon equals the observer's latitude (to within about 1 degree).

**Why this works:**
1. The celestial pole is 90 degrees above the horizon at the North Pole (latitude 90°)
2. The celestial pole is 0 degrees above the horizon at the equator (latitude 0°)
3. At any latitude, the celestial pole is (latitude) degrees above the horizon
4. Polaris is very close to the celestial pole, so its altitude ≈ latitude

**Step 2: Locate Polaris.**

(See Vol 34, Ch 2 for detailed instructions)

Quick method: Find the Big Dipper, extend the line through its end stars toward Polaris.

**Step 3: Measure Polaris's altitude.**

Using a sextant:
1. Position the sextant so the horizon is visible in the lower half of the eyepiece
2. Move the index arm until Polaris appears in the upper half, aligned with the horizon
3. Read the angle on the sextant scale
4. This is the altitude

Using a cross-staff:
1. Hold the cross-staff at arm's length
2. Align the bottom bar with the horizon
3. Slide the cross-piece until its top aligns with Polaris
4. Measure the angle between the bottom bar and cross-piece

Using hand-span method (rough):
1. Extend your arm
2. Your fist width is about 10 degrees
3. Count how many fist-widths from horizon to Polaris
4. Multiply by 10 to estimate altitude

**Step 4: Account for Polaris's offset.**

Polaris is not exactly at the celestial pole. Its offset is small but noticeable:
1. Polaris is about 0.7 degrees from the pole (in 2026)
2. For rough navigation, ignore this error
3. For precision, subtract 0.7 degrees if Polaris is above the pole, add if below
4. (The offset direction and magnitude change slowly due to precession)

**Step 5: Record your latitude.**

The altitude of Polaris = your latitude (approximately).

**Example:** If Polaris appears 42 degrees above the horizon, you are at latitude 42°N.

### Part 2: The Noon Sun Method (Works Everywhere)

**Step 1: Understand the principle.**

The sun reaches its highest point (culmination) at local noon. The sun's altitude at noon depends on declination and latitude.

**Formula:**
Latitude = 90° - (sun altitude at noon) - (sun declination)

(Sign convention: North is positive, South is negative)

**Why this works:**
1. The sun's declination changes with seasons (0° at equinoxes, +23.5° at summer solstice, -23.5° at winter solstice)
2. At the equator, the sun is 90° above the horizon when on the observer's meridian
3. As you move north, the sun's altitude decreases by the same angle (your latitude)

**Step 2: Determine the sun's declination.**

The sun's declination varies throughout the year:
- January 1: Dec ≈ -23°
- April 1: Dec ≈ 0°
- July 1: Dec ≈ +23°
- October 1: Dec ≈ 0°

(More precise values from astronomical almanacs)

**Step 3: Observe the sun's altitude at noon.**

**Safety:** Never look directly at the sun. Use a projection method or dark filter.

**Projection method:**
1. Use a shadow stick or sextant
2. Measure the angle of the sun's rays at local noon

**Sextant method:**
1. At local noon, measure the sun's altitude with a sextant
2. Record the value

**Shadow stick method:**
1. Erect a vertical stick of known height
2. At noon, measure the shadow length
3. Calculate the angle: tan(altitude) = stick height / shadow length
4. Find altitude using trigonometric tables

**Step 4: Calculate latitude.**

Latitude = 90° - (sun altitude) - (sun declination)

**Example:**
- Observation date: July 1 (sun declination = +23°)
- Sun altitude at noon: 60°
- Latitude = 90° - 60° - 23° = 7°N

The observer is at latitude 7°N (near the equator).

**Another example:**
- Observation date: January 1 (sun declination = -23°)
- Sun altitude at noon: 25°
- Latitude = 90° - 25° - (-23°) = 88°N

The observer is at latitude 88°N (far north).

### Part 3: Using Other Stars (Circumpolar Stars)

**Step 1: Understand the method.**

Any star with a known declination can be used to find latitude, but stars near the meridian (due south or north) are easiest.

**Principle:**
- Latitude = altitude of star - star declination
- (If star is above celestial equator, declination is positive)
- (If star is below, declination is negative)

**Step 2: Observe a star at culmination.**

A star culminates (reaches its highest point) when it crosses the meridian (due south in Northern Hemisphere, due north in Southern).

**How to tell when a star culminates:**
- Observe the star's position for several hours
- It moves in an arc from east to west
- At culmination, it is directly above a cardinal direction and stops rising

**Step 3: Measure the star's altitude at culmination.**

Use the same techniques as for Polaris (sextant, cross-staff, or hand-span).

**Step 4: Calculate latitude.**

Latitude = star altitude - star declination

**Example:**
- Star: Vega (declination +38°47')
- Altitude at culmination: 50°
- Latitude = 50° - 38° = 12°N

The observer is at latitude 12°N.

### Part 4: Southern Hemisphere Methods

**Step 1: Find the south celestial pole.**

Unlike Polaris in the north, there is no bright star near the south celestial pole. Instead:

**Method 1: Use the Southern Cross (Crux):**
1. Find the Southern Cross (four bright stars in a cross shape)
2. Extend a line down from the long axis about 4 times its length
3. This points toward the south celestial pole

**Method 2: Use the False Cross (easier):**
1. The False Cross is less useful but nearby
2. The Southern Cross is the better choice

**Step 2: Measure the pole's altitude.**

Since the pole is invisible, extend a line from nearby stars toward where the pole should be.

**Step 3: Use the noon sun method.**

This works the same way in the Southern Hemisphere:
- Latitude = 90° - (sun altitude) - (sun declination)
- Result is negative (south) if you're south of the equator

### Part 5: Refining Latitude Measurements

**Step 1: Correct for sextant error.**

Sextants have systematic errors:
1. Collimation error: The mirrors aren't perfectly aligned (typical: 0-2 arcminutes)
2. Perpendicularity error: The index mirror isn't perfectly perpendicular (typical: 0-5 arcminutes)
3. Side error: The sextant reads slightly differently on the left vs. right side

**Correction:**
- Manufacturer should provide calibration data
- Alternatively, observe the sun's diameter (known to be 0.53°) and measure it; if the result differs, note the error

**Step 2: Account for atmospheric refraction.**

Light bends as it enters the atmosphere, making objects appear higher than they actually are.

**Effect:**
- Near the horizon: Refraction is about 0.5 degrees
- At 45 degrees altitude: Refraction is about 1 arcminute
- At the zenith: No refraction

**For latitude finding, ignore refraction near the meridian (small effect).**

**Step 3: Account for observer height above sea level.**

The horizon appears lower at higher elevations, affecting altitude measurements.

**Effect:**
- At sea level: No correction
- At 1000 m elevation: About 3 arcminutes lower horizon
- At 10,000 m elevation: About 10 arcminutes lower horizon

**For rough navigation, ignore this correction.**

## Verification

A successful latitude measurement results in:

- **Polaris method:** Accuracy of 1-2 degrees with improvised tools, 0.1 degrees with a sextant
- **Noon sun method:** Accuracy of 1-2 degrees with improvised tools, 0.1 degrees with a sextant
- **Consistency:** Repeated measurements agree within the tool's precision

A failed measurement shows:

- **Large spread:** Repeated measurements differ by over 5 degrees
- **Implausible result:** Latitude over 90 degrees or negative when positive is expected
- **Systematic error:** All measurements offset by a consistent amount

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing altitude with declination:** Altitude is measured from your horizon; declination is on the celestial sphere
- **Forgetting sun angle reversal:** The sun's declination changes; use the correct value for your observation date
- **Measuring at wrong time:** Latitude from the noon sun is only accurate at local noon (when sun is highest)
- **Not accounting for time zones:** Local noon (highest sun) differs from clock noon if you're far from your time zone's meridian
- **Using invisible stars in daylight:** Some bright stars are invisible during twilight; observe at proper darkness
- **Ignoring refraction near horizon:** Refraction errors are large near the horizon; measure stars higher up if possible
