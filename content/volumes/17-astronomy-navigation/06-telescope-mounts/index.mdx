## Overview

A telescope's optical quality is only half the battle. The mount determines what you can observe.

A **simple alt-azimuth mount** lets you point at any part of the sky but requires constant adjustment as stars move.

An **equatorial mount** aligns with Earth's axis, allowing a single rotation to track stars as they move across the sky. Add a **clockwork drive**, and the telescope tracks automatically.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of altazimuth and equatorial coordinate systems (Vol 34, Ch 1)
- Understanding of mechanical systems (gears, bearings)
- Metalworking and machining skills
- Basic understanding of telescope optics (focal length, eyepiece magnification)

## Materials & Tools

### For Building an Altazimuth Mount

- Two perpendicular axes (vertical and horizontal)
- Bearings or pivot points
- Adjustment mechanisms (gears, worms, or friction)
- Structure (metal or wood)
- Weighting systems

### For Building an Equatorial Mount

- Base structure (tripod or pedestal)
- Right ascension axis (aligned with celestial pole)
- Declination axis (perpendicular to RA axis)
- Bearings
- Clockwork drive (optional but highly valuable)
- Setting circles or scales

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Simple Altazimuth Mount

**Step 1: Understand altitude-azimuth coordinates.**

- **Altitude:** Angle above the horizon (0° = horizon, 90° = zenith)
- **Azimuth:** Compass direction (0° = north, 90° = east, 180° = south, 270° = west)

Every star has an altitude and azimuth at any given moment, and these change continuously as the star moves.

**Step 2: Design a simple two-axis mount.**

**Vertical axis (azimuth):**
- Rotates left and right
- Allows the telescope to point to any compass direction

**Horizontal axis (altitude):**
- Tilts up and down
- Allows the telescope to point from horizon to zenith

**Step 3: Build the structure.**

**Base:**
1. A sturdy pedestal (wood or metal)
2. Must support the telescope's weight without tipping

**Vertical bearing:**
1. A pivot that allows smooth rotation around the vertical axis
2. Can be a bearing (if available) or a simple pivot point (less smooth)
3. Friction must be low enough to adjust by hand

**Horizontal bearing:**
1. A pivoting point that allows the telescope tube to tilt up and down
2. The pivot should be near the telescope's center of mass (for balance)

**Step 4: Add adjustment mechanisms.**

**For smooth pointing:**
1. Use fine-pitch worms and wheels (gear pairs with high reduction ratios)
2. A worm drive (a screw that meshes with a gear) provides fine control
3. One turn of the worm = small angle adjustment at the telescope

**Examples:**
- Azimuth: 200-turn worm on the vertical axis; 1 full worm turn = 1.8° azimuth change
- Altitude: 100-turn worm on the horizontal axis; 1 full worm turn = 3.6° altitude change

**Step 5: Add a finder scope.**

The main telescope's field of view is narrow (especially at high magnification). A finder scope (a low-magnification reflex scope) helps locate objects.

**Components:**
- Small low-power telescope (3-10x magnification)
- Mounted parallel to main telescope
- Cross-hairs for aiming

**Alignment:**
1. Point main telescope at a distant object (church spire, hilltop)
2. Center it in the field of view
3. Adjust finder scope until the same object is centered in its view
4. Now the finder scope's center = main telescope's center

### Part 2: Equatorial Mount

**Step 1: Understand equatorial coordinates.**

- **Right Ascension (RA):** Hours (0-24), like longitude on the celestial sphere
- **Declination (Dec):** Degrees (-90° to +90°), like latitude on the celestial sphere

A star's RA and Dec are constant (don't change much over years).

**Step 2: Design the equatorial mount.**

An equatorial mount has two axes:

**Right Ascension (RA) axis:**
- Points toward the north celestial pole (in Northern Hemisphere)
- Rotates around this axis to change RA (sweep east-west)
- A single rotation of this axis tracks a star as it moves across the sky

**Declination (Dec) axis:**
- Perpendicular to the RA axis
- Tilts the telescope north or south to change Dec

**Why this works:**
- Stars move westward due to Earth's rotation (one rotation per day)
- If the telescope's RA axis is aligned with Earth's rotation axis, one rotation per day keeps the telescope aimed at the same star
- No manual adjustment needed (if a clockwork drive is added)

**Step 3: Build the mount's structure.**

**Tripod or pedestal:**
1. Three legs supporting a head structure
2. Must be level (use a spirit level)
3. Height: Comfortable for observing (usually 1.5-2 meters)

**RA axis bearing:**
1. A bearing that allows rotation around an axis tilted toward the north star
2. Tilt angle = observer's latitude (for Northern Hemisphere)
3. Example: At latitude 40°N, the RA axis is tilted 40° from horizontal

**RA axis installation:**
1. Calculate the tilt angle (= observer's latitude)
2. Mount the bearing on the tripod head at this tilt angle
3. The axis should point directly at Polaris (in Northern Hemisphere) or the south celestial pole (in Southern)
4. **Polar alignment is critical** — if off by more than 1°, tracking will be inaccurate

**Dec axis bearing:**
1. Mounted perpendicular to the RA axis
2. Allows the telescope tube to tilt north or south
3. The telescope tube pivots around this axis

**Step 4: Add the clockwork drive (optional but valuable).**

A clockwork drive rotates the RA axis at exactly the right speed to track the sky (one rotation per sidereal day ≈ 23h 56m 4s).

**Components:**
1. **Clock mechanism:** Spring-driven or weight-driven
2. **Gearing:** Reduces the clock's rotation rate to match sidereal time
3. **Worm and wheel:** Drives the RA axis at the correct speed

**Example gear ratio:**
- If the clock ticks 100 times per minute
- And we want one RA axis rotation per sidereal day (86,164 seconds = 1,436 minutes)
- Gear reduction ≈ 1,436 / 100 = 14.36 : 1

**Tuning the drive:**
1. Observe a star (or planet) for 30 minutes
2. If the star drifts eastward, the drive is too slow; speed it up
3. If the star drifts westward, the drive is too fast; slow it down
4. Adjust until the star stays centered in the field of view

**Step 5: Add setting circles (optional).**

Setting circles allow you to point at a star by its RA and Dec coordinates (without searching).

**RA setting circle:**
- A scale around the RA axis
- Marked in hours (0-24) or degrees (0-360)
- Index pointer shows the current RA
- To point at a star with known RA, rotate the axis until the setting circle reads that RA

**Dec setting circle:**
- A scale around the Dec axis
- Marked in degrees (-90 to +90)
- Index pointer shows the current Dec
- Tilt the tube until the setting circle reads the desired Dec

**Using setting circles:**
1. First, align the telescope with a known bright star (e.g., Polaris)
2. Note the setting circles' readings
3. Rotate the axes to match the target star's RA and Dec
4. The target star should be in the field of view (approximately)

### Part 3: Polar Alignment

**Step 1: Why polar alignment matters.**

If the RA axis isn't pointed at the celestial pole, the telescope will not track stars properly.

**Effect of misalignment:**
- 1° misalignment = star drift of ~1 arcminute per minute
- After 1 hour, the star has drifted 1 degree (larger than the field of view of typical telescopes)

**Step 2: Align the RA axis with the pole.**

**Method 1: Direct visual alignment (Northern Hemisphere):**
1. Locate Polaris
2. Point the RA axis directly at Polaris
3. Use a spirit level to ensure the axis tilt angle equals your latitude
4. Use a sight along the axis to verify Polaris is centered

**Method 2: Star drift method (more precise):**
1. Start with approximate polar alignment
2. Observe a star near the celestial equator (declination ≈ 0°)
3. Position it at the edge of the field of view, near the celestial equator
4. Leave the telescope tracking for 30 seconds with the clockwork drive
5. If the star drifts northward, the RA axis is tilted too far north; tilt back slightly
6. If the star drifts southward, tilt forward slightly
7. Repeat until the star doesn't drift north or south (it can drift east/west; that's normal with an imperfect drive)

**Step 3: Check periodically.**

Over weeks and months, the mount may settle or shift. Recheck polar alignment occasionally.

### Part 4: Equatorial Mount with Clockwork Drive (Full System)

**Step 1: Assemble the components.**

- Tripod or pedestal (leveled)
- RA axis bearing (aligned with celestial pole)
- Dec axis bearing (perpendicular to RA)
- Clockwork mechanism
- Worm and wheel gearing
- Telescope tube mounted on dec axis

**Step 2: Test the drive.**

1. Power the clockwork (wind the spring, or let the weight system run)
2. Observe a star
3. Does it stay in the field of view for several minutes without adjustment?
4. If yes, the drive is working; if no, debug (see tuning section above)

**Step 3: Optional: Add handwheel controls for fine adjustment.**

Even with a clockwork drive, fine adjustments are useful:

**RA slow motion:**
- A separate worm-and-wheel for manual RA adjustment
- Allows small corrections (e.g., 1 turn of the worm = 0.1° RA adjustment)

**Dec slow motion:**
- Similar system for Dec axis
- Useful for nudging the telescope slightly north or south

## Verification

A properly functioning equatorial mount with clockwork drive shows:

- **Stars remain centered:** With the drive running, a star stays in the field of view for 30+ minutes without manual adjustment
- **Polar alignment:** The RA axis points toward the celestial pole (verified by star drift test)
- **Smooth rotation:** Both axes rotate smoothly without binding
- **Accurate tracking:** Star positions don't drift noticeably over hours

A poorly functioning mount shows:

- **Rapid star drift:** Stars leave the field of view within 5-10 minutes (indicates misalignment or drive malfunction)
- **Jerky motion:** Axes move unevenly (indicates friction or bearing issues)
- **Mechanical sounds:** Grinding or clicking during rotation (indicates worn gears or bearings)
- **Slow creep:** Stars drift slowly even with the drive running (drive speed is incorrect)

## Common Mistakes

- **Poor polar alignment:** The most common error; even 2-3 degrees of misalignment causes noticeable star drift
- **Unbalanced telescope:** If the tube is not balanced on the Dec axis, it sags, causing friction and misalignment
- **Clockwork drive speed incorrect:** A drive that's off by 1% causes 1-degree drift per hour
- **Worn bearings:** Friction from worn bearings makes tracking difficult and uneven
- **Not leveling the tripod:** An unlevel base makes accurate alignment impossible
- **Failing to account for refraction:** Refraction near the horizon bends light, shifting star positions; observe at higher altitudes
