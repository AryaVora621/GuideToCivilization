## Overview

A calendar divides time into units (days, months, years) that match natural cycles. The two primary cycles are:

1. **Solar year:** The time for Earth to orbit the sun (about 365.25 days)
2. **Lunar month:** The time for the moon to cycle through phases (about 29.5 days)

A calendar system must choose: align with the solar year (for agriculture), the lunar months (for religious observance), or both (complicated but comprehensive).

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of the sun's apparent annual motion (Vol 34, Ch 1)
- Understanding of the moon's phases
- Basic arithmetic
- Ability to observe sun and moon positions

## Materials & Tools

- Clear sky for observing sun and moon
- Method to measure shadow angles (gnomon stick)
- Ability to track moon phases (naked eye observation)
- Notebooks for recording observations and calculations

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: The Solar Year

**Step 1: Define the solar year.**

The solar year is the time from one vernal equinox (spring) to the next.

**Key dates (Northern Hemisphere):**
- Vernal equinox (spring): March 20-21 (sun's declination = 0°, day length = night length)
- Summer solstice: June 20-21 (sun's declination = +23.5°, longest day)
- Autumnal equinox (fall): September 22-23 (sun's declination = 0°, day length = night length)
- Winter solstice: December 21-22 (sun's declination = -23.5°, shortest day)

**Southern Hemisphere:** Same dates, but seasonal meanings are reversed (winter solstice = June 21, etc.)

**Length of solar year:** 365 days 5 hours 48 minutes 46 seconds (about 365.2425 days, or 365.25 days for rough approximation)

**Step 2: Observe the equinoxes and solstices.**

**Vernal and autumnal equinoxes (day length = night length):**
1. Track the sun's position at sunrise and sunset each day
2. When sunrise is exactly due east and sunset is exactly due west, it's an equinox
3. Mark the date

**Solstices (extreme sun positions):**
1. Measure the sun's noon altitude daily (using a shadow stick or sextant)
2. When the altitude stops increasing (summer solstice) or stops decreasing (winter solstice), mark the date

**Simple gnomon method:**
1. Place a vertical stick (gnomon) in level ground
2. At noon each day, measure the shadow length
3. Plot shadow length vs. date
4. The shortest shadow = summer solstice (highest sun)
5. The longest shadow = winter solstice (lowest sun)

**Step 3: Account for the fractional day.**

A solar year is about 365.25 days. After 4 years, the extra 0.25 days accumulate to 1 full day.

**Leap year solution:**
- Every 4 years, add 1 extra day (leap year)
- This keeps the calendar synchronized with the sun

**Gregorian calendar adjustment:**
- Every 100 years, don't add a leap day (remove it)
- Every 400 years, add the leap day back
- Result: 97 leap days every 400 years = average of 365.2425 days per year
- This is very close to the true solar year

**Simple post-collapse approach:**
- Every 4 years, add a leap day
- Accuracy: Off by about 1 day every 130 years (acceptable for most purposes)

### Part 2: The Lunar Month

**Step 1: Define the lunar month.**

The lunar month (synodic month) is the time for the moon to cycle through its phases, from new moon to new moon.

**Key phases:**
- New moon: Moon is between Earth and sun (invisible)
- Waxing crescent: Moon is growing, visible in the evening
- First quarter: Half moon, half lit, sun-moon angle = 90°
- Waxing gibbous: More than half lit
- Full moon: Moon opposite the sun, fully lit
- Waning gibbous: More than half lit, but decreasing
- Last quarter: Half moon, half lit, sun-moon angle = 90° (other side)
- Waning crescent: Moon is shrinking, visible in the morning

**Length of lunar month:** 29 days 12 hours 44 minutes (about 29.5 days, or 29.530589 days precisely)

**Step 2: Track the lunar month.**

By observation:
1. Mark the date of the new moon (when the moon is invisible, just before sunset)
2. Observe the moon's phases each night
3. Mark the date of the next new moon
4. The time between is one lunar month

**From records:**
- Keep a lunar calendar
- Record new moons, full moons, and the duration
- Average 12-13 full lunar cycles to get the typical lunar month length

**Step 3: Count lunar months in a solar year.**

A solar year contains about 365.25 days. A lunar month is about 29.5 days.

Number of lunar months per solar year ≈ 365.25 / 29.5 ≈ 12.37 months

**This means:** A solar year contains about 12 lunar months plus 11 days.

If a calendar uses 12 lunar months, it drifts about 11 days per year relative to the solar year (and seasons).

### Part 3: The Pure Lunar Calendar

**Step 1: Construct a 12-month lunar calendar.**

Simple approach:
1. Base each calendar month on one lunar month (new moon to new moon)
2. Alternate between 29-day and 30-day months to approximate 29.5-day average
3. Calendar: 30, 29, 30, 29, 30, 29, 30, 29, 30, 29, 30, 29 days
4. Total: 354 days per year

**Tracking lunar months:**
1. Calculate the expected date of the next new moon based on the lunar month length
2. Observe the actual new moon
3. Adjust if necessary (observation might be off by 1 day)

**Step 2: Handle year boundaries.**

Since 354 days is 11 days short of a solar year, a lunar calendar gradually drifts:
- Year 1: Calendar month 1 starts in March
- Year 2: Calendar month 1 starts in February (because it drifted backward 11 days)
- Year 3: Calendar month 1 starts in January
- After 3 years: Calendar has drifted back about 33 days (over 1 month)

**This is acceptable for cultures that care about lunar cycles more than seasons.**

**Islamic calendar example:** Uses a pure lunar calendar; Ramadan (the fasting month) cycles through all seasons over 33 years.

### Part 4: The Lunisolar Calendar

**Step 1: Synchronize lunar months with the solar year.**

A lunisolar calendar uses lunar months but adds intercalary (extra) months to stay aligned with seasons.

**Principle:**
- Use lunar months (approximately 29.5 days each) as the basic unit
- Every 2-3 years, add an extra month to keep the calendar aligned with the solar year

**Step 2: Calculate when to add intercalary months.**

A solar year is about 365.25 days. 12 lunar months is about 354 days. The difference is about 11 days.

**Simple rule:** Every 3 years, add an extra month (approximately correct).

**More precise rule (used by Hebrew calendar):**
- In a 19-year cycle, add 7 intercalary months (called the Metonic cycle)
- Years with intercalary months: 3, 6, 8, 11, 14, 17, 19 of the 19-year cycle
- This aligns lunar months with the solar year very well (off by about 1 day per 219 years)

**Step 3: Construct a lunisolar calendar.**

**19-year cycle (Metonic cycle):**
1. 12 regular years with 12 months each = 144 months
2. 7 intercalary years with 13 months each = 91 months
3. Total: 235 lunar months over 19 years
4. 235 months × 29.5 days = 6,932.5 days
5. 19 solar years = 6,939.75 days
6. Difference: About 7 days over 19 years (very small drift)

**Year assignment:**
- Year 1: 12 months
- Year 2: 12 months
- Year 3: 13 months (intercalary)
- Year 4: 12 months
- Year 5: 12 months
- Year 6: 13 months (intercalary)
- (And so on, repeating the pattern)

**Step 4: Implement the lunisolar calendar.**

**Calendar months:**
- Each month starts with the new moon
- Months alternate 29 and 30 days (approximately 29.5 average)
- Intercalary months are inserted when needed

**Observation:**
1. Track new moons throughout the year
2. When the calendar month is due to start, observe the new moon
3. Begin the new month on the day of the new moon

**Leap month insertion:**
1. At the designated year (3, 6, 8, 11, 14, 17, 19 in a 19-year cycle), add an extra month after the regular 12
2. This keeps spring planting dates consistent

### Part 5: Determining a Date Without a Calendar

**Step 1: Use the sun's position.**

If the date is lost, the sun's declination reveals the season:
- Declination ≈ 0°: Spring or autumn (equinox)
- Declination ≈ +23.5°: Early summer
- Declination ≈ -23.5°: Early winter
- Other values: Between seasons

**Procedure:**
1. Measure the noon sun's altitude (with sextant or shadow stick)
2. Calculate declination from: declination = 90° - altitude + refraction correction
3. Use a table of sun's declination vs. date (from an almanac)
4. Find the approximate date

**Step 2: Use the moon's position.**

The moon's position relative to bright stars reveals the lunar month:
- If the moon is near specific bright stars, use a lunar distance table to find the date

**Step 3: Use the stars' positions.**

The stars visible at nightfall change throughout the year:
- If you know the current night's visible stars, you can estimate the season and approximate date

**Example:** If Orion is high in the south at midnight, it's winter (in Northern Hemisphere).

## Verification

A calendar system is working if:

- **Equinox/solstice dates are consistent:** Spring equinox occurs on the same date each year (within 1 day)
- **Lunar months align with new moons:** Calendar month starts within 1 day of the actual new moon
- **Seasons align with months:** In a solar calendar, spring planting always occurs in the same months
- **Long-term accuracy:** Over a decade, the calendar date hasn't drifted noticeably from the sun's position

A calendar is broken if:

- **Drift:** Spring equinox creeps earlier or later each year
- **Moon phase misalignment:** Full moons don't occur on expected dates
- **Seasonal creep:** Planting seasons shift to different months over years

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing lunar month with calendar month:** A lunar month is 29.5 days; a calendar month might be 29 or 30 days for approximation
- **Ignoring the 0.25-day solar year remainder:** Without leap days, the calendar drifts noticeably
- **Adding leap days incorrectly:** Adding a leap day every year (365 days per year average) overshoots the true solar year (365.25 days)
- **Failing to observe equinoxes/solstices:** Assumption-based dates drift; observation prevents this
- **Losing track of new moons:** For a lunar calendar, missing a new moon throws off the month count
- **Not adding intercalary months:** A purely lunar calendar will drift out of alignment with seasons within a few years
