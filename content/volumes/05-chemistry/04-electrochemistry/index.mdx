## Overview

Electrochemistry is the study of reactions that produce or consume electrical energy. Two key processes:

1. **Galvanic reactions:** Chemical reactions that produce electricity (batteries)
2. **Electrolytic reactions:** Electrical current that drives chemical reactions (electrolysis)

Understanding electrochemistry helps you:

1. **Generate electricity:** Make batteries and harness natural electrical phenomena
2. **Refine metals:** Extract pure metals from ores using electrolysis
3. **Protect materials:** Prevent corrosion and electroplate surfaces
4. **Understand lightning:** Harness or avoid electrical hazards from natural sources

This chapter teaches practical electrochemistry for post-collapse survival.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of chemical reactions and electrons
- Understanding of acids and bases
- Ability to observe and measure electrical phenomena
- Patience for hands-on experimentation

## Part 1: Galvanic Reactions (Batteries)

### How Batteries Work

A battery consists of two different metals (electrodes) immersed in a chemical solution (electrolyte) that conducts ions.

**Mechanism:**

1. **Oxidation at negative electrode (anode):** Metal atoms lose electrons
   - Example: Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻

2. **Reduction at positive electrode (cathode):** Ions in solution gain electrons
   - Example: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu

3. **Electron flow:** Electrons flow from anode to cathode through external circuit (producing electrical current)

4. **Ion flow:** Ions flow through electrolyte to complete the circuit

**Result:** Continuous electrical current as long as reactants are available

### Galvanic Cell (Simple Battery)

**Components:**

1. **Anode (negative):** Metal that oxidizes more easily (loses electrons)
2. **Cathode (positive):** Metal that oxidizes less easily (or metal ion in solution)
3. **Electrolyte:** Solution containing ions that conduct electricity
4. **External circuit:** Wire connecting electrodes

**Example: Zinc-Copper battery (Daniel cell)**

- Anode: Zinc metal
- Cathode: Copper metal
- Electrolyte: Copper sulfate solution (or salt water for simpler version)
- Voltage: ~1 volt per cell

**Process:**

1. Zinc oxidizes: Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻
2. Copper ions reduce: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu (deposits on copper electrode)
3. Electrons flow through external circuit (electrical current)
4. Zinc dissolves gradually; copper accumulates on cathode

### Combining Cells

**Series connection:** Voltages add
- Two 1-volt cells in series = 2 volts
- Useful for higher voltage

**Parallel connection:** Currents add
- Two identical cells in parallel = same voltage, doubled current
- Useful for higher current

### Materials for Batteries

**Good anode materials (easily oxidized):**
- Zinc (Zn)
- Iron (Fe)
- Magnesium (Mg)—very reactive
- Copper (Cu)—weakly reactive; works only against certain cathodes

**Good cathode materials (resist oxidation):**
- Copper (Cu)
- Graphite (C)
- Platinum (Pt)—excellent but expensive

**Good electrolytes:**
- Salt water (NaCl) - simple, available
- Vinegar (acetic acid) - weak but works
- Copper sulfate solution (CuSO₄) - good for stronger cells
- Zinc sulfate solution (ZnSO₄) - works well
- Any solution with ions and pH < 7 or > 7

### For Survival

A zinc-copper battery in salt water is easy to make and produces about 1 volt per cell. Multiple cells in series can power small circuits (LED, radio, etc.). Batteries are essential for post-collapse lighting and communication.

## Part 2: Electrolytic Reactions

Electrolysis is the use of electrical current to drive a chemical reaction that would not happen spontaneously.

### How Electrolysis Works

**Mechanism:**

1. **Power source:** Battery or generator supplies electrical current
2. **Anode (positive electrode):** Oxidation occurs (ions lose electrons)
3. **Cathode (negative electrode):** Reduction occurs (ions gain electrons)
4. **Electrolyte:** Solution containing ions that conduct current

**Key difference from galvanic cells:** In electrolysis, the electrical current DRIVES the reaction (opposite of galvanic cells where reaction PRODUCES current).

### Electroplating

Electroplating deposits a thin layer of one metal onto another surface.

**Purpose:** Protect from corrosion, enhance appearance, improve conductivity

**Process:**

1. **Anode:** Pure metal to be deposited (e.g., copper, nickel, gold)
2. **Cathode:** Object to be plated
3. **Electrolyte:** Salt solution of the metal (e.g., copper sulfate for copper plating)
4. **Power:** Low voltage DC (direct current)

**Mechanism:**

1. Metal anode oxidizes: Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻
2. Metal ions in solution move to cathode
3. Metal ions reduce at cathode: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu
4. Pure metal deposits on cathode (object being plated)

**For survival:**

Electroplating iron tools with copper or zinc prevents rusting. A simple electroplating setup can extend tool life significantly.

### Refining Metals

Electrolysis can purify metals or extract metals from ores.

**Copper refining:**

1. **Anode:** Impure copper
2. **Cathode:** Pure copper sheet
3. **Electrolyte:** Copper sulfate solution

**Process:**

1. Copper anode oxidizes: Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻
2. Cu²⁺ travels to cathode
3. Cu²⁺ reduces: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu (pure copper deposits)
4. Impurities (heavier than copper) fall to bottom as "anode mud"; lighter impurities remain in solution

**Result:** Very pure copper (99.9%+)

**For survival:**

Copper refining is energy-intensive but produces pure copper useful for electrical work. Early industrial societies used this method.

### Electrowinning

Electrowinning extracts metals from ore solutions using electrolysis.

**Example: Copper from ore**

1. Ore is dissolved in acid or leached with water
2. Solution contains metal ions (Cu²⁺)
3. Electrolysis deposits pure metal at cathode
4. Pure metal is separated and melted

**For survival:**

Electrowinning allows extraction of useful metals from available ores if electrical power is available.

## Part 3: Common Electrochemical Processes

### Corrosion (Electrochemical Oxidation)

Corrosion is the unwanted oxidation of metals.

**Example: Rusting of iron**

- Water on iron surface contains dissolved oxygen and ions
- Iron acts as anode, oxygen as cathode
- Iron oxidizes: Fe → Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻
- Electrons transfer to oxygen: O₂ + 4e⁻ + 2H₂O → 4OH⁻
- Fe²⁺ + OH⁻ → Fe(OH)₂ (rust precursor)
- Rust forms and continues spreading

**Prevention:**

1. **Eliminate water:** Keep iron dry
2. **Eliminate oxygen:** Coat with oil or grease (excludes oxygen and water)
3. **Eliminate ions:** Use distilled water (pure water conducts electricity poorly)
4. **Use cathodic protection:** Apply electrical current to make iron cathode (doesn't oxidize)
5. **Electroplate:** Deposit protective metal (copper, zinc) on iron surface
6. **Use stainless steel:** Iron alloyed with chromium resists corrosion

**For survival:**

Understanding corrosion helps preserve tools. Regular oiling and keeping tools dry are the most practical approaches.

### Galvanic Corrosion

Dissimilar metals in contact can cause faster corrosion.

**Example: Iron bolt in copper fitting**

- Iron is more easily oxidized than copper
- Iron acts as anode, copper as cathode
- Iron corrodes rapidly (faster than it would alone)
- Copper is protected

**Prevention:**

- Keep dissimilar metals separated (paint, insulate, or avoid contact)
- Use compatible metals (materials that corrode at similar rates)

### Cathodic Protection

Applying electrical current to make a metal cathode (preventing oxidation).

**System:**

1. **Sacrificial anode:** A reactive metal (zinc, magnesium)
2. **Protected metal:** The object to be protected (steel pipe, ship hull)
3. **Electrolyte:** Soil or seawater conducting ions
4. **Connection:** Wire connecting metals

**Process:**

- Sacrificial anode oxidizes instead of protected metal
- Anode dissolves slowly; protected metal remains intact

**For survival:**

Cathodic protection extends the life of metal structures (especially in saltwater or corrosive environments).

## Part 4: Electrical Generation and Storage

### Simple Battery Production

**Zinc-copper battery (1 volt per cell):**

Materials:
- Zinc strip or penny (zinc-coated)
- Copper strip or penny (copper)
- Salt water or vinegar
- Jar or container

Process:
1. Mix salt with water (or use vinegar directly)
2. Place zinc and copper strips in jar, separated (no contact)
3. Connect with wire outside jar
4. Current flows (electrons through wire)
5. Measure voltage with meter (should be ~0.7–1 volt)

**Multiple cells:**
- Stack several batteries (use stacking disks)
- Series connection: Stack with zinc of one cell touching copper of next cell
- Result: 5 cells = ~5 volts

### Energy Storage

A battery stores chemical energy and converts it to electrical energy on demand.

**Capacity:** Measured in amp-hours (Ah)
- 1 Ah battery can supply 1 amp for 1 hour
- Capacity depends on anode mass and electrolyte volume

**Duration:** How long a battery lasts
- Larger anodes and cathodes = longer duration
- Better electrolyte conductivity = longer duration
- Thicker electrodes = longer duration

### For Survival

Batteries are essential for:
- Lighting (LED powered by battery)
- Communication (radio, signaling device)
- Timekeeping (clock, timer)
- Medical devices (defibrillator, pacemaker)

A simple battery production setup can create reliable power sources.

## Part 5: Safety and Hazards

### Electrical Hazards

1. **Electrocution:** Electrical current through body can disrupt heart rhythm or burn tissue
   - Risk increases with voltage and current
   - Wet skin reduces resistance, increasing danger

2. **Arc flash:** Electrical arc can cause severe burns
   - Risk with high current

3. **Chemical exposure:** Electrolyte solutions may be toxic or corrosive
   - Some batteries use harsh chemicals
   - Acids and bases can cause burns

### Safe Practices

1. **Insulation:** Rubber gloves, dry hands, dry work area
2. **Voltage limits:** Batteries at 12 volts or less are relatively safe
3. **Current limits:** Batteries producing less than 1 amp are safe
4. **Chemical safety:** Wear gloves and eye protection when handling strong electrolytes
5. **Ventilation:** Hydrogen gas is produced in some electrochemical reactions (flammable; ensure ventilation)

### Common Mistakes

1. **Mixing dissimilar batteries:** Different battery types discharge unevenly; can cause leakage
2. **Overcharging:** Can damage batteries and produce dangerous gases
3. **Connecting backwards:** Wrong polarity can damage devices
4. **Ignoring corrosion:** Corroded battery terminals reduce current flow

## Common Mistakes

1. **Assuming more voltage is always better:** High voltage increases hazard and can damage devices. For survival, 1–12 volts is practical.

2. **Using the same battery type everywhere:** Different devices need different voltages and currents. Match battery output to device requirement.

3. **Leaving batteries wet:** Water short-circuits batteries and promotes corrosion. Keep dry.

4. **Ignoring polarity:** Reversing polarity damages devices. Always check + and − connections.

5. **Thinking corrosion is purely chemical:** Corrosion is electrochemical. Electrical isolation (cathodic protection) is one solution.

6. **Underestimating hydrogen production:** In water electrolysis, hydrogen gas is flammable. Ensure ventilation.

7. **Using materials that shouldn't contact:** Dissimilar metals accelerate galvanic corrosion. Keep them separated.

8. **Neglecting safety with electrolytes:** Some electrolytes are toxic or caustic. Wear protection.
