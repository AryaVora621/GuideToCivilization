## Overview

Organic chemistry is the study of compounds containing carbon. Nearly all biological molecules are organic (carbohydrates, proteins, fats, nucleic acids). Understanding organic chemistry helps you:

1. **Produce fermented foods:** Bacteria ferment sugars into acids and alcohol
2. **Produce alcohol:** Fermentation can produce ethanol for fuel or medicine
3. **Extract useful compounds:** Tannins, resins, dyes from plants
4. **Understand biochemistry:** How digestion and metabolism work
5. **Preserve materials:** Understanding how organic compounds degrade and how to prevent it

This chapter teaches the basics of organic chemistry relevant to post-collapse survival.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of chemical bonding (covalent bonds)
- Understanding of atoms, particularly carbon
- Ability to visualize molecular structures
- Familiarity with fermentation

## Materials & Tools

**For fermentation and organic chemistry observation:**
- Sugars (glucose, fruit, molasses)

**For extraction:**

## Part 1: Understanding Carbon and Organic Molecules

### Why Carbon?

Carbon is unique among elements because:

1. **Four valence electrons:** Carbon forms four covalent bonds
2. **Small size:** Allows tight bonding with other atoms
3. **Self-bonding:** Carbon bonds strongly to other carbon atoms
4. **Versatility:** Forms chains, rings, branches, and complex structures

**Result:** Carbon can form nearly unlimited types of molecules

### Carbon Bonding Patterns

**Single bonds (C—C):** Two carbon atoms bonded by one shared electron pair
- Example: Ethane (C₂H₆)

**Double bonds (C=C):** Two carbon atoms bonded by two shared electron pairs
- Example: Ethene/Ethylene (C₂H₄)

**Triple bonds (C≡C):** Two carbon atoms bonded by three shared electron pairs
- Example: Ethyne/Acetylene (C₂H₂)

**Rings:** Carbon atoms form rings instead of chains
- Example: Benzene (C₆H₆)

### Simple Organic Molecules

**Methane (CH₄):** Simplest hydrocarbon
- One carbon bonded to four hydrogens
- Gas; component of natural gas

**Ethane (C₂H₆):** Two carbons linked by single bond
- Gas; component of petroleum

**Ethene/Ethylene (C₂H₄):** Two carbons linked by double bond
- Gas; plant hormone; can polymerize into plastic

**Ethyne/Acetylene (C₂H₂):** Two carbons linked by triple bond
- Gas; used for welding

## Part 2: Hydrocarbons

Hydrocarbons are compounds containing only carbon and hydrogen. They are the basis for fuels (oil, gas, coal).

### Alkanes (Saturated Hydrocarbons)

Alkanes have only single bonds between carbon atoms.

**General formula:** CₙH₂ₙ₊₂

**Examples:**
- Methane: CH₄
- Ethane: C₂H₆
- Propane: C₃H₈
- Butane: C₄H₁₀

**Properties:**
- Generally unreactive
- Nonpolar (don't dissolve in water)
- Burn to produce heat and carbon dioxide

**Uses:**
- Fuels (heating, cooking, transportation)
- Lubricants

### Alkenes (Unsaturated Hydrocarbons)

Alkenes contain one or more double bonds between carbon atoms.

**General formula:** CₙH₂ₙ

**Examples:**
- Ethene/Ethylene: C₂H₄
- Propene: C₃H₆

**Properties:**
- More reactive than alkanes
- Can polymerize (link together) to form plastics
- Participate in addition reactions (double bond breaks and adds atoms)

**Uses:**
- Production of plastics
- Plant hormone
- Starting material for many chemicals

### Alkynes (Highly Unsaturated Hydrocarbons)

Alkynes contain one or more triple bonds.

**General formula:** CₙH₂ₙ₋₂

**Examples:**
- Ethyne/Acetylene: C₂H₂
- Propyne: C₃H₄

**Properties:**
- Most reactive of simple hydrocarbons
- Triple bonds readily break in reactions

**Uses:**
- Welding (burning acetylene produces extreme heat)
- Starting material for chemicals

## Part 3: Functional Groups

Functional groups are clusters of atoms that give molecules specific chemical properties.

### Alcohols (—OH group)

Contain a hydroxyl group (one oxygen and one hydrogen bonded to carbon).

**Examples:**
- Ethanol (C₂H₅OH): Drinking alcohol, fuel
- Methanol (CH₃OH): Toxic; solvent

**Properties:**
- Polar; dissolve in water
- Can form hydrogen bonds
- Flammable
- Volatile (evaporate easily)

**For survival:**
- Ethanol is produced by fermentation
- Used for medicine, fuel, food preservation

### Aldehydes (C=O group)

Contain a double bond between carbon and oxygen, with hydrogen on one side.

**Example:**
- Formaldehyde (HCHO): Preservative
- Acetaldehyde (CH₃CHO): Product of alcohol oxidation

**Properties:**
- Easily oxidized to carboxylic acids
- Often have pungent smells
- Reactive

### Ketones (C=O group)

Contain a double bond between carbon and oxygen, with carbon atoms on both sides.

**Example:**
- Acetone (CH₃COCH₃): Solvent

**Properties:**
- Similar polarity to aldehydes
- Used as solvents
- More stable than aldehydes

### Carboxylic Acids (COOH group)

Contain a carboxyl group (carbon double bonded to oxygen and bonded to a hydroxyl group).

**Examples:**
- Acetic acid (CH₃COOH): Vinegar
- Formic acid (HCOOH): Produced by ants; preservative
- Citric acid: Naturally in citrus

**Properties:**
- Acidic (pH 2–4)
- Polar; dissolve in water
- Pungent smells
- Preserve food by inhibiting bacteria

**For survival:**
- Produced by bacterial fermentation
- Used for food preservation
- Essential in many chemical reactions

### Ethers (C—O—C group)

Contain an oxygen bonded to two carbons (not involving hydrogen).

**Example:**
- Diethyl ether (C₂H₅OC₂H₅): Solvent, anesthetic

**Properties:**
- Relatively nonpolar
- Good solvents
- Flammable
- Can form explosive peroxides on exposure to air

## Part 4: Fermentation and Alcohol Production

### How Fermentation Works

Fermentation is the anaerobic (without oxygen) breakdown of sugar by microorganisms.

**Reaction (yeast fermentation):**

C₆H₁₂O₆ (glucose) → 2 C₂H₅OH (ethanol) + 2 CO₂ (carbon dioxide)

**By-products:**
- Ethanol (useful)
- Carbon dioxide (causes fizzing in wine, beer)
- Heat
- Various esters and other compounds (give fermented drinks flavor)

**Microorganisms involved:**
- Yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae): Produces ethanol
- Bacteria (Lactobacillus): Produces lactic acid (sour flavor)
- Acetic acid bacteria: Produces acetic acid (vinegar)

### Making Alcohol

**Basic procedure:**

1. **Prepare sugar source:** Grains, fruits, honey, or molasses
2. **Add yeast:** Wild yeast on fruit surfaces or cultured yeast
3. **Provide environment:**
   - Anaerobic (sealed container)
   - Warm (70–80°F optimal)
   - pH 3–5 (acidic inhibits unwanted bacteria)
4. **Wait:** 1–2 weeks for fermentation
5. **Separate:** Pour off liquid, leaving sediment (yeast) behind
6. **Result:** Fermented liquid containing 5–15% ethanol

### Ethanol Properties and Uses

**Physical properties:**
- Liquid at room temperature
- Boils at 78°C (lower than water at 100°C)
- Miscible with water (mixes completely)
- Flammable

**Uses:**
- Drinking (if safe fermentation conditions maintained)
- Fuel (burns to produce heat)
- Solvent (for medicines, extracts)
- Preservation (inhibits bacterial growth)
- Disinfection (70% ethanol kills microorganisms)

## Part 5: Carbohydrates and Sugars

Carbohydrates are organic compounds with formula Cₙ(H₂O)ₘ.

### Monosaccharides (Simple Sugars)

**Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆):**
- Found in all living cells
- Produced by photosynthesis in plants
- Substrate for fermentation

**Fructose (C₆H₁₂O₆):**
- Sweetest sugar
- Found in fruits and honey
- Fermentable

**Galactose (C₆H₁₂O₆):**
- Component of milk sugar (lactose)
- Fermentable

### Disaccharides (Two-Sugar Molecules)

**Sucrose (C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁):**
- Table sugar
- Must be broken into glucose and fructose before fermentation
- Some yeasts can ferment directly; others cannot

**Lactose (C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁):**
- Milk sugar
- Not fermentable by common yeast
- Fermentable by lactic acid bacteria (yogurt production)

### Polysaccharides (Many-Sugar Molecules)

**Starch (C₆H₁₀O₅)ₙ:**
- Storage carbohydrate in plants (grains, potatoes)
- Not directly fermentable
- Must be broken down into sugars first (requires enzymes or heating)

**Cellulose (C₆H₁₀O₅)ₙ:**
- Structural carbohydrate in plants
- Cannot be digested by humans or common microorganisms
- Important in fiber

## Part 5b: Lipids (Fats and Oils)

Lipids are organic compounds that are mostly nonpolar and hydrophobic (water-repellent).

### Fatty Acids

Fatty acids are long hydrocarbon chains with a carboxylic acid group at one end.

**Structure:**
- Carboxylic acid group (COOH)
- Long hydrocarbon chain (usually 12–18 carbons)
- End carbon with methyl group (CH₃)

**Examples:**
- Palmitic acid (16 carbons): Saturated, solid fat
- Stearic acid (18 carbons): Saturated, solid fat
- Oleic acid (18 carbons with one double bond): Unsaturated, liquid oil

**Properties:**
- Saturated fats (no double bonds): Solid at room temperature; more stable
- Unsaturated fats (with double bonds): Liquid at room temperature; more reactive to oxidation
- Insoluble in water; soluble in nonpolar solvents

### Triglycerides

Triglycerides are three fatty acids bonded to glycerin (an alcohol).

**Structure:** Glycerin backbone with three fatty acid chains attached

**Found in:** 
- Animal fats (lard, tallow, butter)
- Plant oils (olive, coconut, sunflower)

**Properties:**
- Energy storage molecules
- High caloric content (9 calories per gram)
- Used for cooking, lighting, lubrication
- Saponifiable (can be converted to soap by reacting with base)

### Using Fats in Post-Collapse

**For energy:** Consume as food (high calorie content)
**For light:** Rendered animal fat in lamps (tallow candles)
**For cooking:** Animal fat or plant oils
**For preservation:** Fat protects food from air and microorganisms
**For soap:** React animal fat with lye (saponification)

## Part 5c: Proteins (Basic Concept)

Proteins are complex organic molecules made from amino acids. While detailed protein chemistry is complex, understanding basic protein properties is useful.

### Amino Acids

Amino acids are organic molecules containing:
- Amino group (NH₂)
- Carboxylic acid group (COOH)
- Specific side chain (R group)

**Examples:**
- Alanine: Simple side chain
- Glutamic acid: Acidic side chain
- Lysine: Basic side chain

**Number:** 20 standard amino acids in proteins

### Protein Structure

Amino acids link together in long chains (peptide bonds):

- Primary structure: Sequence of amino acids
- Secondary structure: Coiling (alpha helix) or folding (beta sheet)
- Tertiary structure: 3D shape
- Quaternary structure: Multiple protein subunits

### Protein Functions

- **Enzymes:** Speed up chemical reactions
- **Structural:** Collagen in skin, keratin in hair
- **Transport:** Hemoglobin carries oxygen
- **Defense:** Antibodies fight infection
- **Movement:** Myosin and actin in muscles

### Protein Denaturation

Heat, acid, or mechanical stress can unfold proteins:

- Raw egg: Clear protein (albumin) is soluble
- Cooked egg: Heat denatures protein; becomes opaque and solid
- Curd cheese: Acid (vinegar) denatures milk protein; forms solid curd

## Part 6: Practical Organic Chemistry Applications

### Tannin Extraction (From Tree Bark, Leaves, or Berries)

Tannins are organic compounds used for:
- Tanning leather
- Dying fabric
- Medicinal use (astringent)

**Process:**

1. Collect plant material (oak bark, walnut leaves, pomegranate skin)
2. Soak in water (1 part plant material to 5 parts water)
3. Heat gently (do not boil)
4. Steep for several hours to days
5. Strain and use the liquid

**Result:** Tannin solution for leather or fabric treatment

### Dye Extraction (From Plants)

Many plants produce organic dyes:

- **Indigo (blue):** From indigo plant leaves
- **Madder (red):** From madder root
- **Weld (yellow):** From weld plant
- **Walnut (brown):** From walnut hull

**Process:**
1. Collect and dry plant material
2. Simmer in water to extract dyes
3. Use for dyeing fabric (requires mordants to fix the color)

### Preserving Wood and Materials

Organic compounds can preserve wood:

- **Resins:** Applied to wood surface; water-repellent and protective
- **Tannins:** Preserve wood and prevent rot
- **Oils:** Protect and darken wood

**Application:** Coat wood with these compounds to extend useful life

## Part 7: Troubleshooting Fermentation

### Fermentation Not Starting

**Causes:**
- Temperature too cold (below 60°F)
- Yeast dead or insufficient
- Too much salt or acid

**Solutions:**
- Warm container to 70–80°F
- Add more fresh yeast
- Reduce salt or dilute if over-acidic

### Fermentation Producing Bad Smell

**Cause:**
- Contamination (wrong bacteria or fungi)

**Solution:**
- Discard and start fresh
- Ensure anaerobic conditions (sealed container)
- Sterilize equipment with boiling water

### Final Product Too Acidic or Bitter

**Cause:**
- Over-fermentation or bacterial infection

**Solution:**
- Shorter fermentation time for next batch
- Ensure clean conditions

## Common Mistakes

1. **Confusing fermentation with spoilage:** Fermentation is controlled microbial action; spoilage is contamination. Proper conditions (temperature, pH, anaerobic) ensure fermentation.

2. **Heating fermented products:** Heat kills yeast and stops fermentation. Keep at room temperature until done.

3. **Using wrong container:** Metal containers react with acids produced; use glass or ceramic.

4. **Ignoring anaerobic conditions:** Oxygen allows acetic acid bacteria to produce vinegar instead of alcohol. Seal the container.

5. **Over-fermenting:** Extended fermentation produces increasingly acidic products. Stop at desired alcohol level.

6. **Adding sugar continuously:** Sugar must be present at start or added in pulses; continuous adding confuses fermentation.

7. **Not waiting long enough:** Fermentation takes 1–2 weeks or more. Impatience leads to incomplete reaction.

8. **Using contaminated yeast:** Use only food-grade yeast from reliable sources.
