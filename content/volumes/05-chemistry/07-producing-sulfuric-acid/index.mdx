## Overview

Sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) is the foundational chemical for industrial production. It is used in:

1. **Battery production:** Lead-acid batteries require sulfuric acid as electrolyte
2. **Metal processing:** Dissolving metals, refining copper and other metals
3. **Water treatment:** Removing impurities, pH adjustment
4. **Fertilizer production:** Converting rock phosphate into soluble phosphate (superphosphate)
5. **Food preservation:** Pickling and preservation of vegetables
6. **Textile processing:** Treating fibers
7. **Other chemicals:** Base chemical for making explosives, dyes, and other compounds

Post-collapse, the ability to produce sulfuric acid is essential for technological survival. This chapter teaches practical methods for small to medium-scale production.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of combustion and oxidation reactions
- Understanding of acids and acid properties
- Ability to heat materials to 500–1000°C
- Ability to work with corrosive materials
- Respect for chemical hazards (sulfuric acid is extremely dangerous)

## Materials & Tools

**For sulfuric acid production (pyrite method):**
- Pyrite (FeS₂) or sulfur (S)
- Furnace (capable of reaching 1000°C)
- Air source (bellows or air pump)
- Chamber (for absorbing SO₂ and converting to SO₃)
- Lead or ceramic vessels (for absorption)
- Water (for final absorption)
- Thermometer
- Safety equipment: gloves (rubber or ceramic), eye protection, apron, respiratory protection

**For testing:**
- Indicator papers or pH indicators
- Scale for weighing (if available)

## Part 1: Chemistry of Sulfuric Acid Production

### The Contact Process

The modern industrial method (Contact Process) has three steps:

**Step 1: Oxidize sulfur to sulfur dioxide**

S + O₂ → SO₂ (sulfur dioxide)

Or burning pyrite:

4 FeS₂ + 11 O₂ → 2 Fe₂O₃ + 8 SO₂

- Sulfur or pyrite is heated to about 500°C
- Oxygen (from air) reacts with sulfur
- Product: Sulfur dioxide gas (SO₂)

**Step 2: Oxidize sulfur dioxide to sulfur trioxide**

2 SO₂ + O₂ → 2 SO₃ (sulfur trioxide)

- This reaction is slow at room temperature
- Catalyst (vanadium oxide, platinum, or other catalysts) speeds the reaction
- Temperature around 400–500°C is optimal
- Higher temperature speeds reaction but shifts equilibrium away from SO₃

**Step 3: Absorb sulfur trioxide in water**

SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄ (sulfuric acid)

- Sulfur trioxide dissolves in water
- Reaction is exothermic (releases much heat)
- Produces concentrated sulfuric acid

### Overall Reaction

S (or FeS₂) + 2 O₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₄

Or for pyrite:

2 FeS₂ + 7 O₂ + 2 H₂O → 2 H₂SO₄ + 2 Fe₂O₃

## Part 2: Practical Production — Pyrite Method

This is a practical method using pyrite (iron sulfide), which is common in many regions.

### Procedure: Small-Scale Sulfuric Acid Production

**Materials (approximate quantities):**

- 10 pounds pyrite (FeS₂) or 5 pounds pure sulfur
- Furnace capable of 1000°C
- Lead chamber or ceramic vessel (large pot)
- Water (distilled or rainwater)

**Step 1: Prepare pyrite (30 minutes)**

- Crush pyrite into pea-sized pieces (not powder—too small burns too fast)
- Ensure pieces are dry
- Spread in furnace bed

**Step 2: Ignite pyrite (1 hour)**

- Light a fire under the pyrite bed
- Heat gradually to around 300–400°C until pyrite begins smoking (SO₂ being produced)
- Continue heating to 500–700°C
- Pyrite will burn vigorously when hot enough
- Continue adding fuel and maintaining heat

**Step 3: Collect SO₂ gas**

- Hot SO₂ gas rises from the pyrite bed
- Direct it into a lead chamber or ceramic pot (this is the key step)
- SO₂ gas is colorless but has a pungent, acidic smell (avoid breathing)

**Step 4: Oxidize SO₂ to SO₃ (catalyst step)**

- In the lead chamber, SO₂ reacts slowly with oxygen from air
- Add catalyst (if available): platinum, vanadium oxide, or even charcoal increases reaction rate
- OR: Introduce air into the chamber to increase oxygen
- Reaction proceeds at around 400–500°C
- Products: SO₃ (sulfur trioxide) + some unreacted SO₂

**Step 5: Absorb in water**

- Carefully direct the gas mixture over water in a ceramic or glass vessel
- SO₃ dissolves and reacts with water
- DO NOT add water to the gas directly (violent reaction)
- Instead, direct gas over water surface or through water spray
- Reaction is exothermic—use ice or cold water bath under the absorption vessel

**Step 6: Collect sulfuric acid**

- Liquid collecting in the vessel is dilute sulfuric acid (around 50–70% H₂SO₄)
- For concentrated acid: Evaporate the liquid gently (do not boil—acid is volatile at high temperature)
- Concentrated sulfuric acid is about 98% H₂SO₄

### Key Challenges

**1. Catalyst:** The conversion of SO₂ to SO₃ is slow without a catalyst
- Platinum is ideal but expensive
- Vanadium oxide (V₂O₅) works well
- Iron oxide (Fe₂O₃)—from roasted pyrite—also acts as catalyst
- Charcoal or other carbon materials are weak catalysts but available

**2. Temperature control:** 
- Too hot (above 600°C): SO₃ breaks down and is lost
- Too cold (below 300°C): Reaction is very slow
- Optimal: 400–500°C for best SO₃ production

**3. Gas handling:**
- SO₂ and SO₃ are corrosive and toxic gases
- Apparatus must be airtight
- Ventilation is essential (gas not absorbed escapes to air)
- Never breathe SO₂ (causes respiratory damage)

**4. Water absorption:**
- SO₃ reacts violently with water
- Must be absorbed slowly over water surface
- Never add water directly to SO₃ gas

### Efficiency and Optimization

The overall production efficiency depends on several factors:

**Residence time:** Gas must stay in the catalytic chamber long enough for oxidation
- Slow flow through catalyst chamber: more conversion (85–95%)
- Fast flow: less conversion (50–75%)
- Practical balance: 1–2 seconds residence time

**Excess oxygen:** The reaction requires oxygen
- Stoichiometric oxygen (just enough): 2 SO₂ + O₂ → 2 SO₃
- In practice, use 20–40% excess air to ensure complete reaction
- Furnace air blast provides oxygen naturally

**Reactor design:** Shape and packing of catalyst chamber matters
- Packed bed of catalyst (charcoal, iron oxide, or vanadium) speeds reaction
- Gas channels through catalyst, contacting it constantly
- Larger surface area of catalyst increases conversion

**Practical production rates:**
- Small furnace (5 pounds pyrite): 0.5–1 pound sulfuric acid per hour
- Medium furnace (50 pounds pyrite): 5–10 pounds acid per hour
- Scale is limited by heat control and apparatus sophistication

## Part 3: Alternative Methods

### Sulfur Method (Direct)

If pure sulfur is available:

1. Heat sulfur to 200°C (it melts)
2. Burn in oxygen-rich atmosphere
3. S + O₂ → SO₂
4. Proceed as above (oxidize SO₂ to SO₃, absorb in water)

**Advantage:** Cleaner than pyrite (no iron oxide byproduct)
**Disadvantage:** Requires pure sulfur; pyrite is more common

### Lead Chamber Process (Historical)

Before the Contact Process, the Lead Chamber Process was used:

1. SO₂ gas is bubbled through water in a large lead-lined chamber
2. Dilute sulfuric acid forms (around 50% H₂SO₄)
3. Concentration by evaporation produces concentrated acid

**Advantages:** 
- Simpler apparatus
- No high-temperature catalytic chamber needed

**Disadvantages:**
- Produces only dilute acid (50–70%)
- Requires large chambers
- Very slow (takes days or weeks)

## Part 4: Concentration and Purification

### Dilute to Concentrated Acid

Acid from absorption is typically 50–70% H₂SO₄. To concentrate:

1. Heat gently (do not boil vigorously)
2. Water evaporates
3. Acid concentration increases
4. Stop at 98% (further heating may decompose acid)

**Caution:** Concentrated sulfuric acid boils at 337°C. Overheating causes bumping and spattering—very dangerous.

### Removing Impurities

Acid from pyrite burning may contain:
- Water
- Unreacted SO₂
- Fe₂O₃ particles (iron oxide)

**Removal methods:**

1. **SO₂ removal:** Bubble air through to oxidize SO₂ to SO₃, which is absorbed
2. **Water removal:** Gentle evaporation
3. **Iron oxide removal:** 
   - Allow acid to settle
   - Decant carefully (iron oxide sinks)
   - Or filter through cloth if acid is dilute enough

## Part 5: Practical Applications

### Lead-Acid Batteries

Sulfuric acid is essential for battery production:

- Battery cells: Lead plates in sulfuric acid solution
- Concentration: 30–40% H₂SO₄ for batteries
- Acid conducts electricity between plates
- Charging and discharging involve sulfur chemistry

### Metal Processing

Sulfuric acid dissolves metals:
- Copper: Cu + 2 H₂SO₄ → CuSO₄ + SO₂ + H₂O (requires hot, concentrated acid)
- Iron: Fe + H₂SO₄ → FeSO₄ + H₂
- Gold and silver: Don't dissolve in sulfuric acid (but other metals in ore might)

### Fertilizer Production

Phosphate rock (calcium phosphate) is converted to soluble fertilizer:

- Rock phosphate + H₂SO₄ → Calcium sulfate + Phosphoric acid (soluble phosphate)
- This is called superphosphate, essential for agriculture

### Water Treatment

Sulfuric acid is used for:
- pH adjustment (lowering pH)
- Iron removal (sulfuric acid reacts with iron, precipitating iron oxide)
- Copper sulfate production (algicide)

## Part 6: Safety and Hazards

### Extreme Hazards

**Sulfuric acid is one of the most dangerous chemicals. Respect it completely.**

1. **Severe burns:** Concentrated H₂SO₄ causes instant severe burns
2. **Dehydration:** H₂SO₄ absorbs water; burns tissue by dehydration
3. **Fumes:** SO₂ and SO₃ are toxic gases
4. **Exothermic reaction:** Mixing with water releases tremendous heat
5. **Permanence:** Even small amounts cause permanent scarring

### Safety Procedures

1. **Always wear protection:** Rubber gloves, eye protection (goggles), apron, long sleeves
2. **Respiratory protection:** For SO₂ and SO₃ production, use respirator or mask
3. **Never pour water into acid:** Always add acid to water (slowly, with stirring)
4. **Ventilation:** Ensure excellent airflow to remove SO₂ and SO₃ gases
5. **Segregate apparatus:** Keep production area away from living/working areas
6. **Emergency procedure:** Have water available for rinsing burns; water is the only treatment

### If Sulfuric Acid Contacts Skin

1. Immediately brush off any visible acid
2. Rinse with copious amounts of water (at least 15 minutes)
3. Do not delay—minutes matter
4. Remove contaminated clothing
5. Seek medical care if available

## Verification

**Sulfuric acid is successfully produced when:**

1. Liquid is clear and colorless (or pale yellow from iron content)
2. Liquid is extremely corrosive (burns skin instantly)
3. pH is less than 0 (extremely acidic)
4. Liquid has specific gravity above 1.8 (for concentrated acid)
5. Concentration can be estimated: drops fizz violently on cool iron

## Part 7: Troubleshooting Acid Production

### No Acid Being Produced

**Possible causes:**
- Furnace temperature too low (below 400°C)
- Pyrite not igniting properly
- Apparatus leaking gas
- Water not properly positioned for absorption

**Solutions:**
- Ensure furnace reaches at least 500°C consistently
- Use dried pyrite; add charcoal to furnace to boost temperature
- Check all joints for leaks; seal with clay if necessary
- Ensure water or cold surface is in direct path of gas

### Acid Too Dilute (Below 50%)

**Causes:**
- Absorption vessel too cold (excess water condenses instead of reacting with SO₃)
- Gas flow too slow (SO₂ escapes unreacted)
- Insufficient pyrite burning

**Solutions:**
- Warm absorption vessel slightly (but not so much that water evaporates)
- Increase furnace temperature and air flow to increase SO₂ production
- Use more pyrite or burn longer

### Acid Too Concentrated (Above 90%)

**Cause:**
- Unreacted SO₂ or SO₃ escaping (water is saturated)

**Solution:**
- Concentrate by gentle heating (evaporation)
- Or use more water in absorption vessel

### Acid Smells Strongly of SO₂

**Cause:**
- Incomplete oxidation of SO₂ to SO₃; unreacted SO₂ in product

**Solution:**
- Increase catalyst amount or effectiveness
- Increase residence time in catalyst chamber
- Increase temperature (but not above 600°C)

### Furnace Temperature Unstable

**Causes:**
- Irregular fuel feed
- Pyrite moisture content
- Poor furnace insulation

**Solutions:**
- Feed fuel evenly and continuously
- Dry pyrite thoroughly before use
- Improve furnace insulation with ceramic or clay

## Common Mistakes

1. **Adding water to acid:** Violently exothermic; causes boiling and spattering. Always add acid to water.

2. **Inadequate ventilation:** SO₂ causes respiratory damage; ensure constant airflow.

3. **Poor temperature control:** Too hot (above 600°C), SO₃ breaks down; too cold (below 300°C), reaction is slow.

4. **No catalyst:** Without catalyst, conversion is extremely slow. Charcoal or iron oxide helps significantly.

5. **Mixing concentrations:** Mixing acid of different concentrations causes heat release. Always do slowly with active cooling.

6. **Storing in wrong containers:** Concentrated H₂SO₄ corrodes most materials. Use glass or ceramic only.

7. **Rushing the process:** Each step requires time. Rushing leads to incomplete conversion and wasted fuel. Expect 6–12 hours for meaningful acid production.

8. **Ignoring safety:** One moment of carelessness causes permanent injury. Respect the extreme hazard; protective equipment is not optional.

9. **No backup ventilation:** If primary ventilation fails, SO₂ builds up to dangerous levels. Have secondary outlet or manual ventilation.

10. **Insufficient cooling during absorption:** Exothermic reaction generates intense heat; cooling is essential to maintain efficiency and prevent apparatus damage.

11. **Using wet pyrite:** Water interferes with combustion and lowers furnace temperature. Dry pyrite thoroughly first.

12. **Expecting rapid production:** Sulfuric acid is energy-intensive to produce. Plan for slow production and build inventory over time.
