## Overview

Post-collapse, modern synthetic fertilizers are unavailable. Yet sustaining agriculture for growing populations requires nutrient-rich soil. Understanding how to produce fertilizers is essential for food security.

Three nutrients are critical for plant growth (NPK—Nitrogen, Phosphorus, Potassium):

1. **Nitrogen (N):** Used for leaf growth and protein synthesis
2. **Phosphorus (P):** Used for root development, flowering, and seed formation
3. **Potassium (K):** Used for disease resistance, water regulation, and general plant health

This chapter teaches practical methods for producing these fertilizers from available materials.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of chemical reactions
- Understanding of acids and bases
- Ability to manage composting
- Access to sulfuric acid (from previous chapter)
- Ability to source mineral materials (phosphate rock, potash minerals, nitrogen sources)

## Part 1: Plant Nutrients and Deficiency Symptoms

### Nitrogen (N)

**Role:** 
- Essential for protein synthesis
- Required for chlorophyll production (photosynthesis)
- Promotes vegetative growth

**Deficiency symptoms:**
- Yellowing of older leaves (veins may stay green)
- Stunted growth
- Reduced yield

**Sources:**
- Animal manure (3–5% nitrogen)
- Blood meal (12–15% nitrogen)
- Bone meal (3–4% nitrogen)
- Nitrogen-fixing bacteria in soil

### Phosphorus (P)

**Role:**
- Essential for energy transfer (ATP, NADP)
- Required for root development
- Promotes flowering and fruit/seed production

**Deficiency symptoms:**
- Purple or reddish coloration in leaves
- Delayed maturity
- Poor root development
- Reduced seed/fruit production

**Sources:**
- Rock phosphate (10–20% phosphorus)
- Bone meal (8–10% phosphorus)
- Animal waste (1–2% phosphorus)

### Potassium (K)

**Role:**
- Regulates water uptake and loss
- Improves disease resistance
- Enhances flavor and nutrition in crops

**Deficiency symptoms:**
- Yellowing or browning of leaf edges
- Weak stems (lodging in grain crops)
- Poor disease resistance
- Reduced yield quality

**Sources:**
- Wood ash (4–13% potassium, depending on wood)
- Potash minerals (10–50% potassium)
- Seaweed (1–2% potassium)

## Part 2: Nitrogen Fertilizers

### Nitrogen Fixation

The most sustainable nitrogen source is biological nitrogen fixation—using legume crops with bacterial symbiosis.

**How it works:**
- Legume roots host nitrogen-fixing bacteria (Rhizobium)
- Bacteria convert atmospheric nitrogen (N₂) to ammonium (NH₄⁺)
- Ammonium is used by plant; excess enters soil
- Per acre: 50–200 pounds nitrogen fixed annually

**Legume crops:**
- Alfalfa (most nitrogen: 100–200 lbs/acre/year)
- Clover (50–100 lbs/acre/year)
- Beans and peas (40–80 lbs/acre/year)
- Lentils (40–60 lbs/acre/year)

**Using nitrogen-fixing crops:**
- Plant 1–2 seasons in nitrogen-fixing crop
- Plow crop back into soil (green manure)
- Follow with nitrogen-demanding crop (wheat, corn)
- Result: Sustainable nitrogen cycling without external input

### Composting (Passive Nitrogen Accumulation)

The simplest method for nitrogen fertilizer is composting:

**Materials:**
- Organic waste (manure, food scraps, plant material)
- Water
- Bin or pile

**Process:**

1. **Layer materials:** Mix carbon (dry leaves, straw) with nitrogen (manure, food waste) in ratio 3:1
2. **Add water:** Keep moist but not waterlogged
3. **Turn pile:** Every 2–4 weeks for faster decomposition
4. **Wait:** 3–6 months for cool compost; 4–8 weeks for hot compost (turned frequently)
5. **Result:** Rich, dark compost (~1–2% nitrogen)

**Advantage:** Simple, natural nitrogen source
**Disadvantage:** Slow; low nitrogen concentration

### Making Nitrogen-Rich Solutions

**Animal manure water (liquid manure):**

1. Fill barrel with water
2. Add fresh manure (1 part manure to 10 parts water)
3. Steep for 1–2 weeks
4. Strain
5. Result: Liquid containing nitrogen compounds

**Application:** Dilute and apply to soil or spray on leaves

### Ammonium Sulfate (from Sulfuric Acid)

If sulfuric acid is available:

**Process:**

1. Burn organic nitrogen material (manure, blood meal) to produce ammonia gas (NH₃)
2. Or: React with available ammonia source
3. Add ammonia gas to sulfuric acid solution carefully:
   - NH₃ + H₂SO₄ → (NH₄)₂SO₄ (ammonium sulfate)
4. Result: Solid ammonium sulfate fertilizer (~20% nitrogen)

**Caution:** Ammonia is caustic; handle carefully

## Part 3: Phosphate Fertilizers

### Sources of Phosphorus

Phosphorus is found in:

**Rock phosphate (primary source):**
- Mined mineral containing 10–30% phosphorus
- Insoluble; plants cannot absorb it directly
- Must be processed into soluble form

**Bone meal:**
- Ground animal bones containing 8–10% phosphorus
- Slightly soluble; slow-release
- Requires 3–4 months to become available

**Animal manure:**
- Contains 1–2% phosphorus
- Readily available to plants

**Guano (bird or bat droppings):**
- Very high phosphorus (8–12%)
- Readily soluble

### Making Superphosphate

Superphosphate is produced by treating rock phosphate with sulfuric acid. This converts insoluble phosphate to soluble phosphate that plants can absorb.

**Materials:**

- Rock phosphate (phosphate ore, crushed)
- Sulfuric acid (from previous chapter)
- Water
- Mixing vessel

**Process:**

1. **Prepare rock phosphate:** Crush into powder, dry completely
2. **Add sulfuric acid:** 
   - Pour sulfuric acid into vessel first
   - Slowly add crushed rock phosphate while stirring
   - Ratio: 1 part acid to 1 part phosphate rock (by weight)
3. **Reaction:** Heat is released (exothermic)
   - Ca₃(PO₄)₂ + 3 H₂SO₄ → 3 CaSO₄ + 2 H₃PO₄
4. **Stir continuously:** 20–30 minutes
5. **Cool:** Allow to cool and solidify
6. **Result:** Superphosphate (~15–20% soluble phosphorus)

**Application:** Mix into soil before planting

**Advantage:** Much more soluble than rock phosphate alone
**Disadvantage:** Requires sulfuric acid (energy-intensive to produce)

### Triple Superphosphate (Alternative)

If sufficient acid is available:

Use 3 parts phosphoric acid (from sulfuric acid process) per 1 part rock phosphate.

**Result:** Higher phosphorus concentration (~45–50%)

## Part 4: Potassium Fertilizers

### Wood Ash (Simplest Source)

Wood ash contains 4–13% potassium (varies by wood type):

**Potassium content varies:**
- Hardwoods (oak, hickory, maple): 8–13% potassium
- Softwoods (pine, fir): 2–4% potassium
- Hardwood ash is preferred

**Key consideration:** Wood ash also contains calcium (raises pH) and other minerals
- Over-application raises soil pH excessively
- Acidic soils benefit; alkaline soils can become too alkaline
- Test soil before large applications

**Process:**

1. Burn hardwood (oak, hickory)
2. Collect ash
3. Dry completely
4. Apply directly to soil (50–100 pounds per acre)

**Caution:** Wood ash is alkaline; excessive use raises soil pH

### Making Potassium Carbonate (Potash)

**Process:**

1. Leach wood ash with water:
   - Fill barrel with ash
   - Pour water through ash into a collection vessel below
   - Liquid contains potassium carbonate (K₂CO₃)
2. Evaporate the liquid:
   - Heat the potassium carbonate solution
   - Water evaporates
   - Leaves behind solid potassium carbonate (potash)
3. Result: Concentrated potassium fertilizer (~50% K₂CO₃)

## Part 5: Mixed Fertilizers

### Making Balanced NPK Fertilizer

Combine nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium sources:

**Example recipe (10–10–10):**

- 10% nitrogen: 10 pounds compost or animal manure
- 10% phosphorus: Superphosphate
- 10% potassium: Wood ash or potash

Mix thoroughly and apply to soil.

**Adjustment:** Vary ratios based on crop needs:
- Leafy vegetables: High nitrogen (20–10–10)
- Root vegetables: Higher phosphorus (10–20–10)
- Fruiting plants: Balanced or K-heavy (10–10–20)

## Part 6: Managing Nutrient Cycling

### Crop Rotation

Different crops use and replenish different nutrients:

- **Legumes (beans, peas, clover):** Fix nitrogen with bacteria; add nitrogen to soil
- **Grains (wheat, corn):** Deplete nitrogen; benefit from legume rotation
- **Root crops:** Don't deplete nitrogen; good rotation crops

**Rotation pattern:**
- Year 1: Legumes (nitrogen accumulation)
- Year 2: Grains (nitrogen depletion)
- Year 3: Root crops (neutral)
- Repeat

### Returning Plant Material to Soil

Compost plant residues to return nutrients:

- Nitrogen: Returned in composted material
- Phosphorus: Returned in composted material
- Potassium: Returned in composted material

**Benefit:** Partial nutrient recycling reduces external fertilizer need

### Manure Management

Animal manure is a complete fertilizer (contains N, P, K):

**Composition:**
- Fresh manure: 0.5–2% nitrogen, 0.5–1% phosphorus, 0.5–2% potassium
- Aged compost: 1–2% nitrogen, 1–1.5% phosphorus, 1–2% potassium

**Best use:** Compost for 3–6 months before applying to fields (kills pathogens, reduces smell)

## Part 6b: Optimizing Fertilizer Production

### Composting Best Practices

**Hot composting (faster decomposition):**
- Turn pile every 3–7 days
- Maintain 3:1 carbon:nitrogen ratio
- Keep moist but not waterlogged
- Temperature reaches 140–160°F
- Complete in 4–8 weeks

**Result:** Fully decomposed compost; kills weed seeds and pathogens

**Cold composting (slower but easier):**
- Build pile and leave undisturbed
- 6–12 months decomposition
- No heat—weed seeds survive
- Requires less labor

**Maximizing yield:**
- Use all organic waste (manure, food scraps, plant material)
- Layer appropriately (carbon and nitrogen alternating)
- Maintain moisture
- Store finished compost in bins to prevent nutrient loss

### Manure Quality and Storage

Fresh manure from different animals varies:

**Nitrogen content:**
- Chicken manure: 2–3% nitrogen
- Horse manure: 0.5–1% nitrogen
- Cattle manure: 0.5–1% nitrogen
- Pig manure: 0.5–1% nitrogen
- Sheep manure: 0.5–1% nitrogen

**Storage:**
- Store under cover to prevent leaching
- Pile should not exceed 6 feet height
- Turn occasionally to speed decomposition
- Ready to use after 3–6 months aging

## Part 7: Sustainable Fertilizer Strategy

For long-term post-collapse agriculture:

**Near-term (Year 1–2):**
- Use stored synthetic fertilizers if available
- Begin composting all organic waste
- Plant nitrogen-fixing cover crops (clover, alfalfa)

**Medium-term (Year 3–5):**
- Build compost stockpiles (1–3 years worth)
- Establish crop rotation with legumes
- Make superphosphate from rock phosphate and sulfuric acid
- Produce wood ash from fuel wood for potassium

**Long-term (Year 5+):**
- Rely entirely on nutrient cycling (crop rotation, composting, manure)
- Supplement with superphosphate for critical crops
- Maintain soil organic matter to retain nutrients

## Part 8: Troubleshooting Fertilizer Production

### Compost Not Decomposing

**Causes:**
- Too dry
- Too few microorganisms
- Carbon:nitrogen ratio wrong

**Solutions:**
- Add water
- Add compost starter or aged manure
- Adjust ratio to 3:1 carbon:nitrogen

### Superphosphate Clumpy or Crystallized

**Cause:** Moisture absorption

**Solution:**
- Dry thoroughly before storage
- Store in dry location
- Crush clumps before application

### Wood Ash Leaching Weak

**Cause:** Ash from softwood or burnt completely

**Solutions:**
- Use hardwood ash
- Don't let wood burn to complete ash (retain charcoal)
- Mix with more ash or use multiple leachings

## Verification

**Fertilizers are successfully produced when:**

1. Nitrogen fertilizer increases plant growth and green coloration
2. Phosphate fertilizer improves root development and flowering
3. Potassium fertilizer increases disease resistance and yield quality
4. Mixed fertilizers produce balanced growth
5. Crop yields increase compared to unfertilized plots

## Common Mistakes

1. **Using fresh manure directly:** Depletes nitrogen through ammonia loss and can burn plants. Compost first.

2. **Unbalanced NPK:** Excess nitrogen without phosphorus produces weak plants with poor fruiting. Maintain balance.

3. **Excessive lime (wood ash):** Raises soil pH too much; reduces nutrient availability. Apply cautiously.

4. **Not composting properly:** Results in weed seeds and pathogens. Heat kills these; ensure hot composting (above 140°F).

5. **Ignoring crop rotation:** Eventually depletes nitrogen and wastes fertilizer. Rotate with legumes.

6. **Over-application:** Wastes fertilizer and pollutes water. Apply according to soil testing or crop needs.

7. **Poor storage:** Fertilizer absorbs moisture and cakes up. Store in dry location.

8. **Sulfuric acid exposure:** Extremely hazardous. Use protective equipment when making superphosphate.

9. **Inadequate composting time:** Immature compost contains acids that inhibit germination. Compost at least 3–4 months.

10. **Neglecting soil testing:** Apply fertilizer based on need, not guessing. Some soils already have excess potassium.
