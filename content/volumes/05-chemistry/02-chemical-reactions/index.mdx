## Overview

A chemical reaction is a process where atoms rearrange to form new compounds. In a reaction, bonds between atoms are broken and new bonds are formed. This rearrangement either releases energy (exothermic reaction) or absorbs energy (endothermic reaction).

Understanding chemical reactions helps you:

1. **Control fire:** Know what burns, why it burns, and how to make fire burn efficiently
2. **Preserve food:** Understand fermentation, salt preservation, and acid preservation
3. **Produce useful materials:** Make soap, extract metals, produce ceramics
4. **Avoid hazards:** Know dangerous reactions and prevent them
5. **Manage energy:** Use reactions to generate heat, light, or other useful forms

This chapter teaches practical chemistry of common reactions.

## Part 1: Types of Reactions

### Combustion (Burning)

Combustion is a reaction between a fuel (usually containing carbon and hydrogen) and oxygen.

**Basic equation:**

Fuel + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy (heat, light)

**Example: Wood burning**

- Wood contains cellulose (C₆H₁₀O₅)
- Oxygen from air reacts with cellulose
- Products: Carbon dioxide (CO₂) and water (H₂O)
- Energy released: Heat and light (flames)

**Requirements for combustion:**

1. **Fuel:** A material that can be oxidized
2. **Oxygen:** From air (or another oxidant)
3. **Heat:** Activation energy to start the reaction

Remove any one and combustion stops. This is how fire extinguishers work (remove oxygen, absorb heat, or cool the fuel).

**Combustion completeness:**

- **Complete combustion:** Fuel reacts fully with oxygen, producing only CO₂ and H₂O
- **Incomplete combustion:** Insufficient oxygen, producing CO (carbon monoxide—toxic), soot (carbon), and water
- **Conditions for complete combustion:** Adequate oxygen, high temperature, good mixing

**For survival:**

- Wood, charcoal, oil, and fat are all good fuels
- Dry fuel burns better than wet fuel (water reduces temperature)
- Wood burns best when dry and split into small pieces (greater surface area for oxygen access)
- Inadequate oxygen (poor ventilation) produces toxic carbon monoxide; ensure fires have good airflow

### Acid-Base Reactions

An acid is a substance that provides H⁺ ions (hydrogen ions) when dissolved in water. A base is a substance that provides OH⁻ ions (hydroxide ions) when dissolved in water.

**When acid and base mix:**

H⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O (water)

The acid and base neutralize each other, producing salt and water.

**Example: Vinegar and baking soda**

- Vinegar is acetic acid: CH₃COOH
- Baking soda is sodium bicarbonate: NaHCO₃
- Reaction: CH₃COOH + NaHCO₃ → CH₃COONa (sodium acetate) + H₂O (water) + CO₂ (carbon dioxide gas)
- Visible result: Fizzing and bubbling (CO₂ gas being released)

**Common acids and bases:**

**Acids:**
- Vinegar (acetic acid, 4–6% in solution)
- Lemon juice (citric acid)
- Sour milk (lactic acid from fermentation)
- Stomach acid (hydrochloric acid)

**Bases:**
- Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) - mild base
- Lye (sodium hydroxide) - strong base
- Lime (calcium hydroxide) - strong base
- Soda ash (sodium carbonate) - mild base

**For survival:**

- Acid preserves food (vinegar, fermentation)
- Base can be used to make soap (saponification—reacting fat with base)
- Mixing acid and base generates gas (useful for certain reactions)

### Oxidation Reactions

Oxidation is the loss of electrons (or gain of oxygen). Reduction is the gain of electrons (or loss of oxygen).

**Example: Rusting (iron oxidation)**

- Iron (Fe) loses electrons to oxygen
- Product: Iron oxide (Fe₂O₃ or Fe₃O₄), commonly called rust
- Conditions: Iron in contact with oxygen and water
- Prevention: Keep iron dry; coat with oil or grease

**Example: Browning (food oxidation)**

- Sugars and proteins are oxidized by heat
- Products: Brown compounds with unique flavors
- This is why bread crust is brown; meat crust is brown
- Slower oxidation (exposure to air) causes rancidity in fat

**For survival:**

- Prevent iron oxidation (rusting) by keeping tools dry
- Use controlled oxidation in cooking for flavor and texture
- Understand oxidation in fermentation (bacteria are oxidizing and reducing compounds)

### Neutralization and Precipitation

When some acids and bases mix, they form insoluble compounds (precipitate).

**Example: Lime and soil acidification**

- Acidic soil has excess H⁺ ions
- Lime (calcium hydroxide) provides OH⁻ ions
- Reaction: Acid + Base → Salt + Water
- Result: Soil pH increases (becomes less acidic)

### Decomposition Reactions

A decomposition reaction breaks down a compound into simpler compounds or elements.

**Example: Thermal decomposition**

- Heat breaks bonds in compounds
- Example: Heating calcium carbonate (limestone, chalk) produces calcium oxide (quicklime) and carbon dioxide
- Reaction: CaCO₃ + heat → CaO + CO₂
- Application: Making quicklime for construction or sanitation

**Example: Biological decomposition**

- Microorganisms break down organic matter
- Example: Composting, fermentation, decay
- Complex molecules → simpler molecules + energy (used by microorganisms)

## Part 2: Reaction Rates and Equilibrium

### Factors Affecting Reaction Rate

The speed of a reaction depends on:

1. **Temperature:** Higher temperature increases reaction rate (molecules move faster, collide more often)
2. **Surface area:** Smaller particles (more surface area) react faster than large particles
3. **Concentration:** More concentrated solutions react faster
4. **Catalysts:** Substances that speed up reactions without being consumed
5. **Mixing:** Better mixing increases reaction rate

**For survival:**

- Heat speeds up reactions (cooking, fermentation, decomposition)
- Breaking fuel into small pieces increases burn rate
- Grinding salt and acid makes acid-base reactions faster (useful for making gas)
- Mixing yeast with warm sugar solution speeds fermentation

### Chemical Equilibrium

Some reactions are reversible. They reach an equilibrium where reactants and products are present in a stable ratio.

**Example: Water evaporation and condensation**

- Water evaporates (liquid → gas)
- Water condenses (gas → liquid)
- At equilibrium, evaporation and condensation happen at equal rates
- The ratio of liquid water to water vapor is constant

**Example: Salt dissolution**

- Salt dissolves in water (NaCl → Na⁺ + Cl⁻)
- If enough salt is present, it precipitates out (Na⁺ + Cl⁻ → NaCl)
- At equilibrium, the amount dissolving equals the amount precipitating

**For survival:**

- In salt preservation, equilibrium between salt crystal and dissolved salt maintains a constant salt concentration
- In fermentation, equilibrium between acids produced and acids consumed determines final taste
- Understanding equilibrium helps predict when reactions will stop (reaching stability)

## Part 3: Practical Chemical Reactions for Survival

### Reaction 1: Making Quicklime (Calcium Oxide)

**Purpose:** Quicklime is used for sanitation, construction, and water treatment.

**Materials:**
- Limestone, chalk, or seashells (calcium carbonate)
- Heat source (fire)

**Procedure:**

1. Heat limestone to high temperature (800°C or hotter)
2. Reaction: CaCO₃ + heat → CaO (quicklime) + CO₂
3. Result: White powder (calcium oxide) is produced; carbon dioxide gas escapes

**Properties of quicklime:**
- Reacts with water (exothermic—produces heat)
- Creates slaked lime (calcium hydroxide) when mixed with water
- Kills microorganisms (used for sanitation)
- Hardens when exposed to air (used in mortar and concrete)

**Survival uses:**
- Sanitation (disinfection)
- Construction (mortar, concrete)
- Raising pH of acidic soil

### Reaction 2: Saponification (Making Soap)

**Purpose:** Soap is essential for hygiene and cleaning.

**Materials:**
- Animal fat or vegetable oil
- Lye (sodium hydroxide) or soda ash (sodium carbonate) converted to lye
- Water

**Procedure:**

1. Heat fat and lye solution together
2. Reaction: Fat + Lye → Soap + Glycerin
3. The reaction requires heat and time (hours to days)
4. As reaction progresses, soap separates from water
5. Cool and harvest soap

**Chemistry:**
- Fat is a triglyceride (three fatty acids bonded to glycerin)
- Lye breaks the bonds, releasing fatty acids
- Fatty acids form soap (amphipathic—one end loves water, one end loves oil)
- Glycerin is a byproduct (useful for skin care)

**Survival uses:**
- Hygiene
- Cleaning clothes and tools
- Disinfection

### Reaction 3: Fermentation (Lactic Acid Fermentation)

**Purpose:** Preserve vegetables, dairy, and grains; improve digestibility and flavor.

**Materials:**
- Vegetables (cabbage, beans, cucumbers)
- Salt
- Water
- Anaerobic environment (lid on jar)

**Procedure:**

1. Mix vegetables with 2–5% salt by weight
2. Pack tightly in a jar with a lid
3. Bacteria naturally present on vegetables begin to ferment
4. Bacteria consume sugars and produce lactic acid
5. Over days to weeks, lactic acid accumulates
6. Low pH (acidic environment) prevents pathogenic bacteria from growing

**Chemistry:**
- Lactobacillus bacteria consume glucose (sugar): C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2 C₃H₆O₃ (lactic acid)
- Lactic acid lowers pH
- High salt concentration inhibits pathogenic bacteria but allows beneficial bacteria to thrive

**Survival uses:**
- Preserve vegetables indefinitely
- Improve nutrient availability
- Create probiotics (beneficial bacteria for digestion)

### Reaction 4: Combustion Efficiency

**Purpose:** Get more heat and light from the same amount of fuel.

**Factors for efficient combustion:**

1. **Dry fuel:** Water content reduces temperature and wastes energy vaporizing water
2. **Small pieces:** Maximize surface area for oxygen exposure
3. **Good airflow:** Ensure adequate oxygen
4. **Hot coals:** Pre-heat the combustion zone so burning fuel reaches ignition temperature quickly

**For survival:**

- Split wood thin and dry it before burning
- Create airflow under fire (fire grate allows air underneath)
- Arrange burning wood so new wood comes in contact with hot coals
- Use slow-burning, dense fuels (hardwood, charcoal) when you need sustained heat

## Part 4: Understanding Reaction Direction

### Exothermic vs. Endothermic

**Exothermic reactions:** Release energy (usually heat)
- Combustion
- Neutralization (acid + base)
- Many decomposition reactions
- Result: Temperature increases

**Endothermic reactions:** Absorb energy
- Evaporation
- Melting (if heat source is present)
- Some decomposition reactions
- Result: Temperature decreases

**For survival:**

- Use exothermic reactions to generate heat (fire, quicklime in water)
- Avoid endothermic reactions consuming resources (evaporation wastes water)

### Spontaneous vs. Non-spontaneous Reactions

**Spontaneous reactions:** Happen naturally once started
- Combustion (requires only ignition)
- Rusting (requires only oxygen and water)
- Decay (microorganisms do the work)

**Non-spontaneous reactions:** Require constant energy input
- Photosynthesis (requires light energy)
- Electrolysis (requires electrical energy)
- Heating some materials to break bonds

**For survival:**

- Use spontaneous reactions (combustion, fermentation) for productivity
- Minimize energy cost by avoiding non-spontaneous reactions or automating them (water wheel, wind mill)

## Verification

**Understanding chemical reactions means:**

1. You can explain why fire requires oxygen, fuel, and heat
2. You can predict products of common reactions (combustion, acid-base)
3. You understand how to speed up or slow down reactions
4. You can control reactions for useful purposes (cooking, fermentation, soap-making)
5. You understand why certain reactions are exothermic or endothermic
6. You can recognize when a reaction reaches equilibrium

If you can reason through these examples using reaction principles, you understand the concept.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Assuming all combustion produces only heat:** Incomplete combustion produces toxic carbon monoxide. Ensure good ventilation.

2. **Thinking acid-base reactions are always safe:** Strong bases and acids are corrosive. Handle carefully.

3. **Overestimating reaction speed:** Some reactions are slow (months or years). Fermentation, decay, and rusting take time.

4. **Forgetting that reactions need the right conditions:** A reaction might be theoretically possible but require conditions you cannot provide (very high temperature, specific pH).

5. **Confusing reversible and irreversible reactions:** Some reactions are essentially one-way (combustion). Others can go either direction (dissolution). Understand which is which.

6. **Underestimating energy in exothermic reactions:** Quicklime reacting with water generates enough heat to boil the water and burn skin. Respect the energy.

7. **Assuming equilibrium is permanent:** Equilibrium can shift if conditions change (temperature, concentration). A reaction at equilibrium can be driven further if conditions change.

8. **Not considering side reactions:** Real reactions often produce unexpected byproducts. Pure products require careful control of conditions.
