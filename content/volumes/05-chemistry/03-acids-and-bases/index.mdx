## Overview

An acid is a substance that donates H⁺ ions (hydrogen ions) when dissolved in water. A base is a substance that accepts H⁺ ions (or donates OH⁻ ions) when dissolved in water. Acids and bases are the opposite of each other—when they mix, they neutralize.

Understanding acids and bases helps you:

1. **Preserve food:** Acids inhibit bacterial growth
2. **Make useful materials:** Bases are essential for soap; acids are used in many processes
3. **Manage soil:** pH determines nutrient availability
4. **Handle materials safely:** Some acids and bases are corrosive or toxic
5. **Understand digestion and fermentation:** Both rely on pH control

This chapter teaches practical acid-base chemistry for post-collapse survival.

## Part 1: Understanding Acids and Bases

### The pH Scale

pH measures the concentration of H⁺ ions (hydrogen ions) in a solution.

**pH scale:**

- pH 0–6: Acidic (H⁺ ions in excess)
- pH 7: Neutral (H⁺ and OH⁻ ions in equal concentration)
- pH 8–14: Basic/Alkaline (OH⁻ ions in excess)

**Scale properties:**

- Each unit represents a 10-fold difference in H⁺ concentration
- pH 5 is 10 times more acidic than pH 6
- pH 3 is 1000 times more acidic than pH 6

**Practical pH ranges:**

- Stomach acid: pH 1–2 (very acidic)
- Lemon juice: pH 2–3 (acidic)
- Vinegar: pH 2–4 (acidic)
- Sour milk: pH 4–5 (acidic)
- Pure water: pH 7 (neutral)
- Baking soda solution: pH 8–9 (basic)
- Lye solution: pH 13–14 (very basic)

### Testing pH

**Visual indicators:**

1. **Red cabbage juice:**
   - Chop red cabbage finely
   - Boil in water for 5–10 minutes until water turns purple
   - Filter out cabbage pieces
   - Use the purple liquid as an indicator
   - In acidic solution: turns pink or red
   - In neutral solution: remains purple
   - In basic solution: turns blue-green or yellow

2. **Turmeric powder:**
   - Add a pinch to the solution
   - In acidic or neutral: no color change (yellow remains)
   - In basic solution: turns red-brown

3. **Lemon juice (indicator of acidity):**
   - If a substance has a strong sour taste, it is very acidic
   - Never taste to test; use visual indicators instead

### Properties of Acids

**Chemical properties:**

1. **Sour taste:** Acids taste sour (but never taste to test)
2. **Conduct electricity:** Ionized acids conduct electricity
3. **Reactive with metals:** Some acids react with metals, producing hydrogen gas
4. **Corrosive:** Strong acids corrode skin and materials
5. **React with bases:** Acids neutralize bases
6. **Turn pH indicator colors:** Acidic solutions change indicator colors

**Physical properties:**

1. **Liquid at room temperature:** Most common acids are liquids
2. **Volatile:** Some acids have strong odors (acetic acid in vinegar)
3. **Density varies:** Different acids have different densities

### Properties of Bases

**Chemical properties:**

1. **Bitter taste:** Bases taste bitter (but never taste to test)
2. **Slippery feel:** Bases feel slippery on skin (due to saponification of skin oils)
3. **Conduct electricity:** Ionized bases conduct electricity
4. **Corrosive:** Strong bases corrode skin and materials
5. **React with acids:** Bases neutralize acids
6. **Turn pH indicator colors:** Basic solutions change indicator colors differently than acids

**Physical properties:**

1. **Solid or liquid:** Bases can be solid (lime) or liquid (lye solution)
2. **Odor varies:** Some bases have distinctive odors (ammonia)
3. **Solubility varies:** Some bases dissolve in water; others do not

## Part 2: Common Acids and Bases in Post-Collapse Context

### Acids

**Acetic acid (CH₃COOH) — Vinegar:**
- Concentration: 4–6% acetic acid in water
- pH: 2–3
- Source: Fermentation of sugars by acetobacter bacteria
- Properties: Pungent odor, sour taste
- Uses: Food preservation, cleaning, disinfection, flavor
- Safety: Corrosive to skin in concentrated form; irritates eyes and respiratory tract

**Citric acid (C₆H₈O₇) — Lemon juice:**
- Concentration: 4–6% in fresh lemon juice
- pH: 2–3
- Source: Natural compound in citrus fruits
- Properties: Sour taste, preservative
- Uses: Food preservation, flavor, cleaning
- Safety: Mild corrosive; safe in food concentrations

**Lactic acid (C₃H₆O₃) — Fermented foods:**
- Concentration: Variable (depends on fermentation)
- pH: 3–4 in fermented vegetables
- Source: Lactobacillus bacteria fermentation
- Properties: Sour taste, preservative, probiotic
- Uses: Food preservation, fermentation, health benefit
- Safety: Non-toxic; beneficial

**Hydrochloric acid (HCl) — Stomach acid:**
- Concentration: 0.5% in stomach (dilute)
- pH: 1–2
- Source: Produced by stomach lining
- Properties: Dissolves proteins, kills bacteria
- Uses: Digestion (internal only)
- Safety: Very corrosive; avoid contact

### Bases

**Sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO₃) — Baking soda:**
- Concentration: Pure solid
- pH: 8–9 when dissolved
- Source: Mineral deposits or chemical synthesis
- Properties: Mild base, safe to taste
- Uses: Food preservation, neutralizing acids, cleaning, health uses
- Safety: Non-toxic; safe for internal use in moderation

**Sodium carbonate (Na₂CO₃) — Soda ash:**
- Concentration: Pure solid
- pH: 11–12 when dissolved
- Source: Mined or extracted from seaweed ash
- Properties: Strong base, soluble in water
- Uses: Making lye, water softening, soap-making
- Safety: Corrosive; avoid skin contact

**Sodium hydroxide (NaOH) — Lye:**
- Concentration: Variable (depends on preparation)
- pH: 13–14 when dissolved
- Source: Made by electrolyzing salt water or by reacting soda ash with lime
- Properties: Very strong base, highly corrosive, soluble
- Uses: Soap-making, water treatment, disinfection
- Safety: Extremely corrosive; causes severe burns; eye protection essential

**Calcium hydroxide (Ca(OH)₂) — Slaked lime:**
- Concentration: Solid or suspended in water (lime water)
- pH: 12–13 when dissolved
- Source: Made by adding water to quicklime (CaO)
- Properties: Strong base, slightly soluble
- Uses: Disinfection, raising pH, making mortar
- Safety: Corrosive; eye protection recommended

### Weak Acids and Bases

Weak acids and bases partially ionize in water (not all molecules donate or accept H⁺):

- Acetic acid is a weak acid (about 1% ionized)
- Baking soda is a weak base (partially ionized)
- Weak acids and bases are generally safer than strong acids and bases

## Part 3: Acid-Base Reactions

### Neutralization

When acid and base mix, they neutralize:

H⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O

**Example: Vinegar and baking soda**

- Vinegar: CH₃COOH (acetic acid)
- Baking soda: NaHCO₃ (sodium bicarbonate, weak base)
- Reaction: CH₃COOH + NaHCO₃ → CH₃COONa + H₂O + CO₂
- Products: Sodium acetate (salt), water, carbon dioxide (gas—produces fizzing)

**Example: Lye and fat (saponification)**

- Lye: NaOH (sodium hydroxide, strong base)
- Fat: Triglyceride (three fatty acids bonded to glycerin)
- Reaction: Fat + NaOH → Soap (fatty acid salts) + Glycerin
- Products: Soap (useful), glycerin (byproduct)

### Buffer Solutions

A buffer is a solution that resists pH change when acid or base is added.

**Example: Fermented vegetable brine**

- Salt water with fermented vegetables
- Contains lactic acid (weak acid) and lactate (conjugate base)
- When small amounts of acid are added: Lactate reacts with the added acid, maintaining pH
- When small amounts of base are added: Lactic acid reacts with the added base, maintaining pH
- Result: pH remains stable despite minor additions

**For survival:**

Fermented foods naturally buffer their own pH, making them stable and resistant to spoilage even if slightly contaminated.

## Part 4: Practical Applications of Acids and Bases

### Application 1: Food Preservation with Acids

**Mechanism:**

Acidic environment (pH below 4.5) inhibits growth of pathogenic bacteria. Many safe bacteria (lactobacillus) thrive in acidic conditions.

**Methods:**

1. **Vinegar preservation:**
   - Add vinegar to vegetables or fruits (ratio: 1 part vinegar to 3 parts vegetables)
   - Submerge in vinegar solution
   - Sealed in jars, preserved for months

2. **Fermentation (self-acidifying):**
   - Add salt to vegetables (2–5% by weight)
   - Pack in anaerobic jar (no air contact)
   - Lactobacillus bacteria ferment and produce lactic acid
   - Acidic environment prevents spoilage
   - Preserved for months or longer

3. **Lemon or citrus juice:**
   - Add fresh lemon juice to fish or vegetables
   - Acid denatures proteins in fish (ceviche)
   - Prevents bacterial growth

### Application 2: Soap-Making (Base Reaction)

**Procedure:**

1. **Prepare lye:**
   - Make lye from wood ash or lime
   - Concentration: About 30% NaOH in water

2. **Mix with fat:**
   - Heat fat to about 100°C
   - Add lye solution slowly (exothermic—produces heat)
   - Stir continuously

3. **Saponification:**
   - Reaction continues for hours or days
   - Soap forms as product; glycerin is byproduct
   - As soap forms, it separates from water

4. **Harvest:**
   - Allow to cool
   - Soap floats on top; cut into bars
   - Cure for weeks before use (becomes harder)

**Chemistry:**

- Triglyceride (fat) + NaOH → Soap (sodium fatty acid salts) + Glycerin
- Soap is amphipathic: one end loves water, one end loves oil
- This allows soap to dissolve both oil and water, cleaning effectively

### Application 3: Soil pH Management

**Acidic soil (common in wet climates):**

- Problem: Nutrients are locked in forms plants cannot absorb
- Solution: Add lime (calcium hydroxide) to raise pH
- Effect: Lime reacts with acid in soil, producing water and neutral salt
- Result: pH increases, nutrients become available

**Basic soil (less common, but possible in arid climates):**

- Problem: Nutrients are locked; some are unavailable
- Solution: Add acidic material (sulfur, compost with acidifying microbes)
- Effect: Acids lower pH
- Result: pH decreases, nutrients become available

**For survival:**

Understanding soil pH helps you optimize nutrient availability without purchasing commercial fertilizers. Lime is easily made from limestone or seashells.

### Application 4: Disinfection and Water Treatment

**Lye (sodium hydroxide):**
- pH 13–14 kills microorganisms
- Used to disinfect surfaces, toilets, or water-contaminated areas
- Also used to remove hair (for leather processing) or wood (for pulping)

**Lime (calcium hydroxide):**
- pH 12–13 kills microorganisms
- Milder than lye; safer to handle
- Used for disinfection and water treatment

**Acid (vinegar, sour):**
- pH 2–4 inhibits bacterial growth
- Does not kill spores
- Useful for food preservation and surface cleaning

## Part 5: Safety with Acids and Bases

### Hazards

**Strong acids and bases:**
- Corrosive to skin and eyes (cause severe burns)
- Corrosive to materials (dissolve metals, destroy containers)
- Toxic if ingested (internal damage)
- Volatile (produce harmful fumes)

**Specific hazards:**

1. **Lye (NaOH):**
   - Extremely corrosive
   - Saponifies skin oils (feels slippery; actually destroying skin)
   - Blindness if it contacts eyes
   - Deadly if ingested

2. **Lime (Ca(OH)₂):**
   - Corrosive but less severe than lye
   - Irritates respiratory tract if dust is inhaled
   - Harmful if ingested

3. **Vinegar and citric acid:**
   - Mild acids; safe in food concentrations
   - Concentrated vinegar can irritate skin and eyes
   - Generally low hazard

### Safety Procedures

1. **Protective equipment:**
   - Eye protection (goggles or face shield for strong acids/bases)
   - Gloves (rubber or vinyl; different materials resist different chemicals)
   - Clothing that covers skin

2. **Ventilation:**
   - Work in well-ventilated areas
   - Strong acids and bases produce fumes that irritate respiratory tract

3. **Container material:**
   - Use glass or ceramic containers for acids and bases
   - Metal containers may corrode (except stainless steel, which resists some acids)
   - Plastics may dissolve depending on the acid or base

4. **Dilution:**
   - Always add acid or base to water, not water to acid or base
   - Adding water to concentrated acid produces violent reaction and heat
   - Adding base or acid to water is less hazardous

5. **Cleanup:**
   - Wash skin immediately with water if acid or base is spilled
   - For lye contact with skin: Brush off dry lye, then wash with water
   - Do not use acid to neutralize base burns or vice versa (produces heat, worsens burn)

## Common Mistakes

1. **Assuming pH is linear:** pH is logarithmic. pH 5 is 100 times more acidic than pH 7, not just 2 units different.

2. **Adding water to strong acid:** This produces violent reaction and heat, potentially causing spattering. Always add acid to water.

3. **Using metal containers for acids:** Acids corrode metals (except a few noble metals). Glass or ceramic only.

4. **Overestimating lye safety:** Lye is extremely corrosive. Concentrated lye can cause severe burns in seconds. Respect it.

5. **Forgetting that acid-base reactions produce heat:** Mixing lye with water or with fat is exothermic. Control the temperature carefully.

6. **Assuming fermented foods are always safe:** Poor fermentation technique (insufficient salt, contamination) can allow pathogenic bacteria to grow. Follow recipes carefully.

7. **Ignoring buffering capacity:** Even acidic foods can be spoiled if acid is neutralized by contaminants or microorganisms. Maintain appropriate acid concentration.

8. **Using lye from wood ash without testing:** Wood ash lye concentration varies. Test pH before using for soap to ensure it is strong enough.
