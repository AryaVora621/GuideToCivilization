## Overview

Everything around you—water, wood, metal, soil, food—is made of atoms. Atoms are the smallest unit of matter that retains the properties of an element. Understanding atoms helps you:

1. **Understand material properties:** Why some materials are hard, others soft; why some conduct electricity, others do not
2. **Work with materials:** Know which materials are suitable for different tasks (metal for tools, clay for containers, wood for structure)
3. **Understand chemical reactions:** Why materials react with each other; how to control reactions (burning, fermenting, rusting)
4. **Make useful materials:** Understand how to extract metals, make soap, produce ceramics

This chapter teaches atomic theory and how atoms make up the materials of daily life.

## Part 1: Atomic Structure

### What Is an Atom?

An atom is the smallest unit of an element. It cannot be divided further while retaining the properties of that element.

Every atom contains:

1. **Nucleus:** The dense central core containing protons and neutrons
2. **Electrons:** Negatively charged particles orbiting the nucleus in electron shells

### The Nucleus

The nucleus is incredibly dense and small—about 1/10,000th the size of the atom, but containing nearly all the atom's mass.

**Protons:**
- Positively charged
- Number of protons defines the element (1 proton = hydrogen, 6 protons = carbon, 26 protons = iron)
- Protons are stable (do not decay naturally)

**Neutrons:**
- Neutral (no charge)
- Add mass to the atom
- Number of neutrons varies (isotopes)
- Some neutrons are unstable and decay (radioactivity)

**Nucleus composition:**

- Hydrogen nucleus: 1 proton
- Carbon nucleus: 6 protons, 6 neutrons (common form)
- Iron nucleus: 26 protons, 30 neutrons
- Uranium nucleus: 92 protons, 146 neutrons

### Electrons

Electrons are much smaller and lighter than protons or neutrons. They orbit the nucleus in shells (energy levels).

**Electron shells:**

- Each shell can hold a maximum number of electrons
- Inner shells fill before outer shells
- Electrons in outer shells are involved in chemical reactions

**Shell capacity:**

- 1st shell: 2 electrons
- 2nd shell: 8 electrons
- 3rd shell: 8 electrons (simplified; actually 18, but usually only 8 participate in bonding)

**Example: Carbon atom**

- 6 protons (in nucleus)
- 6 neutrons (in nucleus)
- 6 electrons (in shells): 2 in first shell, 4 in second shell
- The 4 electrons in the outer shell determine carbon's chemical behavior

### Atoms and Elements

An element is defined by the number of protons (atomic number). All atoms of the same element have the same number of protons.

**Common elements relevant to survival:**

- **Hydrogen (H):** 1 proton. Lightest element. Key component of water and organic molecules.
- **Carbon (C):** 6 protons. Forms complex molecules. Key to all life.
- **Nitrogen (N):** 7 protons. Essential for proteins and DNA.
- **Oxygen (O):** 8 protons. Essential for respiration and combustion.
- **Phosphorus (P):** 15 protons. Essential for bones and ATP (energy molecule).
- **Sulfur (S):** 16 protons. Component of proteins and many minerals.
- **Sodium (Na):** 11 protons. Essential for nerve function, osmotic balance.
- **Potassium (K):** 19 protons. Essential for nerve function, muscle contraction.
- **Calcium (Ca):** 20 protons. Essential for bones, teeth, muscle contraction.
- **Iron (Fe):** 26 protons. Essential for blood and electron transport.
- **Copper (Cu):** 29 protons. Conductor of electricity and heat; essential trace element.
- **Zinc (Zn):** 30 protons. Essential for immunity and enzyme function.

### Atomic Mass and Isotopes

The mass of an atom is approximately the number of protons plus neutrons (electrons are negligible).

**Mass number:** Protons + neutrons

**Examples:**

- Carbon-12: 6 protons + 6 neutrons = mass 12
- Carbon-13: 6 protons + 7 neutrons = mass 13
- Uranium-238: 92 protons + 146 neutrons = mass 238

**Isotopes** are atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons (different mass numbers).

- Carbon-12: stable (non-radioactive)
- Carbon-14: radioactive (decays over time; used for dating)

Most elements have multiple isotopes. Some are stable; some are radioactive.

## Part 2: How Atoms Bond to Form Molecules

### Electron Shells and Bonding

Atoms bond together to form molecules. The reason for bonding is to achieve a stable electron configuration (usually a full outer shell).

**Rules for stability:**

- Full shells are stable
- Atoms want to fill their outer shells
- Atoms can do this by sharing electrons (covalent bond) or transferring electrons (ionic bond)

### Covalent Bonds

A covalent bond is formed when two atoms share electrons.

**Example: Water (H₂O)**

- Oxygen atom: 8 electrons (2 in first shell, 6 in second shell). Outer shell needs 2 more electrons.
- Hydrogen atom: 1 electron (in first shell). Needs 1 more electron.
- Two hydrogen atoms bond to one oxygen atom by sharing electrons
- Each hydrogen shares 1 electron with oxygen
- Oxygen shares 1 electron with each hydrogen
- Result: Stable molecule where all atoms have full outer shells

**Example: Methane (CH₄)**

- Carbon atom: 6 electrons. Outer shell needs 4 more electrons.
- Four hydrogen atoms each have 1 electron
- Carbon shares electrons with each hydrogen
- Result: Stable molecule

**Covalent bonds are strong.** Breaking them requires significant energy.

### Ionic Bonds

An ionic bond is formed when one atom transfers electrons to another.

**Example: Salt (NaCl)**

- Sodium atom: 11 electrons (2, 8, 1 in shells). Outer shell has 1 electron; would rather lose it.
- Chlorine atom: 17 electrons (2, 8, 7 in shells). Outer shell needs 1 electron.
- Sodium transfers 1 electron to chlorine
- Sodium becomes positively charged (Na⁺); chlorine becomes negatively charged (Cl⁻)
- The opposite charges attract, forming an ionic bond
- Result: Stable salt crystal

**Ionic bonds are strong in crystals but break easily in water.**

### Hydrogen Bonds

A hydrogen bond is a weak bond between hydrogen and highly electronegative atoms (oxygen, nitrogen, fluorine).

**Example: Water (H₂O)**

- Oxygen is very electronegative and pulls electrons from hydrogen
- The hydrogen of one water molecule is slightly positive; the oxygen of another water molecule is slightly negative
- They attract weakly (hydrogen bond)
- Result: Water is a liquid at room temperature (hydrogen bonds hold molecules together but are weak enough to allow free movement)

**Hydrogen bonds are weak individually but numerous and important.**

## Part 3: Compounds and Chemical Properties

### Compounds vs. Elements

**Elements:** Pure substances made of one type of atom (oxygen gas, iron metal, copper)

**Compounds:** Substances made of two or more elements bonded together (water, salt, glucose)

### Properties of Compounds

Compounds have properties different from their elements:

**Example: Water**

- Hydrogen (element): Flammable gas
- Oxygen (element): Supporter of combustion
- Water (compound): Extinguishes fire, essential for life

The properties of a compound are determined by:
1. What elements it contains
2. How those elements are arranged

### Organic vs. Inorganic Compounds

**Organic compounds:** Contain carbon and usually hydrogen
- Proteins, carbohydrates, fats, nucleic acids
- Most biological compounds

**Inorganic compounds:** Do not contain carbon (or contain only carbon in simple forms)
- Salts, minerals, metals, water, acids, bases
- Most geological compounds

### Common Useful Compounds

**Water (H₂O):**
- Universal solvent
- Essential for life
- Can be broken down into hydrogen (fuel) and oxygen (oxidant)

**Salt (NaCl):**
- Essential for nervous system function
- Preserves food
- Available from seawater or mineral deposits

**Acids (contain H⁺ ions when dissolved in water):**
- Vinegar (acetic acid)
- Lemon juice (citric acid)
- Stomach acid (hydrochloric acid)
- Can dissolve certain materials; preserve food

**Bases (contain OH⁻ ions when dissolved in water):**
- Lye (sodium hydroxide)
- Lime (calcium hydroxide)
- Soda ash (sodium carbonate)
- Can saponify fats (make soap)

## Part 4: Measuring and Predicting Chemical Behavior

### Atomic Number and Mass Number

**Atomic number:** Number of protons (defines the element)

**Mass number:** Protons + neutrons

**Example:** Iron-56

- Atomic number: 26 (26 protons)
- Mass number: 56
- Neutrons: 56 - 26 = 30 neutrons

### Electron Configuration and Reactivity

The number of electrons in the outer shell determines how reactive an atom is:

**Full outer shells:** Stable, non-reactive (noble gases like helium, neon, argon)

**Partially filled outer shells:** Reactive (want to fill or empty the shell)

**1 electron in outer shell:** Very reactive (alkali metals like sodium, potassium)

**7 electrons in outer shell:** Very reactive (halogens like chlorine, fluorine)

### Predicting Reactions

Knowing electron configuration helps predict reactions:

- Sodium (1 electron in outer shell) reacts with chlorine (7 electrons in outer shell) to form salt (NaCl)—sodium gives 1 electron to chlorine
- Oxygen (6 electrons in outer shell) reacts with hydrogen (1 electron in outer shell) to form water (H₂O)—oxygen takes electrons from hydrogen

## Part 5: Practical Atomic Chemistry for Survival

### Application 1: Using Materials Based on Atomic Structure

Different elements and compounds have different properties because of their atomic structure.

**Metals:**
- Electrons in outer shells are loosely bound
- Electrons can move freely
- Result: Conduct electricity and heat; malleable (can be hammered into shapes); ductile (can be drawn into wire)
- Use for: Tools, weapons, conducting heat, electrical circuits

**Ceramics (oxides):**
- Electrons are tightly bound in ionic bonds
- Electrons cannot move
- Result: Poor conductors; hard and brittle
- Use for: Containers, insulation, pottery

**Wood:**
- Organic compounds with carbon-carbon bonds
- Some flexibility in bonds
- Result: Relatively strong, lightweight, can be worked and shaped
- Use for: Structure, tools, fuel

### Application 2: Understanding Chemical Reactions

Reactions are exchanges of electrons between atoms.

**Combustion (burning):**
- Fuel (usually contains carbon and hydrogen) reacts with oxygen
- Electrons are transferred from fuel to oxygen
- Energy is released (fire, heat, light)
- Example: Wood (cellulose, C₆H₁₀O₅) + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy

**Rusting (oxidation):**
- Iron reacts with oxygen
- Electrons are transferred from iron to oxygen
- Iron oxide (rust) forms
- Prevention: Keep iron dry or coat with oil

**Acid-base reactions:**
- Acids provide H⁺ ions; bases provide OH⁻ ions
- They combine to form salt and water
- Example: Vinegar (acetic acid) + Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) → Sodium acetate (salt) + Water + Carbon dioxide

### Application 3: Extracting and Refining Materials

Understanding atomic chemistry helps extract metals and other materials.

**Metal extraction:**
- Metal ores contain metals bonded to oxygen or other elements
- Heat or chemical treatment breaks bonds and releases metal
- Example: Iron oxide + Carbon → Iron + Carbon dioxide (smelting)

**Salt production:**
- Seawater contains dissolved salts
- Evaporation leaves salt crystals behind
- Example: Seawater → Evaporation → Salt crystals

**Water purification:**
- Impurities are bonded in various ways
- Different methods break different bonds
- Boiling breaks hydrogen bonds (kills microorganisms)
- Filtering traps particles
- Settling allows heavy particles to fall

## Common Mistakes

1. **Thinking atoms are divisible:** Atoms are the fundamental units of elements. In a chemical reaction, atoms rearrange but are not broken down into smaller pieces (nuclear reactions are different and rare).

2. **Confusing elements with compounds:** Elements are pure substances (oxygen, iron, carbon). Compounds are combinations (water, salt, rust).

3. **Underestimating atomic scale:** Atoms are incredibly small (about 0.0000000001 meters). One grain of salt contains billions of atoms.

4. **Assuming all atoms of an element are identical:** Isotopes of an element have different numbers of neutrons, so different masses, but same number of protons and same chemical properties.

5. **Thinking covalent bonds are weak:** Covalent bonds are very strong. Breaking them requires significant energy.

6. **Confusing ionic and covalent bonds:** Ionic bonds form when electrons are transferred; covalent bonds form when electrons are shared. They behave very differently.

7. **Assuming atomic structure is random:** Electron shells fill in order. The behavior of atoms is predictable based on electron configuration.

8. **Forgetting that reactions conserve atoms:** In a chemical reaction, atoms are rearranged but not created or destroyed. The same atoms are present before and after.
