## Overview

Soap is one of the most essential post-collapse products. It prevents disease by removing oils and dirt from skin and clothes. Without modern commercial soap, you must make your own. Soap is produced by saponification—reacting fat with a base (lye or potassium carbonate).

This chapter teaches the chemistry of soap making and practical production methods. Understanding soap helps you:

1. **Make soap from available materials:** Animal fat or vegetable oil + lye (from wood ash or limestone)
2. **Maintain hygiene:** Essential for preventing disease
3. **Clean effectively:** Understand how soap works at the molecular level
4. **Optimize production:** Adjust recipes for available materials and conditions
5. **Scale production:** Move from single batches to community-scale production

This is practical, essential chemistry for survival.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of chemical reactions
- Understanding of acids and bases
- Understanding of fats and oils
- Safety awareness (lye is caustic)
- Practical experience heating and measuring

## Materials & Tools

**For soap making:**
- Animal fat (lard, tallow from rendered meat) or vegetable oil
- Lye (sodium hydroxide from wood ash, or commercial lye)
- Water
- Large pot (stainless steel or enamel, not aluminum or tin)
- Wooden spoon for stirring
- Thermometer (if available)
- Scale for weighing (if available)

- Containers for curing (wooden molds or lined boxes)

**For testing lye strength:**
- Fresh egg
- Measuring spoon

## Part 1: Understanding Soap Chemistry

### The Saponification Reaction

Saponification is the reaction between fat and base:

**Chemical equation:**

Fat (triglyceride) + Base (NaOH or KOH) → Soap (fatty acid salts) + Glycerin

**Example: Tallow and lye**

C₃H₅(C₁₇H₃₅COO)₃ + 3 NaOH → 3 C₁₇H₃₅COONa + C₃H₈O₃

- Tallow (animal fat): Triglyceride (three fatty acids bonded to glycerin backbone)
- Lye (NaOH): Sodium hydroxide, strong base
- Products:
  - Soap (C₁₇H₃₅COONa): Sodium stearate, the active cleaning agent
  - Glycerin (C₃H₈O₃): Useful byproduct for skin care

### How Soap Works

Soap molecules are amphipathic—one end loves water (hydrophilic), one end loves oil (hydrophobic).

**Cleaning process:**

1. **Oil trapping:** The hydrophobic end of soap buries itself in oil or grease
2. **Water contact:** The hydrophilic end remains in contact with water
3. **Emulsification:** Soap creates tiny droplets of oil suspended in water
4. **Rinsing:** Water carries the oil-soap droplets away

**Result:** Oils and dirt are removed from skin or fabric

### Factors Affecting Saponification

1. **Temperature:** Reaction proceeds faster at higher temperature (80–100°C optimal)
2. **Mixing:** Better mixing speeds reaction
3. **Type of base:** Sodium hydroxide (NaOH) makes hard soap; potassium hydroxide (KOH) makes soft soap
4. **Type of fat:** Saturated fats (animal tallow, coconut) make harder soap; unsaturated fats (olive oil, fish oil) make softer soap
5. **Water content:** More water slows reaction; less water speeds it but risks incomplete reaction

## Part 2: Making Lye from Wood Ash

Before you make soap, you need lye. Lye can be made from wood ash.

### Understanding Lye Strength

Wood ash contains potassium carbonate (K₂CO₃), which becomes potassium hydroxide (KOH) when dissolved in water.

**Lye from potassium carbonate:**

K₂CO₃ + H₂O → 2 KOH (potassium hydroxide)

**Lye strength varies:** Wood ash lye is weaker than commercial sodium hydroxide (NaOH). You must test its strength.

### Making Lye: Cold Water Method

**Materials:**
- Hardwood ash (from burned hardwood; softwood ash is weaker)
- Water
- Container (wooden barrel, large pot, or cloth-lined basket)
- Filter (cloth, straw, or sand)

**Process:**

1. **Prepare container:** Line with cloth and add about 4 inches of ash
2. **Add water:** Pour water slowly over ash until saturated (water should just cover the ash)
3. **Wait:** Allow to sit for 24–48 hours (longer if cold)
4. **Drain:** Carefully drain liquid from the bottom into a clean container
5. **Result:** Weak lye solution (about 2–5% KOH by weight)

**Reusing ash:** After draining once, discard the ash. Start fresh with new ash for each batch.

### Testing Lye Strength

**The egg test (traditional method):**

1. Place a fresh raw egg in the lye solution
2. If it floats to the top: Lye is strong enough for soap
3. If it sinks: Lye is too weak; continue reducing by evaporation
4. If it floats slightly: Perfect strength

**Why it works:** Proper lye density floats the egg; the specific gravity indicates sufficient lye concentration.

### Making Stronger Lye: Evaporation Method

If lye is too weak, evaporate water to concentrate it:

1. Heat lye solution gently (do not boil vigorously)
2. Water evaporates; potassium hydroxide concentration increases
3. Periodically test with egg
4. Stop when egg floats as desired

**Time required:** Several hours to days, depending on starting strength

## Part 3: Making Soap — Step-by-Step

### Procedure: Basic Cold Process Soap

**Materials (approximate ratios):**

- 5 pounds animal fat (tallow)
- 13 ounces lye (or appropriate amount for your fat; see table below)
- 2.5 pints water

**Step 1: Safety preparation (15 minutes)**

- Put on gloves and eye protection
- Wear old clothes that cover arms
- Have water available for rinsing
- Work outdoors or in well-ventilated area
- Have a clean workspace ready

**Step 2: Prepare fat (30 minutes)**

- If using solid animal fat: Cut into small pieces for faster melting
- Heat gently until completely melted and clear
- Strain through cloth to remove meat particles or moisture
- Allow to cool to about 80–90°C (just barely warm to touch)

**Step 3: Prepare lye solution (10 minutes)**

- Use tested lye solution (from wood ash)
- If using commercial lye: Dissolve in measured water (wear gloves)
- Allow to cool to about 80–90°C

**Step 4: Mix fat and lye (5 minutes)**

- Pour lye solution SLOWLY into melted fat while stirring constantly
- DO NOT pour fat into lye (dangerous thermal reaction)
- Stir steadily for 5 minutes
- Solution will heat up from the reaction; stir frequently

**Step 5: Continue stirring (30–60 minutes)**

- Stir continuously for 30–45 minutes
- Reaction is proceeding; mixture will thicken gradually
- At trace (when soap reaches pudding-like consistency), it's ready to pour
- **Trace:** When a spoon dipped in mixture leaves a brief impression before filling in

**Step 6: Pour into molds (10 minutes)**

- Pour into prepared molds (wooden boxes lined with cloth, or any container)
- Mixture should be pouring consistency (slightly thicker than honey)
- Level the top
- Cover loosely with cloth to keep dust out

**Step 7: Cure (4–6 weeks)**

- Allow to sit undisturbed for 24–48 hours (saponification continues)
- Soap will harden during this time
- Turn out of mold and cut into bars (if desired)
- Stack in well-ventilated area for 4–6 weeks
- Longer curing = harder, milder soap

**Result:** Soap ready to use

### Soap Calculation: Fat to Lye Ratios

Different fats require different amounts of lye:

- **Tallow (beef fat):** 0.128 oz NaOH per 1 oz fat (approximately 13 oz NaOH per 5 lbs fat)
- **Lard (pork fat):** 0.135 oz NaOH per 1 oz fat
- **Coconut oil:** 0.191 oz NaOH per 1 oz fat (requires more lye)
- **Olive oil:** 0.135 oz NaOH per 1 oz fat

**Wood ash lye:** Use approximately 2–3× the amount of NaOH lye (weaker potency)

**Rule of thumb:** For every 5 pounds of fat, use 13 ounces of commercial sodium hydroxide, or 2–3 pints of wood ash lye solution

## Part 4: Variations and Optimization

### Hot Process Soap (Faster)

If you need soap quickly (within days instead of weeks):

1. Follow the cold process procedure
2. After reaching trace, heat mixture in oven or over gentle heat
3. Stir occasionally for 1–2 hours
4. Mixture will become translucent and thick
5. Pour into molds while still warm
6. Soap is usable within 24–48 hours (though 1–2 weeks of curing improves quality)

**Advantage:** Much faster (soap ready in days)
**Disadvantage:** Requires more heat; glycerin byproduct is not recovered

### Soft Soap (From Potassium Hydroxide)

Potassium hydroxide from wood ash produces soft soap (liquid or paste):

1. Use wood ash lye (naturally contains KOH)
2. Follow the same procedure as cold process
3. Product will be softer (paste-like or liquid)
4. More suitable for washing clothes than hard soap

**Advantage:** Wood ash is free and accessible
**Disadvantage:** Lye strength varies; requires testing

### Adding Scent (Optional)

Post-collapse, scent is less critical than function, but may be desirable:

1. Essential oils (if available): Add 0.5–1% by weight to cooled soap
2. Herbs: Add dried, ground herbs (lavender, rosemary) to mixture
3. Spices: Add ground cinnamon or other spices for scent
4. Tallow naturally has tallow scent; coconut oil has pleasant scent

**Safety:** Do not add plant materials that decompose (they may rot soap). Use dried, shelf-stable materials.

### Scaling Production

**Single batch:** ~5 pounds fat produces 6–7 pounds soap

**Community batch:** 
- 50 pounds fat → ~60–70 pounds soap
- Requires larger pot, more stirring time
- Process is the same; increase quantities proportionally

**Production rhythm:**
- Batch every 2–4 weeks
- Soap cures while next batch is being made
- Continuous supply develops

## Part 5: Troubleshooting

### Soap Did Not Harden (Batch Failed)

**Causes:**
- Lye too weak (egg sank instead of floated)
- Incorrect fat-to-lye ratio
- Temperature too low (reaction slowed)
- Insufficient stirring (reaction not completed)

**Solutions:**
- Rebatch: Melt the soap, add more lye (test carefully), and repeat
- Or: Use as soft soap (liquid or paste form)

### Soap Is Too Soft/Greasy

**Cause:** Excess fat (under-lye)

**Solution:**
- Rebatch with additional lye

### Soap Separated Into Phases

**Cause:** Temperature mismatch or poor mixing

**Solution:**
- Remelt and restir at consistent temperature
- Mix more thoroughly

### Soap Discolored or Spotted

**Cause:** Rancid fat or contamination

**Solution:**
- If just surface spots: Trim off
- If throughout: Rebatch with fresh fat

### Soap Doesn't Lather Well

**Cause:** High fat content; possibly excess tallow

**Solutions:**
- Use softer fats (coconut, olive oil) in future batches
- Add commercial lye (if available) to increase saponification

## Safety and Handling

### Lye Safety

1. **Caustic:** Burns skin and eyes. Wear gloves and eye protection.
2. **Respiratory irritant:** Fumes can irritate lungs. Work in ventilated area.
3. **Cleanup:** If lye contacts skin, rinse immediately with copious water. Do not use acid to neutralize; just water.

### Soap Safety

- Finished soap is safe to use (lye is fully reacted)
- If soap feels slippery and burns skin, saponification is incomplete (excess lye); do not use
- Test on small area of skin first

## Verification

**Soap is ready to use when:**

1. Bars are hard (can be cut cleanly)
2. Soap is neutral to skin (doesn't burn)
3. Soap lathers (indicates proper saponification)
4. Soap cleans effectively (removes oil and dirt)

## Common Mistakes

1. **Using softwood ash:** Hardwood (oak, hickory) has higher potassium carbonate. Softwood (pine, fir) has less; lye will be weak.

2. **Not testing lye strength:** Weak lye produces incomplete saponification; excess fat remains. Always test with egg.

3. **Pouring lye into fat:** Violent reaction and spattering. Always pour lye into fat slowly.

4. **Rushing the cure:** Uncured soap may have excess lye. Always cure 4–6 weeks.

5. **Using aluminum pot:** Lye reacts with aluminum. Use stainless steel, cast iron, or enamel.

6. **Ignoring safety:** Lye is hazardous. Always wear protection.

7. **Unclear fat-to-lye ratio:** Incorrect ratios produce unusable soap. Measure carefully.
