## Overview

Optics is the study of light and how it behaves. Understanding optics helps you:

1. **Correct vision:** Make eyeglasses to help people see
2. **Magnify objects:** Make telescopes and microscopes
3. **Control light:** Use lenses and mirrors to focus and direct light
4. **Understand vision:** How eyes see; how to protect vision
5. **Create instruments:** Combine lenses to create useful optical devices

This chapter teaches practical optics for post-collapse survival.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of waves (from Waves and Sound chapter)
- Ability to observe light and shadows
- Understanding of refraction and reflection
- Patience for geometric reasoning

## Materials & Tools

**For observation and experimentation:**
- Light source (sun, candle, lamp)
- Transparent materials (glass, clear plastic, water)
- Mirrors (polished metal, glass)
- Lenses (if available, or can be ground from glass)
- Objects to observe (text, objects for magnification)
- Screens (paper) to project images

**For understanding:**
- Ability to draw ray diagrams
- Understanding of angles and simple geometry

## Part 1: Understanding Light

### What Is Light?

Light is electromagnetic radiation visible to the human eye.

**Wave properties:**
- Wavelength: 400–700 nanometers (visible range)
- Frequency: 400–800 THz
- Speed: 3 × 10⁸ m/s (in vacuum)
- Speed in materials: Slower than vacuum (water: 2.25 × 10⁸ m/s)

**Particle properties (photons):**
- Light consists of particles called photons
- Energy of photon = frequency × Planck's constant
- Higher frequency (blue) = higher energy than lower frequency (red)

### Color

Color depends on wavelength:

- **Red:** 650–700 nm (lowest frequency visible)
- **Orange:** 600–650 nm
- **Yellow:** 570–600 nm
- **Green:** 490–570 nm
- **Blue:** 450–490 nm
- **Violet:** 400–450 nm (highest frequency visible)

**Primary colors:**
- Red, green, blue (light—additive)
- Magenta, yellow, cyan (pigments—subtractive)

**White light:** Contains all visible wavelengths

## Part 2: Reflection

Light bounces off surfaces.

### Law of Reflection

Angle of incidence equals angle of reflection (both measured from normal to surface).

**Example:** Light hits mirror at 30° from normal, bounces at 30° from normal

### Types of Reflection

**Specular (mirror) reflection:**
- Smooth surface (polished metal, glass)
- All rays reflect at same angle
- Produces clear image

**Diffuse reflection:**
- Rough surface (paper, cloth, dirt)
- Rays reflect at different angles
- No clear image
- Most everyday objects visible through diffuse reflection

### Mirrors

**Plane mirrors:**
- Flat surface
- Image appears behind mirror at same distance as object in front
- Image is laterally inverted (left-right reversed)
- No magnification

**Curved mirrors:**
- Concave (curves inward): Converging, can magnify
- Convex (curves outward): Diverging, reduces image size
- Focal length depends on radius of curvature

**For survival:** Mirrors allow seeing around corners, signaling, and examining otherwise inaccessible areas.

## Part 3: Refraction

Light bends when passing between materials with different speeds.

### Snell's Law

n₁ sin(θ₁) = n₂ sin(θ₂)

- n: Refractive index (measure of how much material slows light)
- θ: Angle from normal

**Refractive indices:**
- Vacuum: 1.0
- Air: 1.0003 (approximately 1)
- Water: 1.33
- Glass: 1.5–1.9
- Diamond: 2.42

### Critical Angle

When light travels from denser to less dense medium, total internal reflection can occur.

**Occurs when:** Angle exceeds critical angle = arcsin(n₂/n₁)

**Example:** Light in glass trying to exit to air
- Critical angle = arcsin(1/1.5) = 42°
- Light at angles less than 42° exits; greater than 42° reflects internally

**Application:** Fiber optics use total internal reflection to trap light in glass fiber.

### Dispersion

Different wavelengths refract at different angles.

**Prism:** White light separates into spectrum (rainbow)
- Red (longest): Bends least
- Violet (shortest): Bends most

**For survival:** Dispersion can be observed in water droplets (rainbows) or passed through prisms (glass).

## Part 4: Lenses

Lenses use refraction to focus or diverge light.

### Converging Lenses (Convex)

Curved outward on both sides; focuses light to a point.

**Properties:**
- Focal length (f): Distance where light converges
- Focal length depends on curvature and refractive index
- Stronger curvature: Shorter focal length

**Lens equation:**
1/f = 1/d_o + 1/d_i

- d_o: Object distance
- d_i: Image distance
- f: Focal length

**Image properties:**
- If d_o beyond focal length: Real, inverted, smaller (magnification less than 1)
- If d_o between focal length and lens: Virtual, upright, larger (magnification greater than 1)
- If d_o equals focal length: No image (rays emerge parallel)

### Diverging Lenses (Concave)

Curved inward; spreads light outward.

**Properties:**
- Negative focal length
- Always produces virtual, upright, smaller image
- Image cannot be projected on screen

### Magnifying Power

Magnification = h_i / h_o = d_i / d_o

**Example:**
- Magnifying glass with f = 5 cm at d_o = 4 cm
- 1/5 = 1/4 + 1/d_i
- 1/d_i = 1/5 - 1/4 = -0.05
- d_i = -20 cm (virtual image, 20 cm behind lens)
- Magnification = -(-20)/4 = 5× (upright, 5 times larger)

### Correcting Vision

**Nearsightedness (myopia):**
- Eye focuses light too close
- Distant objects blurry
- Correction: Diverging (concave) lens spreads light
- Negative power lens prescription

**Farsightedness (hyperopia):**
- Eye focuses light too far
- Close objects blurry
- Correction: Converging (convex) lens focuses light closer
- Positive power lens prescription

**Power of lens:** Reciprocal of focal length in meters
- Power (diopters) = 1/f (meters)
- Example: f = 0.5 m → Power = 2 diopters

### Polarization

Light waves can vibrate in different directions (polarization).

**Unpolarized light:** Vibrations in all directions
**Polarized light:** Vibrations in one direction only

**Causes of polarization:**
- Reflection off water or glass at specific angles
- Passing through polarizing filter
- Scattering by atmosphere (sky appears blue and partially polarized)

**For survival:**
- Polarized sunglasses reduce glare from water or snow
- Polarizing filters can be made from special materials
- Sky polarization helps navigate without compass (bees use this)

### Diffraction and Interference in Light

**Diffraction:** Light bends around small obstacles
- Creates diffraction patterns (bright and dark bands)
- Observable with small slits or gratings

**Interference:** Waves from two sources combine
- Constructive: Amplifies light (bright)
- Destructive: Cancels light (dark)
- Creates interference patterns

**Young's double-slit experiment:**
- Light passes through two slits
- Waves from each slit interfere
- Creates alternating bright and dark fringes on screen

**For survival:**
- Diffraction limits how small you can make things with tools (tools can't cut sharper than wavelength of light used)
- Interference affects visibility in certain conditions

## Part 5: Optical Phenomena

### Scattering

Light bounces off small particles in atmosphere.

**Types:**
- **Rayleigh scattering:** Scatters blue light more than red (why sky is blue)
- **Mie scattering:** By larger particles (clouds, fog); scatters all wavelengths equally (clouds appear white)
- **Raman scattering:** Light changes wavelength

**For survival:**
- Blue sky indicates clear air
- Red/orange sky indicates dust or pollution
- Fog/clouds scatter light; visibility reduced

### Refraction by Atmosphere

Light from sun or moon bends as it passes through layers of atmosphere at different temperatures.

**Effects:**
- Sun appears slightly higher than actual position (atmospheric refraction)
- Stars twinkle (atmospheric turbulence)
- Objects near horizon appear distorted

**For navigation:** Horizon appears farther due to refraction; can see distance.

### Lens Aberrations

Real lenses have imperfections:

**Chromatic aberration:**
- Different colors refract at different angles
- Produces color fringes
- Correction: Use multiple lenses of different materials

**Spherical aberration:**
- Edges of lens refract differently than center
- Produces blurred image
- Correction: Use aspherical lenses or stop down aperture

**Astigmatism:**
- Different focal lengths in different directions
- Produces blurred image
- Correction: Cylindrical lenses

## Part 6: Optical Instruments

### Magnifying Glass

Simple converging lens with object between focal point and lens.

- Magnification: 5–10× typical
- Field of view: Large
- Works best for examining objects close to eye

### Binoculars

Two telescopes side-by-side for stereoscopic vision (3D perception).

- Objective lens (long focal length) collects light
- Prism system erects image (telescopes invert)
- Eyepieces magnify

**Magnification × Objective diameter:**
- "8 × 42" binoculars: 8× magnification, 42 mm objective
- Higher magnification = narrower field of view = harder to find objects
- Larger objective = more light collected = brighter image

### Simple Microscope

Two converging lenses (objective and eyepiece).

- Objective: High magnification, short focal length (~0.5 cm)
- Eyepiece: Lower magnification, longer focal length (~2.5 cm)
- Total magnification: M_obj × M_eye = 20–100× easily

### Simple Telescope

**Refracting telescope:** Two converging lenses (objective and eyepiece)
- Objective: Long focal length (focus on distant objects)
- Eyepiece: Short focal length (magnify)
- Total magnification: f_obj / f_eye
- Example: f_obj = 50 cm, f_eye = 2.5 cm → Magnification = 20×

**Reflecting telescope:** Curved mirror instead of lens
- Mirrors don't have chromatic aberration
- Larger mirrors possible (more light collected)
- More complex design

## Part 6: The Human Eye

### Eye Structure

**Cornea:** Curved transparent front; refracts light
**Lens:** Adjusts focus (accommodation)
**Retina:** Light-sensitive layer; converts light to neural signals
**Optic nerve:** Carries signal to brain

### Eye Defects

**Myopia (nearsightedness):**
- Eyeball too long or cornea too curved
- Light focuses before reaching retina
- Distant objects blurry
- Correction: Diverging (minus) lens

**Hyperopia (farsightedness):**
- Eyeball too short or cornea too flat
- Light focuses behind retina
- Close objects blurry
- Correction: Converging (plus) lens

**Astigmatism:**
- Cornea or lens irregular shape
- Causes blurred vision at all distances
- Correction: Cylindrical lens

**Presbyopia:**
- Lens becomes less flexible with age
- Accommodation decreases
- Cannot focus on close objects
- Affects people over 40
- Correction: Bifocals (two focal lengths)

### Visual Acuity

**20/20 vision:** Can see details from 20 feet that average person can see from 20 feet
**20/40 vision:** Need to be at 20 feet to see what average person sees at 40 feet (worse than average)
**20/10 vision:** Can see from 20 feet what average person needs to be at 10 feet to see (better than average)

**Resolution limit:** Ability to distinguish two nearby objects
- Determined by pupil size and light wavelength
- Larger pupil allows seeing finer details
- Limited by diffraction of light

## Part 7: Practical Applications

### Making Lenses

Lenses can be ground from glass if raw glass is available:

1. Start with rough glass sphere
2. Grind to desired curvature
3. Polish to transparency
4. Test focal length experimentally

**Focal length test:**
- Focus sunlight onto paper
- Measure distance from lens to burn point
- That distance is focal length

### Vision Correction Without Prescription

**Pinhole glasses:**
- Glasses with tiny holes instead of lenses
- Holes increase depth of field (everything sharp)
- Works by reducing light cone angle
- Reduces light; vision darker but sharper

**Water lens:**
- Glass or plastic container filled with water
- Acts as lens due to water–glass interface
- Can correct some vision problems

## Common Mistakes

1. **Confusing real and virtual images:** Real images can be projected; virtual images cannot.

2. **Underestimating aberrations:** Real lenses have distortion; multi-lens systems minimize this.

3. **Assuming all transparent materials refract the same:** Different materials have different refractive indices; this affects focal length.

4. **Confusing magnification with resolution:** Magnification enlarges image; resolution determines if details are visible. Resolution limited by lens quality and wavelength.

5. **Not accounting for light loss:** Real lenses absorb some light; not 100% transparent.

6. **Ignoring optical aberrations:** Spherical lenses cause distortion; complex lens designs minimize this.
