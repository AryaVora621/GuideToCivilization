## Overview

Mechanics is the foundation of physics. It explains how objects move, how forces act on them, and how energy transforms. Understanding mechanics helps you:

1. **Use tools effectively:** Levers, pulleys, and inclined planes amplify your effort
2. **Design structures:** Understand stress, strain, and why structures fail
3. **Create machines:** Design water wheels, gears, and power-transmission systems
4. **Do work efficiently:** Understand energy and minimize effort
5. **Predict motion:** Understand ballistics, trajectories, and collisions

This chapter teaches practical mechanics for post-collapse survival and engineering.

## Prerequisites

- Ability to observe motion and forces
- Basic math skills (multiplication, division)

## Part 1: Understanding Forces and Motion

### What Is Force?

A force is a push or pull that causes change in motion or shape.

**Types of force:**
- **Contact forces:** Direct push or pull (hand on object, water pressure on dam)
- **Non-contact forces:** Act at distance (gravity pulling objects down, magnets attracting iron)

### Newton's Laws of Motion

**Law 1 (Inertia):** An object at rest stays at rest; an object in motion stays in motion unless acted upon by a force.

- Implication: Objects have inertia (resistance to change in motion)
- Heavier objects have more inertia (harder to move or stop)

**Law 2 (Force and Acceleration):** Force equals mass times acceleration.

F = ma

- Larger force produces larger acceleration
- Heavier objects accelerate less for same force
- Example: Pushing a rock is harder than pushing a stick

**Law 3 (Action-Reaction):** For every force, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

- When you push something, it pushes back with equal force
- Example: Jumping pushes the ground down; ground pushes you up

### Velocity and Acceleration

**Velocity:** Speed in a direction
- Constant velocity: Moving at steady speed in one direction
- Changing velocity: Accelerating (speeding up, slowing down, or changing direction)

**Acceleration:** Rate of change of velocity
- Positive acceleration: Speed increasing (gas pedal)
- Negative acceleration/deceleration: Speed decreasing (brakes)
- Directional acceleration: Changing direction at constant speed (turning a corner)

## Part 2: Simple Machines

Simple machines amplify your effort by changing the direction of force or multiplying force.

### The Lever

A lever is a rigid bar balanced on a pivot point (fulcrum).

**Mechanical advantage:** How much the lever multiplies your force

Mechanical Advantage = Distance from fulcrum to load / Distance from fulcrum to effort

**Example: Prying a rock**
- Effort point (where you push) is 3 feet from fulcrum
- Load point (rock) is 1 foot from fulcrum
- Mechanical advantage: 3/1 = 3
- Result: You move the rock with 1/3 of the force it weighs

**Types of levers:**
- **Class 1:** Fulcrum between effort and load (seesaw, crowbar, pry bar)
- **Class 2:** Load between effort and fulcrum (wheelbarrow, nutcracker)
- **Class 3:** Effort between load and fulcrum (fishing rod, hammer claw)

**For survival:** Levers allow lifting heavy objects without mechanical advantage disadvantage of moving the effort point farther.

### The Pulley

A pulley is a wheel with a rope over it.

**Single fixed pulley:**
- Changes direction of force (pull down, lift up)
- No mechanical advantage (1:1)
- Useful for convenience

**Movable pulley:**
- Attached to load
- Rope attached to fixed point
- Mechanical advantage = 2
- Result: Pull with half the force needed, but pull twice the distance

**Pulley system (multiple pulleys):**
- Mechanical advantage = number of ropes supporting the load
- 4 ropes supporting load: Mechanical advantage = 4
- Pull 4 feet of rope to lift load 1 foot

### The Inclined Plane

An inclined plane is a slope used to lift objects.

**Mechanical advantage:** Length of slope / Height

**Example:**
- Ramp is 20 feet long
- Height is 5 feet
- Mechanical advantage: 20/5 = 4
- Result: Push with 1/4 the force needed to lift directly, but push 4 times the distance

**For survival:** Moving heavy objects up inclines requires less force but more distance.

### The Wheel and Axle

A large wheel connected to a small axle rotates together.

**Mechanical advantage:** Radius of wheel / Radius of axle

**Example:**
- Wheel radius: 2 feet
- Axle radius: 0.5 feet
- Mechanical advantage: 2/0.5 = 4
- Result: Small force on wheel edge produces large force on axle

**Applications:**
- Grinding wheels (grain mills)
- Water wheels (power generation)
- Door knobs (easy to turn despite friction at hinge)

### The Screw

A screw is an inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder.

**Mechanical advantage:** Circumference of screw / Distance between threads

**For survival:**
- Tight grip for fastening
- Converts rotational force to linear force
- Useful for pressing materials (wine press, olive press)

## Part 3: Work and Energy

### Defining Work

In physics, work is force applied over distance:

Work = Force × Distance

**Example:**
- Push with 10 pounds force over 10 feet distance
- Work = 10 × 10 = 100 foot-pounds of work

**Important:** No work is done if:
- Force is applied but no motion results (pushing on wall)
- Motion happens but force is perpendicular to motion

### Energy

Energy is the ability to do work.

**Types of energy:**

**Kinetic energy:** Energy of motion
- KE = (1/2) × mass × velocity²
- Faster objects have more kinetic energy
- Heavier objects have more kinetic energy

**Potential energy:** Energy of position
- Gravitational PE = mass × gravity × height
- Objects higher up have more potential energy
- Example: Water at top of waterfall has potential energy

**Conservation of energy:** Energy is neither created nor destroyed; it transforms.

- Falling object: PE becomes KE
- Water flowing downhill: Gravitational PE becomes kinetic energy
- Burning fuel: Chemical energy becomes heat and light

### Power

Power is the rate of doing work:

Power = Work / Time

**Example:**
- Lift a rock 10 feet with 100 pounds force
- Work = 100 × 10 = 1000 foot-pounds
- If done in 10 seconds: Power = 1000 / 10 = 100 foot-pounds per second

**For survival:** More power means doing work faster. Machines amplify power by using natural sources (water, wind).

## Part 4: Friction and Efficiency

### Friction

Friction is a force that opposes motion.

**Sources:**
- Surface roughness (rough surfaces have more friction)
- Normal force (heavier objects experience more friction)
- Material properties (different material pairs have different friction)

**Reducing friction:**
- Smooth surfaces (polishing)
- Lubricants (oil, grease)
- Rolling instead of sliding (wheels reduce friction)

**Types:**
- **Static friction:** Prevents motion (effort to start moving)
- **Kinetic friction:** Opposes motion (resistance while moving)
- **Rolling friction:** Opposition to rolling (wheels on ground)

### Efficiency

No machine is 100% efficient; friction wastes energy.

Efficiency = Useful work output / Total work input

**Example:**
- Waterwheel receives 1000 foot-pounds of water energy
- Produces 750 foot-pounds of rotational energy
- Efficiency = 750/1000 = 75%
- 25% lost to friction

**For survival:** Maintain machines regularly (oil wheels, smooth surfaces) to keep efficiency high.

### Momentum and Collisions

Momentum is the quantity of motion an object has:

Momentum = Mass × Velocity

**Example:**
- Heavy object moving slowly: Lots of momentum
- Light object moving fast: Lots of momentum
- Stationary object: No momentum

**Conservation of momentum:** In a collision, total momentum before equals total momentum after.

**Types of collisions:**
- **Elastic:** Objects bounce apart (ball bouncing)
- **Inelastic:** Objects stick together or deform (car crash)

**For survival:**
- Heavier objects moving slowly have more momentum than light objects moving fast
- Stopping momentum requires force over time/distance
- Seatbelts work by spreading impact force over time and distance

### Rotational Motion

Objects can rotate around an axis.

**Angular velocity:** How fast something rotates (revolutions per minute, degrees per second)

**Torque:** Force that causes rotation
- Torque = Force × Distance from axis
- Larger distance from axis = More torque for same force
- Example: Steering wheel is large so small hand force creates large torque

**Moment of inertia:** Resistance to change in rotation
- Similar to mass for linear motion
- Objects with mass far from axis have high moment of inertia
- Example: Spinning disk has more moment of inertia than spinning point mass

**For survival:**
- Longer handles give more torque (larger diameter wheel is easier to turn)
- Rotating tools (drills, pumps) use torque to do work
- Flywheels store rotational energy (continue rotating even if force stops)

## Part 5: Practical Applications of Mechanics

### Water Wheels (Hydropower)

Water falling or flowing provides energy:

1. Water flows with gravitational PE
2. Wheel in water converts PE to kinetic energy (rotating motion)
3. Rotation can power mills, pumps, or generators

**Design considerations:**
- Larger wheel diameter: More mechanical advantage
- More water: More power
- Higher fall: More potential energy

### Traction for Heavy Objects

Moving heavy objects uphill uses gravity:

**Using lever to pry:**
- Fulcrum near load
- Small lever motion at fulcrum creates large load motion

**Using inclined plane:**
- Slope spreads effort over distance
- Less force needed but longer path

**Using pulley system:**
- Multiple ropes divide load
- Human strength sufficient for much heavier loads

### Gears and Power Transmission

Gears are wheels with teeth that mesh together:

**Mechanical advantage from gears:**
- Large gear driving small gear: Increases speed, decreases force
- Small gear driving large gear: Decreases speed, increases force
- Ratio = Teeth on driven gear / Teeth on driver gear

**For survival:**
- Gears transmit rotation between distant points
- Gears amplify or reduce rotational speed
- Useful for mills, pumps, and machinery

## Part 6: Stress, Strain, and Structural Design

### Stress and Strain

**Stress:** Force applied to material
- Compression: Squeezed (pushing together)
- Tension: Pulled apart
- Shear: Pushed sideways

**Strain:** Deformation that results
- Elastic strain: Material returns to original shape (rubber)
- Plastic strain: Material permanently deformed (clay)
- Breaking: Material fails completely

### Safe Design

Structures must support their own weight plus loads:

**Factors in structural design:**
- Material strength (steel stronger than wood)
- Load distribution (wider supports reduce stress)
- Shape (arches and domes distribute stress)
- Connections (joints are weak points; reinforce)

**For post-collapse construction:**
- Use triangular bracing (resists bending better than rectangles)
- Distribute loads over multiple supports
- Use stone/masonry for compression (strong in compression)
- Use wood/rope for tension (strong in tension)

## Common Mistakes

1. **Forgetting Newton's third law:** Forces always come in pairs. The ground pushes you up when you jump.

2. **Confusing mass and weight:** Mass is amount of matter; weight is mass times gravity. The difference matters for calculations.

3. **Over-estimating mechanical advantage:** Machines don't create energy, just redirect it. You work longer to work with less force.

4. **Ignoring friction:** Real machines lose energy to friction. Design assuming 70–80% efficiency, not 100%.

5. **Poor load distribution:** Concentrating force at a point causes failure. Spread loads over multiple supports.

6. **Weak connections:** The strongest machine fails if connections break. Reinforce joints carefully.

7. **Ignoring leverage direction:** Leveraging the wrong end gives disadvantage instead of advantage. Fulcrum position matters.

8. **Underestimating pulley systems:** Multiple pulleys can lift very heavy loads with moderate force. Use them.
