## Overview

Electromagnetism is the unified theory of electricity and magnetism. They are not separate forces but two aspects of the same fundamental interaction. Understanding electromagnetism helps you:

1. **Transmit information:** Radio and wireless communication use electromagnetic waves
2. **Understand light:** Light is an electromagnetic wave
3. **Generate and transmit power:** Electrical power transmission uses electromagnetic fields
4. **Predict behavior:** Understand how electrical and magnetic fields interact
5. **Design devices:** Build more efficient electrical and magnetic devices

This chapter teaches practical electromagnetism theory.

## Part 1: Maxwell's Equations

Maxwell's equations describe how electricity and magnetism work together.

### The Four Equations

**Gauss's Law:**
- Electric charges create electric fields
- More charge = stronger field
- Field radiates outward from positive charge, inward to negative

**Gauss's Law for Magnetism:**
- No magnetic monopoles exist (no single north or south)
- Magnetic field lines always form closed loops
- Consequence: Can't isolate individual magnetic poles

**Faraday's Law:**
- Changing magnetic field creates electric field
- Rate of change matters (faster change = stronger induced field)
- Used in generators, transformers, inductors

**Ampere-Maxwell Law:**
- Electric current creates magnetic field
- Changing electric field also creates magnetic field
- Unifies electricity and magnetism at fundamental level

### Consequences of Maxwell's Equations

From these four equations, several profound conclusions follow:

1. **Electromagnetic waves exist:** Changing electric field creates magnetic field, which creates electric field, etc.
2. **Waves travel at speed of light:** c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀) (fundamental constant)
3. **Light is electromagnetic:** Visible light is just electromagnetic wave in visible frequency range
4. **All electromagnetic waves travel at same speed:** Radio, microwave, light, X-ray—all travel at c in vacuum

## Part 2: Electromagnetic Waves

Electromagnetic waves consist of oscillating electric and magnetic fields perpendicular to each other and to direction of propagation.

### Wave Properties

**Frequency and wavelength:** c = f × λ
- c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s (speed of light)
- Higher frequency = shorter wavelength
- Lower frequency = longer wavelength

**Electromagnetic spectrum:**
- Radio: 10⁶–10⁹ Hz (longest waves)
- Microwave: 10⁹–10¹² Hz
- Infrared: 10¹²–10¹⁴ Hz
- Visible light: 4–8 × 10¹⁴ Hz
- Ultraviolet: 10¹⁴–10¹⁷ Hz
- X-ray: 10¹⁷–10¹⁹ Hz
- Gamma ray: 10¹⁹ Hz+ (shortest waves)

### Electromagnetic Radiation from Accelerated Charges

Accelerating electrical charges radiate electromagnetic waves.

**Examples:**
- Oscillating electrons in antenna → Radio waves
- Oscillating electrons in wire → Infrared heat
- Electrons jumping between atomic levels → Light
- Rapidly stopping electrons → X-rays

**Power radiated:** P ∝ a² (proportional to acceleration squared)
- Rapid acceleration = more radiation
- Slow acceleration = less radiation

**Practical implications:**
- High-frequency oscillations radiate effectively (radio antennas)
- Low-frequency oscillations radiate poorly (power lines don't radiate much)

## Part 3: Radio Waves and Communication

Radio waves are long-wavelength electromagnetic radiation.

### Radio Wave Generation

**Process:**
1. Electrical current oscillates in conductor (antenna)
2. Oscillating current creates changing electric field
3. Changing field radiates as electromagnetic wave
4. Wave propagates outward at speed of light

**Antenna design:**
- Antenna size affects efficiency
- Most efficient when length = λ/4 or λ/2
- Example: 1 MHz radio has λ = 300 m; efficient antenna is 75 m long

**Directional vs. omnidirectional:**
- Parabolic reflector focuses waves (like flashlight beam)
- Dish antennas increase range and directionality
- Yagi antenna (multiple elements) creates focused radiation pattern
- Whip/monopole antenna radiates in all directions (less efficient but simpler)

**Antenna impedance matching:**
- Antenna impedance must match transmission line impedance
- Mismatch causes signal reflection and power loss
- 50 ohm coax cable standard for radio systems

### Radio Wave Propagation

**Ground wave:** Travels along surface
- Follows Earth's curvature
- Works for distances up to ~100 km
- Better at low frequencies

**Sky wave:** Bounces off ionosphere
- Reflects down at great distances
- Used for long-distance communication
- Affected by solar activity

**Direct wave:** Line-of-sight
- Straight path between transmitter and receiver
- Blocked by obstacles
- Used for VHF/UHF communication

### Radio Frequency (RF) Circuits

Radio receivers detect electromagnetic waves and convert to audio.

**Basic receiver:**
1. Antenna captures wave
2. Tuned circuit selects specific frequency
3. Detector extracts information (modulation)
4. Amplifier boosts signal
5. Speaker converts to sound

**Modulation methods:**
- AM (Amplitude Modulation): Varies wave height
- FM (Frequency Modulation): Varies wave frequency
- Digital: Uses on/off pulses

## Part 4: Induction and Energy Transfer

Electromagnetic induction is fundamental to transformers, generators, and wireless power transfer.

### Wireless Power Transfer

Electromagnetic energy can be transmitted wirelessly using coupled coils.

**Mechanism:**
1. Primary coil has AC current
2. Creates changing magnetic field
3. Field induces voltage in secondary coil
4. Power transfers without physical connection

**Efficiency:** Depends on:
- Coupling strength (how well fields overlap)
- Frequency (higher frequency better)
- Distance (decreases rapidly with distance)

**Practical range:** Typically works best under 1 meter with conventional coils.

### Resonant Transfer

When coils are tuned to same frequency, transfer is much more efficient.

**Resonance condition:** Both coils oscillate at same frequency
**Advantage:** Dramatic increase in efficiency at resonance
**Example:** Two coils at 1 MHz can transfer power efficiently at inches distance

**For post-collapse applications:** Wireless charging of batteries, device-to-device power transfer

## Part 5: Practical Electromagnetic Applications

### Power Transmission

Electrical power transmitted over long distances using AC (alternating current).

**Why AC:** Transformers only work with AC
**Efficiency:** Can step up voltage (step down current)
- High voltage, low current loses less heat in wires
- Transformer at destination steps down to usable voltage

**Transmission efficiency:** 
- High voltage (100+ kV): ~95% efficiency over long distances
- Low voltage: Higher losses

### Coupled Resonant Coils

Wireless power transfer works best with resonant coupling.

**Principle:**
1. Primary coil oscillates at specific frequency
2. Sends electromagnetic energy to secondary coil
3. If secondary tuned to same frequency, absorbs energy efficiently
4. Energy transferred without wires or direct contact

**Q factor:** Measure of how selectively tuned coil is
- High Q: Narrow frequency response, high efficiency at resonance
- Low Q: Broad frequency response, lower peak efficiency
- Typical Q: 10-100 for practical coils

**Practical wireless charging:**
- Phone charging pads use ~100 kHz frequency
- Coils separated by 3–5 mm typically
- Efficiency ~70–80% at close distance

**Distance limitations:**
- Power transfer decreases rapidly with distance
- Scales as 1/distance⁴ for certain configurations
- Practical limit ~1 meter with conventional approaches

### Microwave Ovens

Microwave radiation heats food by exciting water molecules.

**Frequency:** ~2.45 GHz (microwaves)
**Wavelength:** ~12 cm
**How it works:**
1. Magnetron generates microwaves
2. Waves reflect around metal box
3. Penetrate food to ~2 cm depth
4. Water molecules absorb energy, vibrate, generate heat

**Why metal blocks:** Wavelength similar to metal dimensions; waves reflect instead of penetrate

**Practical microwave safety:**
- Microwave oven is shielded; waves don't escape
- Metal mesh window allows seeing inside (holes smaller than wavelength)
- Door safety interlock stops microwaves when open
- Non-ionizing radiation; safe exposure levels set by regulations
- Effective shielding prevents leakage

### Signal Modulation and Demodulation

Information is transmitted by modulating electromagnetic waves.

**Amplitude Modulation (AM):**
- Vary the height of waves
- Frequency stays constant
- Information encoded in varying amplitude
- Used in AM radio broadcasts

**Frequency Modulation (FM):**
- Vary the frequency of waves
- Amplitude stays constant
- Information encoded in frequency variations
- More resistant to noise than AM

**Digital Modulation:**
- Uses discrete states (on/off pulses)
- Binary: 1 = signal on, 0 = signal off
- More efficient than analog for data

**Bandwidth requirement:**
- Higher information rate requires wider bandwidth
- Bandwidth = range of frequencies used
- FM radio: ~200 kHz bandwidth per station
- Cell phone: ~2 MHz bandwidth per user

---

### Propagation Losses

Electromagnetic waves weaken over distance.

**Path loss:** Power decreases with distance squared
- Doubling distance reduces power to 1/4
- Equation: Loss (dB) = 20 log(distance)

**Absorption:**
- Water absorbs microwaves (why it heats food)
- Metals reflect waves
- Vegetation attenuates radio waves

**Refraction:**
- Waves bend in atmosphere due to temperature/humidity gradients
- Affects long-distance communication
- Important for satellite communication

**Multipath propagation:**
- Waves reflect off obstacles (buildings, terrain)
- Multiple copies arrive at receiver at different times
- Causes fading and interference

## Part 6: Electromagnetic Effects and Safety

### Radiation Safety

Electromagnetic radiation carries energy. High-frequency waves are more energetic.

**Ionizing radiation (dangerous):**
- X-rays, gamma rays, UV: Can ionize atoms, damage DNA
- Cause burns, cancer, radiation sickness at high doses

**Non-ionizing radiation (generally safe):**
- Visible light: Lower energy, doesn't ionize
- Radio, microwave: Much lower energy
- Still can cause heating if intense

**Safe practices:**
- Limit exposure to high-frequency radiation
- X-ray: Lead shielding blocks effectively
- UV: Use sunscreen or protective clothing
- Radio/microwave: Normal sources are safe; avoid concentrated beams

### Electromagnetic Interference (EMI)

Electromagnetic waves can interfere with electronics.

**Sources:**
- Power lines (60 Hz)
- Radio transmitters
- Electrical motors
- Microwave ovens
- Cell phone transmitters

**Effects:**
- Distortion in radio reception
- Noise in audio equipment
- Malfunction of sensitive electronics

**Reduction methods:**
- Shielding (metal boxes block external fields; Faraday cage principle)
- Grounding (provides path for interference; connects to Earth reference)
- Filtering (removes unwanted frequencies using capacitors and inductors)
- Distance (move away from source; power decreases with distance)
- Twisted pair cables (cancels external field effects)
- Ferrite cores (absorb electromagnetic energy on wires)

## Common Mistakes

1. **Thinking electricity and magnetism are separate:** They are two aspects of one phenomenon unified by Maxwell.

2. **Confusing all radiation as dangerous:** Only ionizing radiation (X-ray, gamma, UV) is significantly dangerous. Radio waves are safe.

3. **Assuming light and radio are fundamentally different:** Both are electromagnetic waves; only frequency differs.

4. **Overestimating antenna efficiency:** Most antennas are inefficient unless properly sized and matched to frequency.

5. **Ignoring impedance matching:** Power transfer is inefficient if impedances don't match between source and load.

6. **Thinking wireless power is magic:** Still limited by physics; efficiency depends on distance and frequency.

7. **Ignoring shielding importance:** Proper shielding of wires prevents both EMI and radiation loss.

8. **Underestimating transformer importance:** Transformers enable efficient long-distance power transmission; without them, power distribution as we know it wouldn't exist.

9. **Assuming simple formulas apply everywhere:** Real-world systems have losses, non-ideal materials, and impedance mismatches. Theory is guide, but measurements verify behavior.

10. **Neglecting frequency-dependent effects:** Resistance, inductance, and capacitance all vary with frequency. Low-frequency approximations don't work for high frequencies.
