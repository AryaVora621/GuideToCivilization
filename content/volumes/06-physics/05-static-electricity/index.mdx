## Overview

Static electricity is the accumulation of electric charge on objects. Understanding static electricity helps you:

1. **Predict lightning:** Know when lightning is likely and how to stay safe
2. **Understand electrical phenomena:** Why objects stick together or repel
3. **Protect equipment:** Ground systems to prevent electrical damage
4. **Generate electricity:** Friction can create charge (starting point for electrical generation)
5. **Work safely:** Avoid electrical hazards

This chapter teaches practical static electricity physics.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of atoms and electrons
- Understanding of forces and fields
- Ability to observe electrical phenomena (attraction, repulsion)
- Awareness of hazards

## Part 1: Understanding Electric Charge

### What Is Electric Charge?

Electric charge is a property of matter. Every electron has negative charge; every proton has positive charge.

**Charge units:** Coulomb (C)
- Electron charge: -1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
- Proton charge: +1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C

**Charge neutrality:**
- Neutral atom: Equal electrons and protons (charges cancel)
- Positively charged: More protons than electrons
- Negatively charged: More electrons than protons

### How Objects Become Charged

**Friction (triboelectric effect):**
- Rubbing two materials together transfers electrons
- Electrons move from one material to another
- One becomes positive (lost electrons), one becomes negative (gained electrons)

**Examples:**
- Rubbing rubber balloon with wool: Wool loses electrons (positive), balloon gains electrons (negative)
- Rubbing plastic rod with cloth: Rod becomes negative, cloth becomes positive

**Contact:**
- Charged object touches neutral object
- Electrons transfer until balanced
- Both objects end up with same charge

**Induction:**
- Charged object near conductor
- Electrons in conductor repel or attract based on charge
- Doesn't require contact

## Part 2: Electric Force

### Coulomb's Law

The force between two charges:

F = k × (Q₁ × Q₂) / r²

- Q₁, Q₂: Charges
- r: Distance between charges
- k: Coulomb constant (9 × 10⁹ N⋅m²/C²)

**Properties:**
- Like charges (same sign) repel
- Opposite charges attract
- Force increases with charge
- Force decreases with distance squared (doubling distance reduces force to 1/4)

### Capacitance

A capacitor stores electric charge.

**Simple capacitor:**
- Two conductors separated by insulator
- Charge one plate positive, one negative
- Creates electric field between
- Stores energy in field

**Capacitance:** C = Q / V
- Q: Charge stored
- V: Voltage (potential difference)
- Larger plates or closer spacing: Higher capacitance

**Uses:**
- Store electrical energy
- Smooth electrical signals
- Protect circuits from voltage spikes

**For post-collapse:** Can be made from metal plates separated by insulating material (air, paper).

### Electric Field

An electric field surrounds every charge. It exerts force on other charges.

**Field strength:** E = F / Q (force per unit charge)

**Field direction:**
- From positive charge: Points outward
- To negative charge: Points inward

**Field pattern:**
- Parallel lines indicate uniform field
- Closer lines indicate stronger field
- Field lines never cross

## Part 3: Lightning

Lightning is a massive static discharge between clouds and ground (or between clouds).

### Formation of Lightning

**Step 1: Charge separation**
- Ice crystals and water droplets collide in thunderstorm
- Friction transfers electrons
- Cloud becomes negatively charged at bottom, positively at top
- Ground becomes positively charged (induced by field)

**Step 2: Electric field buildup**
- Potential difference (voltage) grows
- Field strength increases to billions of volts per meter
- Air normally insulates, but extreme field ionizes it

**Step 3: Stepped leader**
- Negative charge moves from cloud downward in steps
- Ionizes air creating conductive path
- Branch out as it seeks path of least resistance

**Step 4: Return stroke**
- Ground positive charge rushes upward
- Meets stepping leader
- Creates bright channel
- This is the visible lightning we see

**Step 5: Continuing current**
- More charge flows along ionized path
- Creates heat (30,000 K—hotter than sun's surface)
- Expands air violently → thunder
- All of this happens in milliseconds

### Thunder

Thunder is the sound of lightning.

**Mechanism:**
1. Lightning heats air to extreme temperature instantly
2. Air expands rapidly (shock wave)
3. Shock wave spreads outward at speed of sound
4. We hear it as sound (thunder)

**Estimating distance:**
- Light travels ~300,000 km/s (essentially instantaneous)
- Sound travels ~340 m/s
- Delay between flash and thunder indicates distance
- Delay in seconds × 340 m/s = distance in meters
- Or: Count seconds, divide by 3 for distance in kilometers

**Example:** 6 seconds between flash and thunder
- Distance = 6 × 340 = 2,040 meters (about 2 km away)
- Still dangerous; seek shelter

### Multiple strokes

Most lightning consists of multiple strokes:

- First stroke ionizes path
- Path conducts multiple additional strokes
- Creates flickering appearance
- 3–4 strokes typical, up to 40 possible
- Whole process takes less than 1 second

### Lightning Characteristics

**Duration:** Very brief (milliseconds)
**Current:** 20,000–100,000 amperes
**Voltage:** Billions of volts
**Temperature:** 30,000 K (5× sun's surface)
**Power:** Megawatts

**Frequency:** Lightning strikes Earth ~100 times per second globally

### Types of Lightning

**Cloud-to-ground (negative):**
- Negative charge descends from cloud
- Most common (90%)
- Most dangerous

**Cloud-to-ground (positive):**
- Positive charge from cloud top
- Less common (10%)
- Often stronger and longer
- Can strike far from cloud

**Cloud-to-cloud:**
- Within cloud or between clouds
- Usually not a danger to ground
- Sheet lightning (illuminates clouds)

**Intra-cloud:**
- Within same cloud
- Creates flickering appearance
- Not visible as striking channel

**St. Elmo's Fire:**
- Luminous plasma forming on pointed objects
- Occurs in strong electrical fields
- Precursor to lightning
- Often observed on masts, antennas, tall structures

## Part 4: Lightning Safety

### Probability and Safety

**Odds of being struck (in US):** ~1 in 500,000 per year

**Where lightning strikes:**
- Tallest objects (trees, buildings, antennas)
- Open areas (fields, golf courses, water)
- Metal objects (drawn to conductors)

### Protection Methods

**Lightning rod system:**
- Metal rod extending above building
- Conductor path down exterior
- Ground rod buried in earth
- Conducts lightning safely to ground
- Does NOT prevent lightning; just redirects it safely

**Safe locations:**
- Inside building (fully enclosed)
- Inside car (metal frame conducts around occupants)
- Ground level in open (avoid being tallest object)

**Unsafe locations:**
- Under trees (lightning enters tree, branches, seeks you)
- Open field (you're the tallest object)
- Near water
- In small shelters (not protected)

### If Caught in Lightning Storm

**Immediate action:**
- Get indoors or into car immediately
- Avoid windows and metal objects
- Unplug electrical equipment (can conduct lightning)
- Avoid using phone (can conduct)

**If caught outside:**
- Crouch on heels (minimizes contact with ground, doesn't expose head)
- Avoid lying flat (increases body area for current)
- Stay away from trees, water, metal
- Move away from hilltops

## Part 5: Electrical Safety

### Hazards

**Electric shock:**
- Current through body can disrupt heart rhythm
- As little as 0.1 A through chest can cause fibrillation (fatal)
- Voltage determines whether skin breaks down

**Electrical burns:**
- Heat from current damages tissue
- Internal burns worse than external

**Electrocution:**
- Severe shock causing death

### Safe Practices

**Voltage safety:**
- Under 50 V: Generally safe for skin contact
- 50–120 V: Risk of shock if wet
- Over 120 V: Danger even if dry
- Over 600 V: Extreme danger

**Resistance matters:**
- Dry skin: ~100,000 Ω (high resistance, safer)
- Wet skin: ~1,000 Ω (low resistance, more dangerous)
- Sweat, saltwater, blood: Even lower resistance

**Grounding:**
- Provides safe path for electricity
- Three-prong outlets have ground wire
- Prevents shock by providing alternate path

### Practical Safety Rules

1. Never touch electrical equipment with wet hands
2. Use grounded outlets for high-voltage equipment
3. Keep electrical devices away from water
4. Use circuit breakers (safety devices that cut power on overload)
5. Never repair electrical equipment unless qualified
6. Unplug before servicing

## Part 6: Measuring and Detecting Charge

### Electroscope

A simple device to detect and measure electric charge.

**Design:**
- Metal rod with thin foil leaves hanging from bottom
- Charged object brings charge to metal (by contact or induction)
- Foil leaves become similarly charged
- Repel each other

**Use:**
- Observe presence of charge
- Estimate magnitude (larger separation = more charge)
- Determine sign (use known charge to test attraction/repulsion)

**Making one:**
- Metal rod through insulating cork
- Thin foil leaves attached to rod bottom
- Housed in glass case to reduce air effects

### Measuring Electrical Effects

**Conductivity:**
- Materials that allow charge to move freely: Conductors (metals, salt water)
- Materials that prevent charge movement: Insulators (rubber, plastic, dry wood)
- In-between materials: Semiconductors

**Leakage:**
- Charge slowly dissipates from charged objects
- Faster in humid conditions (moisture conducts)
- Isolated objects (on glass supports) hold charge longer

## Part 7: Static Electricity Phenomena

### Attraction and Repulsion

**Charged balloon on wall:**
- Balloon negatively charged (from rubbing)
- Charges in wall paper rearrange (induction)
- Balloon attracted to wall
- Sticks until charge dissipates

**Objects sticking together:**
- Plastic wrap sticks to itself (charged on both sides)
- Clothes stick together from dryer (friction charges them)
- Paper attracted to charged rod

### Electrostatic Discharge

When charged objects touch or come very close, charge equalizes suddenly.

**Visible spark:**
- Ionizes air (creates conductive path)
- Current flows briefly
- Visible light emitted
- Can be heard (snapping sound)
- Common in dry weather

**Example:** Walking on carpet, then touching metal doorknob
- Friction charges you
- Touch causes discharge (small spark, slight shock)

### Practical Applications

**Electrostatic painting:**
- Objects and paint both charged
- Electrostatic attraction draws paint to object
- More efficient coating
- Less overspray

**Air filtration:**
- Charged dust particles attracted to opposite-charge filter
- Removes particles from air
- Used in air purifiers

## Common Mistakes

1. **Underestimating lightning danger:** Lightning is one of nature's most dangerous phenomena. Respect it.

2. **Assuming buildings protect from lightning without rods:** Modern buildings have code-required lightning protection. Older buildings may not.

3. **Running for trees in lightning storm:** Trees attract lightning and conduct it to ground. More dangerous than open.

4. **Thinking you're safe in car because rubber tires:** Rubber doesn't protect; metal frame does. Current flows around occupants.

5. **Touching person struck by lightning:** Lightning doesn't make person radioactive or dangerous to touch. Helps faster if needed.

6. **Using rubber as insulation:** Rubber works for low voltage, but high voltage ionizes it. Rubber gloves not safe for high voltage.

7. **Thinking grounding is optional:** Grounding is essential safety. Never skip it.

8. **Ignoring warning signs:** Dark clouds, hair standing on end, smell of ozone indicate lightning may be imminent.
