## Overview

Thermodynamics is the study of heat energy and how it transforms. Heat is essential for:

1. **Cooking and food preparation:** Heat denatures proteins, breaks down plant material
2. **Heating and comfort:** Fire keeps us warm
3. **Power generation:** Heat engines convert heat to mechanical power
4. **Metallurgy:** Heat treats metals and allows working with them
5. **Water treatment:** Boiling purifies water

This chapter teaches practical thermodynamics for post-collapse survival.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of energy and work (from Mechanics chapter)
- Ability to observe heat effects (temperature change, melting, boiling)
- Understanding of cause and effect

## Part 1: Understanding Heat and Temperature

### Temperature vs. Heat

**Temperature:** Measurement of average kinetic energy of molecules

- Measured in Celsius (°C) or Fahrenheit (°F)
- Higher temperature = faster molecular motion
- Thermometer measures temperature

**Heat:** Energy transfer from hotter to cooler objects

- Heat flows from hot to cold (always)
- Measured in calories (amount of heat) or joules
- Heat is energy in transit; once transferred, becomes internal energy of the object

**Example:**
- Boiling water: Temperature is 100°C; water molecules move very fast
- Heat from the fire transfers to the water
- Heat causes temperature increase

### Thermal Energy

Thermal energy is the total kinetic energy of all molecules in an object.

**Thermal energy increases with:**
- Higher temperature (faster molecular motion)
- More mass (more molecules)

**Example:**
- Cup of boiling water: High temperature (100°C) but small mass
- Bathtub of warm water: Lower temperature (40°C) but huge mass
- Bathtub has more thermal energy than the cup despite lower temperature

## Part 2: Heat Transfer

Heat moves from hot to cold by three mechanisms:

### Conduction

Heat transfer through direct contact between materials.

**Mechanism:** Vibrating molecules transfer motion to neighboring molecules.

**Good conductors:**
- Metals (copper, iron, aluminum)
- Stone

**Poor conductors (insulators):**
- Wood
- Cloth
- Air

**For survival:**
- Metal pots conduct heat from fire to food
- Insulation (cloth, wood) prevents heat loss
- Double walls with air gap improve insulation

### Convection

Heat transfer through movement of hot fluid (liquid or gas).

**Mechanism:**
- Hot fluid becomes less dense, rises
- Cool fluid sinks to replace it
- Circulation carries heat

**Example:**
- Boiling water: Hot water rises from bottom, cool water sinks from top
- Room heating: Hot air rises, spreads across ceiling, cools and falls
- Fireplace: Hot air and smoke rise up chimney, drawing fresh air in at bottom

**For survival:**
- Convection spreads heat efficiently
- Chimneys work through convection (hot gas rises)
- Arrange spaces to use natural convection for heating

### Radiation

Heat transfer through electromagnetic waves.

**Mechanism:**
- All objects emit infrared radiation
- Hotter objects emit more radiation
- Radiation travels through space (unlike conduction or convection)

**Example:**
- Sunlight heating ground (radiative heat)
- Fire warming you from a distance (infrared radiation)
- Heat loss to night sky (cold space is a sink for radiation)

**For survival:**
- Dark objects absorb more radiation than light objects
- Shiny/reflective surfaces reflect radiation
- Orientation relative to sun affects solar heat gain

## Part 3: Laws of Thermodynamics

### First Law: Energy Conservation

Energy cannot be created or destroyed; it transforms.

Total energy in = Total energy out

**Example:**
- Wood (chemical energy) burns in fire
- Chemical energy becomes heat and light
- Heat absorbed by surroundings; remainder escapes to air
- Total energy is conserved

### Second Law: Entropy Always Increases

Heat always flows from hot to cold, never spontaneously backward.

**Practical implication:** 
- No heat engine is 100% efficient
- Some heat is always wasted (sent to cold reservoir)
- Organization always tends toward disorder

**Example:**
- Ice melts in warm room (heat flows to cold ice)
- Warm water in cold room cools (heat flows to cold air)
- This always happens; never reverses

### Temperature and Molecular Motion

Higher temperature means faster molecular motion.

**Absolute zero:** -273°C; all molecular motion stops (theoretical limit)

**Thermal expansion:** Hotter materials expand because molecules move faster and push farther apart.

## Part 4: Heat Capacity and Specific Heat

### Heat Capacity

Heat capacity is the amount of heat needed to raise temperature by 1 degree.

**Heat capacity depends on:**
- Amount of material (more mass = higher capacity)
- Type of material (some materials store more heat)

**Specific heat:** Heat capacity per unit mass

- Water: High specific heat (~1 calorie per gram per °C)
- Iron: Lower specific heat (~0.1 calorie per gram per °C)

**Implication:** Water heats up slowly but holds more heat. Iron heats quickly but loses heat quickly.

### Example: Heating Water

To heat 1 kilogram (1000 grams) of water from room temperature (20°C) to boiling (100°C):

Heat needed = 1000 grams × 1 cal/g/°C × 80°C = 80,000 calories

**For survival:**
- Boiling water takes significant heat (water has high specific heat)
- Hot objects cool slowly (water-filled containers stay warm longer)
- Heavy cast iron holds heat well but takes time to warm

### Specific Heat Comparisons

Different materials have different specific heats:

- **Water:** 1.0 cal/g/°C (highest among common materials)
- **Stone/Rock:** 0.2–0.3 cal/g/°C
- **Iron:** 0.11 cal/g/°C
- **Wood:** 0.3–0.5 cal/g/°C (varies with moisture)
- **Air:** 0.24 cal/g/°C (at constant pressure)

**Implication:** Water heats slowly but stores lots of heat. Stone heats faster but stores less. Iron heats very quickly but cools quickly.

### Temperature Scales

**Celsius (°C):**
- Water freezes at 0°C
- Water boils at 100°C
- Used in science

**Fahrenheit (°F):**
- Water freezes at 32°F
- Water boils at 212°F
- Used in some countries

**Kelvin (K):**
- Absolute zero at 0 K (-273°C)
- Water freezes at 273 K
- Water boils at 373 K
- Used for thermodynamics calculations

**Conversion:**
- K = °C + 273
- °F = (°C × 9/5) + 32

## Part 5: Phase Changes

Materials change phase (solid, liquid, gas) at specific temperatures.

### Melting and Freezing

Transition between solid and liquid.

**Melting point:** Temperature at which solid becomes liquid
- Ice: 0°C
- Tallow: 40–50°C
- Iron: 1538°C

**Heat of fusion:** Energy needed to melt material without temperature change

**Example:**
- Ice at 0°C requires heat to melt into water at 0°C
- Same temperature, but phase changes

### Boiling and Condensation

Transition between liquid and gas.

**Boiling point:** Temperature at which liquid becomes gas
- Water: 100°C at sea level
- Alcohol: 78°C
- Mercury: 357°C

**Heat of vaporization:** Energy needed to boil liquid without temperature change

**Example:**
- Water at 100°C requires heat to become steam at 100°C
- Boiling requires much more energy than melting

**For survival:**
- Boiling purifies water (kills microorganisms)
- Evaporation cools (sweat evaporates, taking heat)
- Condensation releases heat (steam heating via radiators)

## Part 6: Heat Engines and Efficiency

### Heat Engines

A heat engine converts heat energy into mechanical work.

**Components:**
- Hot reservoir (fire, hot water)
- Cold reservoir (cold air, cold water)
- Working substance (air, steam, gas)

**How it works:**
1. Heat enters from hot reservoir
2. Working substance expands (pushing piston or spinning turbine)
3. Some energy exits to cold reservoir
4. Mechanical work is done

**Efficiency:** Fraction of heat converted to useful work

Efficiency = Work output / Heat input = 1 - (Cold temperature / Hot temperature)

**Example:** Carnot engine with:
- Hot reservoir at 373°C (373 Kelvin + 273)
- Cold reservoir at 0°C (273 Kelvin)

Maximum efficiency = 1 - (273/373) = 0.27 = 27%

**Important:** Even theoretically perfect engines waste heat. Real engines are less efficient (10–40%).

### Practical Heat Engines

**Steam engine:**
- Water boils, producing steam pressure
- Steam pushes piston
- Exhaust steam condenses
- Repeated: efficient conversion of heat to work

**Internal combustion engine:**
- Fuel burns inside cylinder
- Expanding gas pushes piston
- Less efficient than steam but portable

**Hot air engine:**
- Gas heats and expands (doing work)
- Gas cools and contracts
- Heat source keeps bottom hot; cooling lets top cool

**For post-collapse:** Simple steam engines using fire can power pumps, mills, and generators.

## Part 7: Furnace Design and Temperature Control

### Basic Furnace Principles

A furnace converts chemical energy (burning fuel) into heat efficiently.

**Design features:**

1. **Insulation:** Ceramic or stone walls prevent heat loss
2. **Draft:** Chimney creates upward flow (hot air rises) for continuous fuel burning
3. **Grate:** Allows air to reach fuel from below (oxygen needed for combustion)
4. **Combustion chamber:** Fuel burns here; heat concentrated
5. **Heat exchanger:** Transfers heat to water or surrounding space

**Efficiency factors:**

- **Complete combustion:** Ensure adequate oxygen (air flow)
- **Heat containment:** Insulate walls to prevent loss
- **Residence time:** Gases must stay in hot zone long enough to fully burn
- **Draft management:** Too little draft = incomplete combustion; too much draft = heat escapes

### Temperature Control

Controlling furnace temperature:

**Increase temperature:**
- Increase air flow (bellows, draft opening)
- Add more fuel
- Improve insulation

**Decrease temperature:**
- Reduce air flow
- Remove fuel from fire
- Open door/windows (increase ventilation)

**For specific temperatures:**
- 100°C (212°F): Boiling water
- 200–300°C: Blacksmith heat (metal glows red)
- 500–700°C: Rapid oxidation (metal becomes brittle)
- 800–1000°C: Metal smelting (ores reduce to metal)
- 1700°C+: Glass melting

## Part 8: Practical Thermodynamics

### Efficient Heating

To heat a space:

1. **Insulation:** Minimize heat loss (walls, ceiling, floor)
2. **Ventilation:** Bring in fresh air as needed, but minimize loss
3. **Heat source:** Concentrated heat (fireplace, stove) heats efficiently if heat is captured
4. **Thermal mass:** Heavy materials (stone, water) store heat and release slowly

### Cooking with Heat

Cooking involves heat transfer and chemical changes:

- **Conduction:** Pan on fire; pan conducts heat to food
- **Convection:** Boiling water circulates heat around food
- **Radiation:** Oven radiates heat onto food

**Efficiency:**
- Covered pot cooks faster (heat is trapped)
- Pressure cooker: Increased pressure allows higher boiling point; cooks faster
- Lower heat for longer time: More fuel but avoids burning

### Fire Management

Efficient fires require:

1. **Fuel:** Dry wood; split into appropriate sizes
2. **Air:** Good airflow (bellows increase oxygen, increase heat)
3. **Temperature:** Hot coals are more efficient than flames
4. **Insulation:** Contain heat in area to be heated

## Common Mistakes

1. **Confusing temperature and heat:** Temperature is a property; heat is energy flow. A large body of warm water has more heat than a small cup of boiling water.

2. **Expecting 100% efficient engines:** Impossible; always some heat wasted. Real engines are 20–40% efficient.

3. **Ignoring insulation:** Heat loss through walls dominates. Insulation is more important than heat source size.

4. **Wasting energy boiling:** Boiling requires huge amounts of energy. Simmer when possible.

5. **Poor fire management:** Uncontained fire wastes 90% of heat. Contain fire in furnace, stove, or fireplace.

6. **Cold air leaks:** Small openings waste disproportionate heat. Seal leaks first.

7. **Not using thermal mass:** Stone and water absorb heat and release slowly; use them.

8. **Underestimating energy for phase change:** Boiling and melting require energy even though temperature doesn't change.
