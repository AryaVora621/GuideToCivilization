## Overview

Waves are disturbances that carry energy through a medium. Sound is a common wave. Understanding waves helps you:

1. **Communicate:** Sound travels farther than expected; understanding carries helps plan signals
2. **Predict sound behavior:** Sound reflects, diffuses, and travels differently in different spaces
3. **Make music:** Vibrating strings and columns of air produce sound at specific frequencies
4. **Detect:** Animals use sound to navigate and communicate; understanding helps you observe
5. **Amplify signals:** Horns and other devices focus and direct sound

This chapter teaches practical wave and sound physics.

## Part 1: Understanding Waves

### What Is a Wave?

A wave is a disturbance that propagates through a medium, carrying energy without carrying matter.

**Properties:**
- **Wavelength (λ):** Distance between successive crests or troughs
- **Frequency (f):** Number of waves passing per second (measured in Hertz, Hz)
- **Amplitude (A):** Height of wave; measure of energy
- **Speed (v):** Distance wave travels per second

**Relationship:**
v = f × λ

Speed equals frequency times wavelength.

### Types of Waves

**Mechanical waves:** Require a medium (water, air, solid)
- Water waves
- Sound waves
- Seismic waves

**Electromagnetic waves:** Don't require a medium; travel through vacuum
- Light
- Radio waves
- Infrared

**Wave directions:**
- **Transverse:** Vibration perpendicular to wave direction (string vibration, light)
- **Longitudinal:** Vibration parallel to wave direction (sound, coil spring)

## Part 2: Sound Waves

### How Sound Works

Sound is a longitudinal wave of pressure changes in a medium.

**Generation:**
1. Vibrating object (vocal cord, drum, string) pushes medium
2. Medium compresses (high pressure) then expands (low pressure)
3. Pressure waves propagate outward
4. Ear detects pressure variations; brain interprets as sound

**Speed of sound:**
- Air (20°C): 343 m/s (1,125 ft/s)
- Water: 1,480 m/s (4,850 ft/s)
- Steel: 5,000 m/s (16,000 ft/s)

**Implication:** Sound travels 4× faster in water than air; 15× faster in steel.

### Frequency and Pitch

**Frequency:** Number of vibrations per second (Hz)

**Pitch:** Perceived highness or lowness of sound (determined by frequency)

**Human hearing:** 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz

**Examples:**
- Low frequency (20–250 Hz): Deep bass (drums, thunder)
- Mid frequency (250–2,000 Hz): Speech, most sounds
- High frequency (2,000–20,000 Hz): Whistles, birds, ultrasonic

**Ultrasound:** Above 20,000 Hz (inaudible to humans; used by bats, dolphins)

**Infrasound:** Below 20 Hz (felt more than heard; earthquakes, large animals)

### Intensity and Loudness

**Intensity:** Power per unit area (energy of wave)

**Loudness:** Perceived volume (depends on frequency and intensity)

**Decibels (dB):** Logarithmic scale of intensity

- 0 dB: Threshold of hearing
- 60 dB: Normal conversation
- 100 dB: Jackhammer, very loud
- 120 dB: Pain threshold
- Above 120 dB: Hearing damage

**Implication:** Every 10 dB increase represents 10× more intensity.

## Part 3: Wave Behavior

### Reflection

Waves bounce off surfaces.

**Hard surfaces (rock, concrete, metal):** High reflection (echo)
**Soft surfaces (cloth, foam, dirt):** Low reflection (absorb sound)

**Practical applications:**
- Parabolic reflectors focus sound (amplify weak signals)
- Canyons create echoes (sound reflects between walls)
- Room shape affects acoustics (hard surfaces = reverberant; soft = dead)

### Refraction

Waves bend when passing between different media.

**Caused by:** Speed change as wave enters new medium

**Example:** Sound bends at water–air interface; warm–cold air boundary

**For survival:** Sound doesn't travel straight in layered media; bends based on temperature gradients.

### Diffraction

Waves bend around obstacles.

**Occurs when:** Obstacle size is comparable to wavelength

**Long wavelengths:** Diffract more (low frequencies bend around obstacles)
**Short wavelengths:** Diffract less (high frequencies reflect)

**Implication:** Low-frequency sounds (thunder) propagate farther and bend around obstacles; high-frequency sounds are blocked.

### Interference

Waves can combine constructively or destructively.

**Constructive:** Waves in phase (peaks align); amplitude increases
**Destructive:** Waves out of phase (peaks cancel); amplitude decreases

**For survival:** Sound can reinforce or cancel depending on path differences.

### Doppler Effect

When a sound source moves, the perceived frequency changes.

**Moving toward observer:**
- Sound waves compressed
- Wavelength decreases
- Frequency increases (higher pitch)
- Example: Ambulance siren gets higher as it approaches

**Moving away from observer:**
- Sound waves stretched
- Wavelength increases
- Frequency decreases (lower pitch)
- Example: Ambulance siren gets lower as it moves away

**Mathematical relationship:**
Observed frequency = Source frequency × (Speed of sound ± Observer speed) / (Speed of sound ± Source speed)

**For survival:** Doppler shift tells you whether something is approaching or receding. Animals use this for hunting.

### Standing Waves

When waves reflect and interfere, standing wave patterns form.

**Nodes:** Points of no motion (destructive interference)
**Antinodes:** Points of maximum motion (constructive interference)

**Resonant frequencies:** Only specific frequencies form stable standing waves

**Example:** Vibrating string
- Fundamental (first harmonic): One antinode at center; two nodes at ends
- Second harmonic: Two antinodes; three nodes
- Frequency increases with number of segments

**For musical instruments:** Only certain frequencies resonate well; others cancel.

## Part 4: Resonance

Resonance occurs when an object vibrates at its natural frequency.

**Natural frequency:** Frequency at which object prefers to vibrate

**Resonance condition:** External vibration matches natural frequency

**Effect:** Amplitude increases dramatically

**Examples:**
- Wine glass resonates at specific frequency; loud sound shatters it
- Pendulum swings high if pushed at right frequency
- Bridge resonates and may collapse if marching troops stay in step
- Musical instruments resonate at fundamental frequencies

**For musical instruments:**
- String frequency depends on tension, length, mass
- Air column frequency depends on length, open/closed ends
- Resonance amplifies sound; without it, instruments are quiet

## Part 5: Sound Propagation in Different Conditions

### In Different Media

**Air:** Sound travels 343 m/s
- Travels farther at low frequencies
- Refracted by temperature gradients

**Water:** Sound travels 1,480 m/s
- Reflects strongly at water–air interface
- Can travel great distances (marine animal communication)

**Solid:** Sound travels 5,000+ m/s
- Travels far with little loss
- Reflected strongly at boundaries

**In vacuums:** Sound cannot travel (no medium to vibrate)

### Environmental Effects

**Temperature inversion:** Warm air below, cold air above
- Sound refracts downward; carries farther
- Common in mornings and over water

**Wind:** Affects sound propagation
- Wind speeds enhance sound in direction of wind
- Against wind, sound is muffled

**Obstacles:** Trees, buildings block sound
- Low frequencies diffract around; penetrate better
- High frequencies are blocked

## Part 6: Practical Applications

### Communication Devices

**Horn:** Parabolic shape focuses sound
- Concentrates sound energy in one direction
- Megaphone amplifies voice

**Resonant chamber:** Enclosed space amplifies specific frequencies
- Violin body resonates and amplifies string vibrations
- Bottle becomes louder when blown across top (air column resonates)

### Musical Instruments

**String instruments:** Vibrating string produces sound
- Frequency = (1/2L) × √(T/μ)
- L: string length; T: tension; μ: mass per length
- Longer or looser string: lower frequency

**Wind instruments:** Vibrating air column produces sound
- Frequency depends on tube length and whether ends are open/closed
- Open tube: frequency = v / (2L)
- Closed tube: frequency = v / (4L)

### Acoustic Design

For a space to have good acoustics:
- Mix reflective surfaces (walls, ceiling) with absorptive (carpets, curtains)
- Minimize echoes (parallel walls reflect sound back-and-forth)
- Match reverberation to purpose (concert hall needs longer decay than speech room)

## Part 7: Practical Sound Applications

### Using Sound for Navigation

**Echolocation in caves or dark spaces:**
1. Make sound (whistle, snap fingers, clap)
2. Listen for echoes
3. Time delay indicates distance: Distance = (Speed of sound × Time) / 2
   - Divide by 2 because sound travels to object and back
4. Direction of echo indicates where object is

**Example:** In air, sound travels 343 m/s
- Echo returns after 0.2 seconds
- Distance = (343 × 0.2) / 2 = 34.3 meters away

**For survival:** Echolocation works best in enclosed spaces where echoes are strong.

### Using Sound for Communication

**Shouting across distance:**
- Voice carries ~100 meters in quiet conditions
- Sound decreases as inverse square of distance (doubling distance reduces intensity to 1/4)
- Low frequencies carry farther than high frequencies

**Signal horns/whistles:**
- Directional; focuses sound in one direction
- Multiple short blasts (SOS) more recognizable than long shouts
- Whistles penetrate wind better than voice

**Drums and percussion:**
- Low-frequency drums carry far (travel around obstacles)
- Patterns can convey meaning (rhythm-based communication)

### Identifying Animals by Sound

**Sound properties reveal animal type:**
- **Low frequency (20–100 Hz):** Large animals (elephant, whale, rhino)
- **Mid frequency (100–1000 Hz):** Medium animals (dog, human, horse)
- **High frequency (1000+ Hz):** Small animals (bird, insect)

**Sound pattern indicates:**
- **Repetitive pulses:** Bird song or insect calls
- **Howling/roaring:** Large predator
- **Silence followed by sound:** Cautious approach

**For survival:** Listening tells you what animals are nearby and their behavior.

## Part 8: Detecting and Using Sound

### Animal Communication

Many animals use sound:
- **Whales:** Low-frequency sound travels vast distances underwater
- **Bats:** Ultrasound echoes off insects; used for navigation
- **Elephants:** Infrasound communicates over long distances
- **Birds:** Complex songs indicate territory, mating

**For survival:** Listening to animal sounds tells you if area is safe/occupied.

### Echolocation

Sound reflects off objects; using echoes to sense objects.

**How it works:**
1. Emit sound
2. Listen to reflections
3. Judge distance from delay (farther = longer delay)
4. Judge direction from which echo comes

**For survival:** Bats and dolphins navigate this way; humans use crude echolocation too.

### Making Instruments

**Simple drum:**
- Membrane stretched tight over hollow chamber
- Tapping causes vibration
- Chamber resonates, amplifying sound
- Tighter membrane = higher pitch

**Simple flute:**
- Hollow tube with holes
- Blowing creates vibrating air column
- Covering holes changes effective length
- Longer tube = lower pitch

**String instrument:**
- String vibrates between fixed points
- Plucking or bowing excites vibration
- Tighter string = higher pitch
- Longer string = lower pitch
- Resonant body amplifies sound

## Common Mistakes

1. **Confusing frequency and amplitude:** Frequency determines pitch; amplitude determines loudness. Independent.

2. **Expecting same speed everywhere:** Sound travels different speeds in different media. Always specify medium.

3. **Thinking sound travels in straight lines:** Sound refracts and diffracts; path depends on frequency and environment.

4. **Ignoring refraction and reflection:** These dominate sound behavior; reflective surfaces and temperature gradients change propagation.

5. **Underestimating low-frequency penetration:** Low frequencies diffract around obstacles and travel far; they're harder to block.

6. **Over-estimating human hearing range:** Most people hear up to 10–15 kHz; upper limit decreases with age.

7. **Not accounting for resonance:** Objects vibrate loudly at resonant frequencies; quiet at others.
