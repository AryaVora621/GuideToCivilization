## Overview

Magnetism is a fundamental force created by moving electrical charges. Understanding magnetism helps you:

1. **Generate electricity:** Moving magnet through coil creates electrical current (generator)
2. **Create motors:** Electrical current in magnetic field creates motion
3. **Navigate:** Earth's magnetic field provides direction (compass)
4. **Transform power:** Transformers use magnetism to change voltage
5. **Detect metals:** Magnetism can detect hidden metals

This chapter teaches practical magnetism physics.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of electric charge and electric fields
- Understanding of forces and motion
- Ability to observe magnetic phenomena
- Basic understanding of electricity

## Part 1: Understanding Magnetism

### Magnetic Poles

Every magnet has two poles:

**North pole:** Points toward Earth's north (actually magnetic south pole of Earth)
**South pole:** Points toward Earth's south (actually magnetic north pole of Earth)

**Note:** Geographic poles and magnetic poles don't align; difference is called declination (varies by location).

### Magnetic Force

Magnets exert force on each other and on magnetic materials.

**Like poles repel:** North-North or South-South push apart
**Opposite poles attract:** North-South attract

**Magnetic field:** Surrounds magnet; exerts force on other magnets and magnetic materials

**Field pattern:**
- Lines go from North pole to South pole (outside magnet)
- Closer lines indicate stronger field
- Field strongest at poles

### Magnetic Materials

**Ferromagnetic materials:** Strongly magnetic
- Iron
- Cobalt
- Nickel
- Alloys containing these

**Paramagnetic materials:** Weakly magnetic
- Aluminum
- Platinum

**Diamagnetic materials:** Repelled by magnets
- Copper
- Bismuth
- Superconductors

**Non-magnetic materials:**
- Most materials (plastic, wood, glass)

## Part 2: Electromagnetism

### Electric Current Creates Magnetism

Moving electric charges (current) create a magnetic field.

**Right-hand rule:**
- Point thumb in direction of current
- Fingers curl in direction of magnetic field
- Used to determine field direction around wire

### Electromagnet

Coil of wire carrying current creates strong magnetic field.

**Components:**
- Wire coil (many turns)
- Electrical current
- Iron core (optional but strengthens field)

**Field strength depends on:**
- Current (more current = stronger field)
- Number of turns (more turns = stronger field)
- Core material (iron core strengthens significantly)

**Equation:** Field strength ∝ Current × Number of turns

**On/off switch:** Current creates field; stop current, field disappears (unlike permanent magnet).

**Making electromagnet:**
1. Wind insulated wire around iron rod or similar core (100+ turns)
2. Connect to battery
3. Current flows through coil
4. Creates strong magnetic field
5. Disconnect to turn off

**Strength characteristics:**
- Can be much stronger than permanent magnet
- Strength adjustable by varying current
- Used in relays, switches, door locks

**For survival:** Electromagnets can be made from wire and battery; useful for creating temporary magnetic fields.

### Permanent vs. Electromagnets

**Permanent magnet:**
- Fixed field strength
- Always on (can't be switched)
- Cannot be strengthened without replacing
- Simpler, more reliable

**Electromagnet:**
- Adjustable field strength
- Can switch on/off
- More complex (requires power)
- More powerful for given size
- Useful for variable applications

**Trade-offs:**
- Permanent: Simpler but less flexible
- Electromagnetic: Complex but more control

## Part 3: Electromagnetic Induction

Moving a magnet through a coil creates electrical current.

**Faraday's Law:** Changing magnetic flux through coil induces EMF (voltage)

EMF = -N × dΦ/dt

- N: Number of turns
- dΦ/dt: Rate of change of magnetic flux
- Larger change = larger voltage
- Faster change = larger voltage

### Generator

A coil rotating in magnetic field generates electricity.

**Components:**
- Coil of wire
- Strong magnet or electromagnet
- Shaft to rotate coil
- Brushes to collect current

**How it works:**
1. Rotating coil cuts through magnetic field lines
2. Magnetic flux through coil changes
3. This induces voltage (Faraday's law)
4. Brushes collect current from rotating coil

**Output:**
- Alternating current (AC): Current reverses direction twice per rotation
- Can be converted to direct current (DC) with rectifier

**Power output:** P = F × v (Force times velocity)
- More force (stronger magnet) = more power
- Faster rotation = more power

### Transformer

Transformer changes voltage using magnetic coupling.

**Components:**
- Primary coil (connected to AC source)
- Secondary coil (provides output)
- Iron core connecting coils

**How it works:**
1. AC current in primary coil creates changing magnetic field
2. Field passes through iron core to secondary coil
3. Changing field induces voltage in secondary
4. Voltage ratio depends on turn ratio

**Equation:** V_secondary / V_primary = N_secondary / N_primary

**Example:** 
- Primary: 100 turns, 120 V input
- Secondary: 10 turns
- Output: 120 × (10/100) = 12 V

**For survival:** Transformers allow stepping down voltage for safer, lower-voltage applications.

## Part 4: Magnetic Field Strength and Calculations

### Measuring Magnetic Field

**Units:**
- Tesla (T): SI unit of magnetic field strength
- Gauss (G): CGS unit (1 Tesla = 10,000 Gauss)

**Typical values:**
- Earth's magnetic field: ~50 microtesla (0.5 Gauss)
- Refrigerator magnet: ~5 millitesla (50 Gauss)
- MRI machine: ~1–3 Tesla
- Strongest laboratory magnets: 45+ Tesla

### Magnetic Force on Current

Current-carrying wire in magnetic field experiences force (Lorentz force).

**Equation:** F = I × L × B × sin(θ)

- I: Current in amps
- L: Length of wire in field
- B: Magnetic field strength (Tesla)
- θ: Angle between wire and field

**Maximum force:** When wire perpendicular to field (sin 90° = 1)
**No force:** When wire parallel to field (sin 0° = 0)

**For survival:** Understanding force allows designing stronger motors and predicting behavior.

### Magnetic Field from Wire

Magnetic field around current-carrying wire circles the wire.

**Field strength:** B = (μ₀ × I) / (2π × r)

- μ₀: Permeability constant
- I: Current
- r: Distance from wire
- Field decreases with distance (inverse relationship)

**Doubling current:** Doubles field strength
**Doubling distance:** Reduces field to 1/4

## Part 5: Electric Motors

Motor converts electrical energy to mechanical motion.

**Components:**
- Coil of wire
- Commutator (split ring)
- Brushes (conduct current to commutator)
- Magnetic field (permanent magnet or electromagnet)
- Bearings to allow rotation

### How Motor Works

1. Current through coil in magnetic field
2. Magnetic field exerts force on current-carrying coil (Lorentz force)
3. Force causes coil to rotate
4. Commutator reverses current direction
5. Reversal keeps force in same rotational direction
6. Coil continues rotating

**Direction rule:**
- Right hand: Fingers point in direction of current, curl in direction of field
- Thumb points in direction of force

**Power:** P = V × I (Voltage times current)
- Higher voltage = more power
- Higher current = more power

**For survival:** Simple motors can be made from wire coil, magnets, and brushes; useful for mechanical power from electrical source.

## Part 5: Practical Magnetic Applications

### Metal Detection

Magnetism can detect hidden metals.

**Principle:**
- Metal object distorts magnetic field
- Distortion detected by sensor
- Indicates presence and approximate location

**Methods:**
- Search coil sweeps area
- Eddy currents induced in metal
- These create secondary magnetic field
- Detected by receiver coil

**Limitations:**
- Works best on ferrous metals (iron, steel)
- Non-ferrous metals (copper, gold) harder to detect
- Depth limited (typically 1–2 feet)

**For survival:** Metal detectors locate abandoned tools, buried metal objects.

### Magnetic Storage

Information can be stored magnetically.

**Mechanism:**
- Small magnetic domains aligned one direction = 1
- Aligned opposite direction = 0
- Binary code stored in magnetic patterns

**Examples:**
- Hard drive magnetic platters
- Magnetic tape
- Credit card strips
- Compass needle position

**For post-collapse:** Magnetic storage can preserve information if devices available to read it.

### Magnetic Shielding

Some materials block magnetic fields.

**Principles:**
- Ferromagnetic materials (iron, mu-metal) redirect field
- Create cage that blocks external field
- Field lines prefer path through shielding material

**Applications:**
- Protecting sensitive electronics
- Containing magnetic fields (electromagnet coils)
- Radiation shields in medical devices

**For survival:** Shield electronics with metal box if electromagnetic interference present.

## Part 6: Earth's Magnetism and Navigation

### Earth's Magnetic Field

Earth acts like a giant magnet:

**Magnetic poles:**
- North magnetic pole: ~82°N, 110°W (near Canadian Arctic)
- South magnetic pole: ~74°S, 111°E (near Antarctic)

**Field strength:**
- Stronger at poles (~60 microtesla)
- Weaker at equator (~30 microtesla)

**Field origin:** Molten iron core rotating, creating electrical currents that generate field.

**Changes over time:**
- Poles gradually move
- Field strength fluctuates
- Reversal happens every few hundred thousand years (rare)

### Magnetic Declination

Difference between true north and magnetic north.

**Varies by location:** East or West of true north
**Example:**
- In New York: ~12° West (magnetic north is 12° west of true north)
- In California: ~10° East
- Changes yearly (~0.1° per year typically)

**For navigation:** Compass points to magnetic north, not true north. Adjust maps accordingly.

### Using a Compass

**How compass works:**
- Magnetized needle aligns with Earth's magnetic field
- North end of needle points to magnetic north

**Using to navigate:**
1. Determine magnetic declination for location
2. Take bearing with compass (angle from north)
3. Hold compass level
4. Rotate so north end aligns with red line on compass housing
5. Follow direction of direction-of-travel arrow
6. Periodically recheck bearing (magnetic field can vary locally)

**Errors and corrections:**
- Local magnetic anomalies (iron deposits, metal structures) cause deviation
- Metal objects interfere (keep compass away from tools, weapons)
- Tilt affects accuracy (keep level and away from body)
- Fluctuations near electrical power lines
- Solar activity can temporarily affect Earth's field

**Finding true north from magnetic compass:**
1. Take magnetic bearing
2. Add declination angle (if east) or subtract (if west)
3. Result is true north bearing

**Backup navigation:** If compass unavailable, use sun, stars, landmarks for direction.

### Magnetic Anomalies

Some locations have unusual magnetic fields.

**Sources:**
- Large iron ore deposits
- Magnetite (iron oxide mineral) concentrations
- Human-made structures (electrical power lines, metal buildings)

**Effects:**
- Compass points to local anomaly instead of true magnetic north
- Can be dramatic (30+ degree error)
- Requires local knowledge to correct

**Notable anomalies:**
- Parts of Canada, Australia, and Russia
- Underwater volcanic regions
- Some mountain regions

**For navigation:** Understand local anomalies in area of travel; consult locals about compass reliability.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Confusing magnetic and geographic poles:** They don't align. Use declination to correct.

2. **Assuming electromagnet works without current:** Requires continuous current. Stop current, field vanishes.

3. **Wrong-hand rule:** Right-hand rule is standard. Using left hand gives wrong direction.

4. **Forgetting transformer needs AC:** Transformer requires changing field. DC won't work.

5. **Thinking stronger magnet always better:** Stronger magnet needs stronger support structure. Overdesign can be waste.

6. **Ignoring magnetic materials near compass:** Any ferrous metal interferes with compass. Keep clear.

7. **Not accounting for changing declination:** Declination changes yearly. Update navigational calculations.

8. **Assuming permanent magnet is forever:** Magnets gradually weaken over time and if heated above Curie temperature.
