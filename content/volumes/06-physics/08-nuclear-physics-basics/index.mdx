## Overview

Nuclear physics studies the nucleus—the dense core of atoms. Understanding nuclear physics helps you:

1. **Understand energy:** Nuclear reactions release enormous energy
2. **Detect radiation:** Understand how radiation detectors work
3. **Protect from radiation:** Know hazards and safety procedures
4. **Generate power:** Nuclear energy is a potent energy source
5. **Medical applications:** Radiation is used in medicine and diagnostics

This chapter teaches practical nuclear physics.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of atoms and electrons
- Understanding of energy
- Understanding of waves (for radiation properties)
- Awareness of hazards

## Materials & Tools

**For observation:**

## Part 1: The Nucleus

### Nuclear Structure

The nucleus contains protons and neutrons (collectively called nucleons).

**Protons:** Positively charged
- Number of protons defines the element
- Hydrogen: 1 proton
- Helium: 2 protons
- Iron: 26 protons
- Uranium: 92 protons

**Neutrons:** Neutral (no charge)
- Number varies; determines isotope
- Generally equal to or greater than protons
- Heavier nuclei have more neutrons than protons

**Nuclear stability:** Depends on proton-neutron ratio
- Light nuclei: Stable at ~1:1 ratio
- Heavy nuclei: Need more neutrons for stability (up to 1.5:1)
- Too many or too few neutrons: Radioactive

### Isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of same element with different neutron numbers.

**Examples:**
- Hydrogen-1: 1 proton, 0 neutrons (stable)
- Deuterium: 1 proton, 1 neutron (stable)
- Tritium: 1 proton, 2 neutrons (radioactive)

**Properties:**
- Same chemical properties (same number of electrons)
- Different mass and nuclear properties
- Different stability and half-lives
- Separated by mass spectrometry, not chemical means

**Notation:**
- Element symbol with mass number: Carbon-14, U-235, Th-232
- Mass number = protons + neutrons

### Nuclear Forces

Nucleus held together by nuclear force (strong force).

**Properties:**
- Extremely strong at short range (femtometer scale)
- Acts only between nucleons (doesn't extend beyond nucleus)
- Repels at very short range (prevents collapse)
- Weakens with distance faster than electromagnetic force

**Result:** Nuclei are stable but contain enormous energy.

### Mass-Energy Equivalence

E = mc²

- Small mass converts to enormous energy
- 1 kg of matter = 90 trillion joules
- Nuclear reactions convert tiny mass to energy
- Chemical reactions release only a tiny fraction by comparison

## Part 2: Radioactivity

Unstable nuclei decay into more stable configurations.

### Types of Decay

**Alpha decay (α):**
- Nucleus emits alpha particle (helium nucleus: 2 protons, 2 neutrons)
- Result: Atomic number decreases by 2, mass number decreases by 4
- Example: Uranium-238 → Thorium-234 + Alpha
- Danger: Low (stopped by paper or skin)

**Beta decay (β⁻):**
- Neutron converts to proton; emits electron (beta particle) and antineutrino
- Result: Atomic number increases by 1, mass number stays same
- Example: Carbon-14 → Nitrogen-14 + Beta
- Danger: Moderate (stopped by aluminum)

**Gamma decay (γ):**
- Nucleus emits high-energy photon
- No change in protons or neutrons
- Result: Nucleus releases energy; atomic number and mass unchanged
- Danger: High (requires lead or thick shielding)

**Beta decay (β⁺):**
- Proton converts to neutron; emits positron (positive electron)
- Result: Atomic number decreases by 1
- Less common than beta-minus

### Half-Life

Half-life is time for half of radioactive sample to decay.

**Decay equation:** N(t) = N₀ × (1/2)^(t/t_half)

**Examples:**
- Carbon-14: 5,730 years (used for dating)
- Uranium-238: 4.5 billion years
- Radium-226: 1,600 years
- Iodine-131: 8 days
- Technetium-99m: 6 hours (medical imaging)

**Implication:** After one half-life, 50% remains; after two, 25%; after three, 12.5%, etc.

## Part 3: Radiation and Hazards

### Types of Radiation

**Ionizing radiation:**
- Alpha, beta, gamma, X-rays: Sufficient energy to ionize atoms
- Damage DNA and living cells
- Cause cancer, radiation sickness, death at high doses

**Non-ionizing radiation:**
- Radio waves, microwaves, visible light, infrared
- Don't ionize; only excite electrons
- Generally safe at normal levels

### Biological Effects

**Acute exposure (high dose, short time):**
- Above 1 Gray (Gy): Radiation sickness
- Above 6 Gy: Fatal
- Symptoms: Nausea, vomiting, hair loss, bleeding, organ failure

**Chronic exposure (low dose, long time):**
- Increases cancer risk
- Genetic damage possible
- Effects accumulate over lifetime

**Safe exposure levels:**
- Background radiation: ~2-3 mSv/year (unavoidable; exists naturally)
- Occupational limit: ~20 mSv/year (regulatory maximum for workers)
- Medical imaging: 1-10 mSv per scan (benefit outweighs risk)
- Emergency exposure: ~50-100 mSv (temporary; expected to recover)
- Lethal exposure: ~6+ Gray (Gy) or ~6000 mSv (99% mortality)

### Radiation Protection

**Time:** Minimize exposure duration
- Shorter contact = less dose
- Double duration = double dose

**Distance:** Increase distance from source
- Inverse square law: Double distance = 1/4 dose
- Most effective protection method

**Shielding:** Block radiation
- Alpha: Paper, skin
- Beta: Aluminum sheet
- Gamma: Lead (1 cm lead blocks ~90%)
- Neutrons: Boron, cadmium

### Radioactive Series

Unstable nucleus decays through series of steps until reaching stable isotope.

**Uranium-238 series:**
- Uranium-238 → ... → Radium-226 → ... → Lead-206 (stable)
- Takes billions of years
- Each decay step releases energy

**Carbon-14 dating:**
- Living organisms maintain constant C-14 ratio (eating/breathing replenishes)
- Dead organisms stop replenishing
- C-14 decays with known half-life (5,730 years)
- Measuring remaining C-14 indicates age
- Accurate up to ~50,000 years

## Part 4: Nuclear Reactions

### Fission

Heavy nucleus splits into lighter nuclei, releasing energy.

**Spontaneous fission:** Heavy nuclei (uranium, plutonium) occasionally split
**Induced fission:** Neutron strikes nucleus, causing split

**Chain reaction:** Splitting releases neutrons that hit other nuclei
- Uncontrolled: Nuclear explosion
- Controlled: Nuclear reactor

**Energy release:** Each fission releases ~200 MeV (equivalent to TNT detonation)

**For post-collapse:** Nuclear reactors can generate power if maintained, but decommissioning and waste management are critical.

### Fusion

Light nuclei combine into heavier nucleus, releasing energy.

**Mechanism:**
- Extreme temperature and pressure force nuclei together
- Strong force overcomes electromagnetic repulsion
- Resulting nucleus has less mass (converted to energy)

**Examples:**
- Hydrogen-1 + Hydrogen-1 → Deuterium + energy
- Deuterium + Tritium → Helium-4 + Neutron + energy

**Conditions required:**
- Temperature: Millions of degrees
- Pressure: Extreme (confine nuclei)
- Example: Sun's core: 15 million Kelvin

**Advantages:** Cleaner than fission; fuel abundant (hydrogen)
**Disadvantage:** Very difficult to control (requires extreme conditions)

---

### Nuclear Reactor Operation

Commercial nuclear reactors use controlled fission for power generation.

**Components:**
1. **Fuel:** Enriched uranium (3-5% U-235)
2. **Moderator:** Slows neutrons (water, graphite)
3. **Control rods:** Absorb neutrons, control reaction rate
4. **Coolant:** Carries heat from core to steam generator
5. **Containment:** Prevents radiation release

**Power generation:**
- Fission generates heat
- Heat boils water to steam
- Steam drives turbines
- Turbines generate electricity

**Safety considerations:**
- Heat must be continuously removed
- Loss of coolant is most dangerous scenario
- Backup power essential for cooling
- Shutdown procedures reliable

**For post-collapse:**
- Existing reactors require continuous maintenance
- Lack of fuel supply becomes critical constraint
- Waste disposal is long-term problem (thousands of years)

## Part 5: Detecting Radiation

### Detection Methods

**Geiger-Müller counter:**
- Ionization in gas chamber produces electrical pulse
- Counts individual radiation events
- Simple, portable, inexpensive

**Scintillation detector:**
- Radiation excites atoms in crystal
- Atoms emit light
- Photomultiplier detects light
- More sensitive than GM tube

**Ionization chamber:**
- Measures electrical current from ionized gas
- Indicates dose rate (not just counts)
- Used in dosimeters and area monitors

**Dosimeter:**
- Personal radiation meter worn by worker
- Indicates total exposure accumulated
- Badge darkens with exposure (older style)
- Electronic dosimeters give real-time readout

**Choosing detectors:**
- GM tube: Counts events, sensitive, portable
- Scintillation: More sensitive, faster response
- Ionization: Measures dose rate directly

### Radiation Measurement Units

**Becquerel (Bq):** Number of decays per second
**Curie (Ci):** Older unit; 1 Ci = 37 billion Bq

**Gray (Gy):** Absorbed dose (joules per kg)
**Sievert (Sv):** Biological effect (accounts for radiation type)
- 1 Gy of alpha = ~20 Sv
- 1 Gy of gamma = ~1 Sv

### Background Radiation

Natural radiation from environment; always present.

**Sources:**
- Cosmic rays: From space (stronger at altitude)
- Terrestrial: From radioactive minerals in rocks and soil
- Radon: Gas from rock decay (largest contributor in many locations)
- Food: Potassium-40, Carbon-14 in all living things
- Medical: X-rays, CT scans, nuclear medicine

**Typical annual dose:** 2-3 mSv (varies by location)
- Radon-rich areas: Up to 10+ mSv
- High altitude: More cosmic rays
- Granite regions: More terrestrial radiation

**Implication:** Some radiation is inescapable; focus on avoiding additional exposure.

### Radioactive Isotopes in Use

**Uranium-235:**
- 0.7% of natural uranium
- Fissile (sustains chain reaction)
- Enriched to 3-5% for reactor fuel
- Enriched above 90% for weapons

**Plutonium-239:**
- Created by neutron bombardment in reactors
- Highly fissile
- Used in weapons and some reactor fuel
- Extremely toxic

**Cobalt-60:**
- Used in medical sterilization
- Used in cancer radiotherapy
- Half-life: 5.3 years

**Iodine-131:**
- Produced in reactor fission
- Collects in thyroid
- Health hazard in nuclear accidents
- Half-life: 8 days

**Strontium-90:**
- Produced in reactor fission
- Similar to calcium; collects in bones
- Long half-life: 29 years
- Health hazard if ingested

## Practical Nuclear Considerations for Post-Collapse

### Reactor Safety

If existing nuclear reactors are nearby:

1. **Understand cooling requirements:** Reactors need continuous cooling even after shutdown
2. **Know evacuation zones:** Be aware of evacuation radius
3. **Monitor for accidents:** Understand warning signs (sirens, alarms)
4. **Prepare shelter:** Know nearest shelter suitable for fallout

### Radiation Safety in Practice

**If exposed to radiation:**
1. Leave area immediately (if possible; time matters)
2. Decontaminate: Remove outer clothes, shower thoroughly with clean water
3. Seek medical attention from trained personnel
4. Monitor exposure with dosimeter if available
5. Report exposure to authorities for tracking

**Long-term strategies:**
- Store potassium iodide tablets (blocks radioactive iodine uptake)
- Know location of shelter suitable for fallout
- Understand which foods are safe if grown near radiation source

## Common Mistakes

1. **Confusing radioactivity and radiation:** Radioactivity is nuclear decay; radiation is the emitted particles/waves.

2. **Thinking all radiation is dangerous:** Background radiation is unavoidable. Some risk is natural.

3. **Overestimating half-life:** After 10 half-lives, ~0.1% remains (very little). Material decays away.

4. **Ignoring inverse square law:** Distance is most effective protection. Doubling distance reduces dose to 1/4.

5. **Underestimating shielding effectiveness:** Lead blocks gamma well; concrete blocks neutrons better.

6. **Assuming lead is complete shield:** Gamma can penetrate thick lead; neutrons require hydrogen-rich materials.

7. **Not using dosimeters:** Can't feel radiation; must measure to know exposure.

8. **Forgetting time matters:** Quick exposure to source less harmful than prolonged.

9. **Confusing activities:** Becquerels measure decay rate, not biological effect (that's Sieverts).

10. **Ignoring background radiation:** Some radiation is natural and unavoidable; focus on minimizing excess exposure beyond background.
