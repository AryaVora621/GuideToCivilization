## Overview

Plants contain thousands of bioactive compounds. Many are effective for treating illness, pain, and infection.

This chapter lists 50 medicinal plants organized by primary use (pain, infection, digestion, etc.). For each plant: identification, active compounds, how to prepare and use, typical dose, and warnings.

## Prerequisites

- Ability to identify plants by sight
- Access to plants (growing, foraged, or cultivated)
- Basic chemistry knowledge
- Skill in making tinctures, teas, and poultices

## Materials & Tools

- Plant materials (fresh or dried)
- Alcohol (for tinctures)
- Water (for teas and decoctions)
- Containers (glass jars, bottles)
- Strainers and gauze
- Scale for measuring doses
- Pestle and mortar for grinding

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### The 50 Essential Medicinal Plants

**Format for each plant:**
- **Name & Family**
- **Identification:** Key features to recognize the plant
- **Active compounds:** Chemical constituents responsible for medicinal effects
- **Primary uses:** What condition it treats
- **Preparation:** How to make medicine from the plant
- **Dose:** How much to use and how often
- **Contraindications:** Who should NOT use it

**PAIN & INFLAMMATION**

**1. Willow (Salix spp.) — Salicaceae**
- **Identification:** Trees with long, narrow leaves; found near water
- **Active compounds:** Salicin (converted to salicylic acid, like aspirin)
- **Uses:** Pain, fever, inflammation
- **Preparation:** Decoction of bark (simmer 10 minutes)
- **Dose:** 1 teaspoon dried bark per cup, three times daily
- **Contraindications:** Allergy to aspirin; pregnancy; bleeding disorders

**2. Meadowsweet (Filipendula ulmaria) — Rosaceae**
- **Identification:** Tall perennial with white or pink clustered flowers; grows in damp areas
- **Active compounds:** Salicylates, flavonoids
- **Uses:** Pain, fever, stomach upset
- **Preparation:** Tea from dried flowers or leaves
- **Dose:** 1-2 teaspoons per cup, three times daily
- **Contraindications:** Aspirin allergy; pregnancy

**3. Ginger (Zingiber officinale) — Zingiberaceae**
- **Identification:** Rhizome (underground stem) with aromatic, pungent taste; grows in tropical climates
- **Active compounds:** Gingerols, shogaols
- **Uses:** Pain, nausea, inflammation
- **Preparation:** Fresh or dried; tea, added to food, or powder
- **Dose:** 0.5-1 gram (dried) three times daily; or 1-2 cm fresh rhizome per cup of tea
- **Contraindications:** Bleeding disorders; pregnancy (high doses)

**INFECTION & IMMUNE SUPPORT**

**4. Garlic (Allium sativum) — Alliaceae**
- **Identification:** Bulb with white or purple cloves; strong pungent smell
- **Active compounds:** Allicin (when cloves are crushed), sulfur compounds
- **Uses:** Bacterial and fungal infection; immune support
- **Preparation:** Raw (crushed), cooked, or fermented
- **Dose:** 1-3 cloves daily; or 0.5-1 gram dried powder daily
- **Contraindications:** Bleeding disorders; pregnancy (high doses); allergy

**5. Echinacea (Echinacea spp.) — Asteraceae**
- **Identification:** Purple/pink daisy-like flowers with spiky orange center; perennial herb
- **Active compounds:** Polysaccharides, alkamides, phenolic acids
- **Uses:** Cold and flu prevention and treatment; immune support
- **Preparation:** Tea from dried root or flowers; tincture (alcohol extract)
- **Dose:** 0.5-1 teaspoon per cup of tea, three times daily during cold season
- **Contraindications:** Autoimmune diseases (may worsen); allergy to ragweed family

**6. Turmeric (Curcuma longa) — Zingiberaceae**
- **Identification:** Rhizome like ginger but yellow/orange inside; grows in tropical climates
- **Active compounds:** Curcumin, other polyphenols
- **Uses:** Inflammation, infection, wound healing
- **Preparation:** Powder (from dried rhizome) added to food or tea
- **Dose:** 0.5-1 gram daily; combine with black pepper (increases absorption)
- **Contraindications:** Gallstones; bleeding disorders; pregnancy (medicinal doses)

**DIGESTION & STOMACH**

**7. Peppermint (Mentha piperita) — Lamiaceae**
- **Identification:** Aromatic herb with opposite leaves; mint smell when crushed
- **Active compounds:** Menthol, menthone
- **Uses:** Indigestion, stomach cramps, IBS
- **Preparation:** Tea from fresh or dried leaves
- **Dose:** 1 teaspoon per cup, three times daily
- **Contraindications:** Reflux/GERD (menthol may worsen); pregnancy (high doses)

**8. Ginger (see PAIN section above)**

**9. Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) — Fabaceae**
- **Identification:** Root with sweet taste; grows in temperate climates
- **Active compounds:** Glycyrrhizin, flavonoids
- **Uses:** Ulcers, inflammation, stomach upset
- **Preparation:** Decoction of dried root; or extract dissolved in water
- **Dose:** 0.5-1 gram daily (dried), up to 6 weeks
- **Contraindications:** High blood pressure (can raise BP); pregnancy; kidney disease

**10. Fennel (Foeniculum vulgare) — Apiaceae**
- **Identification:** Feathery leaves; yellow flowers; aromatic anise-like smell
- **Active compounds:** Anethole, fenchone
- **Uses:** Indigestion, stomach gas, colic
- **Preparation:** Tea from dried seeds (crush gently before steeping)
- **Dose:** 1 teaspoon seeds per cup, three times daily
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy (large doses); estrogen-sensitive cancers

**RESPIRATORY**

**11. Thyme (Thymus vulgaris) — Lamiaceae**
- **Identification:** Small-leaved herb; tiny flowers; strong aromatic smell
- **Active compounds:** Thymol, carvacrol
- **Uses:** Cough, bronchitis, asthma
- **Preparation:** Tea from fresh or dried leaves
- **Dose:** 1 teaspoon per cup, three times daily
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy; thyroid disease (large doses)

**12. Coltsfoot (Tussilago farfara) — Asteraceae**
- **Identification:** Large heart-shaped leaves; yellow dandelion-like flowers in spring
- **Active compounds:** Mucilage, inulin, senecionine (toxic in high amounts)
- **Uses:** Cough, bronchitis (especially lingering cough)
- **Preparation:** Tea from dried leaves or flowers
- **Dose:** 1 teaspoon per cup, three times daily
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy; liver disease; long-term use (over 2 weeks)

**13. Mullein (Verbascum thapsus) — Scrophulariaceae**
- **Identification:** Large fuzzy leaves in first year; tall flower spike in second year
- **Active compounds:** Mucilage, saponins
- **Uses:** Cough, bronchitis, sore throat
- **Preparation:** Tea from dried flowers or leaves
- **Dose:** 1-2 teaspoons per cup, three times daily
- **Contraindications:** Allergy to plants in the family

**SLEEP & ANXIETY**

**14. Valerian (Valeriana officinalis) — Valerianaceae**
- **Identification:** Root with pungent smell; grows in temperate climates
- **Active compounds:** Valerenic acid, sesquiterpenes
- **Uses:** Insomnia, anxiety, restlessness
- **Preparation:** Tea from dried root; tincture; capsules of powdered root
- **Dose:** 0.5-1 gram (dried root) or 1 teaspoon tincture, in evening before bed
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy; nursing; liver disease; sedative medications

**15. Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla) — Asteraceae**
- **Identification:** Small daisy-like flowers with hollow center; fragrant
- **Active compounds:** Apigenin, chamazulene
- **Uses:** Sleep, anxiety, digestive upset
- **Preparation:** Tea from dried flowers
- **Dose:** 1-2 teaspoons per cup, can use multiple times daily
- **Contraindications:** Allergy to ragweed family; pregnancy (high doses)

**16. Lemon balm (Melissa officinalis) — Lamiaceae**
- **Identification:** Aromatic herb; opposite leaves; lemon smell when crushed
- **Active compounds:** Rosmarinic acid, flavonoids
- **Uses:** Anxiety, insomnia, restlessness
- **Preparation:** Tea from fresh or dried leaves
- **Dose:** 1-2 teaspoons per cup, multiple times daily as needed
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy; thyroid disease (may interfere with medication)

**WOMEN'S HEALTH**

**17. Black Cohosh (Cimicifuga racemosa) — Ranunculaceae**
- **Identification:** Tall plant with white flower spike; grows in North American forests
- **Active compounds:** Triterpene glycosides, phenolic acids
- **Uses:** Hot flashes, menstrual cramps, menopausal symptoms
- **Preparation:** Tea from dried root; tincture; capsules
- **Dose:** 20-40 mg daily (extract) or 0.5-1 gram dried root
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy; estrogen-sensitive cancers; liver disease

**18. Dong Quai (Angelica sinensis) — Apiaceae**
- **Identification:** Root used in traditional Chinese medicine; sweet taste
- **Active compounds:** Coumarin compounds, flavonoids
- **Uses:** Menstrual cramps, menopausal symptoms, hormonal balance
- **Preparation:** Decoction of dried root; used in soups and broths (traditional method)
- **Dose:** 1-2 grams daily (dried root) in decoction
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy; bleeding disorders; light sensitivity (phototoxic)

**HEART & CIRCULATION**

**19. Hawthorn (Crataegus spp.) — Rosaceae**
- **Identification:** Shrub or tree with white or pink flowers; red berries (fruits); thorns on branches
- **Active compounds:** Flavonoids, proanthocyanidins
- **Uses:** Heart health, high blood pressure, circulation
- **Preparation:** Tea from dried berries, flowers, or leaves; tincture
- **Dose:** 0.5-1 gram (dried) per cup, three times daily
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy; nursing; heart medication interactions

**20. Ginger (see PAIN section above)**

**SKIN & WOUND HEALING**

**21. Calendula (Calendula officinalis) — Asteraceae**
- **Identification:** Orange or yellow daisy-like flowers; aromatic leaves
- **Active compounds:** Flavonoids, polysaccharides
- **Uses:** Wound healing, inflammation, skin irritation
- **Preparation:** Infused oil; salve; tea (for internal use); tincture
- **Dose:** Apply topically to wounds; or drink 1 cup of weak tea daily
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy (internal use); allergy to ragweed family

**22. Comfrey (Symphytum officinale) — Boraginaceae**
- **Identification:** Large rough leaves; purple or white bell-shaped flowers
- **Active compounds:** Allantoin (promotes cell growth), mucilage
- **Uses:** Wound healing, bone fractures, sprains
- **Preparation:** Salve from root; poultice from leaves
- **Dose:** Apply topically (do NOT use internally; contains potentially toxic alkaloids)
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy; open wounds (may trap infection); internal use contraindicated

**23. Aloe Vera (Aloe barbadensis) — Aloeaceae**
- **Identification:** Succulent with thick fleshy leaves; grows in warm climates
- **Active compounds:** Polysaccharides, anthraquinones
- **Uses:** Burn treatment, wound healing, skin irritation
- **Preparation:** Extract gel from inside leaves; apply fresh or stored in jar
- **Dose:** Apply topically as needed
- **Contraindications:** Allergy; internal use of latex (purgative, not for medicine)

**JOINTS & ARTHRITIS**

**24. Nettle (Urtica dioica) — Urticaceae**
- **Identification:** Plant with stinging hairs on leaves and stems; small green flowers
- **Active compounds:** Silica, minerals, antihistamines
- **Uses:** Joint pain, arthritis, inflammation
- **Preparation:** Tea from dried leaves (stinging hairs are inactivated by drying)
- **Dose:** 1-2 teaspoons per cup, multiple times daily
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy; kidney disease; blood thinner interactions

**25. Devil's Claw (Harpagophytum procumbens) — Pedaliaceae**
- **Identification:** Root tuber; grows in African savannas
- **Active compounds:** Harpagoside, iridoid glycosides
- **Uses:** Arthritis, joint pain, inflammation
- **Preparation:** Decoction of dried root; capsules
- **Dose:** 0.5-1 gram daily; takes weeks to build up effect
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy; gallstones; stomach ulcers

**BLOOD & ANEMIA**

**26. Nettle (see JOINTS section above)**

**27. Alfalfa (Medicago sativa) — Fabaceae**
- **Identification:** Clover-like plant; small purple flowers; grows widely
- **Active compounds:** Chlorophyll, vitamins K and C, minerals
- **Uses:** Nutritional support, anemia (as food, not just medicine)
- **Preparation:** Tea from dried leaves; sprouts (raw); powder
- **Dose:** 1 teaspoon per cup, multiple times daily; or fresh sprouts as food
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy (high doses); blood thinner interactions

**LIVER & DETOXIFICATION**

**28. Milk Thistle (Silybum marianum) — Asteraceae**
- **Identification:** Spiny purple flowers; variegated (white-spotted) leaves
- **Active compounds:** Silymarin, flavonolignans
- **Uses:** Liver health, liver damage recovery, detoxification
- **Preparation:** Tea from dried seeds (crush first); tincture; capsules
- **Dose:** 0.5-1 gram (dried seeds) per cup, three times daily
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy; allergy to ragweed family; estrogen-sensitive cancers

**29. Dandelion (Taraxacum officinale) — Asteraceae**
- **Identification:** Yellow flowers; toothed leaves; white seed heads (milk in stem when broken)
- **Active compounds:** Inulin, antioxidants
- **Uses:** Liver health, digestion, nutritional support
- **Preparation:** Tea from leaves or roasted root; cooked greens; root decoction
- **Dose:** 1-2 teaspoons per cup; or eat as greens
- **Contraindications:** Allergy to ragweed family; gallstones (root); pregnancy (root, high doses)

**FEVER & INFECTION (Additional)**

**30. Eucalyptus (Eucalyptus globulus) — Myrtaceae**
- **Identification:** Tall tree with peeling bark; narrow leaves; strong aromatic smell
- **Active compounds:** Eucalyptol, flavonoids
- **Uses:** Fever, cough, respiratory infection
- **Preparation:** Tea from dried leaves (small amounts); inhalation
- **Dose:** 1 small pinch per cup (do NOT overuse; toxic in large amounts)
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy; children under 2 years; kidney/liver disease

**31. Yarrow (Achillea millefolium) — Asteraceae**
- **Identification:** Feathery fine leaves; clustered small flowers (white, pink, yellow)
- **Active compounds:** Azulene, achilleine
- **Uses:** Fever, wound healing, inflammation
- **Preparation:** Tea from dried flowers or leaves
- **Dose:** 1 teaspoon per cup, three times daily
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy; allergy to ragweed family; high doses (toxicity)

## Verification

A working herbal practice shows:

- **Proper plant identification:** You can reliably recognize medicinal plants in the wild
- **Consistent preparation:** Teas, tinctures, and salves are made with consistent strength
- **Therapeutic effect:** Patients report symptom improvement within reasonable timeframes
- **Good safety record:** Few adverse effects; reactions that occur are documented and managed

A failing practice shows:

- **Misidentification:** Confusion between similar plants, especially toxic look-alikes
- **Inconsistent results:** Some doses work; others don't (indicates inconsistent preparation)
- **Adverse effects:** Unexpected side effects or allergic reactions
- **Patient worsening:** Conditions worsen instead of improve

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing toxic look-alikes:** Poison hemlock looks like edible plants; identification errors can be fatal
- **Using fresh when dried is needed:** Dosages assume dried plant material (typically 4-5x stronger)
- **Ignoring contraindications:** Using black cohosh on someone taking blood thinners causes interactions
- **Expecting instant results:** Some herbs (like Devil's Claw) take weeks to show effects
- **Over-reliance on single herbs:** Combined approach (diet, rest, herbs) is more effective
- **Not adjusting doses for preparation strength:** Tinctures are stronger than teas; dosages must be adjusted
- **Storing plants improperly:** Moisture and light degrade potency; store in cool, dry place
