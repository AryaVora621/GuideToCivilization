## Overview

Malaria is caused by Plasmodium parasites transmitted by mosquitoes. Before synthetic drugs, quinine from cinchona bark was the only effective treatment.

Quinine is an alkaloid (a bitter, nitrogenous plant compound). Extracting it requires understanding plant chemistry and systematic purification.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of extraction chemistry (Vol 5)
- Ability to identify cinchona trees (Vol 31)
- Access to cinchona bark and basic chemical apparatus
- Mathematical skill for dosage calculations

## Materials & Tools

### For Extraction

- Cinchona bark (dried, powdered, or whole)
- Sulfuric acid (dilute)
- Lime or calcium hydroxide
- Alcohol or ether (for purification)
- Distillation apparatus
- Filtration equipment (cheesecloth, sand, charcoal)
- Glass containers and stirring rods
- Scale for measuring materials

### For Administration

- Accurate scales (0.1 gram precision)
- Capsules or powder containers
- Water for dissolving quinine

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Identifying and Harvesting Cinchona

**Step 1: Identify cinchona trees.**

**Cinchona characteristics:**
- Native to South American mountains (Andes)
- Can be cultivated in subtropical/tropical climates
- Tree height: 10-25 meters
- Bark: Reddish-brown, rough texture
- Leaves: Oval, opposite, about 10 cm long
- Flowers: Small, pale pink or white, fragrant
- Fruit: A seed capsule

**Cultivation (if starting from seed):**
1. Cinchona grows at altitude (1500-3000 meters elevation)
2. Requires cool, moist climate (not tropical lowlands)
3. Takes 7-10 years to mature enough for bark harvesting
4. In the post-collapse, wild cinchona might be found in suitable regions

**Step 2: Harvest the bark.**

**Timing:** Best in spring or early summer when the tree is actively growing.

**Harvesting method:**
1. Remove bark from branches (less damaging than stripping the trunk)
2. Cut bark in strips about 5-10 cm wide and 30 cm long
3. Dry the bark in sun or over slow heat (not too hot; this damages alkaloids)
4. Drying takes 1-2 weeks; bark should be brittle when fully dry

**Preparation:**
1. Grind dried bark into powder using a mortar and pestle
2. Finer powder = better extraction (more surface area)
3. Store in a dry place until ready to extract

### Part 2: Extracting Quinine Alkaloids

**Step 1: Understand the chemistry.**

Quinine is an alkaloid base (slightly bitter and oily). It's more soluble in:
- Acidic solutions (forms a salt)
- Organic solvents like ether or alcohol
- Less soluble in water alone

**Extraction strategy:**
1. Use acid to extract alkaloids from the plant material
2. Concentrate the solution
3. Purify using acid-base chemistry

**Step 2: Simple acid extraction.**

**Materials:**
- Ground cinchona bark (10 grams)
- Dilute sulfuric acid (5% solution, about 100 mL)
- Water
- Cheesecloth for filtering

**Procedure:**
1. Mix the ground bark with dilute sulfuric acid in a glass container
2. Stir well
3. Let it sit for 24 hours (room temperature)
4. The acid dissolves the alkaloids into solution
5. Filter through cheesecloth to remove solid residue
6. The filtrate contains quinine sulfate (a salt)

**Step 3: Concentrate the extract.**

**Purpose:** Remove excess water to increase quinine concentration.

**Evaporation method:**
1. Pour the filtered extract into a shallow pan
2. Heat gently (do not boil) until the volume is reduced to 1/4 of the original
3. The solution becomes darker and more concentrated
4. Stop heating when the liquid is thick and syrupy
5. Cool and allow it to crystallize (optional; takes several days)

**Distillation method (more efficient):**
1. Set up a distillation apparatus
2. Distill off water until the liquid becomes syrupy
3. This removes water more efficiently than pan evaporation

**Step 4: Purify the extract (optional, for better quality).**

Raw cinchona extract contains other alkaloids (cinchonine, quinidine) and plant material. Purification is complex but improves effectiveness.

**Simple purification (charcoal treatment):**
1. Mix the concentrated extract with activated charcoal (a few grams)
2. Stir for 1 hour
3. Filter to remove charcoal
4. The charcoal removes some impurities
5. The solution is slightly cleaner but still contains other alkaloids

**Advanced purification (precipitation):**
1. Add lime or calcium hydroxide to the acidic extract (carefully; the reaction is exothermic)
2. This precipitates the alkaloids as free bases
3. Filter to collect the precipitate
4. Wash with water
5. Dissolve in dilute acid again
6. Repeat precipitation and dissolution to purify further

**Result:** Partially purified quinine solution or solid precipitate.

### Part 3: Dosage and Administration

**Step 1: Understand quinine potency.**

The extract contains quinine mixed with other alkaloids and residual plant material. The actual quinine content is hard to determine without laboratory analysis.

**Estimation:**
- Dried cinchona bark contains about 5-15% quinine alkaloids (varies by species and growth conditions)
- Post-collapse extraction might recover 30-50% of the alkaloids
- Example: 100 grams of dried bark containing 10% quinine = 10 grams alkaloids; extraction recovers 3-5 grams of crude extract

**Step 2: Prepare doses.**

**For crude extract:**
1. A therapeutic dose of quinine is about 600 mg three times daily for 7 days
2. If the extract is 50% quinine by mass, 1.2 grams of extract contains 600 mg quinine
3. Divide the extract into capsules or packets of appropriate size

**For concentrated solution:**
1. If the extract is a thick liquid, measure out 5-10 mL doses
2. Administer in water to mask the bitter taste

**Step 3: Dosage for malaria treatment.**

**Acute malaria (uncomplicated):**
- 600 mg quinine (or ~1.2 grams crude extract) three times daily for 7 days
- Total: 12.6 grams quinine (about 25 grams crude extract) over the treatment course

**Severe malaria (cerebral, renal failure):**
- Higher doses are needed
- 10 mg/kg IV or IM every 8 hours, loading dose followed by maintenance
- Post-collapse: Use highest tolerable dose

**Dosing for children:**
- Reduce by body weight ratio (a 25-kg child gets 25/70 of an adult dose, roughly)

**Step 4: Administration methods.**

**Oral (most practical for post-collapse):**
1. Mix the crude extract with water or weak juice to mask bitterness
2. Administer three times daily with food
3. Quinine on an empty stomach causes nausea

**Rectal (if patient cannot swallow):**
1. Dissolve in a small amount of water
2. Use a syringe or tube to administer rectally
3. Less efficient than oral but still effective

**Intramuscular injection (if available):**
1. A purified quinine solution can be injected
2. Requires sterile technique and clean needles
3. More effective than oral for severe malaria

### Part 4: Monitoring and Adverse Effects

**Step 1: Recognize adverse effects.**

Quinine causes side effects in many people:

**Cinchonism (quinine toxicity):**
- Ringing ears (tinnitus)
- Hearing loss
- Blurred vision
- Dizziness
- Nausea and vomiting

**Severe reactions (rare but possible):**
- Allergic reactions (rash, anaphylaxis)
- Low blood sugar (hypoglycemia)
- Blood disorders (thrombocytopenia)

**Step 2: Manage side effects.**

**Cinchonism:**
- Usually mild and reversible
- Reduce dose slightly if tolerable
- Symptoms improve after treatment ends

**Allergic reactions:**
- Stop immediately if rash appears
- Switch to alternative antimalarial if available

**Step 3: Contraindications (when NOT to use quinine):**

- Pregnancy (especially first trimester; quinine can cause miscarriage)
- Severe liver disease (impairs metabolism)
- Heart rhythm disorders (quinine can worsen)
- Myasthenia gravis (quinine worsens muscle weakness)

## Verification

A successful quinine extraction and treatment shows:

- **Malaria fever resolves:** Fever breaks within 48-72 hours of starting quinine
- **Parasites clear:** If blood smears can be examined, parasite count decreases dramatically
- **Patient improves:** Energy returns; symptoms like chills and headache subside
- **Tolerable side effects:** Minor cinchonism is expected; severe reactions are rare

A failed treatment shows:

- **Persistent fever:** Fever doesn't break after 3-4 days (indicates insufficient dose, wrong diagnosis, or drug resistance)
- **Worsening condition:** Complications like cerebral malaria develop
- **Severe adverse effects:** Allergic reaction, severe hypoglycemia, or dangerous blood disorders

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing cinchona species:** Some cinchona species have lower alkaloid content; use C. pubescens or C. officinalis if available
- **Over-drying bark:** Excessive heat destroys alkaloids; dry slowly at moderate temperatures
- **Assuming equal potency:** Extracts vary widely in quinine content; start with lower doses if potency is uncertain
- **Treating without diagnosis:** Fever has many causes; quinine only works for malaria (Plasmodium infection)
- **Stopping treatment early:** Completing the full 7-day course is essential; stopping early risks relapse
- **Administering to pregnant women:** Especially dangerous in first trimester; use alternative antimalarials if available
- **Overdosing to speed recovery:** Higher doses increase risk of serious side effects without improving outcome
