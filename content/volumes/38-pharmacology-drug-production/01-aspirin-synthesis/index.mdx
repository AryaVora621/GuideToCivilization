## Overview

Aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid) is a pain reliever and fever reducer that works through a simple chemical mechanism. Understanding how to synthesize it helps you:

1. **Produce essential medicine:** Pain relief is critical post-collapse
2. **Reduce infection:** Aspirin reduces fever by helping with infection control
3. **Preserve health:** Prevents pain-induced complications
4. **Understand synthesis:** Core principles apply to other drugs
5. **Verify quality:** Know what you're producing

This chapter teaches the synthesis of aspirin from salicylic acid.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of organic chemistry
- Knowledge of acids and bases
- Ability to measure and mix chemicals

- Familiarity with basic lab safety
- Knowledge of extraction and crystallization

## Part 1: Chemistry of Aspirin

### The Reaction

Aspirin is created by acetylating salicylic acid using acetic anhydride and a sulfuric acid catalyst.

**Chemical reaction:**
Salicylic acid + Acetic anhydride → Aspirin + Acetic acid

**Molecular formula:**
- Salicylic acid: C₇H₆O₃
- Acetic anhydride: C₄H₆O₃
- Aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid): C₉H₈O₄
- Acetic acid: C₂H₄O₂

**What happens:**
The hydroxyl group (-OH) on salicylic acid reacts with acetic anhydride, replacing it with an acetyl group (-COCH₃). This creates aspirin.

**Why this works:**
- Salicylic acid is derived from willow bark and natural sources
- It has pain-relieving properties but irritates the stomach
- Acetylating it creates aspirin, which is gentler on the stomach
- The acetyl group protects the core molecule

### Why Aspirin is Effective

Aspirin works by inhibiting prostaglandin synthesis.

**Mechanism:**
1. Prostaglandins are hormone-like substances in the body
2. They promote inflammation, pain, and fever
3. Aspirin blocks the enzyme that makes prostaglandins
4. Result: Reduced pain, inflammation, and fever

**Duration:** Effects last 4-6 hours; repeated doses needed for pain relief.

**Side effects:**
- Stomach irritation (reduced compared to salicylic acid)
- Bleeding risk (aspirin thins blood slightly)
- Allergic reactions (rare)

### Post-Collapse Relevance

Aspirin is one of the most useful post-collapse drugs because:
- Fever control helps manage infections
- Pain relief improves quality of life and speeds recovery
- Simple synthesis from available materials
- Long shelf life (years) when stored dry
- Low toxicity at recommended doses

## Part 2: Obtaining Starting Materials

### Salicylic Acid

**Natural source:** Salicylic acid is found in willow bark and meadowsweet.

**Extraction method:**
1. Harvest willow bark (or meadowsweet leaves)
2. Dry completely
3. Crush into powder
4. Soak in water or dilute alcohol
5. Heat gently to extract
6. Filter and evaporate water to get salicylic acid crystals

**Yield:** Low (~1-3% by weight of dried bark). Takes significant processing.

**Alternative:** Salicylic acid can be synthesized from phenol (difficult) or purchased if supply chains available.

**Purity consideration:** Natural extraction gives impure salicylic acid, which is acceptable for medicine (though less predictable dosing).

### Acetic Anhydride

**Problem:** Acetic anhydride is difficult to produce post-collapse.

**Synthesis challenge:**
Creating acetic anhydride requires acetyl chloride or complex organic chemistry. Not practical for small-scale post-collapse production.

**Solutions:**
1. **Salvage from lab supplies:** Pre-collapse chemistry sets, pharmaceutical labs
2. **Use alternative:** Acetic acid (vinegar) can partially substitute, though less efficient
3. **Simplify approach:** Use salicylic acid directly (less efficient pain relief, more stomach irritation)

**For post-collapse:** Assume salvage or trade for acetic anhydride. If unavailable, aspirin synthesis becomes impractical; focus on other pain relief (willow bark tea, poppy-based options).

### Sulfuric Acid

**Catalyst role:** Sulfuric acid speeds the reaction without being consumed.

**Obtaining sulfuric acid:**
- Salvage from car batteries (lead-acid batteries contain dilute H₂SO₄)
- Industrial chemical salvage
- Production: Burning sulfur and capturing the oxide (advanced chemistry)

**Concentration:** Battery acid is typically 30-40% H₂SO₄. Acceptable for this reaction.

**Safety note:** Sulfuric acid is highly corrosive. Handle with extreme care.

## Part 3: Synthesis Procedure

### Setup and Safety

**Work location:**
- Outdoors or in well-ventilated space
- Away from children and animals
- On heat-resistant surface
- With water nearby for safety

**Personal protection:**
- Wear gloves (rubber or latex)
- Protect eyes
- Wear long sleeves
- Keep hair tied back

### Step-by-Step Synthesis

**Quantities (for moderate batch):**
- 10 grams salicylic acid
- 8 milliliters acetic anhydride
- 1 milliliter sulfuric acid (concentrated, ~98%)
- 50 milliliters water (for final step)

**Procedure:**

1. **Measure salicylic acid**
   - Weigh or measure 10 grams of salicylic acid
   - Place in heat-safe container (flask)

2. **Add acetic anhydride**
   - Carefully add 8 milliliters of acetic anhydride to the salicylic acid
   - Stir to mix
   - Note: Acetic anhydride has strong odor; avoid breathing fumes

3. **Add catalyst**
   - Slowly add 1 milliliter of sulfuric acid to the mixture
   - Add drop by drop while stirring
   - The mixture will heat up (exothermic reaction)
   - Do NOT add sulfuric acid quickly; it could boil over

4. **Gentle heating**
   - Heat the mixture to 80-90°C (warm to touch, not boiling)
   - Maintain this temperature for 15-20 minutes
   - Stir occasionally
   - The mixture will become viscous and may darken slightly

5. **Cool down**
   - Remove from heat and let cool to room temperature
   - This takes 15-30 minutes
   - Stir occasionally to cool evenly

6. **Precipitation**
   - Slowly add 50 milliliters of cold water to the cooled mixture
   - Pour into water carefully (exothermic; may bubble)
   - White crystals of aspirin will form
   - Stir and let sit for 5 minutes

7. **Collection**
   - Pour mixture through filter (use cloth or paper)
   - Collect solid on filter; discard liquid
   - Rinse crystals with small amounts of cold water (3-4 times)
   - This removes impurities

8. **Drying**
   - Spread crystals on clean cloth or paper
   - Air dry for 24-48 hours
   - Keep in dry location
   - Moisture reduces shelf life

### Yield and Purity

**Expected yield:** 10-12 grams of aspirin from 10 grams salicylic acid (100-120% theoretical, due to water of hydration).

**Purity:** Homemade aspirin will be 85-95% pure. Post-collapse, this is acceptable (modern pharmaceuticals are 99%+ pure).

**Impurities in homemade aspirin:**
- Unreacted salicylic acid
- Acetic acid (byproduct)
- Water

**All are relatively safe at typical aspirin doses.**

## Part 4: Recrystallization (Purification)

For higher purity (recommended if materials allow):

**Method:**
1. Dissolve crude aspirin in hot water or ethanol
2. Filter while hot to remove insoluble impurities
3. Cool slowly; crystals form and precipitate
4. Collect crystals and dry

**Advantage:** Removes some impurities, improves consistency.

**Disadvantage:** Requires more time and loses some product.

**Post-collapse decision:** Skip recrystallization if time/materials are limited. Crude aspirin is adequate.

## Part 5: Storage and Dosing

### Proper Storage

**Aspirin degrades:**
- In humid environments (add desiccant packets)
- In heat (store cool)
- In light (store in dark container)
- Near acids/bases (store separately)

**Shelf life:**
- Properly stored: 3-5 years
- In humid conditions: 1-2 years

**Container:**
- Glass or ceramic (not plastic)
- Sealed tightly
- Dark colored (blocks light)
- With desiccant packet (absorbs moisture)

### Dosing

**Adult dose:** 325-500 mg every 4-6 hours as needed for pain or fever.

**Signs you have aspirin:** 
- White crystalline solid
- Slightly bitter taste
- Dissolves in water (slowly)
- Melts around 135°C

**How to know if pure enough:**
- Effective for pain relief (users report improvement)
- Doesn't cause excessive stomach irritation
- Crystallizes properly from solution

### Quality Control

**Simple test (taste-based):**
1. Place small amount on tongue
2. Aspirin tastes slightly bitter
3. Salicylic acid tastes more intensely bitter
4. Acetic acid tastes vinegary
5. If taste is too bitter, likely contaminated with salicylic acid (still effective but less pleasant)

**Purity estimation:**
- Start with small dose (100 mg)
- Wait 1-2 hours to see effect
- If no effect at reasonable dose, purity is likely low
- Crude aspirin is acceptable; just dose accordingly

## Part 6: Safety and Hazards

### Chemical Hazards

**Acetic anhydride:**
- Corrosive to skin and eyes
- Flammable
- Irritating to lungs
- Handle carefully; avoid contact

**Sulfuric acid:**
- Highly corrosive
- Can cause severe burns
- Produces heat when diluted (add acid to water, never water to acid)
- Careful handling essential

**Aspirin:**
- Low toxicity at normal doses
- Can cause allergic reactions (rare)
- Thin blood (avoid before surgery; contraindicated with certain medications)
- Stomach irritation in sensitive individuals

### Safe Handling

**Do:**
- Wear protective equipment
- Work in ventilated area
- Add chemicals slowly
- Keep water nearby
- Clean up spills immediately
- Store chemicals separately

**Don't:**
- Rush the reaction
- Mix chemicals unsupervised
- Inhale fumes
- Touch eyes after handling chemicals
- Store chemicals near food
- Make large batches without experience

### Waste Disposal

**Leftover chemicals:**
- Acetic anhydride: Evaporate in ventilated area or neutralize with water carefully
- Sulfuric acid: Neutralize with baking soda or lime, then dispose
- Aspirin: Non-toxic; can be disposed of normally

**Filtrate from crystallization:**
- Contains acetic acid and water
- Pour down drain (acetic acid is mild)
- Or use for other applications (acetic acid is useful)

## Common Mistakes

1. **Adding sulfuric acid too quickly:** Can boil over or cause exothermic runaway. Add slowly while stirring.

2. **Not cooling mixture before adding water:** Cold water is essential; adding to hot aspirin can cause splattering.

3. **Skipping the water rinse:** Impurities (especially acetic acid) remain, affecting taste and purity.

4. **Storing in humid environment:** Aspirin absorbs water; degrades quickly without desiccant.

5. **Using contaminated salicylic acid:** If extraction impure, final aspirin will be impure. Purify salicylic acid first if possible.

6. **Mistaking impurities for degradation:** Some variation in color/texture is normal for homemade aspirin.

7. **Exceeding recommended dose:** Aspirin is safe but excessive doses can cause bleeding or stomach issues.

8. **Not testing before large-scale production:** Make small batches first to verify you have access to materials and can execute the procedure.
