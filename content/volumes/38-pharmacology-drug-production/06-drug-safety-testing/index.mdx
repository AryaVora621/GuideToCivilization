## Overview

A drug is only useful if the therapeutic dose (dose that helps) is far from the toxic dose (dose that harms).

A **dose-response curve** plots the effect of a drug against the dose given. A **safety margin** is the ratio of toxic dose to therapeutic dose.

Testing requires observation, documentation, and careful escalation of doses to determine safety.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of drug effects and pharmacology
- Ability to observe and record patient symptoms accurately
- Understanding of statistical concepts (average, range, probability)
- Knowledge of the specific drug's expected effects
- Willingness to proceed cautiously with unknown substances

## Materials & Tools

- The drug to be tested
- Test subjects (animals or willing human volunteers)
- Dosage measurement scales (grams, milliliters)
- Stopwatch or clock
- Recording materials (notebook, pen)
- Medical supplies for monitoring (thermometer, pulse meter)
- Reference materials (prior knowledge of similar drugs)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Defining Safety Parameters

**Step 1: Understand key terms.**

**ED50 (Effective Dose, 50%):**
- The dose that produces the desired effect in 50% of test subjects
- Example: ED50 for aspirin for headache relief is about 600 mg

**TD50 (Toxic Dose, 50%):**
- The dose that produces toxic effects in 50% of test subjects
- Example: TD50 might be 2000 mg for a given adverse effect

**LD50 (Lethal Dose, 50%):**
- The dose that causes death in 50% of test subjects
- Example: LD50 for aspirin is about 20,000 mg (very safe margin)

**Therapeutic Index (TI):**
- TI = LD50 / ED50 (or TD50 / ED50 for non-lethal toxicity)
- Higher index = safer drug
- TI > 10 is generally considered safe
- TI < 2 is dangerous (narrow margin between effective and toxic)

**Safety Margin:**
- Similar to TI; shows how far the toxic dose is from the therapeutic dose
- Example: If ED50 is 10 mg and TD50 is 1000 mg, the safety margin is 100-fold

**Step 2: Review historical data.**

Before testing, gather information on similar drugs:

**For plant-based drugs:**
1. Is the plant known to be safe or toxic?
2. What do historical records say about dosages?
3. Are there known toxic effects?

**For synthesized drugs:**
1. What reactions produced the compound?
2. Are side products toxic?
3. What do animal tests suggest?

**Step 3: Establish the goal.**

Define what you're testing:
- Therapeutic effect: What condition is being treated? What signs indicate improvement?
- Toxic effects: What symptoms indicate toxicity? (Nausea, tremors, hallucinations, etc.)
- Lethal dose: Is death a possible outcome? (For necessary drugs like antivenoms, yes; for cosmetics, no)

### Part 2: Dose-Response Curve in Animals

**Step 1: Select test animals.**

Post-collapse animal testing uses available subjects:

**Common choices:**
- Small mammals (mice, rats, guinea pigs): Similar physiology to humans, quick results
- Larger animals (rabbits, dogs): More realistic to human physiology but slower, more expensive
- Insects or invertebrates: Rapid testing but less similar to humans

**Consider ethics:**
- Use the smallest number of animals necessary
- Choose animals that cannot reasonably refuse
- Minimize suffering

**Step 2: Establish baseline measurements.**

Before dosing, measure:
1. Body weight (drugs are often dosed per kilogram of body weight)
2. Baseline vital signs: Heart rate, breathing rate, temperature
3. Baseline behavior: Activity level, alertness, appetite

**Step 3: Administer doses in escalating series.**

**Dose escalation method:**
1. Start with a small dose (1/10 or 1/100 of the expected therapeutic dose)
2. Observe for effects for 1-2 hours
3. If no serious adverse effects, increase to the next dose (typically 2-3 times higher)
4. Repeat until either:
   - The therapeutic effect is observed (ED50 range found)
   - Severe toxicity is observed (TD50 range found)
   - Death occurs (LD50 range found)

**Example: Testing a new herbal extract**
- Starting dose: 10 mg per kg body weight
- Doses: 10, 30, 100, 300, 1000, 3000 mg/kg
- Observe each dose for 1-2 hours before escalating

**Step 4: Monitor and record observations.**

For each dose level:

**Physical signs:**
- Pulse and breathing rate
- Temperature
- Body posture and movement
- Pupil size
- Skin color and texture
- Presence of sweating or tremors

**Behavioral signs:**
- Alertness vs. sedation
- Aggression or docility
- Nausea or vomiting
- Seizures or muscle spasms
- Loss of consciousness
- Death

**Time course:**
- Record when each effect appears
- Record how long it lasts
- Note if effects are reversible (fade with time) or permanent

**Example observation record:**

```
Test Animal: Rabbit #3
Body Weight: 2.5 kg

Dose 1: 10 mg/kg = 25 mg total
Time 0: Baseline - alert, HR 180/min, RR 40/min
Time 15: Slightly drowsy, HR 190/min
Time 60: Fully alert, back to baseline
Effects: Minimal; drug is safe at this dose

Dose 2: 30 mg/kg = 75 mg total
Time 0: Alert, HR 185/min
Time 15: Drowsy, HR 170/min, RR 35/min
Time 30: Very drowsy, HR 160/min
Time 60: Awake but lethargic, returning to baseline
Effects: Clear sedation; drug is tolerable at this dose
```

**Step 5: Determine ED50 and TD50.**

From the dose series, identify:

**ED50 (Effective Dose 50%):**
- The dose where the desired effect appears in about 50% of animals tested
- Use multiple animals at key dose levels to establish this
- Example: If 5 rabbits are tested at 100 mg/kg, and 2-3 show the desired effect, then ED50 is approximately 100 mg/kg

**TD50 (Toxic Dose 50%):**
- The dose where toxic effects appear in 50% of animals
- Example: If tremors appear at 1000 mg/kg in 2 out of 5 rabbits, then TD50 ≈ 1000 mg/kg

**LD50 (Lethal Dose 50%):**
- The dose that causes death
- Observe animals for 24-48 hours after dosing to catch delayed deaths
- Example: If 1 out of 5 rabbits dies at 10,000 mg/kg, LD50 might be 10,000 mg/kg

### Part 3: Human Testing (Ethical Framework)

**Step 1: Decide if human testing is necessary.**

Human testing should only proceed if:
1. Animal testing shows safety (or acceptable risk for severe illnesses)
2. The drug is intended for human use
3. Potential benefits outweigh risks
4. Volunteers understand the risks

**Do NOT conduct human testing for:**
- Cosmetics
- Recreational drugs
- Experiments where animal testing is sufficient

**Do conduct human testing for:**
- New antibiotics for severe infections
- Antimalarial drugs for endemic regions
- Treatments for immediately fatal conditions

**Step 2: Recruit informed volunteers.**

**Voluntary informed consent:**
1. Explain the drug, its purpose, and known risks
2. Explain the testing procedure
3. Explain that volunteers can withdraw at any time
4. Document that the volunteer understands and accepts the risks
5. Do NOT coerce or deceive volunteers

**Criteria for volunteers:**
- Healthy (for Phase 1 safety testing)
- No contraindications to the drug
- Able to communicate symptoms clearly
- Free to withdraw at any time

**Step 3: Conduct Phase 1 testing (safety and dose tolerance).**

**Protocol:**
1. Start with the lowest anticipated therapeutic dose
2. Observe the volunteer for 2-4 hours
3. If no adverse effects, increase dose (typically by 25-50%)
4. Repeat until reaching the expected therapeutic range or observable side effects
5. Stop immediately if serious adverse effects appear

**Monitoring:**
1. Document all symptoms and vital signs
2. Ask the volunteer about subjective sensations (nausea, dizziness, pain)
3. Provide medical support if needed
4. Be ready to provide antidotes or supportive care for overdose

**Example Phase 1 protocol: New herbal pain reliever**
- Volunteer: Healthy adult, 70 kg
- Expected therapeutic dose: Based on animal tests, estimated 500 mg
- Actual doses: 50 mg, 100 mg, 200 mg, 500 mg, 1000 mg (if tolerated)
- Monitoring: Vital signs every 15 minutes; symptom assessment every 30 minutes

**Step 4: Conduct Phase 2 testing (efficacy and optimal dose).**

Only if Phase 1 shows safety:

1. Recruit volunteers with the target condition (e.g., people with malaria for an antimalarial)
2. Test the drug at the expected therapeutic dose
3. Measure the primary outcome (does fever resolve? Do parasites clear?)
4. Adjust dose if needed for efficacy
5. Monitor for adverse effects in the context of the disease

**Step 5: Maintain safety monitoring throughout.**

**Emergency plan:**
- Have antidotes available (if known)
- Have medical supplies for managing overdose (oxygen, IV fluids, etc.)
- Know when to transfer the patient to a hospital (if available)

### Part 4: Interpreting Results and Setting Safe Doses

**Step 1: Calculate the therapeutic index.**

From testing:
- Determine ED50 or therapeutic dose (dose needed for effect)
- Determine TD50 or LD50 (dose causing toxicity or death)
- Calculate: TI = TD50 / ED50 (or LD50 / ED50)

**Example:**
- ED50 = 200 mg (dose for 50% relief of headache)
- TD50 = 2000 mg (dose causing nausea in 50%)
- TI = 2000 / 200 = 10
- Interpretation: 10-fold safety margin; this is safe

**Step 2: Set a safe therapeutic dose.**

The therapeutic dose should be:
1. High enough to be effective (ED50, or slightly higher)
2. Well below the toxic dose (typically 1/10 of TD50 or lower)
3. Account for individual variation

**Formula:**
Safe dose = ED50 / (safety factor)
- Safety factor = 5-10 for drugs with narrow margins
- Safety factor = 2-3 for drugs with wide margins

**Example:**
- ED50 = 200 mg
- TD50 = 2000 mg
- TI = 10 (wide margin)
- Safe dose = 200 mg / 2 = 100 mg (typical therapeutic dose)

**Step 3: Document the dose-response relationship.**

Create a table:

```
Dose (mg) | Effect | % of Subjects | Adverse Effects
50        | None   | 0%            | None
100       | Mild   | 20%           | None
200       | Moderate | 50%         | Mild nausea 10%
400       | Strong | 80%           | Nausea 40%, tremor 10%
800       | Very strong | 95%      | Nausea 70%, tremor 40%, vomiting 5%
1600      | Severe | 100%          | Severe nausea 80%, tremor 60%, vomiting 30%, convulsions 5%
3200      | Lethal | 50% death     | Death in 50%
```

**Step 4: Identify special populations.**

Dose adjustments may be needed for:

**Age:**
- Elderly: Often require lower doses (slower metabolism)
- Children: Require weight-adjusted lower doses
- Infants: Many drugs are contraindicated

**Health status:**
- Liver disease: Reduced metabolism; lower doses
- Kidney disease: Reduced excretion; lower doses
- Pregnancy: Many drugs are contraindicated

**Body composition:**
- Obesity: Fat-soluble drugs may require higher doses
- Malnutrition: May increase sensitivity

## Verification

A well-designed safety testing protocol shows:

- **Clear dose-response curve:** Effect increases predictably with dose
- **Identified ED50 and TD50:** Therapeutic and toxic doses are established
- **Acceptable therapeutic index:** TI is sufficient for safe use (at least 3-5)
- **Complete documentation:** All observations are recorded with dates and times
- **Volunteer safety:** No lasting harm to human volunteers

A failed or problematic testing protocol shows:

- **No clear dose-response:** Effects are unpredictable or absent
- **Narrow therapeutic index:** ED50 is close to TD50 (drug is dangerous)
- **Incomplete data:** Missing observations or lack of documentation
- **Serious adverse effects:** Unexpected toxicity or death
- **Coerced or deceived volunteers:** Ethical violations

## Common Mistakes

- **Insufficient dosing escalation:** Skipping intermediate doses misses the ED50 and TD50
- **Inadequate observation period:** Delayed toxicity is missed if observation is too short
- **Using insufficient test subjects:** Single animals or volunteers give unreliable results
- **Confounding factors:** Not controlling for diet, stress, or disease makes results unclear
- **Misinterpreting results:** Assuming correlation with dose is causation without proper controls
- **Failing to document:** Lost observations mean lost data; detailed records are essential
- **Ethically problematic human testing:** Coerced or deceptive recruitment undermines validity and causes harm
