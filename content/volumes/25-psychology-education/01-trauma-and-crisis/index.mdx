## Overview

A disaster survivor experiences extraordinary stress. Their nervous system goes into fight-flight-freeze response. They may become numb, hypervigilant, dissociated, or panicked. Without intervention, acute stress becomes chronic trauma (PTSD), depression, or psychological breakdown.

Psychological first aid is not therapy. It is immediate support: safety, calm presence, basic information, and connection to others. A trained person can prevent the worst outcomes.

This chapter covers:
1. How the nervous system responds to trauma
2. How to recognize acute stress and trauma
3. Psychological first aid techniques
4. How to prevent long-term psychological damage

## Prerequisites

- Basic first aid training (Vol 3, Ch 7)
- Observation of group behavior and human responses to stress
- Access to another person (you cannot practice alone)

## Materials & Tools

- Your presence and calm
- Basic supplies for comfort: water, blanket, food (if available)
- A quiet, safe space
- Time (psychological first aid is not fast; plan for hours)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Understanding Trauma Response

**Step 1: Know the fight-flight-freeze response.**

When threatened, the body activates the sympathetic nervous system:

- **Fight response:** Anger, aggression, confrontation. The person may lash out or act recklessly.
- **Flight response:** Panic, running, avoidance. The person may flee, hide, or try to escape.
- **Freeze response:** Dissociation, numbness, shock. The person appears calm or emotionless despite danger. (This is deceptive — internally, they are in crisis.)

All three are normal. None means the person is "weak" or "crazy."

**Why this matters:** A frozen survivor may seem fine but is deeply traumatized. A fight-response survivor may seem aggressive but is terrified. A flight-response survivor may seem cowardly but is responding to real danger.

**Step 2: Understand the nervous system window.**

The "window of tolerance" is the range where the nervous system functions normally. Trauma pushes the nervous system outside this window:

- **Hyper-arousal (above the window):** Hypervigilance, anxiety, anger, racing heart, racing thoughts. The person is in fight-or-flight.
- **Hypo-arousal (below the window):** Numbness, dissociation, depression, fatigue, shutting down. The person is in freeze mode.

Psychological first aid brings the nervous system back into the window.

**Step 3: Recognize trauma exposure.**

Trauma comes from events that feel life-threatening:

- Direct threat to life (violence, disaster, accident, illness)
- Threat to a loved one
- Witnessing death or serious injury
- Sudden loss of safety or security (collapse, refugee status, displacement)

Intensity matters. A near-death experience is more traumatic than a car accident. The death of a child is more traumatic than a friend's illness. Repeated trauma (chronic abuse, war, ongoing collapse) is more damaging than a single event.

### Part 2: Recognizing Acute Stress

**Step 1: Identify acute stress symptoms (within hours to days).**

In the first hours and days after trauma, watch for:

**Physical symptoms:**
- Rapid heartbeat or difficulty breathing
- Trembling or muscle tension
- Nausea, dizziness, or fainting
- Sleep disruption
- Excessive fatigue or restlessness

**Emotional symptoms:**
- Numbness or emotional flatness (freeze response)
- Intense fear, anxiety, or panic (flight response)
- Anger, irritability, or rage (fight response)
- Guilt or shame
- Sadness or despair

**Cognitive symptoms:**
- Difficulty concentrating or remembering
- Disorientation (not knowing where/when they are)
- Intrusive thoughts (replaying the trauma repeatedly)
- Denial or disbelief

**Behavioral symptoms:**
- Withdrawal from others
- Hypervigilance (always scanning for danger)
- Avoidance of reminders of the trauma
- Seeming "fine" or emotionally disconnected

**Red flags for severe acute stress:**
- Suicidal thoughts or self-harm impulses
- Inability to care for basic needs (eating, hygiene)
- Violent outbursts toward others
- Severe dissociation (not knowing who/where they are)

**Step 2: Understand acute stress vs. PTSD.**

Not all acute stress becomes PTSD. PTSD is chronic trauma response lasting months or years. However, acute stress that is untreated often becomes PTSD.

**Acute stress:** Lasts hours to 4 weeks. Most people recover with support and time.

**PTSD:** Lasts more than 1 month. Symptoms interfere with daily functioning. Requires sustained intervention.

**Step 3: Know who is at highest risk.**

Some people are more vulnerable to severe trauma:

- Those with previous trauma history (re-traumatization is more harmful)
- Those without strong social support
- Those with existing mental health conditions
- Children and adolescents (developing brains are more vulnerable)
- Those in high-stress continuing situations (refugees, ongoing violence)

### Part 3: Psychological First Aid

Psychological first aid has four goals:

1. **Safety:** Ensure the person is physically safe and feels safe
2. **Calm:** Help the nervous system move back into the window of tolerance
3. **Connection:** Restore social support and sense of belonging
4. **Information:** Explain what is happening and what to expect

**Step 1: Ensure physical safety.**

Before any psychological work, address immediate survival needs:

- Is the person in danger? Remove them from the threat if possible.
- Are they injured? Provide medical first aid (Vol 3, Ch 7).
- Do they have shelter, water, food? Meet basic needs first.

Physical safety is the foundation. A person cannot feel psychologically safe while physically threatened.

**Step 2: Create a calm presence.**

Your demeanor is the most important tool:

**Do this:**
- Sit or kneel at eye level (not standing over them)
- Speak in a calm, slow voice
- Use simple, clear language (avoid complex explanations during crisis)
- Make gentle eye contact (but not staring; respect if they look away)
- Maintain open body language (uncrossed arms, relaxed posture)
- Use their name if you know it
- Move slowly and predictably

**Do NOT do this:**
- Approach suddenly or from behind (may trigger startle response)
- Speak loudly or urgently (increases anxiety)
- Touch without permission (some trauma survivors are triggered by touch)
- Force eye contact
- Tell them to "calm down" or "stop crying" (this invalidates their experience)
- Leave them alone immediately (isolation increases fear)

**Step 3: Provide grounding and regulation techniques.**

Grounding helps bring the nervous system back into the window of tolerance.

**5-4-3-2-1 technique (sensory grounding):**
Ask the person to identify:
- 5 things they see
- 4 things they can touch
- 3 things they can hear
- 2 things they can smell
- 1 thing they can taste

This engages the senses and pulls attention away from internal panic.

**Slow breathing:**
"Let's breathe together. Breathe in slowly (count to 4), hold (count to 4), breathe out slowly (count to 6). The out-breath is longer than the in-breath."

Slow breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system (calming). Longer exhales are most effective.

**Progressive muscle relaxation (for tension):**
"I'm going to name body parts, and you tense and then relax them. This helps your body release tension."
- Tense the fists for 5 seconds, then release
- Tense the arms, then release
- Tense the face, then release
- Continue down the body

**Step 4: Provide information and normalize responses.**

People in crisis often think they are "going crazy." Explaining that their responses are normal reduces shame and fear.

**Say this:**
- "What you are experiencing is a normal response to abnormal events."
- "Your body is in protect mode. This is designed to keep you safe."
- "Many people feel numb or disoriented after trauma. This usually improves."
- "Your feelings are valid. You are not weak or broken."

**Explain what they might expect:**
- "You may have trouble sleeping. This is temporary."
- "You may feel sad, angry, or scared. These feelings will fluctuate."
- "You may not want to be around people. But connection with others helps."
- "Over time, most people recover with support."

**Step 5: Restore connection.**

Humans are social creatures. Isolation deepens trauma; connection heals it.

**Do this:**
- Stay present with the person (do not leave them alone unless they explicitly ask)
- Ask gentle questions about their support system ("Who do you trust?", "Is there someone you want to see?")
- Help them connect with family, friends, or community
- Include them in group activities when appropriate (meals, work, rituals)
- Assign a "buddy" (another person who checks in regularly and provides companionship)
- Facilitate group gatherings (shared meals, evening fires, group ceremonies)

**When to refer to deeper support:**
If the person shows signs of severe mental illness (suicidal thoughts, psychosis, severe depression), help them access a healer, elder, or experienced mental health person if available. In a post-collapse setting, this might be someone with prior psychology training or an experienced community elder.

**Step 6: Establish routine and structure.**

The nervous system recovers faster with predictability. Routines rebuild safety:

- Consistent mealtimes
- Regular sleep schedule
- Meaningful work or activities
- Group rituals (evening gathering, weekly celebration)
- Predictable interactions with caregivers

### Part 4: Supporting Trauma Recovery (Weeks to Months)

**Step 1: Monitor for ongoing trauma response.**

As days and weeks pass, acute stress should gradually improve. If symptoms persist or worsen, more intensive intervention is needed.

**Watch for PTSD development:**
- Intrusive thoughts or flashbacks (replaying the trauma involuntarily)
- Nightmares
- Avoidance of reminders
- Negative beliefs ("I am broken," "I cannot trust anyone," "The world is dangerous")
- Hypervigilance or startle response
- Emotional numbness

**Step 2: Encourage processing and meaning-making.**

Healing requires gradually processing the trauma, not pushing it away:

**Do this:**
- Encourage (do not force) the person to talk about their experience when ready
- Ask open-ended questions ("What happened?", "How are you coping?")
- Validate their feelings ("That must have been terrifying," "Your anger makes sense")
- Help them find meaning ("What have you learned?", "How have you grown?")

**Do NOT do this:**
- Push them to talk before they are ready
- Minimize their experience ("It could have been worse")
- Compare to others ("Someone else had it worse")
- Rush the process ("You should be over this by now")

**Step 3: Facilitate community rituals.**

Collective processing is powerful. Rituals honor the trauma, acknowledge the loss, and restore social bonds:

- **Acknowledgment ceremony:** Gather the community. Have those affected share their experience (optional). Acknowledge the loss. Renew commitment to each other.
- **Grieving ritual:** If death occurred, perform a funeral, memorial, or goodbye ritual specific to your culture.
- **Healing ceremony:** After several weeks, a community ceremony that marks the transition from crisis to recovery (a feast, a fire, a gathering).

**Step 4: Build resilience for future trauma.**

Prevention of re-traumatization requires strengthening:

- **Social bonds:** Invest in relationships before crisis strikes
- **Community cohesion:** Regular gatherings, shared celebration, collective decision-making
- **Agency:** Let people have a say in decisions affecting them
- **Skills:** Teach first aid, practical problem-solving, stress management
- **Meaning:** Connect people to purpose, values, contribution

## Verification

Successful psychological first aid shows:

- **Nervous system calm:** Heart rate normalizes, trembling stops, breathing slows
- **Emotional shift:** Tears come, expressions change; numbness begins to lift
- **Connection:** The person engages with you or others; isolation decreases
- **Understanding:** The person verbally confirms they understand their response is normal
- **Beginning recovery:** Within days to weeks, acute symptoms stabilize and gradually improve

Failure of first aid shows:

- **Worsening symptoms:** Anxiety or numbness increases
- **Isolation:** The person further withdraws from others
- **Risky behavior:** Substance abuse, self-harm, or violence
- **Suicidal statements:** "I cannot go on," "Everyone would be better without me"

## Common Mistakes

- **Rushing to fix it:** Psychological recovery takes time (weeks to months). Do not expect instant improvement.
- **Ignoring physical needs:** A hungry, exhausted person cannot process trauma. Meet basic needs first.
- **Forcing talk:** Do not push trauma survivors to recount their experience before they are ready. Let them initiate.
- **Comparisons:** Saying "At least you survived" or "Others have it worse" minimizes their experience and causes shame.
- **Isolation:** Leaving the person alone under the guise of "giving them space" deepens trauma. Stay present.
- **Expecting return to normal:** Trauma changes people. Recovery is not about returning to who they were, but integrating the experience and finding meaning.
- **Neglecting ongoing support:** Psychological first aid is the initial response. Recovery requires sustained community support for weeks and months.
