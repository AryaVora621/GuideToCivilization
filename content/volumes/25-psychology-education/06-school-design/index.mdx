## Overview

A post-collapse settlement needs education. Children must learn farming, building, crafts, medicine, and basic knowledge. But what kind of school works with limited resources, few trained teachers, and the need for young people to contribute labor?

This chapter covers:
1. School structure for post-collapse settings
2. Multi-age classrooms and heterogeneous grouping
3. Curriculum priorities (what to teach first)
4. Teacher-student ratios and community involvement
5. Making school work without textbooks or advanced technology

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of child development and learning
- Commitment to education despite resource constraints
- Access to at least one person willing to teach

## Materials & Tools

- Physical space (a building, covered shelter, or designated area)
- Basic supplies (pencil, paper or clay tablets, chalk)
- Teachers (trained or experienced adults)
- Learners (children and adolescents)
- Community support and scheduling flexibility

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: School Structure for Post-Collapse Settings

**Step 1: Choose a school model based on community needs.**

**Option A: Full-time school (ideal but resource-intensive)**

- School runs 5+ days per week
- Children attend most of the day (4–6 hours)
- Requires full-time teachers
- Works if community can spare children from labor

**Option B: Part-time school (mixed-effort)**

- School runs 2–3 days per week, or mornings only
- Children spend afternoons doing family labor (farming, crafts, care)
- Teachers may be part-time (farmers or crafts people)
- Suitable for smaller communities with limited trained teachers

**Option C: Rotational schooling (adaptive)**

- Children attend school in rotations (cohort A: Mon-Wed, cohort B: Thu-Sat)
- Older youth help younger children while waiting their turn
- Flexible for communities where labor fluctuates seasonally

**Option D: Apprenticeship-based (practical emphasis)**

- Children learn primarily through doing (apprenticed to farmers, builders, healers)
- Formal schooling is limited to basics (reading, writing, math, shared knowledge)
- Works well for craft-centered communities

**Step 2: Identify and recruit teachers.**

Teachers do not need formal credentials if they have knowledge and willingness to teach.

**Ideal teacher candidates:**
- Someone who has mastered a skill (farming, building, healing, reading/writing)
- Someone patient with children (or willing to learn patience)
- Someone respected in the community
- Someone with time (full-time or part-time depending on the school model)

**Support teachers:**
- Older youth (age 14+) can assist younger children
- Retired specialists can teach part-time
- Parents can rotate teaching days (each teaches their area of expertise)

**Step 3: Choose a schedule.**

**Example 1: Half-day full-time school (4–5 days per week)**
- Morning session: 9 AM to 12 PM (reading, writing, math, general knowledge)
- Afternoon: Children do family labor or apprenticeship
- This balances education with community contribution

**Example 2: Two-day intensive school (full-time)**
- Monday and Tuesday: School runs 8 AM to 4 PM
- Wednesday through Saturday: Community labor and learning
- Older students start taking formal apprenticeships

**Example 3: Seasonal schooling**
- Winter (when labor is light): School runs full-time
- Spring through autumn (planting, harvest, preservation): School is part-time or paused

Choose a schedule that works with your community's labor needs.

### Part 2: Multi-Age Classrooms

**Step 1: Understand multi-age classroom benefits.**

In a post-collapse setting, you likely cannot separate children into grade-level classes. Multi-age classrooms have advantages:

- **Older children teach younger:** A 10-year-old helps a 6-year-old with reading. This deepens both their learning (Vol 7, Ch 5 — teaching is powerful retrieval practice).
- **Less wasted time:** While a teacher works with one group, other ages are productively engaged.
- **Natural pacing:** Children move to the next level when ready, not when a calendar year ends.
- **Social development:** Older children develop leadership and patience; younger learn from older role models.
- **Efficiency:** One teacher can manage a range of ages with structure.

**Step 2: Organize multi-age classrooms by skill level, not age.**

Instead of "6-year-olds learn together," do this: "Reading Group 1 (early readers), Reading Group 2 (intermediate), Reading Group 3 (fluent)."

Children ages 5–10 might be in the same classroom but in different reading groups.

**Advantages:**
- A fast learner age 6 is grouped with others learning at the same pace (not held back by older, slower peers)
- A slower learner age 8 is not shamed by being with younger children; they are grouped by skill
- Flexible movement: As soon as a child masters a level, they move to the next

**Step 3: Design stations or centers for independent work.**

When the teacher is working with one group, others are at stations:

**Possible stations:**
- **Reading corner:** Older students read independently or listen to a story
- **Writing or copying:** Students practice writing by copying stories or composing their own
- **Math:** Counting, calculation practice, problem-solving games
- **Art or craft:** Drawing, painting, sculpting, building
- **Hands-on learning:** Gardening (outdoors), food preparation, simple construction
- **Peer teaching:** Older students teach younger (partner reading, explaining concepts)

**Supervision:** Older students or a parent volunteer supervises stations while the teacher works with one reading group.

### Part 3: Curriculum Priorities

**Step 1: Identify essential knowledge for your community.**

Curriculum should be based on what your community needs, not on a pre-collapse textbook.

**Universal essentials (all communities):**
- Reading and writing (so knowledge can be recorded and transmitted)
- Basic math (counting, fractions, measurement, geometry)
- Community history and values (who we are, what we believe)
- Practical skills (farming, food prep, building, healing, crafts specific to your region)

**Community-specific knowledge:**
- Farming methods suited to your climate and soil
- Trade skills that generate surplus (blacksmithing, weaving, pottery, carpentry)
- Natural history of your region (edible plants, animals, seasons, water sources)
- Governance and decision-making processes (how your community makes decisions)

**Step 2: Create a simple progression for each subject.**

**Reading example:**
- **Level 1:** Letter recognition and phonics (ages 4–6)
- **Level 2:** Decoding and simple sentences (ages 6–7)
- **Level 3:** Fluent reading and comprehension (ages 7–10)
- **Level 4:** Reading to learn from texts (ages 10+)

**Math example:**
- **Level 1:** Counting and number recognition (ages 3–5)
- **Level 2:** Addition and subtraction within 10 (ages 5–7)
- **Level 3:** Addition and subtraction within 100, beginning multiplication (ages 7–9)
- **Level 4:** Multiplication, division, fractions (ages 9–12)
- **Level 5:** Geometry, ratios, more complex fractions (ages 12+)

**Step 3: Prioritize practical skills and apprenticeship.**

As children age, formalize apprenticeships:

**Ages 5–8:** Basic literacy, numeracy, play, observation of skilled work

**Ages 8–12:** Literacy and numeracy mastery, plus apprenticeship (2–3 days per week) with a skilled adult

**Ages 12–16:** Intensive apprenticeship (3–4 days per week) plus continued education in reading, writing, math, history

**Ages 16+:** Primarily work and apprenticeship, with continued learning

**Step 4: Preserve knowledge in the curriculum.**

Beyond practical skills, schools preserve knowledge for future generations:

**What to teach (beyond practical skills):**
- Basic history: How human civilization developed, major discoveries, past mistakes
- Science foundations: How plants grow, how medicine works, how water purifies, basic astronomy
- Ethics and philosophy: What is fair? How should we treat each other? What do we value?
- Arts and music: So culture does not disappear, and so people have beauty and meaning in life

Set aside time each week for these, even if it is only 5–10 minutes of storytelling.

### Part 4: Teacher-Student Ratios and Community Teaching

**Step 1: Determine sustainable ratios.**

One teacher to many students is challenging but doable with structure.

**Realistic ratios for different models:**

- **Full-time, multi-age classroom (ages 5–12):** 1 teacher + 1 aide for 20–25 students
- **Half-day school:** 1 teacher for 15–20 students
- **Mixed with parent/elder assistants:** 1 lead teacher + 2–3 community helpers for 30 students

The key is structure. With stations and clear expectations, students can work independently while the teacher manages.

**Step 2: Train community members as teaching assistants.**

You do not need teachers with formal training. Community members can help:

- Listen to children read aloud
- Supervise stations and enforce expectations
- Help with hands-on activities (gardening, cooking, craft stations)
- Document progress (noting which children are struggling)

Training: A few hours of explanation about the school's approach and what to watch for.

**Step 3: Invite parents to teach their expertise.**

If your community has skilled people, invite them to teach part-time:

- Healer teaches medicine/health (1–2 sessions per week)
- Blacksmith teaches metalworking and mathematics (measurement, ratios)
- Farmer teaches agriculture and science
- Story-teller preserves oral tradition and history
- Elder teaches community history and values

This keeps school embedded in community while leveraging expertise.

### Part 5: Making School Work With Limited Resources

**Step 1: Use oral teaching, not just textbooks.**

Many pre-collapse schools rely heavily on textbooks. Post-collapse schools must use stories, discussion, and demonstration.

**Effective teaching methods without textbooks:**
- **Story-based learning:** History, science, and values come through stories
- **Demonstration:** Show how things work (water filtration, food preservation, building techniques)
- **Hands-on practice:** Children learn by doing (gardening, cooking, crafting)
- **Discussion and questions:** Guide children to discover ideas through dialogue
- **Repetition and memorization:** In the absence of written materials, children memorize key facts, stories, and skills

**Step 2: Use simple recording materials.**

When paper or parchment is scarce, use alternatives:

- **Clay tablets:** Flatten clay, mark with sticks, allow to dry. Reusable for temporary writing.
- **Chalk/charcoal on slate:** Erasable surface for practice
- **Tree bark or wood:** Carve letters or marks
- **Sand or soil:** Practice writing with a stick

Reserve precious paper/parchment for important documents and completed work.

**Step 3: Create a shared knowledge repository.**

Designate a place (a room or a box) where important documents are kept:

- Recipes and instructions (food preservation, healing, building)
- Stories and history
- Laws and agreements
- Scientific observations
- Maps and diagrams

Organize by topic. Multiple people should know where things are and how to add to the collection.

**Step 4: Use natural materials for learning.**

Take advantage of your environment:

- **Outdoors:** Teach math with stones, teach biology with plants, teach geography with landmarks
- **Community activities:** Learn woodworking by helping build, learn cooking in the communal kitchen
- **Seasonal work:** Planting teaches geometry (rows, spacing), harvest teaches math (yields, storage)

## Verification

A successful school shows:

- **Children learn:** Over time, children progress in reading, math, and skills
- **Retention:** Children remember what they learned (spaced practice, not cramming)
- **Practical competence:** Young people can contribute meaningfully to community (farming, building, caring, crafting)
- **Community involvement:** Community members see value in the school and support it
- **Children are engaged:** School is not a chore; children enjoy learning, ask questions, and participate
- **No drop-out:** Children do not flee school; they see its value

A failing school shows:

- **No progress:** Children read no better after a year of school
- **Absenteeism:** Children and families do not prioritize school; many miss sessions
- **Community skepticism:** "Why are we wasting time on school when we need labor?"
- **Limited practical skills:** Graduates cannot do necessary community work
- **Boredom and discipline problems:** Children are unengaged; behavior is difficult

## Common Mistakes

- **Replicating pre-collapse schools:** A factory model (grade levels, standardized curriculum, many students per room) does not work post-collapse. Adapt.
- **Over-emphasis on academic knowledge:** Spending all time on reading and math while practical skills (farming, building, healing) are ignored. Balance both.
- **Under-emphasis on values and history:** Teaching only practical skills creates a community with no shared story or values. Preserve culture and philosophy.
- **One-size-fits-all curriculum:** Teaching everyone the same thing at the same pace. Children learn at different rates. Flexibility is essential.
- **Ignoring community needs:** Teaching subjects (advanced chemistry) that the community will never use instead of what is essential.
- **No continuity:** Teachers change, leadership changes, enthusiasm wanes. Without institutional support, schools collapse. Build systems that survive personnel changes.
- **Isolation of school from community:** School is seen as separate from "real" work. The best schools integrate learning with community labor.
