## Overview

Music notation is a system for writing down music so musicians can play the same piece together without having to memorize it. Post-collapse settlements can use simplified notation systems to coordinate ensemble playing and preserve musical knowledge.

This chapter teaches practical music theory: scales (the basis of pitch), rhythm notation, harmony (multiple notes together), and how to lead an ensemble to play together.

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Understand the Diatonic Scale

The diatonic scale (do-re-mi-fa-sol-la-ti-do) is the foundation of Western music. It divides an octave (doubling of pitch) into 7 intervals.

**Pitch relationships:**

- An octave is a doubling of frequency
- Example: A at 440 Hz; A one octave higher at 880 Hz
- Dividing the octave into intervals creates a scale

**The diatonic scale:**

```
Do   Re   Mi   Fa   Sol  La   Ti   Do (octave)
1    2    3    4    5    6    7    8
```

**Interval sizes (in semitones, the smallest standard intervals):**

```
Do to Re:   2 semitones (whole step)
Re to Mi:   2 semitones
Mi to Fa:   1 semitone (half step)
Fa to Sol:  2 semitones
Sol to La:  2 semitones
La to Ti:   2 semitones
Ti to Do:   1 semitone
```

**Building a scale on instruments:**

- If a string's full length produces Do
- Half its length produces Do an octave higher
- 2/3 of its length produces Sol (5th interval)
- 3/4 of its length produces Fa (4th interval)
- These ratios create the diatonic scale

### Step 2: Create a Music Notation System

A simplified notation for post-collapse use—no complex staves or symbols needed.

**Simple notation:**

1. **Note names:**
   - Write scale degrees: 1 (do), 2 (re), 3 (mi), 4 (fa), 5 (sol), 6 (la), 7 (ti), 8 (do)
   - Or use syllables: do, re, mi, fa, sol, la, ti, do

2. **Duration (rhythm):**
   - Whole note: o
   - Half note: d
   - Quarter note: |
   - Eighth note: ||
   - Example: "1o 2d 3|" means: do (long), re (medium), mi (short)

3. **Arrangement:**
   - Write notes left to right (like reading text)
   - Separate phrases with a line break
   - Mark tempo (speed): Slow, Medium, Fast

**Example notation:**

```
Do-Re-Mi (children's song):
1 2 3
4 5 - -
5 6 7 8

Tempo: Medium
```

**Alternative: Graphic notation (for non-literate musicians):**

- Use circles or dots to represent notes
- Position higher or lower on the page (higher = higher pitch)
- Symbol size indicates duration (larger = longer)
- Simple for teaching musicians who can't read text

### Step 3: Understand Rhythm

Rhythm is the pattern of note durations and rests (silences).

**Basic rhythmic values:**

- Whole note (4 beats): o____
- Half note (2 beats): d__
- Quarter note (1 beat): |
- Eighth note (1/2 beat): ||
- Rest (silence): - (same duration as the note it replaces)

**Time signatures (meter):**

- 4/4 (common): 4 beats per measure, quarter note gets one beat
- 3/4 (waltz): 3 beats per measure
- 2/4 (march): 2 beats per measure

**Counting rhythm:**

1. Establish a steady beat (tap your foot, clap your hands)
2. Count beats: 1-2-3-4, 1-2-3-4 (for 4/4 time)
3. Each measure contains 4 counts
4. Notes fit within the count structure

**Teaching rhythm:**

- Start with simple patterns (quarter notes: 1-2-3-4)
- Progress to mixed rhythms (quarter and half notes)
- Use physical movement (clapping, dancing) to reinforce rhythm

### Step 4: Build Chords and Harmony

Harmony is multiple notes sounding together, creating richness.

**Simple chords (for string instruments):**

- Do-Mi-Sol (major triad): 1-3-5 (bright, happy)
- Do-Fa-La (minor-like): 1-4-6 (dark, sad)
- Sol-Ti-Re (dominant): 5-7-2 (tense, forward-moving)

**Chord progressions (sequences that sound good together):**

- Common progression: 1-4-5-1 (Do-Fa-Sol-Do)
- Minor-like: 1-4-6-5 (Do-Fa-La-Sol)
- These progressions are the basis of most melody-based music

**Teaching harmony:**

- Have one group hold a chord (e.g., Do-Mi-Sol)
- Have another group sing a melody
- The combination creates harmony

### Step 5: Teach Ensemble Coordination

Music requires multiple players to coordinate without a conductor or recording.

**Leading an ensemble:**

1. **Choose a leader:**
   - Often a percussion player (establishes steady rhythm)
   - Or a string player (establishes harmony)

2. **Establish tempo (speed):**
   - Leader counts: "1-2-3-4" at the desired speed
   - All musicians listen and synchronize

3. **Starting together:**
   - Leader counts: "Prepare-2-3-4, One-2-3-4"
   - All musicians start on beat "One"

4. **Listening while playing:**
   - Musicians listen to each other
   - Adjust tempo or pitch if drifting
   - Adjust volume (some instruments louder than others)

**Communication without written notation (traditional method):**

- Leader plays the melody first (musicians listen)
- Musicians learn by ear (listening and repeating)
- Repeat several times until musicians are confident
- Perform together

**Communication with notation (literate method):**

- Write or draw the piece beforehand
- Distribute notation to musicians
- Musicians learn their part by reading notation
- Rehearse together until coordinated

### Step 6: Understand Modes and Alternative Scales

While the diatonic scale is most common, other scales exist and are useful.

**Pentatonic scale (5 notes, very common in world music):**

- Do-Re-Mi-Sol-La (omit Fa and Ti)
- Creates a simpler, more open sound
- Easier to teach to beginners

**Modal scales (different starting points):**

- Dorian: Re-Mi-Fa-Sol-La-Ti-Do (minor-ish)
- Phrygian: Mi-Fa-Sol-La-Ti-Do-Re (exotic, Spanish)
- Lydian: Fa-Sol-La-Ti-Do-Re-Mi (bright, open)

**Alternative systems:**

- Whole-tone scale: C-D-E-F#-G#-A# (atonal, eerie)
- Blues scale: variations on pentatonic (soulful)

**Use in practice:**

- Most post-collapse music will use diatonic or pentatonic
- Alternative scales add variety and character

### Step 7: Compose Simple Music

Create original pieces using scales and chords.

**Composition steps:**

1. **Choose a scale (diatonic or pentatonic)**
2. **Choose a chord progression (e.g., 1-4-5-1)**
3. **Write a melody using scale notes**
4. **Ensure the melody harmonizes with the chords**
5. **Add rhythm (beat, patterns)**
6. **Teach to musicians; rehearse**
7. **Perform**

**Example simple composition:**

```
Scale: Diatonic (Do-Re-Mi-Fa-Sol-La-Ti)
Chord progression: 1-4-5-1 (Do-Fa-Sol-Do)

Melody:
3-3-3-4-5 (Mi-Mi-Mi-Fa-Sol)
5-5-5-6-7 (Sol-Sol-Sol-La-Ti)
8-7-6-5-1 (Do-Ti-La-Sol-Do)

Rhythm:
3| 3| 3| 4| 5d 5d (quarter-quarter-quarter-quarter-half-half)
etc.

Harmony (chord underneath):
Do chord (1-3-5) holds for first phrase
Fa chord (4-6-8) holds for second phrase
Sol chord (5-7-2) holds for third phrase
Do chord (1-3-5) holds for final phrase
```

## Verification

**Success looks like:**

- Musicians can read notation or learn by ear consistently
- Ensemble stays together in rhythm
- Multiple instruments play harmoniously
- Compositions are clear and repeatable
- New musicians can learn pieces from notation or by listening

**Failure looks like:**

- Musicians cannot coordinate (pulling apart rhythmically)
- Notation is too complex for musicians to follow
- No clear melody or harmony (music sounds chaotic)
- Musicians struggle to stay in key or tempo

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll use complex notation like modern sheet music"** — Too complex for post-collapse musicians. Use simple systems.

2. **"Music notation doesn't matter; just play by ear"** — Notation allows pieces to be preserved and transmitted. Worth the effort.

3. **"I'll write in a scale I invent"** — Stick to diatonic or pentatonic (well-understood). Custom scales confuse musicians.

4. **"I won't worry about rhythm; musicians will figure it out"** — Clear rhythm is essential for ensemble coordination.

5. **"I'll compose complex melodies with large intervals"** — Simple melodies with small intervals are easier to sing and remember.

6. **"One musician should play all parts"** — Ensemble music requires multiple players. Specialization improves quality.

7. **"I won't rehearse; musicians will learn the piece the first time"** — Rehearsal is essential. Plan for multiple run-throughs.

8. **"I'll skip teaching scales and theory; musicians will play by intuition"** — Basic theory allows musicians to learn faster and coordinate better.
