## Overview

Percussion instruments produce sound by striking or shaking. They are the easiest instruments to make post-collapse, requiring only basic materials (wood, hide, metal, stone). Drums and rattles can be built in hours; bells in weeks.

Percussion instruments establish rhythm and provide emotional intensity. A single drum supports singing; multiple drums create complex rhythms. By month 1, a settlement can have working percussion instruments and be performing music.

This chapter teaches construction of seven percussion types: drums, rattles, struck stones, bells, gongs, xylophones, and timpani.

## Materials & Tools

**For drums:**

- Wood (for frame/shell)
- Hide or leather (for drumhead)
- Cord or sinew (for tensioning)
- Tools: saw, chisel, knife

**For rattles:**

- Gourds or hollowed wood
- Pebbles or seeds (for rattling)
- Cord (for handle)

**For struck instruments:**

- Stones (for gongs, bells)
- Metal (for bells, gongs)
- Mallets (wood, bone)

**For bells:**

- Metal (copper, iron)
- Casting equipment or metalworking tools
- Clappers (metal or bone)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Make a Drum (Basic Frame Drum)

A drum consists of a wooden frame and a stretched hide (drumhead).

**Drum construction:**

1. **Frame:**
   - Select wood (oak, ash, hardwood preferred)
   - Carve or bend a circular ring, 30 to 60 cm diameter
   - Depth: 10 to 20 cm
   - Thickness: 2 to 5 cm (thicker = deeper sound)

2. **Drumhead:**
   - Animal hide (deer, goat, cow)
   - Clean hide (scrape hair, soften)
   - Stretch wet hide over frame
   - Allow to dry (tightens as it dries)

3. **Tensioning:**
   - As hide dries, it tightens
   - If too tight, soak hide and re-stretch
   - Cord or sinew sewn around edge holds hide in place

4. **Sound adjustment:**
   - Tight drumhead: high pitch, sharp sound
   - Loose drumhead: low pitch, deep sound
   - Adjust by re-stretching or replacing hide

**Playing:**

- Strike with hand or mallet
- Sound varies by where you strike (center = lower tone, edge = higher)

### Step 2: Make a Rattle (Simplest Percussion)

Rattles are the easiest percussion instrument—just a container with objects inside.

**Rattle construction:**

1. **Container:**
   - Dried gourd (natural, easiest)
   - Hollowed wood (bamboo, coconut)
   - Ceramic vessel with cover
   - Diameter: 10 to 20 cm

2. **Fill with sound-makers:**
   - Pebbles (clear sharp rattle)
   - Seeds (mellow rattle)
   - Beans (deeper rattle)
   - Amount: 1/3 to 1/2 fill the container

3. **Seal:**
   - For gourds: natural opening at the stem (seal with wax)
   - For wood: glue or seal the lid tightly
   - For ceramic: glue two pieces together

4. **Handle:**
   - Attach cord or stick to rattle so it can be shaken
   - Handle length: 20 to 30 cm

**Playing:**

- Shake rhythmically
- Can be played fast (jingle, shimmering sound) or slow (deep rattle)

### Step 3: Make a Struck Stone or Gong (Simple Metal)

Striking stones or metal creates resonant, ringing sounds.

**Stone gong:**

1. **Select stone:**
   - Smooth stone, 10 to 30 cm diameter
   - Dense stone (granite, marble) works better than soft stone
   - Test: strike with a mallet; should ring clearly

2. **Mounting:**
   - Suspend from cord or rope (allows vibration)
   - Or place on resilient surface (cloth, straw)

3. **Mallet:**
   - Wooden or bone striker, 20 to 30 cm long
   - Wrapped with cloth at the head (softens impact, changes tone)

4. **Sound:**
   - Strike the flat surface (produces ringing)
   - Edge strikes produce sharper sounds
   - Tone quality depends on stone type (thinner stones ring; thick stones thud)

**Metal gong (if metal available):**

1. **Metal blank:**
   - Cast or forged metal (copper, bronze ideal)
   - Thickness: 2 to 5 mm
   - Diameter: 20 to 60 cm

2. **Shape:**
   - Gongs have a raised center (bosses)
   - Hammer the metal to create the dome shape
   - Uneven shaping produces more complex tones

3. **Mounting and playing:**
   - Same as stone gong
   - Metal gongs produce richer, longer tones than stone

### Step 4: Make Bells (Metal or Clay)

Bells are complex instruments but create beautiful, ringing sounds.

**Simple bell (clay):**

1. **Form:**
   - Hand-build a ceramic vessel shaped like a bell
   - Thick walls (5 to 10 mm)
   - Opening at the bottom
   - Wall thickness affects pitch (thinner = higher)

2. **Firing:**
   - Fire in a kiln to stoneware temperature (1100 to 1200°C; see Vol 15: Ceramics)
   - Slow cooling prevents cracking

3. **Clapper:**
   - Suspend a stone or metal weight inside the bell
   - Attached to cord
   - When shaken, clapper strikes the bell walls

4. **Sound:**
   - Depends on wall thickness and shape
   - Large, thick bells produce deep tones
   - Small, thin bells produce high tones

**Metal bell (more complex):**

1. **Casting method:**
   - Requires metalworking (see Vol 10: Materials, metalworking sections)
   - Metal heated to liquid state and poured into a mold
   - Mold shaped like a bell
   - Complex but produces superior sound

2. **Tuning:**
   - Bell tone depends on thickness and shape
   - Can be modified by hammering (changes wall thickness)

### Step 5: Make a Xylophone (Multiple Struck Boards)

A xylophone is multiple wooden bars of different lengths, tuned to different pitches.

**Xylophone construction:**

1. **Bars:**
   - Select hardwood (rosewood, oak)
   - Cut bars of different lengths
   - Longest bar (roughly 40 cm) = lowest pitch
   - Shortest bar (roughly 20 cm) = highest pitch
   - Spacing between bars: 1 to 3 cm

2. **Mounting:**
   - Bars rested on two supports (wooden posts)
   - Bars not glued; can be adjusted
   - Resonators below each bar (hollow tubes) amplify sound

3. **Tuning:**
   - Longer bars = lower pitch
   - Shorter bars = higher pitch
   - Experiment by cutting bars and listening to pitches
   - Arrange from lowest (left) to highest (right)

4. **Playing:**
   - Strike with wooden mallets
   - Produces melodic tones (not just rhythm)
   - One or two players can create simple melodies

**Resonators (optional but improves sound):**

- Tubes or gourds under each bar
- Length matched to the bar frequency
- Amplifies the bar's tone

### Step 6: Make a Timpani (Large Tuned Drum)

Timpani are large drums with tunable pitch (like kettledrums).

**Timpani construction:**

1. **Shell:**
   - Large wooden bowl (60 to 80 cm diameter)
   - Shaped like a kettle (round, deep)
   - Carved from solid wood or constructed from wooden staves

2. **Drumhead:**
   - Large animal hide (goat, cow)
   - Stretched over the open top
   - Tensioned with rope or metal lugs

3. **Tuning mechanism:**
   - Rope or metal turn-screws around the rim
   - Tightening raises pitch; loosening lowers pitch
   - Allows drummer to change pitch during playing

4. **Stand:**
   - Wooden or metal stand supports the timpani
   - Must be stable (large drum is heavy)

5. **Playing:**
   - Struck with specialized mallets (softer than xylophone mallets)
   - Produces deep, resonant tones
   - Used for establishing fundamental bass tones in music

**Sound control:**

- Tighter drumhead = higher pitch
- Looser drumhead = lower pitch
- Adjust between songs or during extended pieces
- Most complex percussion instrument to master

### Step 7: Build a Percussion Ensemble

Combine multiple percussion instruments for rich, layered sound.

**Simple ensemble (3 musicians):**

1. **Drummer:** Drums (main rhythm)
2. **Rattler:** Rattles or shakers (adds texture)
3. **Striker:** Gongs or bells (adds resonance)

**Medium ensemble (5 to 7 musicians):**

1. **Drums:** 2 to 3 drummers (different sized drums for rhythm variation)
2. **Shakers:** 1 to 2 rattles/maraca players
3. **Pitched percussion:** 1 to 2 xylophone or bell players

**Large ensemble (10+ musicians):**

- Multiple drum types (frame drums, timpani, bassdrum)
- Multiple shaker types
- Pitched percussion (xylophone, bells, gongs)
- Creates complex, polyphonic rhythmic music

**Coordination:**

- Main drummer establishes rhythm
- Other percussionists follow and elaborate
- Coordination through listening (no written notation needed)
- Develops through practice and familiarity

## Verification

**Success looks like:**

- Percussion instruments produce clear, distinct tones
- Sounds carry for reasonable distance (at least 100 meters)
- Instruments can be played together without dissonance
- Musicians can coordinate rhythmically
- Community gathers to listen or participate

**Failure looks like:**

- Instruments produce dull thuds or rattles (poor construction)
- Sounds are weak or inaudible
- Percussion instruments clash (unpleasant together)
- Difficult to keep rhythm while playing

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll make a drum with tight hide for loud sound"** — Too-tight hide can crack. Tension should be firm but not extreme.

2. **"I'll use fresh hide without cleaning"** — Fresh hide rots and smells. Clean thoroughly and allow to dry.

3. **"Wooden mallets are unnecessary; hands are fine"** — Hands tire and can be injured. Make mallets (easier, better sound).

4. **"Metal gongs and bells are too complex; I'll skip them"** — Metal percussion adds richness. Even simple metal gongs are worthwhile.

5. **"I'll make all drums the same size"** — Different sizes produce different pitches. Variety creates richer ensemble sound.

6. **"Percussion instruments don't require practice"** — Mastering rhythm takes practice. Train musicians even as you build instruments.

7. **"I won't make a xylophone; it's too complex"** — Xylophone is relatively simple (just different-length bars). Worth the effort.

8. **"One person plays all percussion"** — Ensembles are much richer. Train multiple musicians to play different instruments.
