## Overview

Paint is pigment (colored powder) mixed with a binder (oily or sticky liquid) that holds the pigment to a surface. Post-collapse settlements can make paint from minerals, plants, and animal products found locally.

Painting creates permanent visual records (art on walls, cloth, or paper) that preserve culture, tell stories, and beautify settlements. By month 1, a settlement can be producing colored paints and creating art.

## Materials & Tools

**For pigments:**

- Minerals: ochre, charcoal, chalk, iron oxide
- Plants: indigo, madder, woad, turmeric
- Insects: cochineal (red dye)

**For binders:**

- Oil: animal fat, vegetable oil, nut oil
- Plant sap: tree resin, plant gums
- Egg: egg yolk (emulsion)
- Animal glue: hide glue

**For application:**

- Brushes: plant fibers, animal hair
- Surfaces: cloth, paper (parchment), pottery, stone walls
- Tools: mortar and pestle (grinding)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Collect and Process Mineral Pigments

Mineral pigments are the most durable, lasting centuries.

**Ochre (yellow, red, brown):**

1. **Source:** Clay rich in iron oxide
2. **Processing:**
   - Dig clay from deposits
   - Dry thoroughly in the sun
   - Grind to powder with mortar and pestle
   - Sieve to remove coarse particles
3. **Yield:** High (most clay contains useful pigment)

**Charcoal (black):**

1. **Source:** Burned wood or bone
2. **Processing:**
   - Burn wood in a fire until charred
   - Cool and grind to powder
   - Sieve for fine particles
3. **Yield:** High (easy to produce)

**Chalk (white):**

1. **Source:** Chalk deposits, ground shells, lime
2. **Processing:**
   - Mine or collect chalk
   - Grind to fine powder
   - Sieve and mix with binder

**Iron oxide (red, yellow, brown):**

1. **Source:** Rusty rocks, iron-rich soil
2. **Processing:**
   - Collect rusty rocks
   - Pound to powder
   - Sieve to remove coarse particles

### Step 2: Produce Plant Dyes as Pigments

Plant dyes create vivid colors but are less permanent than minerals.

**Indigo (blue):**

1. **Source:** Indigo plant (tropical) or woad (temperate)
2. **Processing:**
   - Soak leaves in water
   - Allow to ferment (creates indigo compound)
   - Dry and grind the resulting solid to powder
3. **Characteristics:** Brilliant blue; fairly durable

**Madder (red):**

1. **Source:** Madder plant root
2. **Processing:**
   - Dry roots
   - Grind to powder
   - Extract color by soaking in water (alum mordant helps)
3. **Characteristics:** Deep red; fairly durable

**Woad (blue-green):**

1. **Source:** Woad plant leaves
2. **Processing:**
   - Ferment leaves (like indigo)
   - Dry and grind
3. **Characteristics:** Blue-green; good for painting

**Turmeric (yellow):**

1. **Source:** Turmeric root
2. **Processing:**
   - Dry root
   - Grind to powder
   - Use directly as pigment
3. **Characteristics:** Golden yellow; less durable than others

### Step 3: Make Oil-Based Paint

Oil paint is durable and has a rich appearance.

**Paint preparation:**

1. **Grind pigment:**
   - Start with dry pigment powder
   - Grind finely with mortar and pestle

2. **Mix with oil:**
   - Animal fat or vegetable oil (roughly 2 parts oil to 1 part pigment by volume)
   - Mix thoroughly until smooth
   - Consistency should be creamy (adjustable by adding more oil or pigment)

3. **Storage:**
   - Store in covered container
   - Lasts for months (oil prevents oxidation)

**Painting:**

1. **Apply with brush or tool**
2. **Dries slowly** (12 to 48 hours for thick applications)
3. **Water resistant** once dry
4. **Durable** (lasts years or decades)

### Step 4: Make Water-Based Paint (Tempera)

Water-based paint dries quickly and is easy to work with.

**Tempera paint preparation:**

1. **Make binder:**
   - Mix egg yolk with water (1:1 ratio)
   - Or dissolve hide glue in water (produces liquid glue)

2. **Mix with pigment:**
   - Grind pigment finely
   - Add binder gradually until paint-like consistency
   - Stir thoroughly

3. **Storage:**
   - Use relatively fresh (will not keep long)
   - Or make in small batches as needed

**Painting:**

1. **Apply with brush to dry surface**
2. **Dries quickly** (30 minutes to 2 hours)
3. **Water soluble** until dry (can be reactivated with water)
4. **Moderately durable** (years; less than oil paint)

### Step 5: Make Fresco Paint (for Wall Painting)

Fresco paint is applied to wet plaster and becomes part of the wall as plaster dries.

**Fresco preparation:**

1. **Prepare wet plaster:**
   - Apply fresh lime plaster to wall (1 to 2 cm thick)
   - Keep damp
   - Must paint while plaster is still wet

2. **Use minimal binder:**
   - Pigment mixed with water only (no oil or egg binder)
   - Pigment chemically bonds with wet plaster

3. **Apply pigment:**
   - Brush pigment onto wet plaster
   - Pigment soaks into plaster
   - As plaster dries and hardens, pigment becomes locked in

**Fresco characteristics:**

1. **Very durable** (becomes part of the wall; lasts centuries)
2. **Cannot be modified** (must be done correctly the first time)
3. **Requires skill** (timing is critical; plaster must be right moisture level)
4. **Beautiful finish** (matte, clean appearance)

### Step 6: Paint Surfaces

Different surfaces require different preparation.

**Cloth painting:**

1. **Prepare cloth:**
   - Clean and dry
   - Pre-treat with sizing (thin glue) to prevent pigment absorption
2. **Apply paint:** Use oil or water-based paint
3. **Characteristics:** Portable, flexible, reusable

**Stone or pottery painting:**

1. **Prepare surface:**
   - Clean stone or pottery
   - Slightly dampen (helps paint adhere)
2. **Apply paint:** Any type works
3. **Characteristics:** Very durable, permanent

**Parchment or paper painting:**

1. **Prepare surface:**
   - Use good quality parchment (animal skin) or handmade paper
   - Can size if desired
2. **Apply water-based paint:** (oil paint can soak through)
3. **Characteristics:** Portable, preservable

**Wall painting (murals):**

1. **Prepare wall:**
   - Fresh lime plaster
   - Keep damp
2. **Use fresco technique:** (pigment + water into wet plaster)
3. **Characteristics:** Extremely durable, monumental

### Step 7: Organize a Studio and Train Apprentices

Create a space for artistic production.

**Studio setup:**

1. **Pigment workshop:** Space for grinding and mixing
2. **Painting workspace:** Sheltered, well-lit area
3. **Storage:** Protected from moisture (pigments and finished work)
4. **Drying racks:** For wet paintings

**Training apprentices:**

1. **Basic color theory:** How colors mix and combine
2. **Pigment preparation:** Grinding and mixing
3. **Technique:** Brushwork, layering, planning compositions
4. **Historical references:** Study prior artwork for inspiration

## Verification

**Success looks like:**

- Paint produces vibrant, consistent colors
- Paint adheres well to surfaces
- Dried paint is durable (resists fading or flaking)
- Painted works are visually appealing
- Apprentices are producing quality artwork

**Failure looks like:**

- Paint is muddy or dull-colored
- Paint flakes or cracks when dry
- Color fades quickly after creation
- Painted surfaces look uneven or poorly executed

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll use too much binder; thicker paint is better"** — Too much binder weakens paint. Use just enough for consistency.

2. **"I'll mix different pigment types without testing"** — Some pigments don't mix well. Test combinations first.

3. **"I'll paint on wet walls without letting plaster cure"** — Plaster must cure somewhat (not bone dry, not soaking wet). Test moisture level.

4. **"I won't test pigment durability before major projects"** — Always test pigments in small samples first. Some fade; some are fugitive.

5. **"I'll store oil paint in direct sunlight"** — Oil paint oxidizes in light. Store in cool, dark place.

6. **"I'll use low-quality brushes; they don't matter"** — Good brushes allow fine detail and smooth application. Worth the investment.

7. **"I'll paint on unprepared surfaces without sizing"** — Unprepared surfaces absorb paint unevenly. Always prepare surfaces.

8. **"I'll make large murals without planning the design first"** — Plan major works (sketch, composition) before beginning. Mistakes are permanent.
