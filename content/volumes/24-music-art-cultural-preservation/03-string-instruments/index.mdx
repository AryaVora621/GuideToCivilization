## Overview

String instruments produce melody and harmony by vibrating strings. Unlike percussion (rhythm) or wind (single melody), strings allow multiple notes to sound simultaneously, creating chords and complex musical expression.

String instruments are more complex than percussion but achievable with basic woodworking. A simple lyre can be built in weeks; a lute takes months. By month 2, a settlement can have functional string instruments and perform ensemble music combining rhythm (percussion) and melody (strings).

This chapter teaches construction of five string types: lyre, harp, simple lute, guitar, and dulcimer.

## Materials & Tools

**For wooden bodies:**

- Hardwood (spruce, maple, ash)
- Bent wood (for curved bodies)
- Thin wood (for soundboards)
- Tools: saw, chisel, knife, sandpaper

**For strings:**

- Gut string (animal intestine; traditional, best tone)
- Sinew (animal tendons; durable)
- Plant fiber (flax, hemp; less ideal but functional)
- Wire (if available; metal strings)

**For hardware:**

- Wood or bone (for tuning pegs)
- Metal (for bridges, tuning mechanisms)
- Glue (hide glue or wood glue)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Make a Lyre (Simplest String Instrument)

A lyre consists of a wooden body with strings stretched between two points.

**Lyre construction:**

1. **Frame:**
   - Wooden arms: two parallel sticks (60 to 90 cm long)
   - Yoke: crossbar connecting the tops of the arms
   - Base: wooden block 20 to 30 cm wide
   - Assembly: arms rise from the base and curve together at the top

2. **Soundboard (resonator):**
   - Thin wood (spruce preferred, 0.5 to 1 cm thick)
   - Covers the area between the arms (20 to 30 cm × 30 to 40 cm)
   - Glued to the frame

3. **Strings:**
   - Typically 5 to 10 strings
   - Attached at the base (pegs or holes) and at the yoke
   - Spacing: 2 to 3 cm apart
   - Material: gut or sinew

4. **Tuning:**
   - Strings of different lengths naturally produce different pitches
   - Longer strings = lower pitch
   - Shorter strings = higher pitch
   - All strings tuned to the same note (for simplicity) or to different notes for scales

**Playing:**

- Pluck strings with fingers
- Can play chords (multiple strings together) or melody
- Gentle, quiet sound

### Step 2: Make a Harp (Larger String Instrument)

A harp is larger than a lyre, with many strings (15 to 50) creating rich, complex sound.

**Harp construction:**

1. **Frame:**
   - Soundboard: large wooden board (80 to 120 cm tall)
   - Column: wooden post on one side (supports string tension)
   - Neck: wooden beam at the top (holds string anchors)
   - Assembly: frame triangular in cross-section

2. **Strings:**
   - 20 to 50 strings (more strings = richer sound)
   - Attached to the soundboard at the bottom
   - Attached to the neck at the top
   - Strings of progressively different lengths (longest at one end, shortest at the other)
   - Material: gut or sinew

3. **Tuning:**
   - Many tuning systems possible
   - Simple system: all strings tuned to the same note (monochord sound)
   - Complex system: strings tuned to a diatonic scale (do-re-mi-fa-sol-la)
   - Tuning pegs at the neck allow pitch adjustment

4. **Soundboard features:**
   - Curved shape (improves resonance)
   - Decorative carving (optional)
   - Smooth finish (allows fingers to slide across strings)

**Playing:**

- Pluck strings with both hands
- Can play complex melodies and chords
- Full, resonant sound

### Step 3: Make a Lute (Fretted String Instrument)

A lute is a plucked string instrument with a pear-shaped body and frets (raised ridges that help define pitch).

**Lute construction (simplified):**

1. **Body:**
   - Pear-shaped wooden body (25 to 30 cm tall)
   - Made from multiple wooden strips glued together (staves)
   - Soundboard: thin wood on the front
   - Back: thicker wood for structural support

2. **Neck:**
   - Long wooden stem (60 to 80 cm) extending from the body
   - Slightly curved (allows strings to not touch the body)
   - Width: 5 to 8 cm

3. **Strings:**
   - Typically 6 to 12 strings
   - Attached at the body base (bridge) and at the headstock (pegbox)
   - Higher strings produce higher pitches
   - Multiple strings often tuned in pairs (unison or octave)

4. **Frets:**
   - Raised ridges on the neck
   - Made from wood, bone, or gut
   - Positioned to create correct intervals (spacing determines pitch)
   - Pressing string against fret shortens the vibrating length, raising pitch

5. **Tuning pegs:**
   - Wood or bone pegs at the headstock
   - Allow string tension adjustment

**Playing:**

- Pluck strings with fingers or pick
- Press strings against frets to play different notes
- Can play melodic lines and chords
- Complex, mellow sound

### Step 4: Make a Simple Guitar (Fretted String Instrument)

A guitar is similar to a lute but larger, with a flat body instead of a pear shape.

**Guitar construction (simplified):**

1. **Body:**
   - Rectangular or figure-8 wooden body (25 to 30 cm wide)
   - Made from wooden strips glued together
   - Soundboard: thin wood on the front (0.5 to 1 cm)
   - Back: thicker wood on the back
   - Sound hole: circular opening in the soundboard (allows sound to project)

2. **Neck and headstock:**
   - Same as lute (60 to 80 cm long)
   - Headstock at the end holds tuning pegs

3. **Strings:**
   - Typically 6 strings (for simplicity)
   - Range from low to high
   - Tuning: typically E-A-D-G-B-E (same as modern guitar)

4. **Frets:**
   - Same as lute (raised ridges)
   - Spacing follows musical intervals

5. **Bridge:**
   - Wooden bridge attached to the soundboard
   - Strings rest on the bridge
   - Bridge height affects playability

**Playing:**

- Pluck strings with fingers or pick
- Press strings against frets
- Can play full chords and complex melodies
- Bright, resonant sound

### Step 5: Make a Dulcimer (Struck String Instrument)

A dulcimer is a string instrument struck with hammers rather than plucked.

**Dulcimer construction:**

1. **Body:**
   - Trapezoidal (wider at one end) wooden body
   - 60 to 120 cm long, 30 to 60 cm wide
   - Soundboard on top (thin wood)
   - Sound holes (allow vibration to project)

2. **Strings:**
   - Many strings (20 to 100+)
   - Arranged in groups (multiple strings per note)
   - Strings run over the soundboard lengthwise
   - Typically tuned diatonically (scale intervals)

3. **Bridges:**
   - Two wooden bridges support strings in the middle
   - Divides each string into multiple vibrating sections
   - Different lengths produce different pitches

4. **Playing surface:**
   - Top of the body is the playing area
   - Hammers (wooden mallets) strike the strings

**Playing:**

- Strike strings with wooden hammers
- Develops complex rhythmic and melodic patterns
- Bright, percussive sound

### Step 6: Make Strings (Gut and Sinew)

High-quality strings are crucial for good instrument sound.

**Gut strings (best sound):**

1. **Source:**
   - Clean animal intestines (sheep, goat, or cattle)
   - Scraped free of all tissue

2. **Processing:**
   - Soak in water for several hours
   - Scrape with a smooth stone or bone
   - Dry in the sun for several weeks
   - Bundle multiple strands for thicker strings

3. **Characteristics:**
   - Warm, mellow tone
   - Stretches initially (requires frequent re-tuning)
   - Durability: 6 to 12 months with use

**Sinew strings (durable):**

1. **Source:**
   - Animal tendons (deer, elk, or cow)
   - Dried tendons from the legs

2. **Processing:**
   - Pound with a stone to separate fibers
   - Twist fibers together into string
   - Wax for strength (optional)

3. **Characteristics:**
   - Sharper tone than gut
   - More durable (1 to 3 years with use)
   - Less stretching than gut

**Plant fiber strings (functional but less ideal):**

- Flax or hemp fibers twisted together
- More brittle than gut or sinew
- Acceptable for basic instruments

### Step 7: Build a String Ensemble

Combine different string instruments for rich ensemble sound.

**Simple ensemble (3 to 5 musicians):**

- One lyre or harp player (chords and base harmony)
- One or two lute or guitar players (melody)
- Percussion players (from Vol 24, Ch 2) for rhythm

**Performance:**

- Harp or lyre establishes chords (harmony)
- Lute or guitar plays melody
- Percussion provides rhythm
- All coordinate through listening

## Verification

**Success looks like:**

- String instruments produce clear, sustained tones
- Multiple notes can sound simultaneously (chords)
- Strings stay in tune for extended playing
- Instruments can be played together without clashing
- Melodies are clear and expressive

**Failure looks like:**

- Strings produce dull thumps (poor construction)
- Strings go out of tune quickly
- Difficult or impossible to play chords
- Strings buzz or rattle against the body
- Melodies are hard to hear or control

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll use any wood for the soundboard"** — Thin, light wood (spruce) resonates better. Hard, heavy wood deadens sound.

2. **"I'll string the instrument as tightly as possible for loud sound"** — Over-tightening damages the structure. Moderate tension is best.

3. **"Gut strings are too expensive; I'll use synthetic"** — Post-collapse, make gut strings. Synthetic is unavailable.

4. **"I won't bother making frets; players can memorize positions"** — Frets dramatically improve playability. Worth the effort.

5. **"I'll make all strings the same length"** — String length determines pitch. Different lengths create different notes.

6. **"Tuning pegs don't matter"** — Poor tuning mechanisms make instruments unplayable. Well-made pegs are essential.

7. **"One string per note is enough"** — Multiple strings (especially in harps and dulcimers) create richer sound. Use them.

8. **"I'll make strings from whatever fiber is available"** — Quality varies. Gut and sinew are superior; plant fiber is acceptable.
