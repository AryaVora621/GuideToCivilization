## Overview

Wind instruments produce sound by vibrating air columns. A simple flute can be made in hours from bone or bamboo; complex instruments take weeks or months. Wind instruments create clear melodies, carry sound over long distances (useful for communication and celebration), and complement percussion rhythm and string harmony.

By month 2, a settlement can have flutes, pipes, and whistles, creating a full musical ensemble combining rhythm (percussion), melody (wind), and harmony (strings).

## Materials & Tools

**For flutes and pipes:**

- Bamboo (ideal; natural hollow tubes)
- Bone (carved into tubes)
- Wood (hollowed or split tubes)
- Reed plants (for reed instruments)

**For tools:**

- Knife, chisel (carving holes)
- Drill (boring holes precisely)
- Saw (cutting tubes to length)
- Sandpaper or stone (smoothing)

**For reeds:**

- Reed plants (phragmites, arundo)
- Knife (carving reed shape)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Make a Simple Flute (From Bamboo or Bone)

A flute is a tube with holes; blowing across one end creates vibration.

**Bamboo flute construction:**

1. **Select bamboo:**
   - Hollow bamboo segment, 20 to 30 cm long
   - Diameter: 1 to 2 cm (larger = deeper sound)
   - Natural nodes (walls inside bamboo) create acoustic properties

2. **Mouthpiece:**
   - One end is the blowhole (where you blow air)
   - Cut a notch or angled edge (creates turbulence, produces sound)
   - Notch depth: 1 to 2 cm

3. **Finger holes:**
   - Drill or carve holes along the length
   - Typically 4 to 8 holes (depends on range desired)
   - Hole diameter: 0.5 to 1 cm
   - Spacing: determines which notes are playable
   - Start first hole about 5 cm from mouthpiece

4. **Tuning:**
   - Each hole, when open, raises the pitch
   - Covering holes with fingers lowers pitch
   - Adjust hole positions to create desired scale

5. **Finish:**
   - Smooth all edges (prevents splitting)
   - Burnish (smooth) the interior if possible

**Bone flute construction:**

1. **Select bone:**
   - Animal leg bone (deer, elk), 20 to 30 cm long
   - Large enough diameter to hollow (3 to 5 cm)
   - Hollow bones (bird bones) can be used as-is

2. **Hollowing (if necessary):**
   - Drill lengthwise through the bone
   - Ream out the interior to create a smooth tube

3. **Mouthpiece and holes:**
   - Same as bamboo flute
   - Bone is easier to carve precisely (better tuning)

**Playing:**

- Blow across the notched mouthpiece (not into it)
- Cover finger holes to change pitch
- Clear, bright sound

### Step 2: Make a Panpipe (Multiple Tubes)

A panpipe is multiple flutes of different lengths, creating different pitches.

**Panpipe construction:**

1. **Tubes:**
   - Select 5 to 12 bamboo segments of different lengths
   - Longest tube (30 cm) = lowest pitch
   - Shortest tube (10 cm) = highest pitch
   - Diameter: same for all tubes (consistency helps)
   - Cover one end of each tube (cork, wax, or tied fabric)

2. **Mouthpieces:**
   - Open end at the top of each tube (where you blow)
   - Notch or angled edge for each tube (same as single flute)

3. **Assembly:**
   - Tie tubes together side-by-side with cord
   - Arrange from longest (left) to shortest (right)
   - Spacing: 1 to 2 cm between tubes

4. **Playing:**

- Blow across the top of each tube sequentially
- Creates melody by moving lips across tubes
- No finger holes needed (each tube = one note)

### Step 3: Make a Simple Recorder (End-Blown Flute)

A recorder is a flute with a shaped mouthpiece that directs air across a sound-hole.

**Recorder construction:**

1. **Tube:**
   - Wood or bamboo, 25 to 40 cm long
   - Diameter: 1.5 to 2.5 cm
   - Hollowed lengthwise

2. **Mouthpiece head:**
   - Carved wooden block attached to the top of the tube
   - Creates a narrow windway (air passage)
   - Sound-hole: small opening (5 to 10 mm) where air strikes the tube interior

3. **Finger holes:**
   - 6 to 8 holes along the tube length (same as simple flute)
   - Spacing creates the desired scale

4. **Characteristics:**

- Easier to play than side-blown flutes (air doesn't have to be directed across an edge)
- Louder than flutes (sound-hole directs air efficiently)
- Mellow, warm tone

### Step 4: Make a Reed Pipe (Double Reed)

A reed pipe uses a vibrating reed (two pieces of cane) to produce sound.

**Reed construction:**

1. **Reed material:**
   - Cut cane or reed plant stem
   - Length: 5 to 10 cm
   - Width: 1.5 to 2 cm
   - Split the cane lengthwise into thin pieces

2. **Reed shaping:**
   - Carve two pieces to create a gap (aperture) when bound together
   - Aperture width: 2 to 5 mm (determines pitch)
   - The two pieces vibrate against each other when blown

3. **Binding:**
   - Wrap cord tightly around the reed pieces
   - Hold them parallel with a small gap between

4. **Pipe:**
   - Tube below the reed (30 to 50 cm)
   - Can be wood or bamboo
   - Finger holes allow pitch variation
   - Reed sits in a holder at one end of the pipe

5. **Sound:**

- Reeds produce a penetrating, buzzing sound
- Louder than flutes
- Used in oboes and clarinets (more complex versions)

### Step 5: Make a Simple Whistle (Bird Call)

A whistle is the simplest wind instrument—a tube with a mouthpiece and no finger holes.

**Whistle construction:**

1. **Tube:**
   - Bone, wood, or bamboo, 10 to 15 cm long
   - Small diameter (1 to 1.5 cm)

2. **Mouthpiece:**
   - Notched or angled end (same as flute)
   - Or plug the top 1 cm and create a narrow windway (like a recorder)

3. **Finger holes (optional):**
   - Can include 1 to 3 holes for pitch variation
   - Or make as a pure single-note whistle

4. **Use:**

- Simple signaling (long distance communication)
- Ornamentation in music (high, bright accents)
- Bird calls (hunting, signaling)

### Step 6: Make Bagpipes (Complex Wind Instrument)

Bagpipes are a bag with air supply and one or more pipes (advanced but achievable).

**Bagpipe construction:**

1. **Air bag:**
   - Animal bladder or stomach (cleaned)
   - Holds air that is squeezed to produce continuous sound
   - Size: 20 to 30 cm across

2. **Pipes:**
   - Chanter: melody pipe with finger holes (8 to 12 holes)
   - Drones: one or more pipes producing continuous note(s) (no finger holes)
   - Typical setup: 1 chanter + 2 to 4 drones

3. **Assembly:**
   - Pipes inserted into the bag at one end
   - Player holds the bag and squeezes while playing
   - Air flows from the bag through the pipes

4. **Playing:**

- Requires coordination: squeezing bag + fingering chanter + controlling air flow
- Produces loud, complex sound
- One player can sound like multiple instruments

### Step 7: Build a Wind Ensemble

Combine different wind instruments for varied, complex music.

**Simple wind ensemble (3 to 5 musicians):**

- One or two flute/whistle players (melody)
- One recorder player (secondary melody or harmony)
- One panpipe or bagpipe player (drones or chords)

**Full ensemble (combining Vol 24, Chapters 2–4):**

- Percussion (rhythm): drums, shakers, gongs
- Wind (melody): flutes, recorders, whistles
- Strings (harmony): harps, lyres, lutes

## Verification

**Success looks like:**

- Wind instruments produce clear, distinct tones
- Pitch can be controlled (raising and lowering by covering holes)
- Multiple instruments can play together in harmony
- Sound carries for distance (useful for communication)
- Musicians coordinate without written notation

**Failure looks like:**

- Instruments produce weak, breathy sounds
- Difficult to change pitch or control
- Holes are positioned incorrectly (wrong notes play)
- Wind leaks from cracks or poor seals
- Difficult to coordinate with other instruments

## Common Mistakes

1. **"I'll make holes without testing pitch"** — Hole position determines pitch. Test by drilling small holes, enlarging if needed.

2. **"I'll make all instruments the same size"** — Different sizes produce different pitches. Variation creates musical range.

3. **"The mouthpiece notch doesn't matter; any opening works"** — Notch shape affects tone quality. Smooth, well-shaped notches produce better sound.

4. **"I'll use fresh bamboo without drying"** — Fresh bamboo cracks as it dries. Use seasoned or completely dried bamboo.

5. **"Reed instruments are too complex; I'll skip them"** — Simple reeds are feasible. They add richness to ensemble sound.

6. **"I won't make panpipes; they're inefficient"** — Panpipes are simple and produce beautiful melodies. Worth the effort.

7. **"I'll make instruments without testing playability"** — Test each instrument before finishing. Adjustments are easier on unfinished instruments.

8. **"One musician can play multiple instruments in ensemble"** — Each musician should specialize. Coordination improves with practice and consistency.
