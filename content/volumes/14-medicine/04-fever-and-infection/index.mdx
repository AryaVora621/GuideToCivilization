## Overview

Fever kills more people than the original wound. A small cut becomes a fatal infection when fever spirals into sepsis. Your job is to recognize when fever is helping and when it is killing, manage temperature and hydration, spot the signs of sepsis early, and know what interventions work without antibiotics.

Fever is not always bad. A fever of 38–39°C is your body's defense: bacteria die, immune cells activate. A fever of 40°C and climbing, with delirium and organ shutdown, is sepsis — a medical emergency.

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Understanding and Measuring Fever

**Step 1: Understand normal body temperature.**

Normal core temperature: 37°C (98.6°F). Rectal temperature is 0.5–1°C higher. Oral temperature is 0.5°C lower. Armpit temperature is 0.5°C lower still.

**Step 2: Recognize the fever stages.**

**Fever 38–39°C (100–102°F):** Mild fever. Patient is conscious, can drink and eat. Fever is helping the body fight infection. Keep the patient comfortable; do not cool aggressively.

**Fever 39–40°C (102–104°F):** High fever. Patient may have chills, sweating, muscle aches. Risk of dehydration increases. Monitor hydration carefully.

**Fever 40–41°C (104–106°F):** Dangerous fever. Risk of delirium, seizure, and organ damage. Aggressive cooling is necessary.

**Fever >41°C (>106°F):** Critical. Heat damage to organs is occurring. This is a medical emergency.

**Step 3: Measure temperature accurately.**

Using a thermometer:
- Oral: Place under the tongue for 3 minutes with lips closed
- Rectal: More accurate; insert the thermometer 3–5 cm for 2 minutes
- Armpit: Less accurate; requires 5 minutes

Without a thermometer:
- Check pulse rate: Each 1°C of fever increases heart rate by ~10 bpm. A resting pulse of 100 suggests ~38°C. A pulse of 120 suggests ~40°C.
- Check skin: Hot, dry skin suggests fever. Flushed face and hot forehead suggest moderate fever.

### Part 2: Managing Uncomplicated Fever (38–39.5°C)

**Step 1: Do not aggressively cool mild fever.**

Fever is the body's healing response. Reducing fever below 38°C interferes with recovery. A patient with a 38.5°C fever fighting a wound infection is healing normally. Do not force cooling.

**Step 2: Ensure hydration.**

Fever causes sweating and fluid loss. Dehydration complicates infection. Give the patient water to drink regularly — at least 1 liter per 4 hours of fever.

**Step 3: Monitor wound care.**

Check the wound every 6 hours. Look for spreading redness, increased warmth, or pus. If these signs worsen, the fever is not controlling the infection; escalate care (see Part 4).

**Step 4: Use herbal fever support (optional).**

Willow bark, meadowsweet, or feverfew reduce fever slightly and ease discomfort. These are not antibiotics; they do not cure infection, but they make fever more tolerable. See Vol 31, Ch 4 for dosing.

**Step 5: Maintain nutrition.**

A patient fighting infection needs calories. Provide broth, grains, or any available food. Hunger worsens outcomes.

**Step 6: Monitor daily for 7–14 days.**

Uncomplicated wound infections often resolve in 7–14 days as the immune system wins. If fever persists beyond 2 weeks or worsens, the infection is complicated.

### Part 3: Managing High Fever (39.5–40.5°C)

**Step 1: Begin active cooling.**

At 39.5°C and above, cooling interventions are justified. The patient's comfort matters; high fever causes delirium and confusion.

**Step 2: Cool the body.**

- Sponge the patient with lukewarm (not cold) water. Focus on high-blood-flow areas: neck, armpits, groin, forehead.
- Use damp cloths on the forehead and under the neck.
- Increase airflow: fan or open windows (if weather allows).
- Remove excess clothing and blankets.

**Step 3: Alternate between cooling and blankets.**

As fever breaks, the patient will shiver and feel cold. This is normal — the body is resetting its "thermostat" down. Provide light blankets during shivering phases, then remove them as the patient becomes hot again.

**Step 4: Maintain hydration aggressively.**

High fever increases sweating dramatically. Force drinking if the patient is conscious: 250 ml (8 oz) of water every 30–60 minutes. If the patient cannot drink, use damp cloths to wet the mouth and lips.

**Step 5: Monitor urine output.**

Dark, concentrated urine means severe dehydration. Pale or clear urine is normal. If urine is dark and the patient has not been drinking, they are becoming dehydrated — increase fluid intake.

**Step 6: Plan for 24–72 hours of high fever.**

Severe infections (deep wounds, abscesses) often run high fevers for 2–3 days. This is expected if the patient remains conscious and hydrated. If fever exceeds 40.5°C, escalate to Part 4.

### Part 4: Recognizing and Managing Sepsis

**Step 1: Know the sepsis timeline.**

Sepsis usually develops 2–7 days after infection begins. A small cut on day 1, spreading redness by day 2–3, fever and chills by day 3–4, and sepsis by day 5–7 is a typical dangerous trajectory.

**Step 2: Recognize early sepsis signs.**

- **Fever >40°C that does not respond to cooling**
- **Rapid pulse >120 bpm at rest** (more than 100 bpm suggests sepsis in a resting patient)
- **Rapid breathing >20 breaths per minute**
- **Confusion or delirium** (patient does not know where they are or who you are)
- **Chills and shaking** (severe, not the mild shivering of normal fever)
- **Nausea and vomiting**
- **Spreading redness** around the wound (red streaks traveling up the arm or leg)
- **Swollen lymph nodes** (lumps in the neck, armpit, or groin)
- **Low blood pressure** (patient feels faint when standing)

**Step 3: Escalate wound care immediately.**

If sepsis is suspected, aggressive wound management is the only hope:

1. **Drain the abscess if present.** If the wound has formed a pocket of pus (feels swollen, fluid-filled), it must be drained. See Vol 4, Ch 2 (Wound Care) for abscess drainage.
2. **Debride dead tissue.** Remove any black, gray, or obviously dead tissue. This infected tissue fuels the sepsis.
3. **Leave the wound open.** Do not stitch or bandage tightly. Sepsis thrives in closed, airless spaces.
4. **Wash aggressively.** Clean the wound 3–4 times daily with clean water. Sepsis spreads in dirty wounds.

**Step 4: Manage the septic fever.**

- Cool aggressively as in Part 3.
- Maintain hydration obsessively: the patient's survival depends on preventing dehydration.
- Monitor urine output: dark urine signals kidney shutdown, a sign of severe sepsis.
- Monitor consciousness: increased confusion is a critical sign.

**Step 5: Use herbal or folk remedies with caution.**

- Honey applied to open wounds has mild antibacterial properties (though it is not a cure for sepsis).
- Vinegar or salt water applied to wounds may slow some bacteria (no scientific proof, but low risk).
- Herbal infusions of echinacea or goldenseal are traditional but not proven. They may help slightly.

**None of these replace drainage and cleaning.** They are supplements only.

**Step 6: Know the mortality rate.**

Sepsis is fatal in 30–50% of cases even with modern antibiotics and ICU care. Without antibiotics, the mortality rate is 70–90%. A patient with sepsis has days to weeks to live if untreated.

**Your job is:** Recognize it early, drain the infection source, keep the patient hydrated and cool, and prepare for the worst.

### Part 5: Amputation as Last Resort

**Step 1: Know when amputation is necessary.**

If the infection has progressed to gangrene (tissue death spreading up the limb), and the patient is conscious and capable, amputation of the limb may save the patient's life.

**Red flags for gangrene:**
- Black or gray tissue in the wound spreading despite treatment
- Severe swelling above the wound
- Red streaks traveling up the arm or leg (spreading rapidly)
- Patient is becoming delirious and septic despite drainage and cleaning

**Step 2: Make the decision.**

Amputation is traumatic and final. But if untreated gangrene will kill the patient, amputation is the only option. Discuss with the patient if conscious: "Your arm is dying. If we remove it now, you may survive. If we do not, the infection will kill you in days."

## Verification

**Uncomplicated fever is being managed correctly if:**
- Fever stays below 40°C
- Patient remains conscious and able to drink
- Wound shows no spreading redness or increasing pus
- Patient recovers within 2–3 weeks

**High fever is being managed correctly if:**
- Fever responds to cooling (decreases by 0.5–1°C per hour of cooling)
- Patient remains hydrated (pale urine, alert)
- Fever breaks by day 3–5 and does not return as severely

**Sepsis is being recognized and managed if:**
- You have recognized the signs early (within the first 24 hours of sepsis symptoms)
- You have drained any abscess
- You have aggressively managed temperature and hydration
- The patient survives (though prognosis remains poor)

## Common Mistakes

- **Cooling mild fever aggressively.** Fever of 38–38.5°C is helping the body fight infection. Aggressive cooling interferes with recovery. Let mild fever run unless the patient is in extreme discomfort.
- **Not recognizing sepsis early.** The window to save a septic patient is narrow (first 24–48 hours of sepsis symptoms). If you wait until the patient is delirious, it is too late.
- **Assuming fever = infection.** Fever can result from heatstroke, dehydration, or inflammation. Check for wound infection specifically.
- **Not draining the abscess.** Antibiotics work because they diffuse into tissue. Without antibiotics, a pus-filled pocket will not heal without drainage. Drain it or the patient dies.
- **Closing a septic wound.** A closed wound in a septic patient is a coffin. Leave it open to drain. Stitches come later (if the patient survives).
- **Forcing food on a vomiting patient.** If the patient is vomiting from sepsis, food will not help and may cause aspiration. Focus on hydration alone.
- **Giving up too early.** A patient who appears hopeless may still survive with aggressive management. Keep trying until the patient is objectively dead.
