## Overview

Wounds kill through blood loss or infection. A deep cut can cause death in hours from bleeding. A minor scratch can cause death in days from infection. This chapter covers the three essential interventions: stop bleeding, clean the wound, and close it properly.

The goal is not perfection. You will not have sterile gloves, modern sutures, or antibiotics. You will have clean cloth, sharp needles, plant fiber, fire, and your knowledge. That is enough to prevent most wound deaths.

Infection is your main enemy. A cleaned, properly closed wound often heals without antibiotics. An infected wound kills even with antibiotics if the infection becomes severe. Prevention (keeping the wound clean, closing it, monitoring for signs of infection) is your primary strategy.

## Materials & Tools

### Essential Equipment
- Cloth (linen, cotton, or clean fabric)
- Sharp blade (for debriding damaged tissue)
- Needle (bone, antler, or sharpened metal)
- Thread (plant fiber twisted together, sinew from animal tendons, or hair)
- Fire (for sterilization)
- Clean water (boiled is better, but any clean water is acceptable)
- Bandage material (cloth strips, plant fiber)

### Optional but Valuable
- Honey (natural antibacterial)
- Salt (for cleaning solution)
- Plant poultice (plantain, yarrow, or other plants with antimicrobial properties—see Vol 4, Ch 9)
- Alcohol (for disinfection, if available)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Assess the Wound

Before you do anything, determine the severity:

**Minor wound (flesh wound, less than 1 cm deep):**
- Bleeding is minor (stops with pressure in less than 5 minutes)
- Wound is clean or only lightly contaminated
- No visible fat, muscle, or deeper layers

**Action:** Clean, close, monitor.

**Moderate wound (deep flesh, 1–3 cm, or gaping):**
- Bleeding is significant but controllable with pressure (10–15 minutes)
- Wound edges are separated (gaping open)
- You can see tissue layers but not major organs

**Action:** Stop bleeding, clean, close with sutures, monitor closely.

**Severe wound (deep, more than 3 cm, or involves major blood vessels or organs):**
- Bleeding is severe (continues after 15+ minutes of pressure)
- Wound involves chest, abdomen, neck, or major joint
- You can see fat, muscle, organs, or bone

**Action:** Stop bleeding immediately (tourniquet if necessary), keep wound clean, stabilize, and seek help if possible. This chapter covers stopping bleeding and stabilization. Field surgery (amputation, organ suturing) is covered in Vol 4, Ch 7.

### Step 2: Stop Bleeding (For All Wound Types)

**Step 2a: Apply direct pressure.**

Use clean cloth to apply firm, continuous pressure to the wound. Do not remove the cloth to check progress. Leave it in place for at least 5 minutes.

- Minor wound: Usually stops after 5 minutes.
- Moderate wound: Usually stops after 10–15 minutes.
- Severe wound: May not stop with pressure alone (move to Step 2b).

**Step 2b: Elevate (if possible without causing other injury).**

If the wound is on an arm or leg and you have not broken a bone, raise it above heart level. This reduces blood pressure in the limb and helps clotting.

**Step 2c: Apply pressure to pressure points (if wound continues bleeding).**

Large blood vessels can be compressed between muscle and bone:

- Arm bleeding: Press the artery against the bone on the inside of the upper arm (between shoulder and elbow). Locate the pulse with your fingers. Press firmly. Maintain pressure while continuing to apply cloth directly to the wound.

- Leg bleeding: Press the artery against the bone in the groin area (where leg meets torso). The pulse is located on the inner thigh. Maintain pressure.

- Neck or head bleeding: Do not apply pressure to the neck (risk of cutting off air). Instead, apply direct pressure to the wound and tilting the head to compress the jugular vein against the neck muscles.

**Step 2d: Tourniquet (only for severe limb bleeding that will not stop).**

A tourniquet is a last resort—it cuts off blood to the entire limb below it, and tissue dies after 2–4 hours. Use it only if the alternative is death from bleeding.

- Apply a cloth strip (5–10 cm wide) around the limb above the wound, between the wound and the heart.
- Tighten until bleeding stops.
- Apply as close to the wound as possible (but above it).
- Mark the time the tourniquet was applied (write on the skin if you have charcoal or ink).
- Tourniquet should be tight enough to stop bleeding but not so tight it cuts off the limb completely and causes immediate pain (intense pain means loss of circulation to nerves, which is a bad sign).

### Step 3: Clean the Wound

Do not let a wound sit dirty. Infection grows fast in contaminated tissue.

**Step 3a: Remove large debris.**

Use clean cloth or a clean stick to gently remove obvious dirt, leaves, or debris from the wound. Do not dig or probe—you can cause more damage and introduce more bacteria deeper.

**Step 3b: Prepare cleaning solution.**

- Best option: Boiled water (cool it first). Boiling kills bacteria.
- Alternative: Salt water (1 teaspoon salt per liter of water). Salt has mild disinfectant properties.
- If no other option: Any clean water is better than leaving the wound dirty.

**Step 3c: Flush the wound.**

Pour water gently over the wound or use cloth to wipe away debris and blood. The goal is to remove visible contamination, not to scrub (scrubbing causes more bleeding and damage).

- For a minor, clean wound: One or two careful washes are enough.
- For a contaminated wound: Wash multiple times (3–5) until no visible debris remains.

**Step 3d: Remove dead tissue (debridement).**

This is optional for minor wounds but important for deeper wounds:

- Look for tissue that is obviously dead: gray-black color, soft, broken apart.
- Use a clean, sharp knife to carefully remove obviously dead tissue.
- Do not cut living tissue (bleeding tissue is alive; non-bleeding tissue is dead).
- Removing dead tissue reduces infection risk (bacteria grow better in dead tissue).

**Step 3e: Allow the wound to bleed slightly.**

Clean bleeding washes out bacteria. Let the wound bleed for 1–2 minutes after cleaning if it continues. Do not re-apply pressure unless bleeding is heavy.

### Step 4: Decide on Closure Method

**Minor wounds (clean, not gaping):**
Option A: Leave open, monitor for infection.
Option B: Close with butterfly strips (see Step 5).

**Moderate wounds (gaping or deep):**
Close with sutures (Step 6) or leave open if risk of infection seems high (see risk assessment below).

**Severe wounds:**
Stabilize. Do not close if infection risk is extremely high (e.g., animal bite, dirty/rusty metal). Leave open and monitor.

**Risk factors for infection (increases if wound has any of these):**
- Contact with soil, animal spit, rust, or feces
- Bite wound (human, animal, insect)
- Jagged edges or crushed tissue
- More than 6 hours old
- Inability to keep it clean in the coming days

**If infection risk is moderate to high:** Leaving the wound open is safer than closing it. An open wound can drain pus if infection develops. A closed wound can trap infection inside and cause abscess (trapped pus collection) or sepsis (infection entering the bloodstream).

**Default strategy:** For heavily contaminated wounds, leave open for 24–48 hours. Monitor for signs of infection. If the wound stays clean and shows no signs of infection after 48 hours, you can close it then (or leave it open if it seems to be healing well).

### Step 5: Close the Wound with Butterfly Strips (For Minor Gaping Wounds)

Butterfly strips are simple closures for minor wounds that are gaping but not deep.

**Step 5a: Prepare the strips.**

- Tear cloth into strips 2–3 cm wide and 10–15 cm long.
- If you have any adhesive (tree sap, plant glue), rub it on one side. If not, the strips will still work by holding the wound edges together through tension.

**Step 5b: Position the wound edges.**

- Gently pull the wound edges together.
- Hold them in the closed position with your fingers or an assistant.

**Step 5c: Apply the strips.**

- Place one strip perpendicular to the wound, bridging across the wound.
- Press the strip down firmly onto skin on both sides of the wound.
- Apply a second strip parallel to the first, 1–2 cm away.
- Apply a third strip, if needed, parallel to the others.

**Step 5d: Cover with bandage.**

- Place cloth over the strips to protect from dirt.
- Change the bandage daily and monitor for infection signs.

Butterfly strips typically hold for 3–5 days. After that, the wound is usually strong enough not to gape, and strips can be removed.

### Step 6: Close the Wound with Sutures (For Deeper or Larger Wounds)

Suturing is more complex than butterfly strips but necessary for wounds that will not stay closed on their own.

**Step 6a: Prepare needle and thread.**

- Needle: Bone needle (made by sharpening a thin bone splinter) or a thorny plant spine works as well as metal.
- Thread: Sinew (extracted from animal tendon), thin plant fiber twine, or even thin strips of cloth work. Thread should be strong enough that it does not snap when you pull it.
- Sterilize: Hold the needle in flame (5 seconds) until it is hot. This kills most bacteria. Let it cool before using (touch to a drop of water to cool it quickly).

**Step 6b: Practice the suture stitch (if you have not done this before).**

Spend time practicing on cloth before doing a wound. The stitch you will use is the "simple interrupted stitch":

- Push the needle through the wound edge on one side, about 3–5 mm from the edge.
- Pull thread through until about 5 cm remains on the other side.
- Push the needle through the opposite edge, also 3–5 mm from the edge, at approximately the same depth.
- Pull thread tight so the wound edges meet.
- Tie a knot (square knot: right over left, left over right) to secure.
- Move to the next stitch, 1–1.5 cm away from the first.
- Repeat until the entire wound is closed.

**Step 6c: Suture the wound.**

The goal is to bring the deepest layers of tissue together first, then the surface layers. For a wound with clear layers (skin, fat, muscle):
- First suture the deepest layer (muscle, if visible)
- Then suture the fat layer
- Finally suture the skin

In a survival situation, you likely do not have the skill or materials for multi-layer suturing. Instead, follow these steps:
- Suture the deepest part of the wound that you can access without causing more damage
- Space sutures 1–2 cm apart
- Pull tight enough to bring the edges together but not so tight that you cut off blood supply (if tissue blanches/turns white, you are too tight)

**Step 6d: Tie off the thread.**

At the end, tie the thread using a square knot (right over left, left over right). Make two knots if you have extra thread. Cut the excess thread about 1 cm from the knot (leaving a tail helps you find the knot later to remove the stitch).

**Step 6e: Cover the wound.**

- Apply honey (if available) as an antibiotic coating.
- Cover with clean cloth.
- Change the bandage daily.

**Suture removal timing:**

Sutures are typically left in place for 7–14 days depending on location:
- Face: 3–5 days (heals fast, less stress)
- Arm/leg: 7–10 days
- Joint areas (elbow, knee): 10–14 days (joints move, put stress on sutures)

After the time has passed, carefully cut each knot and pull the suture out. The wound should have enough strength by then to stay closed on its own.

### Step 7: Manage the Wound After Closure

**Step 7a: Keep it clean.**

Change the bandage daily. Use clean cloth. If the wound gets dirty, you can rinse it gently with clean water.

**Step 7b: Monitor for infection.**

Check the wound daily for signs of infection:

**Early signs (appear within 24–48 hours, still treatable):**
- Increased redness or warmth around the wound
- Swelling increases after the first day (some swelling is normal in the first 24 hours)
- Pus appears (yellow, green, or cloudy drainage)
- Increasing pain

**Action:** Clean more frequently. Apply heat (warm cloth applied for 10 minutes, 2–3 times daily) to help the body fight the infection. Consider using plant poultices with antimicrobial properties (plantain, yarrow).

**Late signs (appear after 3+ days, more serious):**
- Fever (body temperature >38°C / 100.4°F)
- Red streaks extending from the wound up the limb (sign of spreading infection)
- Swelling that spreads far from the wound
- Pus that is thick, foul-smelling, or increasingly copious
- Wound that reopens or breaks apart

**Action:** See "Treating Wound Infection" (Step 8).

**Step 7c: Keep weight off a limb wound (if possible).**

If the wound is on a leg, try not to walk on it for the first 2–3 days. Walking puts strain on the sutures and increases swelling. If you must move, keep the leg elevated when resting.

### Step 8: Treating Wound Infection

If signs of infection appear, escalate care immediately. Untreated infection can spread to the bloodstream (sepsis) and cause death.

**Early infection (localized, not spreading):**

- Open the wound partially (if you closed it). An infected wound needs to drain. If the wound is closed, it can trap pus inside (abscess).
- Clean aggressively: Flush with boiled water 2–3 times daily.
- Apply heat: Warm cloth 2–3 times daily for 10 minutes.
- Poultice: Plantain (crushed leaves applied directly to wound) or yarrow.
- Elevation: Keep the wound elevated above heart level if possible.
- Oral medications: If you have herbal antibiotics (see Vol 4, Ch 9), use them.

**Late infection (spreading, or fever present):**

Follow the same steps above, plus:
- Rest completely. Infection depletes energy; the body needs rest to fight it.
- Hydrate and eat. Support the immune system.
- Monitor body temperature every few hours (if you have a thermometer) or feel the forehead frequently (fever feels hot).
- If fever is above 39°C (102°F), or if red streaks extend far from the wound, the infection is becoming systemic (throughout the body). This is life-threatening. See Vol 4, Ch 4: Fever & Infection for additional steps.

## Verification

**Successful wound care results in:**

- Bleeding is controlled (stops after 5–15 minutes depending on severity)
- Wound is visibly clean (no dirt, debris, or foreign material visible)
- Wound edges are pulled together if it was gaping
- No signs of infection appear in the first 24–48 hours

**If infection does appear:**

- You detect it early (before fever, before red streaks spreading)
- You begin aggressive cleaning and drainage
- Infection resolves within 3–5 days

**If the wound fails:**

- Bleeding continues after 30+ minutes of pressure (you may need a tourniquet; see severe wound instructions)
- Infection spreads (fever, red streaks, or pus increasing despite treatment): You need systemic antibiotics or more aggressive care (see Vol 4, Ch 7: Field Surgery)
- Sutures burst open or wound reopens: Clean and re-suture, or leave open if infection risk is high

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Closing a contaminated wound immediately.**

A dirty wound closed too early traps bacteria inside, where they multiply rapidly. Always clean first. If contamination is heavy (soil, animal bite, rust), wait 24–48 hours before closing.

**Mistake 2 — Suturing too tightly.**

Tight sutures cut off blood supply to the tissue. The tissue dies, encouraging infection. Sutures should hold edges together but not strangle them. If tissue blanches (turns white), you are too tight.

**Mistake 3 — Ignoring early signs of infection.**

Early infection (mild redness, slight swelling, minimal pus) is easily treated with cleaning and heat. Late infection (fever, spreading red streaks, thick pus) requires emergency treatment. Act at the first sign.

**Mistake 4 — Using dirty water.**

"Any water is better than none" is not quite true. Water full of bacteria (stagnant pond water, water from downstream of a latrine) can introduce more infection than it prevents. Boil water if possible. If boiling is not possible, use moving water (stream) rather than standing water (pond).

**Mistake 5 — Covering a wound before it is clean.**

Bandaging a dirty wound speeds up infection. Always clean before bandaging.

**Mistake 6 — Over-dressing a minor wound.**

A scraped knee or small cut does not need a bulky bandage. If the wound is clean and not deep, expose it to air (unless it is in a very dirty environment). Air helps drying and healing. Constant dampness encourages infection.

**Mistake 7 — Removing sutures too early.**

The wound is not strong until day 7–14, depending on location. Removing sutures early (before day 5) can cause the wound to reopen. Leave them in place as long as possible, then remove only after you are sure the wound has healed underneath.

**Mistake 8 — Not recognizing a wound that is too deep to close.**

If you can see bone, organ, or muscle fiber deeper than the wound, you are not equipped for closure (unless the bleeding is severe). Stabilize and seek help. Attempting to close a very deep wound can cause more damage.
