## Overview

You cannot see germs, but you can understand how they spread and how to kill them. Infection kills more people in collapse scenarios than any single cause. But infection is preventable. This chapter teaches you to sterilize instruments and environments using heat, chemicals, and physical methods.

The stakes are high: a contaminated scalpel can turn a routine surgery into amputation or death. A sterile dressing prevents wound infection that would be fatal without antibiotics. Sterilization is foundational to post-collapse medicine.

This chapter is practical: you will learn to prepare tools and spaces for medical procedures, understand what levels of cleanliness matter for which tasks, and recognize when sterilization has failed.

## Materials & Tools

**For heat sterilization:**
- Water (boiling requires clean water)
- Fire or heat source capable of sustaining boiling temperature (100 degrees Celsius at sea level)
- Metal or clay vessels (glass works but breaks easily)
- Metal instruments to be sterilized
- Cloth or linen for wrapping

**For chemical sterilization:**
- Alcohol (ethanol or isopropyl, minimally 70 percent concentration)
- Household bleach (sodium hypochlorite, 5–8 percent concentration)
- Iodine solution or tincture of iodine if available
- Vinegar (acetic acid)
- Salt (sodium chloride)
- Containers for chemical storage

**For steam sterilization (field method):**
- Pot or vessel with lid
- Cloth or packed material to create steam environment
- Heat source

**For UV sterilization (supplemental):**
- Direct sunlight (UV radiation)
- Clear glass or plastic containers
- Time (4–6 hours minimum of direct sun)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Heat Sterilization: Boiling

Boiling is the most reliable sterilization method with simple equipment. It kills most microorganisms within 15–20 minutes at 100 degrees Celsius.

#### Step 1: Prepare Instruments

Remove all visible blood, tissue, and debris from instruments before sterilization. Organic material shields microorganisms from the sterilizing agent. Use clean cloth or running water to wipe instruments. This is the cleaning step—not sterilization yet.

For instruments with lumens (hollow tubes, syringes, needles), flush with clean water multiple times to remove internal material.

#### Step 2: Arrange Instruments for Boiling

Place instruments in a clean metal or clay vessel. Do not stack them tightly; water must circulate around each item. Space them out. If you are sterilizing surgical instruments, place them in a bundle wrapped loosely in clean cloth, leaving gaps for water circulation.

#### Step 3: Fill the Vessel with Water

Cover instruments with at least 5 centimeters of water. Water must reach every surface. For thick bundles, ensure water penetrates the interior.

#### Step 4: Bring to a Rolling Boil and Maintain Temperature

Place the vessel on high heat until water reaches a vigorous rolling boil (large bubbles breaking the surface continuously). Once boiling, maintain this temperature for 20 minutes minimum. The clock starts when water first reaches a rolling boil, not when you place the instruments in hot water.

Critical note: At sea level, boiling water is 100 degrees Celsius. At higher elevations (above 1,000 meters), boiling point is lower. For every 300 meters of elevation, boiling point drops about one degree Celsius. At 3,000 meters elevation, boiling is 91 degrees Celsius. Increase boiling time: add 5 minutes for every 300 meters above sea level. So at 3,000 meters, boil for 40 minutes instead of 20.

#### Step 5: Remove and Dry Instruments

Use sterile tongs or clean cloth (boiled separately) to remove instruments from water. Do not use unsterilized tongs, as this recontaminates the instruments. Place them on a clean cloth in a clean, sheltered location (not on the ground). Air drying is preferred; heat drying (near fire, but not touching flame) prevents moisture that encourages regrowth.

Once dry, instruments remain sterile if kept in a clean container away from dust and water splash. Store in cloth wrapping, sealed bags, or clean containers with lids.

#### Step 6: Test That Boiling Occurred

You cannot directly verify internal temperatures, but you can verify that boiling occurred: instruments emerge hot, water has been visibly churning, and steam is visible. If any doubt exists, repeat the boiling cycle.

### Chemical Sterilization: Alcohol

Alcohol (70 percent ethanol or isopropyl alcohol) kills most vegetative bacteria and viruses rapidly. It does not kill spores, so it is not a complete sterilization method, but it is fast and useful for pre-sterilization or maintenance.

#### Step 1: Clean Instruments Thoroughly

Remove blood and organic material with water and cloth before alcohol sterilization. Alcohol does not penetrate organic material effectively.

#### Step 2: Immerse in 70 Percent Alcohol

Prepare a 70 percent alcohol solution. If you have 95 percent alcohol, dilute it with one part water for every one part alcohol. For example, mix 1 cup of pure alcohol with 0.33 cups of water to approximate 70 percent strength.

Submerge cleaned instruments completely in the alcohol solution. Make sure all surfaces are in contact with liquid. Leave for 10 minutes minimum.

#### Step 3: Air Dry

Remove instruments and place them on clean cloth. Allow them to air dry completely. Alcohol evaporates quickly, leaving instruments dry and sterile (so long as they remain undisturbed).

**Limitations of alcohol:** Does not sterilize spore-forming bacteria. Does not penetrate thick bundles of instruments well. Evaporates quickly, so containers must be sealed. Use alcohol sterilization when speed matters and complete sterilization is less critical (such as skin prep before injection).

### Chemical Sterilization: Bleach

Household bleach (sodium hypochlorite, 5–8 percent) is a powerful disinfectant. A 0.5 percent solution (one part bleach to 9 parts water) kills most bacteria, viruses, and fungi in 10 minutes.

#### Step 1: Dilute Bleach Properly

Undiluted bleach damages instruments and is corrosive. Create a sterile solution:
- Measure 1 part household bleach (approximately 6 percent concentration)
- Measure 9 parts clean water
- Mix in a clean container

Example: 100 milliliters of bleach + 900 milliliters of water = 1 liter of 0.5 percent solution.

#### Step 2: Clean Instruments First

Remove visible blood and debris. Bleach does not penetrate organic material well.

#### Step 3: Immerse for 10 Minutes

Submerge instruments completely in the diluted bleach solution for a minimum of 10 minutes. Ensure all surfaces are exposed to liquid.

#### Step 4: Rinse Thoroughly

Remove instruments and rinse them in clean, preferably boiled, water at least three times. Bleach residue is corrosive and irritating to tissues. Thorough rinsing is critical before using instruments on patients.

#### Step 5: Air Dry

Dry instruments on clean cloth in a protected location.

**Advantages of bleach:** Kills spores better than alcohol. Cheap and easy to produce (see Vol 28, Ch 5 — Making Soap, which includes alkali production; bleach is essentially sodium hydroxide plus chlorine from brine). Penetrates organic material better than alcohol.

**Disadvantages:** Corrosive to some metals with prolonged exposure (extended soaking degrades steel instruments). Requires thorough rinsing.

### Chemical Sterilization: Iodine

Iodine is a powerful antimicrobial. Even weak iodine solutions (1 percent) kill most bacteria. Tincture of iodine (2–7 percent iodine in alcohol) is ideal if available.

#### Step 1: Clean Instruments

Remove blood and debris with water and cloth.

#### Step 2: Prepare or Obtain Iodine Solution

If you have tincture of iodine (2–7 percent iodine in alcohol or water), use it directly. If you have pure iodine, prepare a solution: dissolve iodine crystals in alcohol or water. Aim for 1–2 percent concentration. The solution should be reddish-brown.

#### Step 3: Immerse for 5–10 Minutes

Submerge instruments in iodine solution for 5 to 10 minutes. All surfaces must be exposed.

#### Step 4: Rinse if Needed

Iodine stains and is irritating in high concentrations. If you will use instruments on living tissue (wounds, surgical field), rinse briefly in clean water. For storage or non-tissue use, rinsing is not necessary.

#### Step 5: Air Dry

Dry on clean cloth.

**Advantages of iodine:** Very broad antimicrobial spectrum (bacteria, fungi, viruses, some spores). Fast acting. Penetrates organic material. Available from natural sources (seaweed contains iodine; burning seaweed and dissolving the ash in water yields iodine-rich solution).

**Disadvantages:** Stains skin and cloth. Can cause allergic reactions in sensitive individuals. Iodine vapors are irritating; container should be sealed.

### Environmental Sterilization: Steam Without Pressure

In the absence of an autoclave (which requires pressure and high temperature), steam sterilization is possible using simpler equipment.

#### Step 1: Prepare a Steaming Vessel

Use a large pot with a tight-fitting lid. Place a cloth or wooden grate at the bottom (about 3 centimeters above the bottom) to keep instruments off direct contact with boiling water.

#### Step 2: Add Water

Add water to the pot until it reaches just below the grate. Do not let water contact the instruments directly during steaming; steam transfer is sufficient.

#### Step 3: Arrange Instruments

Place instruments on the grate or cloth, spaced out to allow steam circulation. Leave space between items.

#### Step 4: Cover and Heat

Place the lid on the pot. Heat to produce steam (steam becomes visible as vapor rising from beneath the lid). Maintain steam generation for 30 minutes minimum. The higher temperature of steam (above 100 degrees Celsius when pressurized, but still at 100 degrees in this open system) combined with longer duration compensates for lack of pressure.

#### Step 5: Cool and Remove

Once steaming is complete, allow the system to cool slightly. Use sterile tongs to remove instruments. Cloth wrapping the instruments will be damp; air dry completely before storage.

### Surface Sterilization: Sunlight and UV

Ultraviolet radiation from sunlight kills many microorganisms. This is not reliable sterilization but helps reduce contamination.

#### Step 1: Expose Surfaces to Direct Sunlight

Place instruments, bandages, or cloths in direct sunlight (not in shade). Ensure the sun's rays directly contact all surfaces. For best effect, place on clean cloth or in clear glass containers.

#### Step 2: Expose for 4–6 Hours Minimum

UV radiation requires time and intensity. On a clear day, 4 to 6 hours of direct sun provides moderate disinfection. Cloudy days are less effective. Ultraviolet intensity is highest between 10 AM and 3 PM.

#### Step 3: Store Protected from Dust

Once UV-treated, store instruments in clean, covered containers to prevent recontamination.

**Limitations:** Only sterilizes surfaces exposed to direct sunlight. Does not penetrate thick materials. Not reliable for critical sterilization but useful for maintenance and additional decontamination.

### Instrument Storage After Sterilization

**In a clean, dry container:**
- Place sterilized instruments in sealed containers (cloth-wrapped bundles, sealed jars, or closed boxes).
- Store in a protected location away from dirt, dust, animals, and water splash.
- Keep the environment clean: sweep, limit foot traffic, and maintain moisture control.

**Expected sterility duration:**
- Unwrapped instruments: Hours (exposed to air contamination).
- Cloth-wrapped or containerized instruments: Days (if container remains sealed and environment is clean).
- Sealed, boiled, and dried instruments in glass jars: Weeks (if seal is not broken).

Assume instruments lose sterility if exposed to moisture, dust, or visible contamination. When in doubt, re-sterilize.

## Verification

**Sterilization has been effective if:**
- Boiled instruments emerge hot and have been in contact with rolling boil water for the required duration.
- Chemical-sterilized instruments have been fully submerged and have air-dried without contamination.
- Steam-sterilized instruments have been exposed to visible steam for the required duration.
- Instruments stored after sterilization remain in sealed, clean containers without visible contamination.

**Sterilization has failed if:**
- Instruments have been touched with unsterilized hands or tools after sterilization.
- Containers have been opened in a contaminated environment (near animals, food, or dust).
- Boiling water was not visibly at a rolling boil or time was cut short.
- Chemical solutions were not at proper concentration or instruments were not fully submerged.

## Common Mistakes

**Mixing contaminated and sterile instruments:** Once an instrument is sterilized, keep it separated from unsterilized items. Mixing them contaminates all. Use separate clean containers.

**Not cleaning before sterilizing:** Organic material (blood, tissue) protects microorganisms from sterilizing agents. Cleaning before sterilization is essential. A dirty, boiled instrument may still harbor pathogens beneath organic buildup.

**Insufficient boiling time:** The timer starts when water reaches a rolling boil, not when you place instruments in hot water. Minimum 20 minutes at sea level; more at altitude.

**Using contaminated water for boiling:** Water itself must be clean. If boiling water in a river or pond, pre-filter and use the clearest water available, or use water that has been boiled previously and stored clean.

**Exposing sterile instruments to air without protection:** Once sterilized, instruments remain sterile only while protected. Place them immediately in clean containers or wrapped bundles.

**Assuming chemical sterilization works through organic material:** Bleach, alcohol, and iodine all require direct contact. Do not expect them to sterilize instruments bundled in bloodstained cloth.

**Over-trusting surface-only methods:** Sunlight and surface chemical dips reduce contamination but do not reliably sterilize instruments with lumens, crevices, or hinges. Use heat sterilization (boiling or steam) for critical surgical instruments.

**Storing instruments in damp environments:** Moisture encourages microorganism growth and causes rust on metal instruments. Store in dry containers in dry environments.
