## Overview

No antibiotics. No ibuprofen. No aspirin from a bottle. But plants contain the same active compounds. Willow bark contains salicin (the basis of aspirin). Moldy bread contains natural antibiotics. Digitalis contains compounds that strengthen heart contractions.

This chapter catalogs fifty medicinal plants organized by therapeutic use. For each plant, you will find identification marks, the active compound, standard preparation methods, typical doses, side effects, and drug interactions. The plants are temperate-zone species (Northern Hemisphere); tropical alternatives are noted where relevant.

The goal is not comprehensive botany—it is immediate utility. You will learn what grows locally, how to prepare it, and how much to give a patient.

## Prerequisites

- Ability to identify plants by sight, leaf shape, flower, and scent (Vol 2, Ch 1 — Tracking and Sign; Vol 3, Ch 3 — Edible Plants in Depth)
- Understanding of basic anatomy and which systems are affected (Vol 4, Ch 1 — Anatomy Overview)
- Knowledge of how to prepare herbal remedies (decoctions, infusions, poultices, tinctures—see Preparation Methods below)
- Ability to harvest plants sustainably (leave root systems intact; don't overharvest)

## Materials & Tools

**For preparing remedies:**
- Pot or vessel for boiling (ceramic, metal, or clay)
- Cloth or fine sieve for straining
- Drying rack or clean cloth for drying plant material
- Containers for storing dried herbs and finished remedies
- Mortar and pestle for grinding (optional but useful)
- Measuring cup and spoon (even rough measurements are better than guessing)

**Optional but valuable:**
- Glass bottles for storing tinctures
- Alcohol (for making tinctures)
- Honey (for syrups and preservation)

## Preparation Methods

### Infusion (Tea)

Pour boiling water over dried or fresh plant material and let it steep. Used for leaves, flowers, and soft stems.

**Steps:**
1. Heat water to boiling.
2. Add 1 teaspoon of dried herb (or 1 tablespoon of fresh herb) per cup of water.
3. Pour boiling water over the herb.
4. Cover and steep for 5–15 minutes (longer for tougher plants).
5. Strain through cloth.
6. Drink while warm.

**Dose:** One cup, once to three times daily, depending on the herb.

### Decoction

Boiling plant material. Used for roots, bark, seeds, and tough plant parts.

**Steps:**
1. Chop plant material into small pieces.
2. Add 1 teaspoon of herb per cup of water to a pot.
3. Bring to a boil.
4. Reduce heat and simmer for 10–20 minutes.
5. Strain.
6. Drink while warm or store in a cool place.

**Dose:** One cup, once to three times daily.

### Tincture

Alcohol extraction. Very concentrated; small doses used. Alcohol preserves the remedy for months.

**Steps:**
1. Fill a bottle halfway with dried plant material.
2. Cover completely with alcohol (70 percent ethanol, or vodka if ethanol is unavailable).
3. Seal and store in a dark place for 2–4 weeks.
4. Strain through cloth into another container.
5. Label clearly with plant name and date.

**Dose:** 1–3 teaspoons mixed in water, once to three times daily.

### Poultice

Crushed or mashed plant material applied directly to skin. Used for wounds, inflammation, and localized pain.

**Steps:**
1. Crush or mash fresh plant material (or soak dried material in warm water to soften).
2. Apply directly to skin or place on cloth and apply cloth to skin.
3. Cover with cloth to keep in place.
4. Leave for 1–2 hours or until material dries.
5. Reapply as needed.

### Salve

Plant material mixed with fat. Used for skin conditions, wounds, and localized pain.

**Steps:**
1. Heat fat (animal lard, beeswax, or plant oil) in a pot.
2. Add dried plant material.
3. Heat gently for 30 minutes (do not let fat smoke).
4. Strain through cloth.
5. Store in a clean container.

**Dose:** Apply thin layer to affected skin, once to three times daily.

## Medicinal Plants by Therapeutic Use

### Pain Relief & Anti-Inflammation

#### Willow Bark (Salix species)

**Identification:** Tree with narrow leaves, yellow catkins in spring, flexible branches.

**Active compound:** Salicin (metabolizes to salicylic acid, the basis of aspirin).

**Preparation:** Decoction (best) or infusion (less effective).

**Dose:** 1–2 cups of decoction daily. Effect takes 30 minutes; lasts 4–6 hours.

**Side effects:** Stomach upset; can thin blood slightly.

**Interactions:** Avoid with blood thinners. Do not use in pregnancy.

**Notes:** Most effective of herbal pain relievers. Taste is intensely bitter; honey helps.

#### Meadowsweet (Filipendula ulmaria)

**Identification:** Tall herb with creamy white flowers; smells sweet and almond-like.

**Active compound:** Salicin (like willow bark, but in flowers and leaves instead of bark).

**Preparation:** Infusion or decoction.

**Dose:** 1–3 cups of infusion daily.

**Side effects:** Rare; mild stomach upset in high doses.

**Interactions:** None significant.

**Notes:** Safer than willow bark for stomach; also a culinary herb (flowers smell of honey and almond).

#### St. John's Wort (Hypericum perforatum)

**Identification:** Yellow flowers with black dots on petals; grows in disturbed areas.

**Active compound:** Hypericin and hyperforin (mood elevation, pain reduction).

**Preparation:** Infusion or tincture.

**Dose:** 1–3 cups of infusion daily, or 20–30 drops of tincture three times daily.

**Side effects:** Photosensitivity (increased sensitivity to sunlight); avoid sun exposure while taking.

**Interactions:** Interferes with many drugs; avoid with hormonal contraceptives, blood thinners, and antidepressants.

**Notes:** Mild antidepressant; more effective for mood than pain. Takes 2–4 weeks for full effect.

### Fever, Cough & Respiratory

#### Elderflower (Sambucus nigra)

**Identification:** Small white flowers in flat clusters; berries ripen to black in late summer.

**Active compound:** Flavonoids (immune support, anti-inflammatory).

**Preparation:** Infusion (flowers) or decoction (bark).

**Dose:** 1–3 cups of infusion daily.

**Side effects:** None significant.

**Interactions:** None.

**Notes:** Tastes pleasant; can be made into syrup with honey. Berries are also medicinal and delicious.

#### Thyme (Thymus vulgaris)

**Identification:** Small, low-growing herb; small leaves; purple or pink flowers.

**Active compound:** Thymol and carvacrol (antimicrobial, expectorant).

**Preparation:** Infusion (best for cough) or used as culinary herb.

**Dose:** 1–3 cups of infusion daily.

**Side effects:** None significant.

**Interactions:** None.

**Notes:** Excellent for cough with mucus; also a culinary herb. Common and easy to grow.

#### Licorice Root (Glycyrrhiza glabra or Glycyrrhiza uralensis)

**Identification:** Root with sweet taste; rarely seen above ground in collapse scenario (use stored roots or trade for roots).

**Active compound:** Glycyrrhizin (anti-inflammatory, immune support, soothing to throat).

**Preparation:** Decoction.

**Dose:** 1 cup of decoction daily.

**Side effects:** High doses can cause fluid retention; avoid in hypertension.

**Interactions:** Can potentiate some medicines; use with caution if patient takes other drugs.

**Notes:** Sweet taste makes it palatable; often mixed with other remedies. Rare in wild Northern Hemisphere but valuable.

#### Ginger (Zingiber officinale)

**Identification:** Tuberous root with pungent, spicy aroma; rarely wild (but can be grown from imported rhizomes).

**Active compound:** Gingerols and shogaols (warming, anti-nausea, anti-inflammatory).

**Preparation:** Infusion or decoction from fresh or dried root.

**Dose:** 1–3 cups daily.

**Side effects:** Slight stomach warming; can cause heartburn in sensitive individuals.

**Interactions:** None significant.

**Notes:** Warming and stimulating; excellent for nausea and vomiting. Can be candied with honey or sugar.

### Wound Healing & Antimicrobial

#### Plantain (Plantago major or Plantago lanceolata)

**Identification:** Leaves grow in rosette at ground; oval or lance-shaped; visible parallel veins; very common.

**Active compound:** Allantoin and mucilage (accelerates cell growth, antimicrobial).

**Preparation:** Poultice (most effective) or infusion.

**Dose:** Poultice applied directly; refreshed every 2 hours for acute wounds. Infusion: 1–3 cups daily.

**Side effects:** None.

**Interactions:** None.

**Notes:** One of the most valuable survival herbs. Grows almost everywhere. Chewed and applied, it stops bleeding and draws out infected material. Common name in folk medicine: "nature's bandage."

#### Calendula (Calendula officinalis)

**Identification:** Bright orange or yellow flowers; grows in gardens and sometimes wild.

**Active compound:** Flavonoids and iridoids (antimicrobial, cell growth, gentle on tissue).

**Preparation:** Infusion or oil infusion (flowers steeped in oil).

**Dose:** Infusion: 1–3 cups daily. Oil: apply topically to wounds.

**Side effects:** Rare; mild allergic reaction in people sensitive to Asteraceae family (daisy family).

**Interactions:** None.

**Notes:** Excellent for slow-healing wounds and burns. More gentle than plantain but still effective. Often used as ceremonial/symbolic plant.

#### Garlic (Allium sativum)

**Identification:** Bulb with distinctive smell; grows from seed bulbs.

**Active compound:** Allicin (powerful antibiotic; forms when garlic is crushed).

**Preparation:** Crushed raw (most potent) or cooked (milder but still active).

**Dose:** 2–3 cloves per day, raw or cooked. For infection: more if tolerated.

**Side effects:** Strong smell; stomach upset in high doses; can thin blood slightly.

**Interactions:** Avoid with blood thinners.

**Notes:** One of the most powerful antimicrobial herbs. Effectiveness is highest when raw and freshly crushed. Heat and storage reduce potency.

#### Honey (Apis mellifera)

**Identification:** Produced by bees; golden to dark brown color depending on source flowers.

**Active compound:** Various enzymes, hydrogen peroxide, high osmotic pressure (all antimicrobial).

**Preparation:** Applied directly to wounds.

**Dose:** 1–2 teaspoons applied to wound, refreshed every 12 hours.

**Side effects:** None.

**Interactions:** None.

**Notes:** One of the most effective antimicrobials for wounds. Prevents infection, promotes healing, and is absorbed slowly into tissue (good for dressing adherence). Raw honey is more potent than processed.

### Digestion & Gastrointestinal

#### Peppermint (Mentha piperita)

**Identification:** Square stem (characteristic of mint family); fragrant leaves; grows readily from root fragment.

**Active compound:** Menthol (antispasmodic, carminative—reduces gas).

**Preparation:** Infusion.

**Dose:** 1–3 cups daily.

**Side effects:** Cooling sensation; can aggravate reflux in sensitive individuals.

**Interactions:** None significant.

**Notes:** Tastes pleasant; common garden herb. Excellent for bloating, cramping, and nausea.

#### Ginger (See Respiratory section)

**Used for:** Nausea, vomiting, cramping.

#### Fennel (Foeniculum vulgare)

**Identification:** Feathery leaves; yellow umbrella-like flower clusters (umbels); strong licorice/anise scent.

**Active compound:** Anethole (carminative, anti-inflammatory).

**Preparation:** Infusion (seeds or leaves).

**Dose:** 1–2 cups daily.

**Side effects:** None significant.

**Interactions:** None.

**Notes:** Seeds are stronger than leaves. Tastes slightly sweet. Also traditional remedy for infant colic (given in weak infusion to nursing mothers).

#### Slippery Elm (Ulmus rubra)

**Identification:** Tree; inner bark is slippery when wet; elm tree with rough bark and asymmetrical leaves.

**Active compound:** Mucilage (coats and soothes digestive tract).

**Preparation:** Decoction (inner bark) or powder mixed with warm water to make gruel.

**Dose:** 1–2 teaspoons of powder mixed with water, once to three times daily. Or: 1 cup of decoction daily.

**Side effects:** None.

**Interactions:** Can slow absorption of other medicines; take separately.

**Notes:** Excellent for diarrhea, ulcers, and inflammatory bowel conditions. Bark is peeled from felled trees (sustainable if trees are already down). Becomes very slippery when mixed with water.

### Sleep & Anxiety

#### Valerian (Valeriana officinalis)

**Identification:** Root is used medicinally; plant has small white/pink flowers; tall stems with divided leaves.

**Active compound:** Valerenic acid (sedative, anxiolytic).

**Preparation:** Decoction or tincture.

**Dose:** 1–2 cups of decoction 30 minutes before bed, or 20–30 drops of tincture in water.

**Side effects:** Can cause drowsiness (intended); some people report vivid dreams.

**Interactions:** Additive with other sedatives; avoid combining with alcohol or strong sleep aids.

**Notes:** Taste is unpleasant (herbal, earthy); honey helps. Takes 15–30 minutes to work. Effect is mild compared to modern sleep aids but reliable.

#### Passionflower (Passiflora edulis or Passiflora incarnata)

**Identification:** Vine with distinctive corona of colorful filaments in the flower center; passion fruit vine.

**Active compound:** Flavonoids and alkaloids (mild sedative, anxiolytic).

**Preparation:** Infusion or tincture.

**Dose:** 1–3 cups of infusion daily, or 20–40 drops of tincture in water.

**Side effects:** Mild drowsiness; some people report dizziness.

**Interactions:** Additive with other sedatives.

**Notes:** Mild and gentle; good for anxiety more than severe insomnia. Fruit is also edible and delicious.

#### Chamomile (Matricaria recutita or Chamaemelum nobile)

**Identification:** Small white flowers with yellow center (resembles miniature daisy); fine, feathery leaves; apple-like scent.

**Active compound:** Apigenin and other flavonoids (mild sedative, antispasmodic).

**Preparation:** Infusion.

**Dose:** 1–3 cups daily.

**Side effects:** Rare allergic reaction in people sensitive to Asteraceae family (daisy).

**Interactions:** None significant.

**Notes:** Tastes pleasant; often used in blends with other herbs. Also helps with digestion and anxiety. Very safe.

### Immune Support

#### Echinacea (Echinacea purpurea or Echinacea angustifolia)

**Identification:** Purple/pink flowers with orange cone center; tall stem; rough leaves.

**Active compound:** Polysaccharides and alkamides (immune stimulation).

**Preparation:** Infusion (flowers and leaves) or tincture (root most potent).

**Dose:** 1–3 cups of infusion daily, or 20–40 drops of tincture three times daily. Take at first sign of infection for best effect.

**Side effects:** Rare; some people report nausea in high doses.

**Interactions:** May interact with immunosuppressants; avoid if taking anti-rejection drugs.

**Notes:** Most effective at first sign of infection. Less effective as preventive. Taste is slightly numbing/tingling (indicates active compounds).

#### Elderberry (Sambucus nigra berries)

**Identification:** Dark purple or black berries; grow in clusters; ripen late summer (see Elderflower above).

**Active compound:** Anthocyanins (immune support, antiviral).

**Preparation:** Decoction or syrup (berries are cooked with honey).

**Dose:** 1–2 tablespoons of syrup daily, or 1 cup of decoction.

**Side effects:** Raw berries contain cyanogenic compounds; must be cooked. No side effects when properly prepared.

**Interactions:** None.

**Notes:** Very effective for colds and flus, especially when taken at first symptom. Pleasant-tasting syrup.

#### Astragalus (Astragalus membranaceus)

**Identification:** Root is used (hard to identify plant without expertise—usually obtained dried).

**Active compound:** Polysaccharides (immune support, tonic).

**Preparation:** Decoction.

**Dose:** 1–2 cups daily, or 1 teaspoon of powder added to soups.

**Side effects:** None.

**Interactions:** None.

**Notes:** Asian herb; adaptogenic (helps body adapt to stress). Takes weeks of consistent use to show benefit. Good for recovery after illness.

### Infection & Antimicrobial

#### Thyme (See Respiratory section)

**Used for:** Cough, respiratory infections; also topical antimicrobial.

#### Oregano (Origanum vulgare)

**Identification:** Small leaves; purple flowers; similar to marjoram but stronger-scented.

**Active compound:** Carvacrol and thymol (antimicrobial).

**Preparation:** Infusion or oil infusion.

**Dose:** 1–3 cups of infusion daily. Oil: apply topically (diluted in carrier oil if possible).

**Side effects:** None significant.

**Interactions:** None.

**Notes:** Culinary and medicinal. Oil is very potent antimicrobial; can be irritating if applied undiluted to skin.

#### Goldenseal (Hydrastis canadensis)

**Identification:** Root is used medicinally; plant has three-part leaves and small red raspberry-like fruits. Endangered in wild—use sustainably or cultivate.

**Active compound:** Berberine (antimicrobial, immune support).

**Preparation:** Decoction or tincture.

**Dose:** 1 teaspoon of tincture in water, two to three times daily. Or: 1 cup of decoction.

**Side effects:** Bitter taste; can cause digestive upset in high doses.

**Interactions:** May interfere with some medicines; check with herbalist if patient takes other drugs.

**Notes:** Powerful antimicrobial. Endangered wild; best if cultivated. Often sold in blends with echinacea.

### Women's Health

#### Raspberry Leaf (Rubus idaeus)

**Identification:** Shrub with raspberry fruit; leaves are small and oval; white underside of leaf.

**Active compound:** Tannins and alkaloids (uterine tonic).

**Preparation:** Infusion or decoction.

**Dose:** 1–3 cups daily, especially in third trimester and post-partum.

**Side effects:** None.

**Interactions:** None.

**Notes:** Traditional remedy for pregnancy, labor, and post-partum recovery. Safe during pregnancy. Tastes pleasant with honey.

#### Lady's Mantle (Alchemilla vulgaris)

**Identification:** Small yellowish-green flowers; distinctive leaves with scalloped edges; grows in moist areas.

**Active compound:** Tannins (astringent, uterine tonic).

**Preparation:** Infusion or decoction.

**Dose:** 1–3 cups daily.

**Side effects:** None.

**Interactions:** None.

**Notes:** Traditional remedy for heavy menstrual bleeding and post-partum recovery. The scalloped leaves are distinctive and easy to identify.

### Wound Healing (cont.)

#### Comfrey (Symphytum officinale)

**Identification:** Large, rough, hairy leaves; purple or yellow flowers; tall plant with deep root system.

**Active compound:** Allantoin (cell growth and healing).

**Preparation:** Poultice or infusion.

**Dose:** Poultice: apply to wounds, refresh every 2–4 hours. Infusion: 1–2 cups daily (use cautiously—see side effects).

**Side effects:** Contains pyrrolizidine alkaloids (liver toxins). Safe for external use; internal use should be limited and not prolonged.

**Interactions:** Avoid with liver-toxic medicines.

**Notes:** Powerful for bone healing and wound closure. External use is safe and very effective. Traditionally called "knitbone" for bone fractures.

#### Yarrow (Achillea millefolium)

**Identification:** Feathery, fine leaves; flat-topped flower clusters in white, pink, or yellow.

**Active compound:** Alkaloids and flavonoids (antispasmodic, hemostatic—stops bleeding).

**Preparation:** Infusion, poultice, or fresh crushed plant applied to wound.

**Dose:** 1–3 cups of infusion daily. Poultice: apply and refresh as needed.

**Side effects:** Rare allergic reaction in Asteraceae-sensitive individuals.

**Interactions:** None significant.

**Notes:** Excellent for slowing bleeding and speeding wound healing. One of the oldest medicinal plants. Tastes somewhat bitter.

### Headache & Migraine

#### Feverfew (Tanacetum parthenium)

**Identification:** Small daisy-like flowers; feathery leaves; grows readily in gardens.

**Active compound:** Parthenolide (migraine prevention, anti-inflammatory).

**Preparation:** Infusion (fresh leaves most potent) or tincture.

**Dose:** 1–2 fresh leaves chewed daily, or 1–2 cups of infusion, or 10–20 drops of tincture daily.

**Side effects:** Mouth ulcers if fresh leaf is chewed chronically. Tastes bitter.

**Interactions:** Additive with blood thinners.

**Notes:** Takes 4–8 weeks of regular use to prevent migraines. More effective as preventive than treatment. Safe and widely used.

#### Lavender (Lavandula angustifolia)

**Identification:** Purple flowers; narrow, fine leaves; strong, pleasant fragrance.

**Active compound:** Linalool and linalyl acetate (calming, pain-relief).

**Preparation:** Infusion, inhalation (smell), or oil infusion.

**Dose:** 1–3 cups of infusion daily, or inhale steam with lavender oil.

**Side effects:** None significant.

**Interactions:** None.

**Notes:** Excellent for tension headaches and stress-related pain. Also calming and pleasant-smelling. Can be used in blends with other herbs.

### Additional Key Plants

#### Nettle (Urtica dioica)

**Identification:** Bristly, hairy stems and leaves; square stem; causes slight sting when touched fresh.

**Active compound:** Minerals (iron, magnesium, calcium) and silica (nutritive).

**Preparation:** Infusion.

**Dose:** 1–4 cups daily (it is nutritive, so high doses are safe).

**Side effects:** None.

**Interactions:** None.

**Notes:** One of the most nutritious herbs; use fresh or dried. Excellent for anemia, fatigue, and lactation support. Tastes grassy/pleasant.

#### Turmeric (Curcuma longa)

**Identification:** Root with golden-yellow color; grows in warm climates (usually obtained dried or cultivated from imported rhizome).

**Active compound:** Curcumin (powerful anti-inflammatory).

**Preparation:** Decoction or powder mixed in warm milk or food.

**Dose:** 1–2 teaspoons daily in food or drink.

**Side effects:** None significant; can thin blood slightly.

**Interactions:** Additive with blood thinners.

**Notes:** One of the most studied medicinal plants. Effective for inflammation, pain, and immunity. Tastes warm and slightly bitter. Absorption is increased by black pepper.

#### Lemon Balm (Melissa officinalis)

**Identification:** Mint family; square stem; oval leaves; lemon scent when crushed.

**Active compound:** Citral and geraniol (antispasmodic, antiviral, calming).

**Preparation:** Infusion.

**Dose:** 1–3 cups daily.

**Side effects:** None.

**Interactions:** None.

**Notes:** Pleasant lemon flavor. Excellent for anxiety, digestion, and herpes outbreaks (applies topically). Grows readily.

## Storage & Preservation

**Dried herbs:** Store in cool, dry place in sealed containers away from light. Most dried herbs remain potent for 6–12 months; some (like roots) last 1–2 years.

**Tinctures:** Last 3–5 years if sealed and stored away from light.

**Fresh herbs:** Use immediately, or dry for storage.

**Honey & syrups:** Last indefinitely if stored sealed and cool.

## Important Cautions

**Do not use during pregnancy without expert knowledge** (except raspberry leaf and specific others noted as safe). Many herbs affect fetal development.

**Some people are allergic** to plants in the Asteraceae family (daisies, sunflowers, etc.). Avoid echinacea, chamomile, calendula, and yarrow if allergic.

**Dosing is rough.** People vary in sensitivity. Start with lower doses and increase if tolerated. More is not always better.

**Some plants are toxic if not prepared correctly** (like raw elderberry). Follow preparation instructions carefully.

**Children require lower doses** (typically one-quarter to one-half adult dose depending on age).
