## Overview

Field surgery is emergency surgery without proper tools, anesthesia, or sterile conditions. You are operating in a tent, on a kitchen table, or in a cave with fire as your only light. Your tools are a sharp blade and knowledge. Your goal is not a perfect surgical outcome — it is keeping the patient alive long enough for infection to spare them.

This chapter covers three scenarios: amputation (when the limb must go), abscess drainage (when poison pockets must be released), and emergency trauma surgery (when something is inside that must come out).

Do not read this chapter thinking you will become a surgeon. Read it knowing that without these skills, your patient dies. With them, your patient has a chance.

## Materials & Tools

### Essential Surgical Equipment

- **Sharp blades:** Multiple (sterilized knife, razor, or scalpel)
- **Clamps or forceps:** Any tool that can grip tissue (if available; manual pressure works)
- **Tourniquets:** Cloth strips to cut off blood flow
- **Bone saw or heavy knife:** For cutting through bone
- **Suture material:** Plant fiber, sinew, or thin cloth
- **Thread:** For closing wounds
- **Cloth for bandaging:** Clean, sterilized by boiling
- **Boiling water:** For sterilization and cleaning

### Pain Management (Limited Options)

- **Alcohol or opium (if available):** To dull pain
- **Willow bark or similar herbs:** Minimal pain relief
- **Ice or cold water:** For local numbing (if available)
- **Leather strap:** For the patient to bite during surgery

### Blood Management

- **Cloth:** To control bleeding
- **Tourniquets:** To stop blood flow
- **Cautery (optional):** Hot iron or wood to seal large blood vessels

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Amputation (Last Resort)

**Step 1: Understand when amputation is the only choice.**

Amputation is a death sentence for the limb but can save the patient's life. Indications:

- **Gangrene:** Tissue is dying and spreading (black, foul-smelling)
- **Severe crush injury:** Limb is crushed beyond function and will become gangrenous
- **Severe infection spreading up the limb:** Red streaks (spreading infection), swelling above the wound, sepsis developing
- **Severe frostbite:** The limb is frozen, non-viable, and will rot

**Step 2: Make the decision with the patient (if conscious).**

"Your arm is dying. If we amputate now, we can save your life. If we do not, the infection will kill you in days. We must cut it off."

The patient must understand the stakes. If they refuse amputation and understand they will die, respect their choice. If they are unconscious or delirious, make the decision as the responsible party.

**Step 3: Prepare the patient psychologically and physically.**

- Have someone hold or restrain the patient
- Provide pain relief: alcohol, opium, herbal infusions, or bite on leather
- Place the patient in a position where you can work (usually lying down, limb supported)
- Have all tools ready before starting — you cannot stop mid-amputation to look for a blade

**Step 4: Apply a tourniquet above the amputation site.**

Place a cloth tourniquet 5–10 cm above where you will cut. Tighten until the limb goes pale and bleeding stops. This is critical — it reduces blood loss from 2–3 liters to manageable levels.

**Step 5: Choose your cutting line.**

- **High amputation:** Cut above the dead tissue, as close to the healthy tissue as possible. This leaves more limb for function but increases risk of infection.
- **Low amputation:** Cut well below the infection, ensuring all infected tissue is removed. This may require cutting into healthy tissue.

For spreading gangrene or infection, amputate high. For a crush injury, amputate low.

**Step 6: Cut through soft tissue first.**

Use your sharpest blade. The cut should be decisive — one smooth motion is better than sawing. Start at the outside of the limb and cut toward the center, following the natural contours of muscle.

- Cut skin and muscle in one long stroke (or a few smooth strokes)
- Expose the bone
- Ligature (tie off) any large blood vessels as they appear to reduce bleeding

**Step 7: Saw through bone.**

Once you reach bone, use a bone saw or heavy knife to cut through. Saw with firm, steady strokes. A dull saw requires tremendous force and takes longer — sharpen before starting.

If cutting the leg (femur or tibia), you are cutting through substantial bone. This takes 30–60 seconds of steady sawing.

**Step 8: Complete the amputation.**

Once you have sawed through, cut any remaining attachments (tendon, nerves). The limb separates.

**Step 9: Control hemorrhage.**

The stump will bleed heavily. Immediately:
1. Keep the tourniquet in place for 10–15 minutes
2. Pack the stump with clean cloth
3. Tie off any visible large blood vessels using ligatures
4. Consider cautery: hold a hot iron against the stump (extreme pain for the patient, but seals vessels)

**Step 10: Close the stump.**

Once bleeding is controlled, clean the stump wound. Suture the skin closed if possible, leaving small drains to prevent infection.

**Step 11: Aftercare.**

Monitor for:
- Continued bleeding (re-open and ligate more vessels)
- Infection (fever, spreading redness, pus) — see Vol 4, Ch 4
- Phantom pain (normal, expected)
- Psychological trauma (normal, expected)

Pain management is critical — infection and psychological damage kill nearly as many amputees as the surgery itself.

### Part 2: Abscess Drainage

**Step 1: Identify the abscess.**

An abscess is a pocket of pus under skin or in tissue. It feels:
- Swollen, firm or fluctuant (squishy, like fluid)
- Hot to the touch
- Painful to pressure
- Often with red, inflamed skin over it

**Step 2: Locate the "point of maximum fluctuance."**

This is where the pus is closest to the surface — the thinnest tissue between you and the pus. Press gently around the abscess. You will find a spot that feels softest. This is where you will incise.

**Step 3: Prepare the site.**

Clean the area with boiled water. Mark the incision line with a finger dipped in charcoal or ink (optional but helpful).

**Step 4: Incise the abscess.**

Using a sharp blade, make a single decisive cut through the skin, going down until you break into the pus pocket. The length of the cut should be 1–2 cm. Too small, and pus will not drain; too large, and the wound will scar.

You will know you have entered the abscess when pus flows out. The smell is characteristic (foul, like rotten flesh).

**Step 5: Drain the abscess completely.**

Use gentle pressure around the abscess to help pus drain. Do not force — let gravity and gentle pressure do the work. Squeezing too hard can push infection deeper.

**Step 6: Explore the cavity (carefully).**

Once the abscess has drained, the cavity is now exposed. Using a clean finger or probe, gently explore the inside to ensure you have broken all internal walls and that there are no pockets of pus remaining.

If you feel internal walls, gently break them down (a finger is gentler than a blade).

**Step 7: Pack the cavity (optional, depending on depth).**

If the abscess is deep and a large cavity remains, pack it loosely with clean, sterilized cloth. This prevents the outer wound from closing over an infected cavity.

Leave the cloth in place for 24–48 hours, then remove it and allow the cavity to drain and heal from the inside out.

**Step 8: Allow drainage and healing.**

Do not stitch an abscess wound closed immediately. Leave it open to drain. Stitching too early traps remaining infection inside.

Over 1–2 weeks, the cavity should shrivel as the infection clears and pus stops forming. Once drainage stops for 24 hours, the wound is healing.

### Part 3: Emergency Trauma Surgery (Severe Hemorrhage or Penetrating Injury)

**Step 1: Stabilize the patient.**

Before you cut, ensure:
- Patient is alive (breathing, pulse present)
- Severe bleeding is controlled (tourniquet applied if needed)
- Patient's airway is clear

**Step 2: Assess the injury.**

You must find:
- What is causing the bleeding or infection?
- Is there a foreign object (bullet, bone fragment, blade) inside?
- Is there internal bleeding (blood in abdomen, blood in the lungs)?

Physical exam: Look for entry wounds, exit wounds, swelling, deformity.

**Step 3: Surgical access.**

If there is an object inside (arrow, blade, bone fragment), you need to find and remove it.

- For abdominal trauma: Make a cut along the midline of the abdomen (from breastbone to pelvis)
- For chest trauma: Make a cut between ribs (through the intercostal space)
- For limb trauma: Cut directly over the wound

**Step 4: Explore the wound.**

Once inside, explore gently with your fingers and eyes. Find:
- The source of bleeding
- Foreign objects
- Damaged organs

**Step 5: Control bleeding.**

For bleeding vessels:
1. Identify the vessel
2. Tie it off with a ligature above and below the bleed point
3. For large vessels, you may need to ligate both sides and leave a gap in between

For bleeding from multiple small vessels, pack the area with cloth and apply pressure.

**Step 6: Remove foreign objects (if safe).**

Carefully extract any object (blade, bone, bullet). If removal will cause massive bleeding (an arrow shaft is holding back blood pressure in the wound), consult Vol 4, Ch 2 for extended field care. Often, leaving the object in place until proper surgery is safer.

**Step 7: Check for organ damage.**

If you see intestine, organs, or other structures, assess damage:
- Small puncture: Leaking gut contents? If yes, the gut is compromised — see Step 8
- Organ damage: Is the liver, kidney, or lung damaged? Major organ damage is often fatal without modern surgery

**Step 8: Repair or manage damaged organs (if possible).**

**Gut perforation:** If the intestine or stomach is perforated (leaking contents into the abdomen), you must close the hole:
1. Clean the area
2. Stitch the hole closed using sutures
3. Consider draining the abdomen (poke a small hole and let infected fluid drain)

**Organ lacerations:** Small lacerations may stop bleeding on their own. Deep lacerations involving major organs are often fatal.

**Step 9: Close the wound.**

Once you have controlled bleeding and addressed the major injury, close the wound:
1. Wash out the cavity with boiled water
2. Suture the organs back in place (if you have cut through muscle or peritoneum)
3. Suture the outer wound closed

Leave small drains if the wound is deep (to allow infection to drain).

**Step 10: Aftercare.**

Post-surgical care focuses on:
- Pain management
- Infection prevention
- Monitoring for complications

Expect fever, pain, and slow recovery.

## Verification

**Surgery was successful if:**
- The patient survives the immediate post-operative period (first 24 hours without cardiac arrest)
- Bleeding is controlled
- The patient remains conscious and oriented (or wakes up if unconscious)
- Infection does not develop in the first week

**Signs of surgical failure:**
- Uncontrolled bleeding continuing for >30 minutes post-op
- Patient goes into shock (pale, cold, rapid weak pulse)
- Severe infection (fever >40°C, spreading redness, overwhelming sepsis) within days

## Common Mistakes

- **Hesitation during amputation.** Once you start, you must commit. Pausing mid-amputation causes more pain and blood loss.
- **Not applying a tourniquet for amputation.** This alone converts a survivable surgery to likely death from blood loss.
- **Closing an infected wound too soon.** A contaminated surgical wound must drain. Closing it traps infection inside.
- **Operating without pain relief.** Your patient will scream, struggle, and interfere with your work. Provide what pain relief you can.
- **Not ligating large blood vessels.** A single unligated artery can cause death from hemorrhage hours after surgery.
- **Removing an object that is holding back bleeding.** If an arrow is impaled and removing it causes massive bleeding, leave it in place. Mark it, stabilize it, and let a more experienced surgeon (if one arrives) decide.
- **Operating without help.** Surgery without an assistant is nearly impossible. Have someone hold the patient, pass you tools, and apply pressure.
- **Infection.** All field surgery risks infection. Expect it. Manage it aggressively.
