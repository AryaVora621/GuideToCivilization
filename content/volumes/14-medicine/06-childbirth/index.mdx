## Overview

In a post-collapse world without hospitals, cesarean sections, or anesthesia, childbirth returns to what it has been for millennia—a significant medical event with real risks. Maternal and infant mortality were common before modern obstetrics. Knowledge of normal labor, recognition of complications, and skilled assistance dramatically improve survival.

This chapter covers pregnancy recognition, labor stages, normal delivery, and management of common complications.

## Materials & Tools

### For prenatal assessment
- Tape measure (to track uterine growth)
- Ability to feel the fetus through the abdominal wall (palpation)
- Knowledge of pregnancy timeline

### For delivery
- Clean cloth (for delivery, placenta, and baby)
- Warm water (for hand washing, delivery area cleaning)
- Sterile scissors or sharp blade (to cut the umbilical cord)
- Cloth strips (to tie the umbilical cord)
- Bowl (to catch the placenta and blood)
- Cloth wraps (to keep the newborn warm)
- Suction (if available—a rubber bulb syringe; improvised: a cloth and gravity)

### For complications
- Rope or strong cloth (for traction in breech birth)
- Ergot or other uterotonic (to control hemorrhage; see Vol 31: Pharmacology)
- Herbs for pain and infection prevention

## Part 1: Pregnancy Recognition and Timeline

### Signs of pregnancy

- Missed menstrual period (most reliable early sign)
- Nausea and vomiting (morning sickness), especially in the first trimester
- Breast tenderness and enlargement
- Fatigue and increased urination
- Abdominal expansion (becomes obvious in the 4th–5th month)
- Fetal movement (felt by the mother, starting around month 5)

### Timeline of pregnancy

**Month 1–3 (First trimester):**
- Embryo develops rapidly.
- Mother experiences nausea, fatigue, breast tenderness.
- Risk of miscarriage is highest in this period.
- Uterus is still small and not easily felt.

**Month 4–6 (Second trimester):**
- Fetus grows rapidly.
- Mother's abdomen visibly expands.
- Fetal movement becomes apparent.
- Nausea usually resolves; mother feels better.
- Uterus grows to above the level of the umbilicus.

**Month 7–9 (Third trimester):**
- Fetus gains weight rapidly.
- Mother's abdomen reaches maximum expansion.
- Fetal position becomes important (head-down is normal; breech is problematic).
- Braxton-Hicks contractions (practice contractions, painless) may occur.
- Swelling of feet and hands is common.
- Mother may feel pressure in the pelvis as the baby drops lower.

### Estimating due date

Normal pregnancy lasts 266–280 days (about 9 months, or 40 weeks) from conception. If you know when the last menstrual period occurred, add 280 days to estimate the due date. If you don't know, estimate based on uterine size:
- At 3 months: uterus at the pelvic brim (just above the pubic bone)
- At 4 months: uterus halfway between the pubic bone and umbilicus
- At 5 months: uterus at the umbilicus
- At 6 months: uterus above the umbilicus
- At 9 months: uterus at the lower ribs

## Part 2: Prenatal Care

Even without modern medicine, prenatal care improves outcomes.

### Nutrition

The pregnant woman needs:
- Extra 300 calories per day (especially in the third trimester)
- Adequate protein (essential for fetal development)
- Adequate iron (to prevent anemia; iron-rich foods: meat, legumes, leafy greens)
- Adequate calcium (for fetal bone development; sources: dairy, leafy greens, crushed eggshells)
- Adequate fluids

Malnutrition increases risk of miscarriage, low birth weight, and maternal complications.

### Activity

- Light activity is beneficial (walking, daily tasks).
- Heavy labor should be reduced as pregnancy progresses.
- Rest increases the chance of a healthy pregnancy.

### Signs of complications

- Vaginal bleeding (suggests miscarriage or placental problems)
- Severe abdominal pain (suggests placental abruption or ectopic pregnancy)
- Sudden severe headache with vision changes (suggests preeclampsia)
- Swelling of face and hands (suggests preeclampsia)
- Fever (suggests infection)

If complications occur, evacuation or experienced midwifery care is essential.

## Part 3: Labor and Delivery

### Stages of labor

**Stage 1: Cervical dilation (contractions begin until cervix is fully open)**

Duration: 12–24 hours for first-time mothers, shorter for subsequent births.

- **Early phase:** Contractions every 5–20 minutes, mild to moderate. Mother can walk and talk.
- **Active phase:** Contractions every 2–5 minutes, stronger and more painful. Mother is focused.
- **Transition:** Contractions every 1–2 minutes, very strong. Mother may vomit, feel overwhelmed, or doubt her ability. This phase lasts minutes to an hour and is the shortest but most intense.

**Assessment:** Feel the cervix (if trained) to assess dilation (1–10 cm). Dilation greater than 10 cm indicates the cervix is fully open and Stage 2 can begin. Do NOT assess cervical dilation frequently—each assessment introduces infection risk.

**Management:**
- Support the mother's position (she will find what is comfortable).
- Provide fluids and food (if she desires).
- Encourage movement and position changes.
- Apply counterpressure to the back (firm pressure on the lower back reduces pain).
- Provide emotional support and reassurance.
- Use pain management: breathing techniques, movement, herbal teas (red raspberry leaf, nettle), warm compresses.
- Avoid stress and disruption (a quiet, calm environment promotes labor progress).

**Stage 2: Pushing and delivery (cervix fully open until baby is born)**

Duration: 20 minutes to 2 hours.

- Once the cervix is fully dilated, mother feels an irresistible urge to push.
- With each contraction, mother takes a deep breath and pushes (bearing down) for about 10 seconds, rests, and repeats.
- Pushing progresses the baby down through the pelvis and out through the birth canal.
- The head crowns (appears at the vaginal opening) and continues to stretch the vagina.
- Once the head is delivered, the rest of the body follows with the next contractions.

**Assessment:**
- Progress is marked by descent of the baby through the pelvis (monitored by feeling for the position of the head).
- Stretching and thinning of the vagina are normal (though they cause pain and tearing is common).

**Management:**
- Support the mother in a comfortable position (upright, semi-upright, or lying on the side; avoid flat on the back if possible).
- Encourage her to push with contractions and rest between them.
- Provide perineal support (gentle support of the perineum—the area between the vagina and anus—to reduce tearing).
- Apply warm, wet compresses to the perineum to reduce pain and stretching.
- As the head crowns, advise the mother to push slowly and breathe (rather than pushing hard) to allow the perineum to stretch gradually.
- As the head delivers, support it gently.
- Check for nuchal cord (umbilical cord around the baby's neck). If present, gently slip it over the baby's head.
- As the rest of the body delivers, support the baby gently.

**Stage 3: Delivery of the placenta (after baby is born until placenta is delivered)**

Duration: 5–30 minutes, occasionally longer.

- After the baby is born, contractions continue (weaker than before).
- These contractions separate the placenta from the uterus and push it out.
- Signs that the placenta is about to deliver: the uterus contracts, vaginal bleeding increases, the umbilical cord lengthens (more cord comes out).

**Assessment:**
- The baby is now outside the uterus and breathing (check that baby has a clear airway and is breathing adequately).
- Check the mother's vaginal bleeding (heavy bleeding suggests retained placenta or hemorrhage).

**Management:**
- Keep the baby warm (wrap in clean cloth).
- Allow cord clamping and cutting to wait (delayed cord clamping—waiting 1–2 minutes—allows more blood to transfer from placenta to baby, improving the baby's blood volume).
- Do NOT pull on the umbilical cord to speed placenta delivery (this can cause cord rupture and retained placenta, both dangerous).
- Once the mother feels another contraction and the cord has lengthened, she can push gently to deliver the placenta.
- Once the placenta is delivered, examine it (should be intact, with no fragments left in the uterus—retained fragments cause hemorrhage and infection).
- Palpate the uterus. It should be firm and contracted (a firm uterus controls bleeding). If the uterus is soft (atonic), massage it firmly (circular motions) to stimulate contraction. If available, administer ergot or misoprostol (see Vol 31: Pharmacology) to contract the uterus.

## Part 4: Newborn Care in the First Hour

### Airway and breathing

- Clear the baby's mouth and nose of fluid (use a cloth, bulb syringe if available, or gravity—hold the baby head-down briefly).
- Listen and feel for breathing. Most babies cry and breathe within seconds of birth.
- If the baby is not breathing after 30 seconds, perform infant resuscitation (gentle stimulation, drying, warmth may help; assisted ventilation requires training).

### Warmth

- The baby loses heat rapidly. Dry the baby immediately.
- Wrap the baby in warm, dry cloth.
- Keep the baby skin-to-skin with the mother (warmth and bonding).

### Cord and placenta

- Once the cord has stopped pulsing (1–2 minutes), tie it off (using cloth strips or cord) close to the baby's abdomen.
- Cut the cord on the placental side of the tie (if you have scissors or a knife) or leave it intact (the cord will separate over several days).
- The cord stump should be clean and dry. If possible, avoid contact with contamination (especially fecal matter, which introduces infection).

### Feeding

- The newborn has an instinctive suckling reflex. Most babies attempt to nurse within minutes of birth.
- Breastfeeding provides antibodies and optimal nutrition. Encourage the mother to nurse.
- If the mother cannot nurse (exhaustion, low milk supply), alternative feeding methods (animal milk, porridge) are necessary but require careful hygiene to prevent infection.

## Part 5: Complications

### Prolonged labor

If labor has been ongoing for more than 24 hours and little progress has been made, labor has stalled.

**Causes:**
- Small pelvis (cephalopelvic disproportion)
- Weak contractions
- Baby in a poor position (posterior, transverse, asynclitic)
- Maternal exhaustion or fear

**Management:**
- Rest the mother (sleep and food).
- Change positions frequently.
- Provide reassurance and support.
- If labor has stalled for more than 12 hours despite rest and position changes, complications may be developing. Prepare for potential operative delivery (cesarean section is not possible without surgery—evacuation or manual extraction may be necessary, both high-risk).

### Hemorrhage

Excessive bleeding during or after delivery is life-threatening. Normal blood loss during and after delivery is about 500 mL (about 1 pint).

**Signs of hemorrhage:**
- Bleeding that soaks a cloth in minutes (rather than seconds)
- Bright red blood pouring from the vagina
- Lightheadedness, weakness, pale skin, rapid pulse (signs of shock)

**Management:**
- Palpate the uterus. If it is soft (atonic), massage firmly to make it contract.
- Administer ergot or other uterotonic (if available) to contract the uterus.
- Apply bimanual compression: place one hand on the abdomen over the uterus, the other hand inside the vagina, and squeeze the uterus between both hands.
- Keep the mother recumbent and elevated legs.
- Have her nurse the baby (nursing stimulates uterine contraction).
- Establish IV access if available (but likely not in a collapse scenario).
- Treat for shock: keep warm, elevate legs, provide fluids.
- If bleeding continues despite these measures, evacuation is essential (blood transfusion and/or surgical intervention are needed).

### Retained placenta

If the placenta has not been delivered within 30 minutes:

- Manual removal: If trained, the provider can gently insert a hand into the uterus and carefully separate the placenta from the uterine wall and remove it. This is high-risk (infection, perforation, hemorrhage).
- If untrained, wait and provide uterotonincs (ergot). Give the mother time and support.
- If the placenta has not delivered after 1–2 hours, retained placenta is suspected. Treat for hemorrhage (massage, uterotonincs). Evacuation is ideal but may not be possible.

### Cord prolapse

If the umbilical cord prolapses (comes out of the vagina before the baby), it becomes compressed, cutting off blood flow to the baby. This is an emergency.

**Management:**
- Do NOT push the cord back in.
- Place the mother in knee-chest position (on her knees, chest near the ground, hips elevated) to take pressure off the cord.
- Gently hold the cord but do not compress it.
- Prepare for emergency delivery (the baby must be delivered immediately—if labor has not progressed to Stage 2, this requires operative intervention).

### Breech presentation

The baby is positioned buttocks or feet-first (rather than head-first). This occurs in 3–4% of pregnancies.

**Recognition:**
- Palpate the mother's abdomen. The head is felt at the top (the bulge in the upper abdomen); breech babies have the buttocks at the top and hard head at the bottom.
- Ultrasound (if available) confirms the diagnosis.

**Management:**
- Some breech babies can be vaginal delivered with experienced assistance (external version or breech extraction).
- Others require operative delivery (cesarean section, which is not possible in a collapse scenario).
- If the mother and baby are otherwise healthy and labor is progressing, a vaginal breech delivery may be attempted with skilled guidance, though the risk of complications (cord prolapse, head entrapment) is high.

### Fetal distress

Signs that the baby is not tolerating labor:
- Fetal heart rate changes (very slow or very fast—most difficult to assess without monitoring equipment)
- Meconium staining (the amniotic fluid is dark/greenish, indicating fetal stress)
- Lack of fetal movement

**Management:**
- Change the mother's position.
- Encourage hydration and rest.
- Reduce stress and stimulation.
- If distress signs continue, prepare for urgent delivery (the baby may need to be delivered immediately to survive).

## Verification

Successful delivery is marked by:

1. **Healthy mother:** Conscious, stable vital signs, uterus firm and contracted, bleeding controlled, no signs of infection.
2. **Healthy baby:** Breathing, responding to stimuli, skin color normal, crying vigorously.
3. **Intact placenta:** All tissues delivered, no fragments remaining (check for retained products).
4. **No major complications:** No severe hemorrhage, no infections, no cord injuries.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Pulling on the umbilical cord:** Can cause rupture or retained placenta.
2. **Inadequate uterine massage:** Allows the uterus to relax, increasing hemorrhage.
3. **Allowing cold exposure:** The newborn cannot maintain body temperature; hypothermia is fatal.
4. **Poor asepsis:** Introduces infection (puerperal fever, tetanus, neonatal infection).
5. **Ignoring signs of complications:** Delaying treatment of hemorrhage or infection.
6. **Forcing breech delivery without training:** High risk of fetal death.
