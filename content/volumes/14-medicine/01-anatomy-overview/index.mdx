## Overview

To practice medicine without understanding anatomy is to treat blindly. Knowing where your organs are, how they connect, and what happens when they fail is the foundation of surviving illness and injury in a post-collapse world.

This chapter is a practical map of the human body, organized by system. It focuses on the anatomy that matters for survival medicine: what you can see, where major vessels and nerves lie, and how injuries to different areas produce different symptoms.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of cells and tissues (Vol 27: Biology)
- Ability to observe and reason about cause and effect
- Anatomical diagrams are helpful but not required (you can learn from examination of actual bones and diagrams)

## Materials & Tools

### For learning (optional but valuable)
- Skeleton or skeletal model (to identify bones and their landmarks)
- Anatomical diagrams or books
- Access to deceased animals (to study organ position and structure)
- A willing partner to examine (for learning surface anatomy and pulse points)

### For practical application
- Measuring tape (to identify landmarks)
- Ability to palpate (feel) bones and pulses
- Understanding of your own body as a reference

## Part 1: The Skeletal System (Framework and Protection)

The skeleton is your body's framework. It protects vital organs, produces blood, and anchors muscles.

### Major bones

**Skull:** 22 fused bones protecting the brain. The face includes the jaw (mandible), cheekbones (zygomatic), and nasal bones. The base of the skull houses the brainstem (connecting to the spinal cord).

**Spine (vertebral column):** 33 stacked bones running from the base of the skull to the tailbone. The spine protects the spinal cord and supports the body's weight. The vertebrae are:
- **Cervical (neck):** 7 vertebrae. These are small and allow great mobility. Injury here risks paralysis.
- **Thoracic (mid-back):** 12 vertebrae. These attach to the ribs and are less mobile than cervical vertebrae.
- **Lumbar (lower back):** 5 large vertebrae bearing most of the body's weight.
- **Sacral:** 5 fused vertebrae forming the sacrum (base of the spine).
- **Coccygeal:** 4 fused vertebrae forming the tailbone (functionless in humans).

**Ribs:** 12 pairs of curved bones protecting the lungs, heart, and liver. The first 7 pairs (true ribs) attach directly to the sternum. The next 3 pairs (false ribs) attach indirectly. The last 2 pairs (floating ribs) do not attach to the sternum.

**Sternum:** A flat bone in the center of the chest to which most ribs attach. It protects the heart and upper lungs.

**Pelvis:** Two hip bones fused together, forming a basin that protects the reproductive organs, bladder, and rectum. The pelvis bears the weight of the upper body when sitting or standing.

**Limb bones:**
- **Arm:** Humerus (upper arm), radius and ulna (forearm), carpals (wrist), metacarpals (hand), phalanges (fingers).
- **Leg:** Femur (thighbone—the strongest bone in the body), tibia and fibula (shin), tarsals (ankle), metatarsals (foot), phalanges (toes).

### Landmarks and injury assessment

Each bone has palpable landmarks—points where you can feel the bone under the skin.

- **Collarbones (clavicles):** Feel along the top of the shoulders—they stick out.
- **Shoulder blade (scapula):** Feel along the back of the shoulder; it is a flat, triangular bone.
- **Hipbones:** Feel the prominent bumps on each side of your lower abdomen.
- **Kneecap (patella):** The round bone in front of the knee.
- **Shinbone (tibia):** Run your hand down the front of the shin—the tibia is the prominent ridge.

When examining an injured person, palpate the area around the injury. If you feel crepitus (a grinding feeling) or unusual movement, assume a fracture.

### Bone markings and blood production

Bones are not solid. They contain:
- **Bone marrow:** Inside the central cavity. Red marrow (in ribs, vertebrae, pelvis) produces blood cells. Yellow marrow stores fat.
- **Blood vessels and nerves:** Bones have rich blood supply (why bone fractures bleed). Nerves run through channels in the bone.

Injury to bone damages blood vessels and nerves. Severe fractures can cause life-threatening bleeding inside the bone cavity.

## Part 2: The Muscular System (Movement and Strength)

Muscles attach to bones via tendons and contract to produce movement. Muscles also generate heat and protect underlying organs.

### Muscle types

- **Skeletal muscles:** Voluntary muscles (you control them consciously). These produce all deliberate movement—walking, lifting, running.
- **Cardiac muscle:** The heart. Involuntary (you don't consciously control it).
- **Smooth muscle:** Lines the digestive tract, blood vessels, and urinary system. Involuntary.

### Major muscle groups (skeletal)

- **Neck:** Sternocleidomastoid, trapezius, and others. Control head and neck movement.
- **Chest:** Pectoralis major and minor. Control arm movement and assist breathing.
- **Back:** Latissimus dorsi, erector spinae, and others. Support the spine and enable trunk movement.
- **Abdomen:** Rectus abdominis, obliques, transverse abdominis. Support the trunk and protect abdominal organs.
- **Arm:** Biceps, triceps, forearm muscles. Enable arm and hand movement.
- **Hip and thigh:** Gluteus maximus, quadriceps, hamstrings. Enable leg movement and support body weight.
- **Lower leg:** Tibialis anterior, calf muscles (gastrocnemius, soleus). Enable foot and ankle movement.

### Muscle injuries

Muscles can be strained (overstretched, fibers torn), bruised (blunt trauma), or crushed (compression injury). Muscle injuries are painful and can limit movement. Severe muscle damage can release myoglobin into the bloodstream (rhabdomyolysis), which damages the kidneys.

## Part 3: The Cardiovascular System (Circulation and Blood)

The cardiovascular system delivers oxygen and nutrients to every cell and removes waste.

### The heart

A fist-sized muscle divided into four chambers:
- **Right atrium:** Receives deoxygenated blood from the body.
- **Right ventricle:** Pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs.
- **Left atrium:** Receives oxygenated blood from the lungs.
- **Left ventricle:** Pumps oxygenated blood to the body (the strongest chamber).

The heart beats approximately 60–100 times per minute at rest. Each beat circulates blood through the entire body. Damage to the heart—from blunt trauma, infection, or bleeding—is immediately life-threatening.

### Blood vessels

- **Arteries:** Carry blood away from the heart. Arteries are thick-walled and muscular. Major arteries are:
  - Aorta (from the left ventricle, branches to the whole body)
  - Carotid arteries (to the head and brain, run along the neck)
  - Femoral arteries (to the legs, run through the groin)

- **Veins:** Carry blood back to the heart. Veins are thin-walled and collapse when empty. Veins have one-way valves preventing backflow.

- **Capillaries:** Tiny vessels where oxygen and nutrients diffuse into tissues and waste diffuses out.

### Pulses and bleeding

Arteries are palpable (you can feel the pulse) at certain points:
- **Neck:** Carotid pulse (on the side of the neck, between the windpipe and muscle)
- **Wrist:** Radial pulse (inside the wrist, toward the thumb)
- **Groin:** Femoral pulse (where the leg meets the abdomen)

A normal pulse is 60–100 beats per minute at rest. A weak or absent pulse indicates shock or death. A rapid pulse indicates blood loss, fever, or fear.

Major artery injury (e.g., femoral artery) causes rapid, life-threatening bleeding. Application of direct pressure and tourniquets can control bleeding. See Vol 14, Ch 3 (First Aid) for emergency management.

### Blood

Blood consists of:
- **Red blood cells (erythrocytes):** Carry oxygen. Low red blood cells = anemia (weakness, fatigue).
- **White blood cells (leukocytes):** Fight infection. High count = infection. Low count = immunocompromised.
- **Platelets:** Enable clotting. Low platelets = excessive bleeding.
- **Plasma:** Liquid carrying nutrients, hormones, and proteins.

Blood loss of 1–2 liters (20–30% of total blood) causes mild shock (dizziness, weakness). Loss of 2–3 liters causes severe shock (confusion, organ failure). Loss of more than 3 liters (40%+) is usually fatal without transfusion.

## Part 4: The Respiratory System (Breathing and Gas Exchange)

The respiratory system brings oxygen into the body and removes carbon dioxide.

### Major structures

- **Nasal cavity and sinuses:** Warm, humidify, and filter air.
- **Pharynx (throat):** Common passageway for air and food. Contains the tonsils (lymph tissue).
- **Larynx (voice box):** Contains the vocal cords. Also protects the airway.
- **Trachea (windpipe):** Connects the larynx to the lungs. Rigid tube with rings of cartilage preventing collapse.
- **Lungs:** Two organs in the chest cavity. The right lung has three lobes; the left has two (leaving room for the heart).
- **Bronchi and bronchioles:** Branches inside the lungs carrying air to the alveoli.
- **Alveoli:** Tiny air sacs where oxygen diffuses into blood and carbon dioxide diffuses out. The lungs contain approximately 300 million alveoli.
- **Diaphragm:** Large muscle beneath the lungs that contracts to draw air in (inhalation) and relaxes to push air out (exhalation).

### Breathing rate

Normal breathing is 12–20 breaths per minute at rest. Rapid breathing (tachypnea) indicates anxiety, fever, pain, or respiratory distress. Slow breathing (bradypnea) indicates depression of the brain stem (drugs, head injury, fatigue) or extreme calm.

### Respiration failure

Failure to breathe for more than 4–6 minutes causes brain death (lack of oxygen to the brain). Causes of respiratory failure include:
- Airway obstruction (choking, swelling, fluid)
- Lung damage (pneumonia, fluid, bleeding, collapse)
- Muscular failure (paralysis, extreme fatigue)
- Central nervous system failure (brain injury, overdose)

## Part 5: The Nervous System (Information and Control)

The nervous system collects information (from the senses), processes it (in the brain and spinal cord), and coordinates responses.

### Central nervous system (CNS)

- **Brain:** Divided into regions with specific functions:
  - **Cerebral cortex:** Conscious thought, decision-making, movement control
  - **Cerebellum:** Balance and coordination
  - **Brainstem (midbrain, pons, medulla):** Vital functions (heart rate, breathing, consciousness)

- **Spinal cord:** Runs through the vertebral canal from the brain to the lower back. Transmits signals between the brain and body. Also processes reflexes (e.g., pulling your hand away from heat without conscious thought).

### Peripheral nervous system (PNS)

- **Somatic nervous system:** Controls voluntary movement (skeletal muscles).
- **Autonomic nervous system:** Controls involuntary functions:
  - **Sympathetic:** "Fight or flight"—increases heart rate, breathing, blood sugar. Active under stress.
  - **Parasympathetic:** "Rest and digest"—decreases heart rate, increases digestion. Active at rest.

### Sensory and motor nerves

- **Sensory nerves:** Carry signals from the body to the CNS (touch, temperature, pain, position).
- **Motor nerves:** Carry signals from the CNS to muscles, causing movement.

Nerve damage causes loss of sensation and movement in the affected area. Spinal cord injury above the neck can cause total paralysis below the injury (quadriplegia). Injury in the mid-back causes paralysis of the legs (paraplegia).

## Part 6: The Digestive System (Nutrition and Waste)

The digestive system breaks down food, absorbs nutrients, and eliminates waste.

### Major organs

- **Mouth:** Teeth break down food mechanically. Saliva begins chemical digestion of carbohydrates.
- **Esophagus:** Tube carrying food to the stomach.
- **Stomach:** Acid and enzymes begin breaking down proteins. Food becomes a paste (chyme).
- **Small intestine:** The site of most digestion and nutrient absorption. Divided into duodenum, jejunum, and ileum.
- **Large intestine (colon):** Absorbs water and electrolytes. Produces stool.
- **Rectum and anus:** Store and eliminate stool.
- **Pancreas:** Produces digestive enzymes and insulin (controls blood sugar).
- **Liver:** Produces bile (aids fat digestion) and detoxifies poisons. Stores glucose and fat-soluble vitamins.
- **Gallbladder:** Stores bile.

### Digestion timeline

- Mouth: 1 minute
- Stomach: 2–4 hours
- Small intestine: 3–5 hours
- Large intestine: 1–2+ days

Food typically takes 24–72 hours to transit the entire digestive system.

### Digestive problems

- **Diarrhea:** Rapid transit causing loose stool. Caused by infection, food poisoning, or malabsorption.
- **Constipation:** Slow transit or hard stool. Caused by dehydration, low fiber, or immobility.
- **Appendicitis:** Inflammation of the appendix (a small dead-end pouch at the junction of small and large intestine). Usually requires surgery.
- **Peritonitis:** Infection of the abdominal lining. Life-threatening.

## Part 7: The Reproductive System (Continuation of Species)

The reproductive system produces the next generation.

### Male reproductive anatomy

- **Testes (two):** Produce sperm and testosterone. Located in the scrotum (external pouch).
- **Epididymis:** Stores sperm.
- **Vas deferens:** Carries sperm to the urethra.
- **Prostate:** Produces fluid that mixes with sperm to form semen.
- **Urethra:** Carries urine and semen through the penis.

### Female reproductive anatomy

- **Ovaries (two):** Produce eggs (ova) and hormones (estrogen, progesterone).
- **Fallopian tubes:** Carry eggs from the ovaries to the uterus.
- **Uterus (womb):** A muscular organ where a fertilized egg implants and develops.
- **Cervix:** The narrow opening of the uterus into the vagina.
- **Vagina:** The birth canal and receptacle for sperm.
- **Vulva:** External female genitalia (labia, clitoris).

### Menstrual cycle

In reproductive-aged females, a 28-day cycle (range 21–35 days) regulates reproduction:
- **Menstruation (days 1–5):** Shedding of the uterine lining.
- **Follicular phase (days 1–13):** Rising estrogen stimulates egg maturation.
- **Ovulation (day 14):** The ovary releases an egg.
- **Luteal phase (days 15–28):** Rising progesterone prepares the uterus for implantation. If no pregnancy occurs, hormone levels drop and menstruation begins.

## Verification

You understand anatomy when you can:

1. **Locate major organs and structures** on yourself or another person.
2. **Identify pulse points** and feel a pulse.
3. **Predict consequences** of injury to specific areas (e.g., "Damage to the femoral artery causes life-threatening bleeding").
4. **Recognize abnormal findings** (unusual swelling, deformity, or lack of movement).
