## Overview

A fracture is a break or crack in bone. A dislocation is when the end of a bone is forced out of its joint. Both injuries are common in survival situations—falls from heights, blunt trauma, crush injuries. Without modern imaging, you must rely on physical examination and deduction.

This chapter teaches how to recognize fractures and dislocations, assess severity, and manage them with the resources available in a post-collapse world.

## Prerequisites

- Anatomy (Vol 14, Ch 1)—where major bones are and what structures lie near them
- First aid principles (Vol 14, Ch 3)—wound care, circulation assessment, pain management
- Ability to create splints and slings from available materials

## Materials & Tools

### For examination
- Your hands (palpation is the primary assessment tool)
- A means to check sensation and movement below the injury

### For immobilization
- Cloth (for slings and wraps)
- Padding (cotton, cloth, leaves, pine needles)
- Splinting materials: sticks, cardboard, folded newspapers, SAM splints (if available)
- Rope or cordage (to secure splints)
- Ice or cold water (if available, for swelling reduction)

### For pain management
- Willow bark or other natural analgesics (see Vol 31: Pharmacology)
- Alcohol or narcotics (if available; dangerous in overdose)
- Immobilization itself reduces pain

## Part 1: Types of Fractures

Fractures are classified by:

### Fracture pattern

- **Simple (closed) fracture:** The bone is broken but the skin is intact. Lower infection risk.
- **Compound (open) fracture:** The broken bone pierces the skin. High risk of infection and bleeding.
- **Comminuted fracture:** The bone is shattered into multiple pieces. Difficult to align and may lose blood supply to distal (far) fragments.
- **Greenstick fracture:** An incomplete break (common in children with more flexible bones). Less severe.

### Location and complexity

- **Spiral fracture:** A twisting break, common in long bones. Caused by rotational force.
- **Transverse fracture:** A straight break perpendicular to the bone axis. Caused by direct blow.
- **Oblique fracture:** A diagonal break. Caused by combined bending and force.

### Displacement

- **Nondisplaced:** The broken pieces remain aligned. Healing is faster.
- **Displaced:** The broken pieces are out of alignment. Healing is slower and may require realignment.

## Part 2: Recognizing Fractures

### Signs and symptoms

**Immediate:**
- Pain (sharp, severe, worsens with movement)
- Swelling (begins within minutes)
- Deformity (visible misalignment or angulation)
- Inability to use the injured limb
- Crepitus (grinding sensation or sound when the fractured ends rub together)
- Discoloration (bruising beneath the skin, may appear hours after injury)

**Delayed:**
- Loss of sensation or weakness distal to the fracture (suggests nerve injury)
- Cool or pale distal limb (suggests vascular injury)
- Fever (if compound fracture becomes infected)

### Assessment techniques

**Inspection:**
Look for deformity, swelling, bruising, and open wounds. Compare the injured limb to the uninjured side.

**Palpation:**
Gently feel along the bone and joint. Press lightly to identify the exact point of pain. Crepitus indicates a fracture. Stop if the patient shows signs of severe pain.

**Range of motion:**
Do NOT attempt full range of motion testing if a fracture is suspected (this can worsen the injury). Instead, ask the patient to gently try to move the joint. Inability to move indicates a fracture or severe ligament injury.

**Distal assessment:**
Check:
- **Color:** Is the distal limb (below the fracture) the same color as the uninjured side?
- **Temperature:** Is it cool (suggests reduced blood flow)?
- **Sensation:** Can the patient feel your touch below the fracture? If not, nerve injury is likely.
- **Movement:** Can the patient wiggle fingers or toes below the fracture?
- **Pulse:** Is a pulse present distal to the fracture?

If the distal limb is cool, pale, numb, or pulseless, vascular injury is suspected. This is a medical emergency requiring immediate realignment and evacuation.

## Part 3: Specific Fractures

### Upper extremity

**Clavicle (collarbone):**
- Mechanism: Fall on the shoulder or direct blow.
- Signs: Pain at the collarbone, visible deformity, shoulder droops.
- Management: Sling and swathe (arm in a sling, then secured to the torso with a wrap). Immobilize for 4–6 weeks. Most clavicle fractures heal well without surgery.

**Humerus (upper arm):**
- Mechanism: Fall on an outstretched arm or direct blow.
- Signs: Severe pain, swelling, deformity, inability to move the arm.
- Management: Check distal pulse and sensation. Create a splint (rigid splint along the inside of the arm) or sling. Immobilize for 8–12 weeks. If the distal limb is cool or pulseless, attempt gentle realignment.

**Radius or ulna (forearm):**
- Mechanism: Fall on an outstretched hand or direct blow.
- Signs: Pain and swelling at the forearm, deformity, inability to rotate the forearm.
- Management: Create a forearm splint (rigid splint along the length of the forearm). Sling and immobilize for 6–8 weeks.

**Wrist (carpal fracture):**
- Mechanism: Fall on an outstretched hand (the most common mechanism in falls).
- Signs: Pain and swelling at the wrist, deformity if displaced.
- Management: Wrist splint (rigid splint on the palm side) or buddy-taping (taping the injured finger to an uninjured neighbor). Immobilize for 4–6 weeks.

**Fingers:**
- Mechanism: Direct blow, crush injury, or jamming.
- Signs: Pain, swelling, deformity.
- Management: Buddy-tape the injured finger to an uninjured neighbor (prevents movement). Splint if severely deformed. Most finger fractures heal in 3–4 weeks.

### Lower extremity

**Femur (thighbone):**
- Mechanism: High-energy trauma (fall from height, motor vehicle accident, gunshot).
- Signs: Severe pain, swelling, deformity (femur fractures often show external rotation of the leg), inability to stand or move the leg.
- Management: This is a life-threatening injury. Large blood loss can occur internally (the femur contains large arteries; fracture can bleed 1–2 liters internally). Immobilize with a full-leg splint (extending from the hip to the foot). Apply traction if trained to do so (gentle longitudinal pull to align the bone and reduce bleeding). Elevate the leg. Treat for shock. Evacuation is essential.

**Tibia or fibula (shin):**
- Mechanism: Direct blow or crushing injury.
- Signs: Pain and swelling at the shin, deformity, difficulty bearing weight.
- Management: Create a full-leg splint (extending from below the knee to the foot). Check distal pulse and sensation. Immobilize for 8–12 weeks. If the fracture is open (bone penetrating skin), treat as a surgical emergency.

**Ankle (talar or calcaneal fracture):**
- Mechanism: Fall from height landing on feet, twisting, or direct blow.
- Signs: Severe pain and swelling at the ankle, inability to bear weight.
- Management: Create an ankle splint (rigid splint on the side of the ankle and calf). Elevate. Apply cold. Immobilize for 8–12 weeks. Avoid bearing weight initially.

**Pelvis:**
- Mechanism: High-energy trauma (crush, motor vehicle accident, fall from great height).
- Signs: Pain, inability to bear weight, pain with squeezing the pelvis (palpate gently on both sides and note pain).
- Management: This is a life-threatening injury. Pelvic fractures can damage abdominal organs and cause massive internal bleeding. Immobilize with a pelvic binder (wide wrap around the pelvis at the level of the hip bones) to compress the fracture and reduce bleeding. Treat for shock. Evacuation is essential.

## Part 4: Dislocations

A dislocation occurs when the end of a bone is forced out of the joint socket. The most common dislocations are:

### Shoulder (most common dislocation)

**Mechanism:** Fall on an outstretched arm, direct blow to the shoulder, or violent muscular contraction.

**Signs:** Severe pain, visible deformity (the shoulder appears displaced), inability to move the arm, numbness or weakness in the arm (suggests nerve injury).

**Assessment:** Check distal pulse and sensation (nerve and artery injury is common).

**Management:** 
- **Reduction (realignment):** If trained and if there is no evidence of fracture, attempt gentle reduction. One method (Cunningham technique or scapular manipulation):
  1. Have the patient relax (pain medications help).
  2. Gently manipulate the scapula (shoulder blade) to help pop the shoulder back into place.
  3. Do NOT force—forcing can cause fracture or vascular injury.
- If reduction is not possible or if fracture is suspected, immobilize with a sling and swathe.
- Apply ice and pain relief.
- Do NOT repeatedly attempt reduction if the first attempt fails.
- Immobilize for 3–4 weeks after reduction to allow ligaments to heal.

### Anterior shoulder dislocation (most common, 95% of shoulder dislocations)

The head of the humerus is displaced anteriorly (forward), making the shoulder appear square. Reduction is usually possible with gentle manipulation.

### Posterior shoulder dislocation (less common, 5%)

The head of the humerus is displaced posteriorly (backward). This is easier to miss on examination (the deformity is less obvious). Often associated with seizures or electrocution.

### Knee

**Mechanism:** High-energy direct blow or hyperextension (bending backward excessively).

**Signs:** Severe pain and swelling, visible deformity, inability to bear weight, absence of distal pulse or sensation (indicates vascular or nerve injury—a medical emergency).

**Management:**
- Check distal pulse immediately. If absent, attempt gentle realignment (straightening the leg) to restore blood flow.
- Immobilize with a full-leg splint.
- If the distal limb is cool or pulseless, this is an emergency requiring urgent evacuation.

### Other joints

Dislocations of the elbow, wrist, hip, and ankle are less common but follow similar principles:
1. Assess for vascular and nerve injury distal to the dislocation.
2. Attempt gentle reduction if trained and if no fracture is evident.
3. Immobilize.
4. Apply ice and pain relief.

## Part 5: Complications and Warning Signs

### Compartment syndrome

Severe swelling within a muscular compartment (a fascial-enclosed space) can compress blood vessels and nerves, causing tissue death if untreated. Signs:
- Severe, unrelenting pain out of proportion to the injury
- Pain with passive stretching of muscles in that compartment
- Numbness or weakness
- Cool or pale skin
- Absence of pulse (late sign)

This requires emergency fasciotomy (surgical release of pressure). If untreated, amputation may be necessary.

### Fat embolism

Trauma to long bones (especially femur) can release fat globules into the bloodstream. Signs (appear 24–72 hours after injury):
- Respiratory distress
- Confusion or altered mental status
- Petechial rash (small red dots on the skin)
- Fever

Treatment is supportive (immobilization, pain relief, oxygen if available). Mortality is high without modern intensive care.

### Rhabdomyolysis (crush syndrome)

Severe crushing injuries or extensive muscle damage release myoglobin into the bloodstream, which damages the kidneys. Signs:
- Dark or cola-colored urine
- Weakness
- Muscle pain

Treatment: Aggressive hydration to flush the kidneys. Without access to IV fluids, oral fluids are the only option.

### Infection (in compound fractures)

Bone protruding through the skin is vulnerable to bacterial colonization. Signs:
- Fever
- Pus or foul odor from the wound
- Increased pain or swelling beyond the initial injury

Prevention: Keep the wound clean, cover with sterile dressing, and monitor for signs of infection. If infection occurs, treat with antibiotics (if available) and immobilization.

## Management Principles

### RICE protocol (modified for primitive settings)

- **Rest:** Stop using the injured limb. Pain will prevent further injury if you listen to it.
- **Ice:** Cold reduces swelling and pain. Use ice, cold water, or a cold compress if available. Apply for 15–20 minutes several times a day.
- **Compression:** Wrap the injury with cloth to reduce swelling. Do not wrap so tightly that you compromise circulation.
- **Elevation:** Raise the injured limb above the level of the heart to reduce swelling.

### Pain management

- Immobilization is the most effective pain relief for fractures and dislocations.
- Natural analgesics (willow bark, opium poppies if available) can provide additional relief.
- Do NOT overuse narcotics (addiction risk, respiratory depression).

### Timeline for healing

- Small bone fractures: 4–6 weeks
- Large bone fractures: 8–12 weeks
- Dislocations: 3–4 weeks (after reduction)
- Complex fractures: 12–24 weeks or longer

Healing is slower in malnutrition, infection, or poor immobilization.

## Verification

Successful fracture or dislocation management:

1. **Reduced distal swelling:** Swelling should decrease with RICE.
2. **Maintained distal circulation:** Pulse remains present; skin color is normal.
3. **Maintained sensation:** No progressive numbness.
4. **Pain relief:** Pain decreases, especially with immobilization.
5. **Stable alignment:** The fracture or dislocation does not re-displace.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Attempting reduction without assessing for fracture:** A fracture can appear as a dislocation. Forcing reduction of a fractured joint can cause catastrophic damage.
2. **Ignoring vascular or nerve compromise:** A cool, pale, or numb limb is a medical emergency. Prompt realignment may be lifesaving.
3. **Inadequate immobilization:** Poor splinting allows movement, pain, and delayed healing.
4. **Premature weight-bearing:** Starting to use a fractured limb too early can displace the fracture.
5. **Ignoring signs of compartment syndrome:** Untreated compartment syndrome leads to amputation.
