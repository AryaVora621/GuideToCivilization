## Overview

Nylon and polyester are condensation polymers—polymers formed when monomer molecules link by releasing water. Unlike addition polymers (PE, PP), which form by opening double bonds, condensation polymers build through formation of new covalent bonds (amide, ester) with loss of a small molecule.

Nylon and polyester dominate the synthetic fiber industry because they are:
- **Strong:** Hydrogen bonding between polymer chains provides high tensile strength
- **Durable:** Resistant to UV degradation (better than natural fibers)
- **Processable:** Melt at accessible temperatures and can be drawn into fibers with excellent properties
- **Tunable:** Chemical structure can be modified to adjust properties for specific applications

This chapter covers the two most important polymers in this family:

1. **Nylon (polyamide):** Made from diacids and diamines; primary use is fiber (clothing, carpets) and engineering plastics (gears, bearings)
2. **Polyester:** Made from diacids and diols; primary use is fiber (textiles) and bottles (PET)

Both require sophisticated synthesis and processing to achieve their properties.

## Materials & Tools

### For Nylon 6,6 Synthesis

**Raw materials:**
- Adipic acid (HOOC-(CH₂)₄-COOH): MW = 146
- Hexamethylenediamine (H₂N-(CH₂)₆-NH₂): MW = 116
- Water (as solvent for initial salt formation)
- N-methylpyrrolidone (NMP) or dimethylformamide (DMF): high-boiling solvents (optional, for solvent polymerization route)

**Alternative (direct melt polymerization):**
- Adipic acid
- Hexamethylenediamine
- No solvent; polymer forms in molten state

**Equipment:**
- Reactor: glass-lined or stainless steel, suitable for 250–280°C
- Vacuum pump: capable of achieving 0.1–1 mmHg (torr)
- Condenser: to recover water vapor during polymerization
- Stirrer: for mixing molten polymer
- Temperature sensors and controls
- Nitrogen supply: for atmosphere control

### For Polyethylene Terephthalate (PET)

**Raw materials:**
- Terephthalic acid (dimethyl terephthalate as starting material, or direct TPA): aromatic diacid
- Ethylene glycol (HOCH₂CH₂OH): diol
- Catalyst: antimony trioxide (Sb₂O₃) or titanium isopropoxide (Ti(OiPr)₄)
- Nitrogen: inert atmosphere during polymerization

**Equipment:**
- Reactor: stainless steel or glass-lined, rated for 250–290°C and vacuum
- Vacuum pump
- Nitrogen supply
- Condenser: to condense ethylene glycol vapors and water
- Stirrer for molten polymer

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Nylon 6,6 Synthesis

Nylon 6,6 is made from two monomers: adipic acid (6 carbons) and hexamethylenediamine (6 carbons), hence the designation "6,6". It is one of the most important industrial polymers.

**Step 1: Form the nylon salt complex (optional but common).**

In industrial practice, adipic acid and hexamethylenediamine are first reacted in water to form a salt, which is then polymerized. This pre-salt formation helps control stoichiometry and reaction kinetics.

1. In a vessel, dissolve adipic acid (100 g, 0.68 mol) in hot water (~70°C)
2. Add hexamethylenediamine (100 g, 0.86 mol) to the adipic acid solution (hexamethylenediamine is a base; it will react with the carboxylic acid in a 1:1 ratio)
3. A white or pale precipitate forms: this is the "nylon salt" complex (adipic acid + diamine in stoichiometric ratio)
4. Cool to room temperature and filter the salt
5. Dry the salt at 80–100°C for several hours

**Why this matters:** The salt forms with exact 1:1 stoichiometry, ensuring the final polymer is balanced (crucial for high molecular weight).

**Step 2: Charge the reactor with salt.**

1. Add the nylon salt to a clean, dry reactor
2. Seal the reactor under nitrogen (to exclude water and oxygen)
3. Ensure nitrogen flow is continuous but gentle (prevents moisture from entering)

**Step 3: Heat and initiate polymerization.**

1. Heat the reactor slowly from room temperature to 100°C (water still absorbed in salt will evaporate)
2. Continue heating to 200–220°C (salt melts into a viscous liquid)
3. The first condensation steps begin at this temperature; water is released and escapes as vapor
4. Apply vacuum gradually (to prevent violent boiling and loss of monomers):
   - At 200°C: 1 atm pressure (normal)
   - Then: reduce to 10 mmHg over 30–60 minutes
   - Finally: reduce to 0.1–1 mmHg (very low pressure; water is continuously removed)

**Key principle:** As water is removed by vacuum, the equilibrium shifts toward longer chains (Le Chatelier's principle). Without vacuum, the reaction reaches equilibrium with limited molecular weight.

**Step 4: Increase temperature for higher molecular weight.**

1. Once water removal is active (and pressure stable at low levels), increase temperature to 250–280°C
2. The molten polymer becomes increasingly viscous as chains grow
3. Temperature must be held steady; thermal gradients cause local degradation
4. Continue vacuum application (0.1–1 mmHg)

**Monitoring polymerization:**
- **Viscosity increase:** As chains grow, the mixture becomes very thick; mechanical torque on the stirrer increases
- **Water removal:** The amount of water collected (in the condenser and trap) decreases as reaction proceeds
- **Time:** Typical polymerization takes 2–4 hours from salt to final polymer

**Step 5: Assess degree of polymerization.**

Molecular weight can be estimated by:
- **Intrinsic viscosity:** Dissolve a small sample in a solvent (formic acid, sulfuric acid), measure viscosity; higher viscosity correlates with longer chains
- **Melting point:** Higher MW nylon melts at higher temperature (~260°C for MW 15,000)
- **Mechanical properties:** Higher MW nylon is stronger and tougher

**Targeting specific molecular weight:**
- Lower temperature (250°C) = slower reaction = shorter chains possible
- Higher temperature (280°C) = faster reaction = longer chains
- Longer reaction time = longer chains (if vacuum is maintained)
- Imbalanced monomer ratio = limited chain length (excess of one monomer limits growth)

**Step 6: Stop polymerization and cool.**

1. Once desired viscosity is reached (estimated from motor torque), stop heating
2. Reduce vacuum gradually (prevent polymer from foaming as pressure drops)
3. Introduce nitrogen at atmospheric pressure as temperature drops to 150–200°C
4. Cool to room temperature
5. Open the reactor; the polymer is a solid or semi-solid, white to pale yellow color

### Part 2: Polyethylene Terephthalate (PET) Synthesis

PET is made from terephthalic acid (or dimethyl terephthalate) and ethylene glycol. PET is the most common polyester and is used for beverage bottles, fibers, and films.

**Step 1: Prepare monomers and catalyst.**

**If using dimethyl terephthalate (DMT) - the ester precursor:**
1. Charge the reactor with:
   - Dimethyl terephthalate (DMT): 200 g (1.04 mol)
   - Ethylene glycol (EG): 100 g (1.61 mol) - note slight molar excess
   - Catalyst (titanium isopropoxide): ~0.02 wt% (about 0.06 g for this batch)
2. The molar ratio is DMT:EG = 1:1.55 (excess glycol compensates for loss by evaporation)

**If using terephthalic acid (TPA) directly:**
1. Charge with:
   - Terephthalic acid: 200 g (1.2 mol)
   - Ethylene glycol: 100 g (1.61 mol)
   - Catalyst: titanium isopropoxide 0.02 wt%
2. Ensure reactor is dry; TPA is less soluble than DMT initially

**Step 2: Esterification phase (DMT route) or direct reaction (TPA route).**

**DMT route - Esterification:**
1. Heat to 150–200°C under gentle nitrogen flow (not vacuum yet)
2. EG reacts with DMT to form dimethylene terephthalate (monomeric ester) and methanol:
   ```
   DMT + EG → HO-CH₂CH₂-O-CO-Ph-CO-OCH₂CH₂-OH + 2 CH₃OH
   ```
3. Methanol vapors escape (collected in condenser if desired)
4. Temperature gradually increased to 220–250°C
5. As EG is consumed, longer segments form:
   ```
   Segment₁ + Segment₂ → Longer segment + EG (released water-like byproduct)
   ```
6. This phase takes 1–2 hours; mixture remains relatively low viscosity

**Step 3: Polycondensation phase (high vacuum).**

1. Once esterification is largely complete (check by gas chromatography if available; methanol and EG peaks should be small):
   - Reduce temperature slightly to 250–270°C
   - Apply vacuum: start at 10 mmHg, gradually reduce to 0.1–1 mmHg
   - Maintain stirring
2. Now the real polycondensation occurs; ethylene glycol is the primary byproduct:
   ```
   Polymer-(EG) + Polymer-(EG) → Longer polymer + EG ↑ (removed by vacuum)
   ```
3. As vacuum increases, viscosity increases (polymer chains grow)
4. The mixture becomes increasingly thick and amber-colored

**Temperature and vacuum optimization:**
- Too low temperature (under 250°C): reaction is slow
- Too high temperature (over 290°C): thermal degradation occurs (polymer darkens, strength decreases)
- Insufficient vacuum (over 10 mmHg): equilibrium limits MW
- Too vigorous vacuum (under 0.01 mmHg): can vaporize the polymer itself (never go below 0.1 mmHg in practice)

**Step 4: Monitor polymerization progress.**

- **Viscosity:** This is the primary indicator. As chains grow, the mixture becomes progressively thicker.
- **Color:** Should remain pale yellow to colorless. Darkening indicates thermal degradation (reduce temperature immediately).
- **EG removal:** Ethylene glycol is continuously removed; reduced EG vapor pressure indicates reaction completion.

**Typical polymerization time:** 2–4 hours for industrial high-MW PET.

**Step 5: Stop and cool.**

1. Once target viscosity is achieved, stop heating
2. Reduce vacuum gradually (re-introduce nitrogen) to prevent foaming
3. Cool to room temperature
4. Remove the polymer; it is typically a pale yellowish or colorless solid

### Part 3: Fiber Formation and Orientation

Once the polymer is synthesized, it must be converted into fiber form. This requires additional steps: melting, extrusion, cooling, and drawing.

**Step 1: Prepare for fiber spinning.**

1. Heat polymer to melt temperature:
   - Nylon 6,6: 250–270°C
   - PET: 260–290°C
2. Extrude through a spinneret (a metal plate with small holes, typically 0.5–2 mm diameter)
3. Quench the extruded filaments in air or water (cooling solidifies the polymer as it exits)

**Step 2: Draw the fiber (critical for properties).**

Freshly spun fiber is weak. It must be mechanically "drawn" (stretched) to align polymer chains and increase crystallinity:

1. Pass the cooled filaments through drawing rollers set at progressively higher speeds
2. **Draw ratio:** Typically 4–6× (fiber is stretched 4–6 times its original length)
3. As fiber is stretched:
   - Amorphous (disordered) regions crystallize (polymer chains align)
   - Tensile strength increases dramatically
   - Elongation decreases (more rigid)
   - Melting point may increase slightly
4. Heat can be applied during drawing (~80–100°C for nylon, ~100–120°C for PET) to enhance crystallization

**Example:** A fiber extruded at 100 m/min is drawn by passing through a set of rollers spinning at 400–500 m/min (depending on desired draw ratio).

**Step 3: Post-treatments.**

1. **Annealing:** Brief heating at the final temperature (90–100°C for nylon, 150–160°C for PET, held for 1–10 minutes) stabilizes the fiber structure
2. **Relaxation:** Controlled heating with partial relaxation (allowing slight contraction) reduces residual stress in fibers
3. **Sizing:** Apply a thin coating (water or polymer lubricant) to reduce friction for subsequent processing (weaving, knitting)

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Monomer Stoichiometry for Nylon 6,6

You want to synthesize 1 kg of nylon 6,6. Both adipic acid and hexamethylenediamine are needed in equimolar amounts.

- Adipic acid: MW = 146 g/mol
- Hexamethylenediamine: MW = 116 g/mol

For every adipic acid molecule, one hexamethylene diamine is needed.

Let's assume 95% yield (5% lost to incomplete reaction and as water):
- Target final polymer mass: 1 kg
- Monomers needed: 1 kg / 0.95 = 1.053 kg monomers

The combined MW of one unit of each monomer minus one water molecule:
- MW(adipic acid) + MW(hexamethylenediamine) - MW(H₂O) = 146 + 116 - 18 = 244 g/mol per linking step

If we need 1.053 kg of monomers:
- Adipic acid: 1053 g × [146/(146+116)] = 1053 × 0.557 = 587 g
- Hexamethylenediamine: 1053 g × [116/(146+116)] = 1053 × 0.443 = 466 g

### Example 2: Draw Ratio for Fiber Strength

You have PET fiber just after quenching (undrawn). Its tensile strength is about 20 MPa (megapascals). After drawing at a 5× draw ratio, tensile strength increases to ~80 MPa.

This 4× increase in strength is due to:
- Crystallinity increase: ~30–40% to ~70–80% crystalline
- Chain alignment: chains are now parallel, not random
- Reduced chain entanglement: straightening reduces voids

This demonstrates why drawing is essential for synthetic fiber properties.

### Example 3: Molecular Weight Estimation by Intrinsic Viscosity

You've synthesized nylon and want to estimate its molecular weight. You dissolve 1 g in 100 mL of formic acid and measure the solution viscosity using a viscometer.

**Measurement:** Relative viscosity (solution viscosity / solvent viscosity) = 2.5

**Intrinsic viscosity:** [η] ≈ 0.8 dL/g (rough estimate from relative viscosity for nylon)

**Mark-Houwink relationship for nylon (typical):**
[η] = K × M^a, where K ≈ 0.000435 and a ≈ 0.80

Solving for M:
0.8 = 0.000435 × M^0.80
M^0.80 = 0.8 / 0.000435 ≈ 1840
M ≈ 15,000 g/mol (approximate)

This is a moderate molecular weight nylon, suitable for textiles or engineering plastics.

## Verification Criteria

Successful nylon or polyester synthesis is confirmed by:

1. **Melting point:** Measure the temperature at which the polymer softens and flows
   - Nylon 6,6: ~260°C
   - Nylon 6: ~215°C
   - PET: ~250–260°C

2. **Solubility test:** 
   - Nylon: soluble in formic acid, sulfuric acid; insoluble in water
   - PET: slightly soluble in heated dichloromethane; insoluble in most common solvents

3. **Mechanical properties of spun fiber:**
   - Tensile strength after drawing: nylon 70–100 MPa, PET 70–100 MPa
   - Elongation at break: nylon 15–20%, PET 10–15%

4. **Molecular weight:** By intrinsic viscosity or gel permeation chromatography (if available)
   - Industrial nylon: 15,000–25,000 g/mol
   - Industrial PET: 25,000–35,000 g/mol (higher MW for bottle-grade)

## Common Mistakes

1. **Stoichiometric imbalance:** Using wrong ratio of diacid to diamine. If adipic acid is in excess, all diamine is consumed and chain growth stops. **Fix:** Verify molar ratio carefully; use the pre-salt method for nylon (ensures stoichiometry).

2. **Insufficient vacuum during polycondensation:** Water or other byproducts remain, limiting molecular weight. **Fix:** Ensure vacuum pump is functioning; use a molecular sieve trap to dry the vacuum line; maintain vacuum continuously throughout reaction.

3. **Thermal degradation:** Temperature too high causes polymer to darken and lose strength. **Fix:** Keep temperature under 280°C for nylon, under 290°C for PET; use nitrogen blanket to exclude oxygen (reduces oxidative degradation).

4. **Inadequate cooling before opening reactor:** Removing hot polymer can cause oxidation. **Fix:** Cool to below 150°C before opening; let polymer cool in inert atmosphere.

5. **Poor fiber drawing:** Under-drawn fiber is weak and stretches; over-drawn fiber may break. **Fix:** Use 4–6× draw ratio; monitor for filament breakage; heat slightly during drawing to enhance crystallization.

6. **Contamination with water:** Moisture hydrolyzes the polymer chains, reducing MW. **Fix:** Dry all monomers before use; use dry nitrogen gas; avoid opening reactor to humid air.
