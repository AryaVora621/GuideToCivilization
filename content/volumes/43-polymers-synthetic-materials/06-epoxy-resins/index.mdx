## Overview

Epoxy is a polymer that hardens through a chemical reaction between two components: resin and hardener. When mixed, they create an extremely strong adhesive or coating material.

Epoxies are valuable because:

- **Strength:** Bonds stronger than the materials they join
- **Durability:** Resists water, chemicals, heat, and UV
- **Versatility:** Works on wood, metal, ceramics, concrete
- **Reliability:** Cures predictably; performance is consistent

In a post-collapse world, epoxy would replace synthetic adhesives (gone) and outperform natural glues (animal hide, plant starches).

This chapter teaches epoxy chemistry, how to formulate it from precursor chemicals, and how to use it.

## Part 1: Understanding Epoxy Chemistry

### Basic Structure

Epoxy has two main precursors:

**Epichlorohydrin (EPI):**

A three-membered ring compound (epoxide group) that is highly reactive.

**Bisphenol A (BPA) or similar:**

A phenol compound with two reactive hydroxyl groups. When heated with alkali, BPA reacts with epichlorohydrin to form a prepolymer (pre-epoxy resin).

**Polymerization reaction:**

```
Epichlorohydrin + Bisphenol A + NaOH → Epoxy prepolymer (liquid resin)
```

The prepolymer is a viscous liquid with epoxide groups at each end.

### Hardening (Cross-linking)

When mixed with a hardener (such as an aliphatic amine or anhydride), the epoxide groups react with the hardener, creating cross-links between polymer chains.

**Amine hardening (room temperature):**

```
Epoxide + Amine → Cross-linked polymer (solid)
```

Reaction is exothermic (releases heat). Curing time: hours to days depending on temperature and hardener type.

**Anhydride hardening (heat-activated):**

```
Epoxide + Anhydride (heated) → Cross-linked polymer
```

Requires heating to 80–150°C. Slower reaction, but produces harder final product.

### Properties of Epoxy

The final hardened epoxy has:

- **High tensile strength:** 50–90 MPa (stronger than wood or many metals)
- **Shear strength:** 25–40 MPa (resistance to tearing)
- **Elongation before breaking:** 3–6% (relatively brittle, but strong)
- **Adhesive strength:** Bonds to almost any material
- **Chemical resistance:** Resists acids, bases, solvents
- **Water resistance:** Cured epoxy doesn't absorb water
- **Temperature resistance:** Withstands up to 120–200°C (depends on hardener)

## Part 2: Formulating Epoxy

### Step 1: Synthesis of Epoxy Prepolymer

This requires alkali-catalyzed reaction between epichlorohydrin and bisphenol A.

**Procedure:**

1. **Prepare vessel:** Use glass or stainless steel reactor; add stirrer

2. **Initial charge:**
   - BPA: 200g
   - Distilled water: 300ml
   - Sodium hydroxide: 8g (NaOH helps dissolve BPA)

3. **Heat gently:** Warm to approximately 50–60°C while stirring. BPA dissolves.

4. **Add epichlorohydrin:** Slowly add 500g epichlorohydrin to the warm solution while stirring continuously (exothermic reaction). Temperature will rise to 70–80°C.

5. **React:** Maintain temperature at 70–90°C and stir for 2–4 hours. The reaction between epichlorohydrin and BPA proceeds.

6. **Cool:** Allow mixture to cool to room temperature.

7. **Extract resin:** The resin separates from water. Use separatory funnel to drain water; rinse resin with distilled water to remove salt (byproduct of NaOH + HCl neutralization).

**Result:** Liquid epoxy resin, viscous, amber-colored.

**Yield:** Approximately 600–700g of usable epoxy resin.

### Step 2: Select and Prepare Hardener

**Options:**

**Aliphatic amine hardener (room temperature cure):**
- Example: Aliphatic polyamine
- Mixing ratio: Approximately 1:1 or 2:1 (resin:hardener) by weight (depends on specific hardener)
- Cure time: 4–24 hours at room temperature
- Advantages: Fast, no heating required
- Disadvantages: Volatile (fumes), less heat-resistant
- Availability: Can be synthesized or sourced from industrial suppliers

**Cycloaliphatic amine hardener:**
- Lower volatility than aliphatic
- Better heat resistance
- Similar cure characteristics

**Anhydride hardener (heat-activated):**
- Example: Phthalic anhydride or succinic anhydride
- Mixing ratio: Approximately 1:0.5 (resin:anhydride) by weight
- Cure temperature: 80–150°C, cure time 1–4 hours at temperature
- Advantages: Very hard, heat-resistant, non-volatile
- Disadvantages: Requires heating; longer initial cure time
- Availability: Anhydrides are more readily available in post-collapse scenarios than amines

### Step 3: Formulation for Specific Applications

**General-purpose adhesive (20 parts resin : 10 parts hardener):**

1. Mix resin and hardener by weight
2. Stir well for 2–3 minutes
3. Air is trapped; allow to stand 5 minutes to release bubbles
4. Apply immediately
5. Cure time: 4–24 hours depending on hardener and temperature

**Two-part epoxy putty (filler added):**

1. Mix resin and hardener
2. Add talc, chalk, or finely ground sand (up to 50% by volume)
3. Form into putty-like paste
4. Mold into shape before curing

**Coating or casting resin (low viscosity):**

1. Mix resin and hardener
2. Thin with solvent (if needed) to reduce viscosity
3. Pour into mold or apply as coating
4. Vacuum degas (if equipment available) to remove bubbles
5. Cure in mold

## Part 3: Practical Application

### Step 1: Surface Preparation

For maximum adhesion:

1. **Clean:** Remove dust, oil, and contaminants with solvent
2. **Roughen:** Sand or scrape surface lightly (provides mechanical grip)
3. **Dry:** Ensure surface is completely dry (moisture weakens bond)

### Step 2: Mixing and Application

1. **Measure components:** Weigh resin and hardener separately (accuracy is important)

2. **Mix thoroughly:** Combine resin and hardener; stir for 2–3 minutes. Thorough mixing ensures even reaction.

3. **De-bubble (optional):** Let mixture sit for 5 minutes so trapped air bubbles rise and escape.

4. **Apply:** Use brush, spreader, or pour depending on application.

5. **Clamp:** For bonding, clamp parts together firmly to ensure good contact. Clamp pressure: approximately 5–20 PSI (varies with surface area).

### Step 3: Curing

**Room-temperature cure (amine hardener):**

- Initial set: 30 minutes to 2 hours (not fully hard, but won't flow)
- Intermediate cure: 24 hours (most strength is developed)
- Full cure: 3–7 days (maximum strength and chemical resistance)

**Heat-accelerated cure (anhydride hardener):**

- Room temperature: Essentially no reaction
- At 100°C: Cure time approximately 2–4 hours
- Full cure: After cooling to room temperature

### Step 4: Post-cure for Maximum Strength

For critical applications, heat-cure the epoxy after initial setting:

1. **Allow initial set:** Let epoxy cure at room temperature for 12–24 hours
2. **Heat cycle:** Gradually increase temperature to 80–120°C (depending on hardener)
3. **Hold temperature:** Maintain for 2–4 hours
4. **Cool slowly:** Reduce temperature gradually to avoid thermal shock

This improves heat resistance and strength.

## Worked Example: Epoxy Adhesive for Structural Repair

**Scenario:** A broken wooden beam needs to be repaired (two pieces must be bonded).

**Preparation:**

1. **Cut surfaces:** Cut the broken beam at clean angles (flat surfaces bond better than jagged)

2. **Clean:** Scrape away any debris or mold; wipe with cloth

3. **Roughen:** Sand both surfaces lightly with coarse sandpaper

4. **Dry:** Ensure surfaces are completely dry (let air-dry for at least 1 hour)

**Epoxy preparation:**

- Formula: Standard adhesive (20:10 ratio)
- Resin: 40g
- Hardener: 20g
- Total: 60g of working epoxy

**Application:**

1. Weigh resin and hardener
2. Mix thoroughly for 3 minutes
3. Spread epoxy on both bonding surfaces
4. Press beam pieces together firmly
5. Clamp with C-clamps or similar, applying steady pressure

**Curing:**

- Initial set: 2 hours (can remove light clamps after this)
- Full cure: 24 hours (before heavy loading)
- For maximum strength: Optional heat-cure at 100°C for 2 hours after 24-hour room-temperature cure

**Result:** Structural bond stronger than original wood. Beam can be sanded flush after curing.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Incorrect mixing ratio.** Too much or too little hardener prevents full reaction. Weigh carefully; follow the ratio exactly.

2. **Inadequate mixing.** Unmixed epoxy doesn't harden fully. Stir thoroughly for at least 2–3 minutes; scrape bowl bottom frequently.

3. **Wet surfaces.** Water inhibits epoxy curing and weakens bonds. Ensure complete dryness before application.

4. **Dirty or oily surfaces.** Contaminants prevent epoxy from bonding. Clean thoroughly.

5. **Loose clamps.** Without pressure, epoxy doesn't form a good bond. Clamp firmly; maintain pressure until initial set.

6. **Rushing to use the bond.** Full strength takes 24+ hours. Loading too early causes failure.

7. **Applying in cold conditions.** Below 10°C, epoxy cures very slowly or not at all. Warm the epoxy and work area if cold.

8. **Too much viscosity for desired application.** If epoxy is too thick to pour or spread, thin with solvent (acetone or ethanol) to desired consistency.

9. **Expired hardener.** Amine hardeners absorb water from air and degrade. Use fresh hardener; store in sealed container.

10. **Ignoring safety.** Epoxy can be irritating. Use gloves, eye protection, and ensure ventilation.
