## Overview

Polyvinyl chloride (PVC) is the world's third-most produced polymer after PE and PP, accounting for roughly 15% of global plastic production. PVC has unique properties: it is naturally flame-resistant, has excellent chemical resistance, and is easy to formulate with additives. However, PVC synthesis and processing are more complex than simple PE or PP because:

1. **Vinyl chloride monomer (VCM) is toxic:** It requires careful handling and closed-loop recycling
2. **PVC thermally unstable:** Pure PVC decomposes above 60–70°C, releasing hydrogen chloride gas, which degrades the polymer further (autocatalytic degradation)
3. **PVC requires stabilizers and plasticizers:** To prevent degradation and to achieve flexible or rigid properties

This chapter covers vinyl chloride monomer synthesis, PVC polymerization, and the formulation principles needed to make PVC a usable material.

## Materials & Tools

### For Vinyl Chloride Monomer (VCM) Synthesis

**Raw materials (via the oxychlorination route - industrial standard):**
- Ethylene (C₂H₄): industrial-grade, 95–99% pure
- Chlorine gas (Cl₂): from chlor-alkali process or on-site generation
- Water vapor (as oxygen source in some processes)
- Catalyst: copper(II) chloride on silica or alumina support

**Alternative route (direct chlorination):**
- Ethylene: industrial-grade
- Chlorine gas: 99%+ pure
- Light (UV radiation): to initiate free radical chlorination

**Equipment for VCM synthesis:**
- Fixed-bed reactor: for catalytic oxychlorination or direct chlorination
- Heat exchanger: to control reaction temperature (200–220°C typical)
- Chlorine generation system (if making your own): electrochemical cell or limestone-salt reaction
- Gas drying system: to remove water vapor (for some routes)
- Absorption tower: to trap unreacted chlorine (alkaline solution, e.g., NaOH)
- Distillation column: to separate VCM from byproducts (1,2-dichloroethane, higher chlorine compounds)

### For PVC Polymerization

**Raw materials:**
- Vinyl chloride monomer (VCM), pure: >99.5%
- Suspension polymerization:
  - Water (deionized, degassed)
  - Initiator: benzoyl peroxide or azobisisobutyronitrile
  - Suspending agent: polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) or gelatin (to keep PVC particles suspended)
- Emulsion polymerization (alternative):
  - Water
  - Emulsifier: sodium dodecyl sulfate or other anionic surfactant
  - Initiator: potassium persulfate or similar

**Equipment for polymerization:**
- Sealed reactor (stainless steel): rated for monomer vapors and pressure (VCM boils at 14°C, so vapor pressure is significant)
- Cooling jacket: capable of removing ~200 kJ/kg heat (polymerization is exothermic)
- Agitator: for suspension polymerization (gentle stirring to suspend particles without breaking them)
- Temperature sensor and controls
- Vacuum system: to remove unreacted VCM after polymerization

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Vinyl Chloride Monomer (VCM) Synthesis via Oxychlorination

The most economical industrial route for VCM production is oxychlorination: ethylene + chlorine + oxygen/water vapor → vinyl chloride + water, with copper(II) chloride as catalyst.

**Step 1: Prepare the reactor.**

1. Load a fixed-bed reactor (tube reactor or reactor filled with catalyst pellets) with copper(II) chloride catalyst (3–5 wt% Cu on silica or alumina support)
2. Heat the reactor to 200–220°C
3. Set up gas flows:
   - Ethylene: 1–2 moles per hour (adjust based on reactor size)
   - Chlorine: 1.5–2.5 moles per hour (slight excess to ensure complete conversion)
   - Water vapor (from heated water): 0.5–1 mole per hour (oxygen source for the catalytic cycle)
4. Mix the gases upstream of the reactor
5. Ensure all lines are dry; water-cooled condenser downstream of the reactor

**Step 2: Run the oxychlorination reaction.**

The reaction proceeds as:
```
C₂H₄ + Cl₂ → C₂H₄Cl₂ (1,2-dichloroethane, intermediate)
C₂H₄Cl₂ + CuCl₂ → C₂H₃Cl + HCl + CuCl (VCM formation with hydrogen chloride)
```

**Under steady-state conditions:**
- Conversion of ethylene: 95–99%
- Selectivity to VCM: 85–90% (rest goes to higher chlorinated products: trichloroethane, tetrachloroethane)
- Temperature: 200–220°C (higher = faster but more byproducts)
- Contact time: 1–5 seconds (brief contact prevents over-chlorination)

**Monitoring:**
- Exit gas composition (using gas chromatography if available): should show VCM as the major product
- Reactor temperature (keep steady by adjusting flow rates)
- Pressure drop (indicates catalytic activity; increases as catalyst fouls, decreases if catalyst breaks down)

**Step 3: Condense and separate VCM.**

Downstream of the reactor:
1. Cool the exit gases to condense unreacted water and VCM (cool to -5 to +10°C in a condenser)
2. Separate condensed products (water, VCM, 1,2-dichloroethane) from unreacted gases (Cl₂, ethylene, HCl vapor)
3. Send unreacted HCl vapor to an absorption column with NaOH solution
4. Recycle unreacted ethylene and Cl₂ back to the reactor inlet (with fresh feed)

**Step 4: Distill and purify VCM.**

The condensed product mixture contains:
- Water: 10–20%
- 1,2-dichloroethane: 70–80%
- VCM: 5–15%
- Higher chlorinated compounds: trace to a few percent

**Distillation column specifications:**
- Boiling points at 1 atm:
  - Water: 100°C
  - 1,2-dichloroethane: 83°C
  - VCM: 14°C (boils even at room temperature; must be handled pressurized or cryogenically)

**Distillation procedure:**
1. In the first column, separate VCM from heavier compounds:
   - Use a packed or tray column, pressurized (5–10 atm) to keep VCM liquid
   - Condense VCM vapors with a chilled condenser
   - Recover pure VCM as the overhead product
   - Recycle 1,2-dichloroethane to the reactor (it acts as intermediate and reactant)

2. VCM final purity should be under 99.5% before polymerization (trace impurities do not affect polymerization significantly).

### Part 2: PVC Polymerization by Suspension Method

Suspension polymerization is the industrial standard for PVC because it produces granules directly (easier to handle and store than latex) and gives good control over particle size and final polymer properties.

**Step 1: Charge the reactor.**

1. Into a 10–100 liter sealed reactor, add:
   - Deionized, degassed water: 60–70% by weight of the total charge
   - VCM: 30–40% by weight
   - Suspending agent (polyvinyl alcohol): 0.5–1% by weight (maintains fine particle suspension)
   - Initiator (benzoyl peroxide or similar): 0.05–0.2% by weight
2. Ensure the reactor is sealed and inert (nitrogen atmosphere preferred)
3. Stir gently (50–100 rpm) to keep suspending agent and initiator well distributed

**Critical point:** VCM is volatile and toxic. Do not allow losses; must be handled in sealed system.

**Step 2: Heat and control temperature.**

1. Turn on cooling jacket before heating (have cooling ready)
2. Heat slowly to 50–60°C (VCM exerts vapor pressure; ensure reactor is sealed)
3. Once at temperature, turn on agitator (gentle stirring)
4. The reaction becomes very exothermic; cooling jacket must remove ~200 kJ per kg of VCM polymerized

**Reaction kinetics:**
- At 50°C: slow start, gradual heat generation
- At 60°C: heat generation peaks; requires continuous cooling
- Avoid temperatures above 70°C (side reactions and degradation)

**Step 3: Monitor polymerization progress.**

The polymerization typically proceeds over 6–15 hours. Monitor:

**Pressure:** VCM exerts vapor pressure:
- At 50°C: ~3 atm
- At 60°C: ~5 atm
- Pressure increases as water vapor also builds up
- Monitor for leaks; pressure should remain stable

**Heat generation:** This is the best real-time indicator
- First 2–4 hours: significant heat generation (reaction active)
- Middle 4–10 hours: steady heat generation (reaction continuing)
- Final 2–4 hours: declining heat generation (approaching completion)

**Polymer formation:**
- The mixture becomes opaque (white); water phase remains clear
- Particles form in water; suspension remains uniform if agitation is good

**Degree of polymerization:** Targeted monomer conversion is typically 85–95%; higher conversion increases polymer viscosity but residual VCM is harder to remove.

**Step 4: Stop polymerization and cool.**

1. Once 85–90% conversion is reached (estimated from heat generation rate), stop heating
2. Continue cooling to room temperature (takes 2–4 hours)
3. Reduce agitation speed gradually as temperature drops (to avoid excessive shear on polymer granules)
4. The polymer settles as a white granular suspension in water

**Step 5: Recover and dry the PVC.**

1. Filter or centrifuge the suspension to separate PVC granules from water
2. Wash the granules 2–3 times with distilled water to remove residual water-soluble salts and suspending agent
3. Drain well and transfer to a drying vessel
4. Dry under vacuum at 60–80°C for 2–4 hours (important: do NOT exceed 80°C, as PVC begins to degrade)

**Final product:** White granular PVC, typically 0.1–0.5 mm diameter, with residual VCM content under 1% (remainder is removed by vacuum drying and heating).

### Part 3: PVC Stabilization and Formulation

Pure PVC is thermally unstable above 60–70°C and undergoes rapid degradation, releasing HCl, which catalyzes further degradation (autocatalytic). To make PVC useful, stabilizers must be added.

**Step 1: Choose stabilizer system.**

Common stabilizer packages:

**Metal-based stabilizers:**
- Lead compounds (lead stearate, lead carbonate): very effective but toxic
- Cadmium compounds: effective but highly toxic
- Barium/zinc combinations (BaZn): good heat stability, no toxicity concerns
- Calcium/zinc (CaZn): lower cost than BaZn, adequate for many applications

**Mechanism:** Metal ions chelate or deactivate the HCl and free radicals produced during polymer degradation.

**Typical stabilizer loading:** 2–5 phr (parts per hundred resin, by weight).

**Step 2: Add plasticizers (for flexible PVC).**

Pure PVC is rigid and brittle. To make it flexible (e.g., for vinyl tubing, shower curtains, cables), plasticizers are added. Plasticizers are solvents that disrupt polymer chain packing, increasing flexibility.

**Common plasticizers:**
- Dioctyl phthalate (DOP): most common, 25–50 phr typical
- Diisononyl phthalate (DINP): higher molecular weight, less volatile
- Epoxidized soybean oil: lower toxicity alternative
- Citrates: for specialized applications

**Effect of plasticizer:**
- At 0 phr: rigid, brittle
- At 20–30 phr: semi-flexible
- At 50+ phr: flexible, rubbery

**Step 3: Mix PVC with additives.**

1. Weigh PVC granules and additives (stabilizers, plasticizers, processing aids)
2. Dry-blend in a tumble mixer or high-speed mixer (5–10 minutes)
3. Feed into an extruder or mixing equipment
4. At 170–190°C, the mixture fuses (PVC melts, plasticizer dissolves, stabilizer disperses)
5. Mix thoroughly to ensure uniform distribution
6. Cool and extrude, or form into pellets

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Vinyl Chloride Yield Calculation

In an oxychlorination reactor, you feed:
- Ethylene: 100 kg/hour (3.6 kmol/hour)
- Chlorine: 120 kg/hour (1.7 kmol/hour)

At 85% conversion of ethylene and 90% selectivity to VCM:

Ethylene converted = 3.6 kmol/hr × 0.85 = 3.06 kmol/hr
VCM produced = 3.06 kmol/hr × 0.90 = 2.75 kmol/hr
VCM mass = 2.75 kmol/hr × 62.5 g/kmol = 171.875 kg/hr

**Energy equivalent:** Each kg of VCM requires ~10 MJ of heat (rough estimate including reactor losses).

### Example 2: PVC Formulation for Flexible Tubing

You want to produce 1000 kg of flexible PVC for medical tubing with the following formulation:

- PVC resin: 100 phr
- Dioctyl phthalate plasticizer: 40 phr
- Barium/zinc stabilizer: 3 phr

**How much of each component?**

Total parts = 100 + 40 + 3 = 143 phr

Per kg of final product:
- PVC = 1000 × (100/143) = 699 kg
- DOP = 1000 × (40/143) = 280 kg
- Stabilizer = 1000 × (3/143) = 21 kg

**Total = 1000 kg** ✓

### Example 3: Conversion and Residual VCM

You're polymerizing VCM in suspension. Starting with 500 kg of VCM in the reactor, you achieve 87% conversion.

Unreacted VCM remaining = 500 kg × (1 - 0.87) = 65 kg

After drying under vacuum at 70°C for 3 hours, residual VCM drops to under 1% of the final product mass.

Final PVC mass ≈ 435 kg (from 500 kg VCM)
Residual VCM in final product = 435 kg × 0.005 = 2.2 kg

This remaining VCM is acceptable for most applications (food-contact regulations allow under 1 ppm, roughly equivalent).

## Verification Criteria

Successful PVC production is confirmed by:

1. **Visual inspection:** White, granular product; no dark colors or odor of HCl
2. **Thermal stability test:** Heat a small sample at 200°C; a stable polymer will soften without discoloration; unstable PVC darkens (degradation)
3. **Solution properties:** Dissolve PVC in tetrahydrofuran (THF) or cyclohexanone; it should dissolve completely to a clear, colorless solution
4. **Mechanical properties:** Unplasticized PVC is hard and brittle; plasticized PVC (with DOP) is flexible and tough
5. **Residual VCM:** Using gas chromatography, residual VCM should be under 1% (better under 0.1%)

## Common Mistakes

1. **Inadequate stabilization:** Unstable PVC darkens at 60–80°C during drying or processing. **Fix:** Add sufficient stabilizer (3–5 phr minimum); verify stabilizer is properly mixed.

2. **Temperature excursion during drying:** If PVC exceeds 80°C during vacuum drying, degradation starts. **Fix:** Use controlled heating, monitor temperature, use lower temperature with longer time.

3. **Incomplete VCM removal:** Residual VCM above 1% causes odor and health concerns. **Fix:** Increase drying time, lower pressure (vacuum), or heat slightly higher (but not above 80°C).

4. **Chlorine gas handling:** Chlorine is toxic; improper handling causes personnel injury. **Fix:** Use proper absorption (NaOH scrubber) for excess chlorine; work in a fume hood; use appropriate PPE.

5. **Vinyl chloride monomer exposure:** VCM is carcinogenic; it requires closed-loop handling. **Fix:** Do not allow unsealed containers; use proper ventilation; recover and recycle unreacted VCM.

6. **Wrong plasticizer ratio for application:** Too little plasticizer = brittle; too much = sticky, exudes. **Fix:** Test formulation on small batches; verify mechanical properties.
