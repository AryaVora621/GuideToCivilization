## Overview

Polyethylene (PE) and polypropylene (PP) are the simplest and most abundant synthetic polymers. Together, they represent roughly 60% of global plastic production. These materials are everywhere: plastic bags, bottles, milk jugs, food containers, pipes, fibers, and films.

What makes these polymers so valuable is their combination of ease of synthesis, low cost, versatility, and decent mechanical properties. Unlike more complex polymers, both PE and PP can be synthesized at scale using relatively simple chemistry.

However, their synthesis is not trivial. The properties of PE and PP depend critically on:

1. **Catalyst type:** Influences chain structure (linear vs. branched), crystallinity, and reaction rate
2. **Pressure:** Determines reaction kinetics and final polymer properties
3. **Temperature:** Controls reaction rate and polymer degradation
4. **Monomer purity:** Impurities can poison catalysts
5. **Reactor design:** Determines heat removal capacity and product quality

This chapter focuses on the chemistry and engineering of producing PE and PP with controlled properties.

## Materials & Tools

### For Free Radical Polyethylene (LDPE)

**Raw materials:**
- Ethylene gas (C₂H₄), high purity (remove water, oxygen, acetylene)
- Initiator: benzoyl peroxide, diisopropyl peroxydicarbonate, or peroxydisulfate
- Solvent (optional): ethane, propane, or hydrocarbons
- Stabilizers: hindered amines (to prevent oxidation) and phenols (to inhibit free radicals post-reaction)

**Equipment:**
- High-pressure reactor (1000–3000 atm pressure-rated, usually steel vessel with internal cooling)
- Gas compression system: compressor, dryer, filter, regulator
- Temperature control: cooling jacket or internal cooling coils
- Agitator: high-speed stirrer (300–1000 rpm) for good mixing and heat transfer
- Relief valve: critical for safety; prevents overpressure
- Product recovery system: cyclone separator or filtration for polymer isolation

### For Catalyst-Based Polyethylene (HDPE) and Polypropylene (PP)

**Raw materials:**
- Ethylene (C₂H₄) or propylene (C₃H₆), high purity
- Ziegler-Natta catalyst:
  - Titanium chloride (TiCl₃ or TiCl₄): transition metal component
  - Aluminum alkyl (AlEt₃ or AliBu₃): co-catalyst
  - Internal donor (diisobutyl phthalate, isobutyl benzoate): for stereocontrol in PP
- Monomer feed system
- Inert solvent (hexane, cyclohexane): inert to catalyst

**Equipment:**
- Low-pressure reactor (20–50 atm, suitable for slurry or gas-phase processes)
- Catalyst feeding system: controlled, synchronized introduction
- Agitator: for slurry polymerization or gas-phase contact
- Temperature control: cooling jacket (exothermic reaction releases significant heat)
- Gas inlet: for continuous monomer supply in gas-phase processes
- Product recovery: filtration, washing, drying

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Low-Density Polyethylene (LDPE) by Free Radical Polymerization

Low-density polyethylene is made by polymerizing ethylene at extremely high pressure (1000–3000 atm) and moderate temperature (100–300°C). The high pressure keeps ethylene in a liquid-like state, increasing monomer concentration and reaction rate. Free radicals cause branching as the growing chain occasionally transfers to itself, creating side branches.

**Step 1: Prepare the reactor and safety systems.**

1. Inspect the high-pressure reactor for leaks, cracks, or damage
2. Check all seals and gaskets (must handle extremes of pressure and temperature)
3. Ensure the relief valve is set to approximately 10% above maximum operating pressure
4. Install pressure gauges on inlet and outlet
5. Verify cooling water flow rate (typically 10–20 liters per minute for a lab-scale reactor)
6. Run a pressure test with inert gas (nitrogen) to 120% of target pressure to verify integrity

**Step 2: Charge ethylene and initiator.**

1. Pressurize the reactor with dry nitrogen to 500 psi (35 atm) to verify no leaks
2. Vent nitrogen carefully
3. Attach the ethylene supply cylinder to the inlet, with regulators and flow meter
4. Slowly introduce ethylene gas while monitoring pressure
5. When target pressure is reached (e.g., 2000 atm), disconnect the ethylene supply
6. Dissolve the initiator (0.01–0.2% by weight) in a small amount of ethane or propane solvent
7. Using a high-pressure syringe pump, inject the initiator solution into the reactor

**Critical pressure control:** Keep pressure constant by venting excess ethylene or injecting more as needed. Pressure fluctuations cause uneven polymerization.

**Step 3: Heat and control temperature.**

1. Turn on the cooling water supply
2. Heat the reactor slowly (10–20°C per minute) to target temperature (120–200°C for LDPE)
3. Once at temperature, the exothermic reaction will generate additional heat
4. Adjust cooling flow rate to maintain steady temperature (±5°C)
5. Monitor pressure; expect it to increase slightly due to heat

**Heat balance:** For a 1-liter reactor at 2000 atm and 150°C, the reaction releases enough heat to increase temperature by ~1–2°C per minute if polymerization is rapid. The cooling system must remove this heat continuously.

**Step 4: Monitor polymerization progress.**

- **Initial phase (0–30 min):** Pressure may increase as heat builds up; monitor closely
- **Active polymerization (30 min–several hours):** Steady heat generation; cooling system maintains temperature
- **Completion (depends on initiator, usually 4–8 hours):** Heat generation slows; reaction naturally stops as initiator is consumed and ethylene concentration drops

Reaction completion is indicated by:
- Slow temperature rise even with heating
- Gradual pressure decrease (ethylene is consumed and not replaced)
- Viscosity visible in the window (if reactor has one): viscous product indicates polymer formation

**Step 5: Stop the reaction and recover the polymer.**

1. Turn off heating and cooling
2. Allow reactor to cool naturally to room temperature (may take several hours)
3. Slowly open the outlet valve to depressurize
4. Open the top of the reactor and remove the polymer (solid or very viscous)
5. If polymer is in the form of a slurry in solvent, filter and wash with excess solvent (hexane or ethane)
6. Dry the polymer in a vacuum oven at 80–100°C for 2–4 hours

**Product appearance:** LDPE from free radical polymerization is typically white, waxy, and soft. It can be easily deformed by hand at room temperature.

### Part 2: High-Density Polyethylene (HDPE) Using Ziegler-Natta Catalysts

High-density polyethylene uses a completely different synthesis route: Ziegler-Natta catalysts at much lower pressure (10–50 atm). These catalysts produce linear polyethylene chains with minimal branching, resulting in higher density, higher melting point, and greater stiffness compared to LDPE.

**Step 1: Prepare the Ziegler-Natta catalyst.**

A Ziegler-Natta catalyst has two essential components:

**Transition metal (TiCl₃ or TiCl₄):**
- Acts as the active site
- Ti exists in oxidation state +3 (usually) or +4
- Surface of solid TiCl₃ particles contains reactive sites

**Co-catalyst (aluminum alkyl, e.g., AlEt₃):**
- Aluminum triethyl: Al(C₂H₅)₃
- Alkylates the Ti surface, creating an active Ti-alkyl species
- Provides alkyl groups that polymerize the monomer
- Usually needed in 10–20 fold molar excess of Ti

**Catalyst preparation:**
1. Weigh TiCl₃ (or similar titanium compound): typically 0.5–2 grams for a 2-liter reactor
2. Suspend in a small volume of dry, inert solvent (cyclohexane, hexane)
3. In a separate container, prepare aluminum alkyl solution: AlEt₃ in hexane
4. Cool both solutions to 0–10°C (exothermic reaction when mixed)
5. Add aluminum alkyl slowly to TiCl₃ suspension with vigorous stirring
6. Allow the mixture to age at 20–25°C for 1–24 hours (activation period; crystal structure rearranges)
7. After aging, the catalyst is ready for use

**Critical control:** Moisture and oxygen destroy Ziegler-Natta catalysts instantly. Use only dry, degassed solvents. Handle under inert atmosphere (nitrogen or argon).

**Step 2: Prepare the reactor (slurry process).**

The slurry process is the most common route for HDPE:

1. Add inert solvent (cyclohexane) to the reactor: 1–2 liters
2. Heat to 60–80°C (reaction temperature, typically lower than free radical processes)
3. Purge with dry ethylene gas for 5 minutes to saturate the solvent with monomer
4. Adjust ethylene pressure to 20–40 atm (monomer supply)
5. Using a syringe or pump, inject the pre-activated catalyst (TiCl₃ + AlEt₃) into the reactor
6. Turn on agitator to 300–600 rpm (vigorous stirring to keep solid polymer particles suspended)

**Step 3: Monitor and control polymerization.**

Once catalyst is added:

**Fast initiation:** The polymerization starts immediately and rapidly. Polymer particles form in the solvent (slurry).

**Temperature control:** The reaction is **highly exothermic**. Each mole of ethylene polymerized releases ~100 kJ/mol of heat. With catalyst, polymerization is rapid:
- Without active cooling, temperature can jump from 70°C to 100°C in minutes
- Cooling water flow must be adjusted to maintain target temperature (±2–5°C)

**Reaction rate:** With Ziegler-Natta catalysts, the reaction rate can be adjusted by:
- Temperature: higher = faster (but risk of side reactions)
- Monomer pressure: higher = faster
- Catalyst concentration: more catalyst = faster
- Aluminum alkyl concentration: more cocatalyst = faster

**Typical polymerization time:** 1–4 hours for industrial-scale HDPE (depending on desired conversion).

**Step 4: Stop the polymerization.**

Polymerization can be stopped by:

1. **Reducing monomer supply:** Close ethylene inlet; pressure drops and reaction slows
2. **Cooling:** Reduce temperature; reaction rate drops with lower kinetic energy
3. **Catalyst deactivation:** Inject water or alcohol (in controlled amount) to hydrolyze the active Ti-alkyl site and stop polymerization
   - This is common in industry for precise control
4. Once stopped, allow reaction mixture to cool

**Step 5: Recover the polymer.**

1. Filter the slurry to separate solid polymer from solvent
2. Wash the polymer with fresh solvent (hexane) several times to remove residual TiCl₃ and AlEt₃
3. Dry in a vacuum oven at 80–120°C for 2–4 hours
4. The product is a fine powder of HDPE with narrow molecular weight distribution

**Product properties:** HDPE from Ziegler-Natta catalysts is:
- White, granular powder
- Higher density (0.94–0.96 g/cm³ vs. 0.92 for LDPE)
- Higher melting point (~130–138°C vs. ~105–115°C for LDPE)
- Stiffer and stronger, but less flexible
- Fewer branches; more crystalline

### Part 3: Polypropylene (PP) Using Ziegler-Natta with Stereocontrol

Polypropylene (PP) is made similarly to HDPE, but with an added complication: **stereochemistry**. Each unit of propylene (C₃H₆) has an asymmetric carbon, and the way these units link (head-to-tail orientation and the stereochemistry of the methyl side group) determines the polymer's properties.

Three stereoisomeric structures are possible:
1. **Isotactic PP:** All methyl groups on the same side of the polymer backbone; highly crystalline, strong
2. **Syndiotactic PP:** Methyl groups alternate sides; less common, lower melting point
3. **Atactic PP:** Random arrangement; amorphous, soft, like rubber

Industrial PP is almost always **isotactic** because it has the best combination of strength and processability.

**Step 1: Special catalyst for isotactic PP.**

Stereospecific Ziegler-Natta catalysts for isotactic PP use:

**Titanium source:** TiCl₄ or solid TiCl₃
**Aluminum alkyl:** AlEt₃ or AliBu₃
**Internal donor (crucial for stereocontrol):** A compound that modifies the catalyst surface to prefer isotactic insertion
- Diisobutyl phthalate (DIBP): most common
- Isobutyl benzoate
- Diethyl phthalate

**Why donors are needed:** The internal donor coordinates to the titanium center, creating a chiral (handed) binding pocket. This pocket preferentially accepts propylene molecules in the orientation that produces isotactic chains.

**Catalyst formulation for isotactic PP:**
- TiCl₄: 0.5–1 g
- AlEt₃: in 10–15× molar excess of Ti
- DIBP: 0.1–0.2 moles per mole of Ti
- Inert solvent: cyclohexane

**Preparation:**
1. Pre-complex TiCl₄ with DIBP (heat to 60°C for 30 min)
2. Cool to 0°C
3. Add AlEt₃ slowly while stirring
4. Age for 1–24 hours
5. Use for polymerization

**Step 2: Gas-phase polymerization of PP (common industrial route).**

1. Load the reactor with pre-activated catalyst (Ziegler-Natta with DIBP)
2. Heat to 70–75°C
3. Introduce propylene gas at 8–15 atm pressure
4. Stir/cycle propylene gas to provide continuous monomer contact
5. Monitor reactor temperature; cool to maintain 70–75°C

**Reaction kinetics:** Propylene polymerization is typically slower than ethylene (steric hindrance from the methyl group slows insertion). Typical reaction times: 2–8 hours.

**Step 3: Control isotacticity.**

The percentage of isotactic linkages depends on:
- **Temperature:** Lower (60–70°C) = higher isotacticity; higher (80–90°C) = more errors (less isotactic)
- **Catalyst selection:** Catalyst structure determines stereoselectivity
- **Internal donor type and ratio:** DIBP is industry standard; fine-tuning the ratio adjusts isotacticity

For commercial isotactic PP, typically 90–98% of the units are in the correct isotactic configuration. This produces a crystalline, strong material.

**Step 4: Remove unreacted monomer and recover polymer.**

1. After reaction completion (check for steady-state conditions: temperature and heat release stabilize)
2. Stop monomer supply
3. Vent residual propylene carefully
4. Extract the reactor and allow polymer to cool
5. The polymer is typically a granular solid that can be washed and dried

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Calculating LDPE Production Rate

You're running an LDPE polymerization at 2000 atm and 150°C in a 10-liter reactor. The ethylene concentration under these conditions is approximately 8 moles/liter. Your free radical initiator produces radicals at a rate corresponding to 10 kg of polymer per hour.

**Question:** How much LDPE can you produce in an 8-hour run?

**Answer:**
Production rate = 10 kg/hour × 8 hours = 80 kg of LDPE

**Practical note:** Not all ethylene will be converted; typically 20–40% conversion is achieved in one pass. Unreacted ethylene is recycled.

### Example 2: Ziegler-Natta Catalyst Amount for HDPE

You're setting up a 50-liter HDPE reactor and want a catalyst loading (Ti) of 5 mmol/liter of reactor volume.

**How much TiCl₃ (MW = 154 g/mol) do you need?**

Total Ti needed = 5 mmol/L × 50 L = 250 mmol
Mass of TiCl₃ = 250 mmol × 0.154 g/mmol = 38.5 g

**Cocatalyst (AlEt₃):** Use 15× molar excess
= 250 mmol × 15 = 3750 mmol AlEt₃
≈ 430 g of AlEt₃ (MW = 114 g/mol)

**Step 3: Verify Isotacticity of PP by Solubility Test**

You've synthesized polypropylene with your Ziegler-Natta catalyst with DIBP. To verify that it's isotactic (and not atactic), perform a quick solubility test:

1. Dissolve 1 g of PP in boiling xylene (120°C)
2. Cool to 25°C
3. High-isotacticity PP precipitates out (insoluble at room temperature)
4. Low-isotacticity or atactic PP remains dissolved (more soluble in cold xylene)

If your PP precipitates, it's highly isotactic. If it stays dissolved, the catalyst did not effectively control stereochemistry.

## Verification Criteria

Successful LDPE, HDPE, or PP synthesis is confirmed by:

1. **Physical appearance:** 
   - LDPE: soft, waxy, translucent or white
   - HDPE: rigid granules, white, opaque
   - Isotactic PP: white, granular, relatively rigid

2. **Melting point:** Use a simple melting point apparatus or hot plate
   - LDPE: 105–115°C
   - HDPE: 130–138°C
   - Isotactic PP: 160–175°C

3. **Solubility test:** At room temperature, small pieces of PE or PP are insoluble in water, insoluble in ethanol, slightly soluble in hot xylene or hot toluene.

4. **Mechanical properties:** The polymer should be flexible or rigid (not brittle), depending on type.

5. **Density measurement:** Weigh a known volume of water, then weigh the displaced water when polymer is submerged
   - LDPE: 0.92 g/cm³
   - HDPE: 0.94–0.96 g/cm³
   - PP: 0.90–0.91 g/cm³

## Common Mistakes

1. **Moisture contamination in Ziegler-Natta catalysts:** Even trace water destroys the catalyst activity. **Fix:** Use only rigorously dried and degassed solvents and equipment; keep all operations under dry nitrogen or argon.

2. **Excessive pressure in LDPE:** Pressure over 3000 atm risks explosion. **Fix:** Verify relief valve setting before operation; never exceed 90% of maximum reactor rating.

3. **Insufficient cooling in HDPE/PP:** Exothermic reaction can runaway, degrading the polymer. **Fix:** Pre-chill cooling water; ensure adequate flow rate; monitor temperature continuously.

4. **Wrong temperature for PP isotacticity:** Too high = atactic PP (useless); too low = slow reaction. **Fix:** Run at 70–75°C for industrial isotactic PP.

5. **Atactic PP when expecting isotactic:** Usually caused by wrong catalyst, missing internal donor, or overheated reaction. **Fix:** Use stereospecific catalyst with DIBP; verify catalyst preparation; control temperature tightly.

6. **Incomplete catalyst deactivation:** Residual catalyst continues to polymerize after removal, or damages the polymer during handling. **Fix:** Wash polymer thoroughly with solvent and water after recovery.
