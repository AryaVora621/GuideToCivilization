## Overview

Bakelite is the first fully synthetic plastic, invented by Leo Baekeland in 1907. It represents a turning point in material science: for the first time, humans created a material that had no natural equivalent and that could be mass-produced.

What makes Bakelite distinctive is that it is a **thermoset** plastic. Unlike thermoplastics (PE, PP, PET), which can be melted and re-molded repeatedly, Bakelite undergoes an irreversible chemical change during curing. Once cured, it cannot be re-melted or re-molded. This permanent cross-linking gives Bakelite exceptional heat resistance, hardness, and dimensional stability—properties that make it valuable for applications like electrical insulators, pot handles, circuit boards, and decorative items.

Bakelite is made by condensing phenol (a simple aromatic compound) with formaldehyde (a volatile organic compound). The reaction proceeds in two stages: first, creating a liquid or semi-solid resin; second, curing the resin with heat and pressure to form a rigid, cross-linked solid.

Today, Bakelite has been largely replaced by more modern thermosets (epoxies, polyesters) and by thermoplastics for most applications. However, understanding Bakelite manufacturing is important because it demonstrates thermoset chemistry and remains relevant for specialty applications.

## Materials & Tools

### For Bakelite Production

**Raw materials:**
- Phenol (C₆H₅OH): aromatic compound with hydroxyl group (highly toxic; requires safe handling)
- Formaldehyde (CH₂O): volatile aldehyde, typically supplied as formalin (aqueous solution, 37–40% formaldehyde)
- Catalyst: ammonia (NH₃) or sodium hydroxide (NaOH) for resin stage; acid catalyst (phosphoric acid, oxalic acid) for curing
- Filler (optional): wood flour, asbestos fiber, or mica (improves mechanical properties and reduces cost)

**Equipment:**
- Reactor: glass-lined or stainless steel, with reflux condenser (to capture formaldehyde vapor)
- Temperature control: heating to 80–150°C with precise control
- Condenser: to condense and recycle formaldehyde vapor
- Molds: steel or aluminum, machined to desired shape
- Press: hydraulic or steam press, capable of 5–50 atm pressure and 150–180°C temperature
- Vacuum pump: optional, to remove air from molds before curing

### Equipment for Experimental Work

- Heat source (oil bath, electric heater)
- Glassware: round-bottom flask, condenser, thermometer
- Mixing containers and stirrers
- Mold (simple steel or aluminum block, 50 mm x 50 mm x 10 mm)
- Oven: capable of 150–180°C
- Clamp: to press mold together

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Two-Stage Bakelite Process

Bakelite production has two distinct stages: **resin formation** (liquid or semi-solid resin) and **curing** (solid, hard plastic).

**Stage 1: Resin Formation (Resole or Novolak Process)**

There are two main routes: **resole** (base-catalyzed) and **novolak** (acid-catalyzed). Both start with phenol and formaldehyde but differ in catalyst and reaction conditions.

**Resole process (more common for Bakelite):**

**Step 1: Load the reactor.**

1. In a glass-lined reactor or round-bottom flask, combine:
   - Phenol: 100 grams (1.06 mol)
   - Formaldehyde solution (37–40%): 125 mL (containing ~45–50 grams of HCHO, ~1.5 mol)
   - Water: 50 mL (solvent)
2. The ratio of formaldehyde to phenol is typically 1.3–1.5:1 (excess formaldehyde favors the resole process)
3. Add ammonia catalyst: ~0.5 mL of concentrated ammonia solution (or sodium hydroxide: ~1 gram)

**Safety note:** Formaldehyde vapor is hazardous. Work in a fume hood with good ventilation. Phenol is corrosive; handle with care.

**Step 2: Heat and monitor the reaction.**

1. Attach a reflux condenser to the reactor
2. Heat slowly to 60°C
3. The mixture becomes warm; a strong formaldehyde smell indicates the reaction is starting
4. Exothermic reaction: the mixture heats up on its own; moderate heating to keep temperature at 60–80°C
5. Water and excess formaldehyde vapor condense in the reflux condenser and return to the reactor

**Reaction chemistry:**
```
Phenol (C₆H₅OH) + Formaldehyde (CH₂O) → Hydroxymethylphenol (C₆H₄(OH)CH₂OH) + water
```

This reaction creates methylol compounds, which then condense to form larger molecules (oligomers and polymers).

**Step 3: Monitor viscosity increase.**

1. As the reaction proceeds (30 minutes to 2 hours), the mixture becomes increasingly viscous
2. At 60°C: mixture is thin and free-flowing
3. At 90 minutes (still at 60–80°C): mixture thickens to a syrup-like consistency
4. At 2 hours: mixture is very thick, nearly a gel

**Stopping the reaction:**
- Target viscosity: the mixture should be thick enough to pour but still liquid (not fully gelled)
- Stop the reaction by cooling to room temperature (or 60°C) and removing heat
- Alternatively, add a small amount of acid (acetic acid, ~1 mL) to halt the reaction

**Product:** A liquid to semi-solid resin, amber to reddish-brown in color. This is the **resole resin**—an intermediate that is used to prepare molding compounds.

**Step 4: Cool and store the resin.**

1. Cool the reactor to room temperature (slow cooling helps prevent cracking)
2. The resin solidifies as it cools (becoming waxy or brittle, depending on extent of reaction)
3. Can be stored at room temperature; reheating to 60–100°C makes it pourable again (viscosity increases over time due to continued slow reaction)

### Part 2: Molding and Curing (Bakelization)

The resole resin from Part 1 is now mixed with fillers (optional) and compressed in a mold while heat and pressure are applied. This "bakelization" step causes final cross-linking.

**Step 1: Prepare the molding compound.**

**If making unfilled Bakelite (simple, but brittle):**
1. Use the resole resin directly from Part 1
2. Reheat to 80–100°C to make it pourable

**If making filled Bakelite (stronger, more practical):**
1. Resole resin (from Part 1): 100 parts
2. Filler: 50–150 parts (wood flour, mica powder, or asbestos fiber)
3. Heat the resin to 80–100°C
4. Mix the filler into the hot resin thoroughly (5–10 minutes of stirring)
5. Allow the mixture to cool; it becomes a crumbly powder or chunk

This is the **molding compound**—ready to be placed in a mold.

**Step 2: Prepare the mold.**

1. Clean the mold thoroughly (any dirt will mar the finished product)
2. Coat the mold with a release agent (e.g., fine powder or thin oil) to prevent sticking
3. Close the mold tightly with a clamp or press

**Step 3: Load and heat the mold.**

1. Place molding compound into the mold (fill to slightly over-flowing to ensure no voids)
2. Insert the mold into a heated press (or oven with external clamp)
3. Set temperature to 150–180°C (typically 160–170°C is optimal)
4. Increase temperature gradually (10–20°C per minute) to avoid thermal shock and voids

**Step 4: Apply pressure.**

Once at temperature, apply pressure:
1. Start with moderate pressure (5–10 atm) to allow gases to escape
2. Increase to full pressure (20–50 atm) after 1–2 minutes
3. Hold pressure steady throughout the cure

**Why pressure?**
- Compacts the material (removes voids and air pockets)
- Brings molding compound into close contact with mold walls
- Ensures uniform density in the final product

**Step 5: Monitor curing time.**

Curing time depends on thickness and desired final properties:

- **Thin parts (5 mm):** 3–5 minutes at 170°C
- **Medium parts (10 mm):** 5–15 minutes at 170°C
- **Thick parts (20 mm):** 15–60 minutes at 170°C

**What happens during curing:**
- Resole resin undergoes final cross-linking (creating a 3D network)
- Formaldehyde and water are driven off as vapor (must escape; mold vents allow this)
- Material transitions from plastic/pourable state to hard, brittle, solid state
- The product shrinks slightly (2–5% linear shrinkage typical)

**Monitoring progress:**
- Pressure and temperature are the primary indicators
- Visual: watch for vapor escaping (early curing), then vapor flow slows (completion)
- Torque on press: if required pressure increases, material is hardening (good sign)

**Step 6: Cool and de-mold.**

1. Once curing is complete (time has elapsed), reduce pressure gradually
2. Allow the mold to cool to room temperature (can take 15–30 minutes)
3. Carefully remove the molded part from the mold
4. Post-cure (optional): place the part in an oven at 100–150°C for 4–8 hours to improve final properties

**Final product:** Bakelite part is rigid, hard, dark amber to black, dimensionally stable, and cannot be re-melted.

### Part 3: Thermosetting Chemistry (Why Bakelite Cannot Be Re-Melted)

Understanding the permanent nature of Bakelite requires understanding the chemistry of thermosets.

**Thermoplastic vs. Thermoset:**

**Thermoplastic (PE, PP, Nylon):**
- Linear or weakly branched polymer chains
- No permanent bonds between chains (only van der Waals forces)
- When heated: chains can slide past each other → material softens and flows
- Can be re-melted and re-molded

**Thermoset (Bakelite, Epoxy):**
- Three-dimensional network: chains are covalently cross-linked
- Permanent covalent bonds hold chains together
- When heated: cross-links resist chain movement → material does not soften
- Cannot be re-melted; will degrade (break down) at very high temperature

**Bakelite cross-linking chemistry:**

Initial resole resin has reactive methylol groups:
```
—O—CH₂—OH (methylol group)
```

During curing, these groups condense with each other and with phenolic nuclei:
```
—O—CH₂—OH + HOH—CH₂—O— → —O—CH₂—O— + H₂O (ether linkage)
```

This creates ether bonds (—CH₂—O—) linking different phenolic units. More importantly, further cross-linking creates methylene bridges:
```
—O—CH₂—O— + more methylol → three-dimensional network
```

The final product is a rigid, three-dimensional lattice where phenolic units are covalently bonded to each other via carbon and oxygen bridges. Heating cannot break these covalent bonds (would require over 300°C, at which point the material degrades rather than melts).

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Phenol-Formaldehyde Ratio Calculation

You want to synthesize resole resin starting with 500 grams of phenol. The formaldehyde-to-phenol molar ratio for resole is 1.4:1.

**Phenol (C₆H₅OH):** MW = 94 g/mol
- Moles = 500 g / 94 g/mol = 5.32 mol

**Formaldehyde needed:** 5.32 mol × 1.4 = 7.45 mol

**Formaldehyde solution (37% HCHO):** MW of HCHO = 30 g/mol
- Mass of pure HCHO = 7.45 mol × 30 g/mol = 223.5 g
- Mass of 37% solution = 223.5 g / 0.37 = 604 g (about 600 mL)

**Result:** Start with 500 g phenol and 600 mL of 37% formaldehyde solution.

### Example 2: Molding Compound Preparation

You've made 200 grams of resole resin and want to prepare a molding compound with 30% filler (by weight).

**Total final weight:** 200 g / 0.70 = 286 g (since resin is 70% of total)

**Filler needed:** 286 g × 0.30 = 86 g of wood flour

**Preparation:**
1. Heat resole resin to 90°C
2. Mix in 86 g of wood flour
3. Cool to room temperature
4. Crush into smaller particles (molding powder)

**Result:** 286 g of molding compound ready for the press.

### Example 3: Estimating Curing Time for Thick Parts

You're molding a Bakelite gear 25 mm thick at 165°C. Using standard cure charts for filled Bakelite:

| Thickness | Time at 165°C |
|-----------|---------------|
| 5 mm | 4 min |
| 10 mm | 8 min |
| 20 mm | 20 min |
| 25 mm | ~25–30 min (interpolated) |

**Answer:** Expect 25–30 minutes at 165°C for a 25 mm thick part.

**Note:** If temperature increases to 170°C, time decreases by roughly 20–30%. At 170°C, the same part would cure in about 18–22 minutes.

## Verification Criteria

Successfully produced Bakelite is confirmed by:

1. **Appearance:** Dark amber to black, glossy or satin finish (depending on mold), no cracks or voids
2. **Hardness:** Very hard; cannot be dented with fingernail
3. **Heat resistance:** Does not soften at 100–120°C (unlike thermoplastics)
4. **Chemical resistance:** Resists dilute acids and bases; insoluble in organic solvents
5. **Dimensional stability:** No significant shrinkage after cooling; holds its shape
6. **Mechanical properties:** Brittle but strong in compression; tensile strength 50–60 MPa

## Common Mistakes

1. **Insufficient cure time:** Under-cured Bakelite remains somewhat plastic and flexible. **Fix:** Monitor time carefully; increase temperature or pressure if needed to ensure complete cure.

2. **Excessive cure time:** Over-curing causes brittleness and cracking. **Fix:** Follow cure time recommendations; reduce temperature if parts are over-curing.

3. **Formaldehyde vapor loss:** Formaldehyde can escape if the reactor is not well-sealed or refluxed. **Fix:** Use a good condenser; ensure tight seals on reactor.

4. **Air bubbles in mold:** Incomplete filling or trapped air creates voids. **Fix:** Fill mold generously; vent mold to allow air to escape; use vacuum if available.

5. **Temperature overshoot:** Heating too fast causes thermal cracking. **Fix:** Heat gradually (10–20°C per minute); monitor temperature continuously.

6. **Mold sticking:** Finished part cannot be removed. **Fix:** Use adequate release agent; ensure mold is clean; open mold carefully after cooling.
