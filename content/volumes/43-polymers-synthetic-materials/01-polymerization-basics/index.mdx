## Overview

A polymer is a long-chain molecule built from thousands of smaller, repeating units called monomers. The word "polymer" comes from Greek: poly (many) + meros (parts). In a typical polymer chain, monomers link together in sequence, creating molecules with molecular weights from thousands to millions of times that of a single monomer.

Understanding polymerization—the process of linking monomers into polymers—is the foundation for producing synthetic materials: plastics, fibers, rubbers, and adhesives. Without polymerization, there would be no synthetic textiles, no plastic containers, no modern life as we know it.

This chapter teaches the two fundamental polymerization mechanisms that account for the vast majority of industrial polymers:

1. **Addition polymerization:** Monomers with carbon-carbon double bonds (C=C) open up and link directly to each other, with no byproducts.
2. **Condensation polymerization:** Monomers with two reactive functional groups link together by releasing a small molecule (water, methanol, or hydrogen chloride) as a byproduct.

Both processes create the same result: long chains of carbon atoms with various side groups. The difference is in mechanism, reaction conditions, and how the byproducts (or lack thereof) affect the reaction.

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Addition Polymerization

Addition polymerization is the linking of monomers that contain carbon-carbon double bonds (C=C). When initiated, the double bond opens, and the monomer adds directly to the growing polymer chain, with no loss of atoms.

**Step 1: Choose a monomer.**

Common monomers for addition polymerization:
- **Ethylene (C₂H₄):** The simplest monomer; produces polyethylene (PE), the most common plastic
- **Propylene (C₃H₆):** Produces polypropylene (PP), stronger than PE
- **Vinyl chloride (C₂H₃Cl):** Produces polyvinyl chloride (PVC), used for pipes and vinyl
- **Styrene (C₆H₅CH=CH₂):** Produces polystyrene, used for foam and rigid plastic

Each monomer has different properties and reaction conditions. For this example, we'll focus on polyethylene from ethylene gas.

**Critical property of monomers:** The C=C double bond is the reactive site. Monomers with different substituents (groups attached to the carbons) on the double bond will polymerize at different rates and produce polymers with different properties.

**Step 2: Select an initiator.**

An initiator is a molecule that starts the polymerization chain reaction. Common initiators:
- **Benzoyl peroxide:** Thermal decomposition at temperatures between 50–100°C produces free radicals
- **Azobisisobutyronitrile (AIBN):** Thermal initiator effective at 60–80°C
- **Potassium persulfate:** Water-soluble initiator for aqueous polymerizations
- **Diisopropyl peroxydicarbonate:** More reactive, useful for lower temperatures

**How initiators work:** They decompose under heat (or sometimes light) to create free radicals—atoms or molecules with unpaired electrons. Free radicals are highly reactive and attack the C=C double bond, opening it and creating a reactive site. The growing polymer chain adds monomer after monomer, always keeping a reactive free radical at the tip.

**Typical initiator concentration:** 0.1–2% by weight of the monomer, depending on desired chain length and reaction rate. More initiator = faster reaction = shorter average chain length (more chain initiation events).

**Step 3: Prepare the reactor.**

1. Charge the reactor with monomer and initiator
2. For gaseous monomers (like ethylene), maintain pressure (20–100 atm typical for industrial polyethylene)
3. For liquid monomers, may dissolve in solvent or proceed in bulk (monomer only, no solvent)
4. Ensure the reactor is sealed and pressure-safe
5. Purge with inert gas (nitrogen) to remove dissolved oxygen, which can inhibit polymerization by reacting with free radicals

**Step 4: Control temperature.**

Addition polymerization is **exothermic**—it releases heat. The reaction rate and final polymer properties depend critically on temperature.

- **Low temperature (50–60°C):** Slow reaction, tight control, high-quality polymer but low productivity
- **Moderate temperature (70–90°C):** Balanced reaction rate and quality
- **High temperature (100–150°C):** Fast reaction, but may cause side reactions (branching, cross-linking, degradation)

Temperature control strategy:
1. Heat the reactor to target temperature
2. Add initiator to start polymerization
3. Monitor temperature continuously; use cooling jacket if heat generation exceeds cooling capacity
4. Keep temperature within a narrow band (±2–5°C) for consistent results

**Critical point:** If the reaction gets too hot, it can run away—accelerating uncontrollably and potentially rupturing the reactor. Always have a plan to cool or reduce temperature.

**Step 5: Monitor polymerization.**

As polymerization proceeds:
- Viscosity of the mixture increases (the polymer molecules are very long and create resistance to flow)
- Temperature rises (from heat of reaction) even with temperature control
- The monomer is progressively consumed
- In bulk polymerization, the mixture may change color or clarity

Typical reaction times: 4–12 hours for slow polymerization, 1–2 hours for faster processes.

**Conversion monitoring:** The percentage of monomer converted to polymer can be estimated by:
- Measuring viscosity (higher viscosity = more polymer)
- Direct weighing of the polymer (if it separates)
- Gas chromatography or other analytical methods (if available)

**Step 6: Stop the polymerization.**

When the desired conversion is reached:
1. Cool the reactor (reduce temperature to below 50°C)
2. Add an inhibitor or chain-transfer agent to stop free radical generation
   - Hydroquinone, phenol, or amine-based inhibitors work
   - These react with free radicals, deactivating them
3. Once cooled, the polymer is stable and will not continue polymerizing

**Note on byproducts:** Addition polymerization produces **no chemical byproducts**—only the polymer and unreacted monomer (if conversion is incomplete). This makes it simpler than condensation polymerization.

### Part 2: Condensation Polymerization

Condensation polymerization links monomers that have **two** reactive functional groups each. When two monomers react, they release a small molecule (usually water, H₂O) and form a new covalent bond.

**Step 1: Choose monomers with two functional groups.**

For condensation polymerization, you need monomers with reactive groups at both ends. Common examples:

**Polyester (from adipic acid and ethylene glycol):**
- Adipic acid (HOOC-(CH₂)₄-COOH): two carboxylic acid groups (-COOH)
- Ethylene glycol (HO-CH₂-CH₂-OH): two hydroxyl groups (-OH)
- Reaction: carboxylic acid + hydroxyl → ester bond + water

**Polyamide/Nylon (from adipic acid and hexamethylenediamine):**
- Adipic acid (HOOC-(CH₂)₄-COOH): two carboxylic acid groups
- Hexamethylenediamine (H₂N-(CH₂)₆-NH₂): two amine groups (-NH₂)
- Reaction: carboxylic acid + amine → amide bond + water

**Polyurethane (from diisocyanate and diol):**
- Diisocyanate (OCN-R-NCO): two isocyanate groups (-NCO)
- Diol (HO-R'-OH): two hydroxyl groups
- Reaction: isocyanate + hydroxyl → urethane bond + **no water** (this is the exception)

The key principle: **for every 1000 monomer units you add, you release about 1000 water molecules** (approximately one per monomer added). This water must be removed or the reaction stalls due to equilibrium effects.

**Step 2: Prepare the reactor.**

1. Load both monomers into the reactor in stoichiometric amounts (or slight excess of one if desired)
2. Optionally add a solvent (toluene, xylene) to reduce viscosity and improve mixing
3. Add a catalyst to accelerate the ester or amide bond formation
   - For polyesters: tin(II) chloride (SnCl₂), antimony trioxide (Sb₂O₃), or titanium(IV) isopropoxide
   - For polyamides: phosphoric acid or aliphatic amines
4. Seal the reactor

**Step 3: Heat and apply vacuum to remove water.**

1. Raise temperature gradually to 150–280°C (depending on monomer type and desired product properties)
2. Once monomers melt and mix, apply vacuum (0.1–1 mmHg or torr)
3. As water forms, vacuum pulls it out of the system, driving the equilibrium toward polymer formation
4. The reaction can proceed as long as heat and vacuum are applied

**Why vacuum is critical:** Condensation polymerization is reversible:
```
Monomer₁ + Monomer₂ ⇌ Monomer₁-Monomer₂ + H₂O
```

If water accumulates, the reaction reverses (hydrolysis), and you don't build long chains. Vacuum removes water, pushing the equilibrium to the right (toward polymer).

**Step 4: Monitor viscosity.**

As polymer chains grow, the mixture becomes increasingly viscous. Viscosity is your best real-time indicator of polymerization progress:
- At startup: monomer flows easily (low viscosity)
- After 2–4 hours: mixture resembles honey (moderate viscosity)
- After 6–10 hours: mixture moves like thick paste (high viscosity)
- Final product: solid or very thick liquid

Mechanical stirring becomes harder as viscosity increases. Some reactors use special high-torque stirrers or switch to molten state operations at high temperature.

**Step 5: Control molecular weight.**

The final molecular weight (average number of monomer units per chain) depends on:
- **Reaction time:** Longer reactions = longer chains (generally)
- **Temperature:** Higher temperature = faster reaction (but may cause side reactions)
- **Vacuum:** Lower pressure = more water removal = longer chains possible
- **Monomer ratio:** Exact 1:1 ratio produces very long chains; imbalance limits chain length
- **Additives:** Chain-transfer agents (like acetic acid in polyesters) deliberately limit chain length to control viscosity

**Targeting specific molecular weight:** If you want a polymer with a specific average chain length:
1. Add slightly more of one monomer (e.g., 1% excess adipic acid)
2. Add a chain-transfer agent in calculated amount
3. Stop the reaction at the right viscosity (which correlates to molecular weight)

**Step 6: Stop the reaction and cool.**

1. Turn off heat and vacuum
2. Cool slowly to room temperature (rapid cooling can cause thermal stress and cracking)
3. If the product is a solid, extrude or break it into pellets
4. If it's still molten, pour into molds to solidify

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Calculating Average Molecular Weight (Addition Polymerization)

You polymerize ethylene (C₂H₄, MW = 28 g/mol) and measure the final polymer mass as 500 g. The viscosity indicates approximately 20,000 repeat units in the average chain.

Average molecular weight = (MW of monomer) × (number of repeat units)
= 28 × 20,000 = 560,000 g/mol

This is typical for polyethylene used in plastic films. Polyethylene used in bottles or injection-molded parts is typically 250,000–500,000 g/mol.

### Example 2: Stoichiometry in Condensation Polymerization

You're synthesizing polyester from 1000 kg of adipic acid (MW = 146 g/mol) and ethylene glycol (MW = 62 g/mol). How much of each should you add for stoichiometric balance?

For every monomer of adipic acid, you need one monomer of ethylene glycol:
- Moles of adipic acid = 1000 kg / 0.146 kg/mol = 6,849 mol
- Ethylene glycol needed = 6,849 mol × 0.062 kg/mol = 425 kg

Check stoichiometry: both reactants will be consumed simultaneously, producing long chains with no unreacted monomer left behind.

### Example 3: Impact of Initiator Concentration

You're comparing two polyethylene synthesis runs:

**Run A:** 1% benzoyl peroxide initiator
- Result: faster reaction (3 hours), viscosity reaches target quickly
- Average chain length: 5,000 repeat units
- Molecular weight: 140,000 g/mol

**Run B:** 0.1% benzoyl peroxide initiator
- Result: slower reaction (8 hours), steady viscosity increase
- Average chain length: 25,000 repeat units
- Molecular weight: 700,000 g/mol

Higher initiator concentration = more initiation events = shorter average chains (but higher total number of chains). For flexible films, shorter chains (Run A) work fine. For tough containers, longer chains (Run B) are better.

## Verification Criteria

A successful polymerization is confirmed by:

1. **Visual inspection:** The product is solid or very viscous (not liquid like the starting monomer).
2. **Solubility test:** Attempt to dissolve a small amount of polymer in an appropriate solvent (toluene for PE, DMSO for nylon). If it's insoluble or partially soluble, polymerization occurred.
3. **Melting point test:** Heat the polymer; it should soften at a characteristic temperature (PE melts at 120–130°C, PET at 250–260°C).
4. **Mechanical properties:** The polymer should be strong and flexible, not brittle or sticky.
5. **Molecular weight confirmation:** Viscosity should be high (difficult to stir) and correlate with expected chain length.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Insufficient vacuum in condensation polymerization:** Water accumulates, reaction stalls, and you get a low-molecular-weight oligomer (short chains). **Fix:** Ensure vacuum pump is working and water trap is clear.

2. **Wrong initiator concentration:** Too high = short chains, cloudy polymer; too low = slow reaction, incomplete polymerization. **Fix:** Follow recipe; adjust in 0.1–0.2% increments.

3. **Temperature runaway in addition polymerization:** Reaction accelerates uncontrollably, releasing so much heat that cooling cannot keep up. **Fix:** Add initiator slowly, not all at once; use a water bath for small batches; ensure adequate cooling.

4. **Monomer imbalance in condensation polymerization:** If you use 60% adipic acid and 40% hexamethylenediamine, the excess adipic acid will limit chain growth. **Fix:** Measure carefully; stoichiometric balance is critical.

5. **Contamination with water or oxygen:** These quench polymerization (for free radical) or slow it (for condensation). **Fix:** Dry monomers and solvents; purge with dry nitrogen before starting.

6. **Overheating:** Produces branched, cross-linked, or degraded polymer (weaker, darker color). **Fix:** Monitor temperature; use moderate temperatures unless specifically targeting branching.
