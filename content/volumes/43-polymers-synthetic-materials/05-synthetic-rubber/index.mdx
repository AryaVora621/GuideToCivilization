## Overview

Rubber is one of the most important elastomeric materials. Elastomers are polymers with exceptional elastic properties: they can stretch to many times their original length under load and return to their original shape when the load is removed. Rubber is used in tires (the single largest application), seals, gaskets, vibration dampers, and countless other applications.

Natural rubber comes from rubber trees (Hevea brasiliensis) and is made of polyisoprene. But rubber was also synthesized early in the 20th century, initially out of necessity during wars when natural rubber supplies were cut off. Today, synthetic rubbers are produced by polymerization of specific monomers chosen to have elastic properties. The most common synthetic rubber is SBR (styrene-butadiene rubber), which accounts for over 50% of synthetic rubber production and is the standard material for most automotive tires.

The key to making rubber useful is vulcanization—a chemical process where cross-linking (usually with sulfur) bonds polymer chains together. Vulcanization transforms raw rubber (tacky, weak, temperature-sensitive) into modern rubber (strong, elastic, and thermally stable). Understanding synthetic rubber synthesis and vulcanization is essential for producing reliable elastomeric products.

## Materials & Tools

### For Synthetic Rubber Synthesis (SBR Example)

**Raw materials:**
- Butadiene (CH₂=CH-CH=CH₂): diene monomer (C₄H₆)
- Styrene (C₆H₅-CH=CH₂): vinyl monomer (C₈H₈)
- Emulsifier (e.g., sodium dodecyl sulfate): to form latex
- Initiator (e.g., potassium persulfate or cumene hydroperoxide): free radical source
- Solvent: water (emulsion polymerization)
- Chain transfer agent: to control molecular weight

**Equipment:**
- Reactor: 10–100 liter capacity, jacketed for temperature control
- Stirrer: for emulsion mixing
- Temperature control: cooling for exothermic reaction
- Condenser: to prevent monomer loss
- Agitator for thorough mixing

### For Vulcanization

**Raw materials:**
- Raw rubber (synthetic or natural): the base material
- Sulfur (S): cross-linking agent, typically 2–3 phr (parts per hundred rubber)
- Accelerators: zinc diethyldithiocarbamate (ZDEC), mercaptobenzothiazole (MBT), or others (to speed vulcanization)
- Activators: zinc oxide (ZnO), stearic acid (to enhance accelerator efficiency)
- Fillers: carbon black (for strength and color), silica (for reinforcement)
- Anti-oxidants: to prevent degradation during and after vulcanization

**Equipment:**
- Mixing mill: to blend rubber, sulfur, and additives
- Vulcanizing press or autoclave: applies heat and pressure
- Temperature control: accurate to ±2°C for consistent vulcanization
- Molds: shapes the rubber during vulcanization

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Synthetic Rubber (SBR) Synthesis via Emulsion Polymerization

SBR is made by polymerizing a mixture of butadiene and styrene monomers in an emulsion (dispersed in water).

**Step 1: Prepare the reactor and emulsion.**

1. Load the reactor with:
   - Water (deionized): 300 mL
   - Emulsifier (sodium lauryl sulfate): 2–3 grams (dissolved in the water)
   - Potassium persulfate (initiator): 0.5 grams (dissolved in water)
2. Heat the reactor to 50°C
3. Stir gently to ensure emulsifier is fully dissolved; the solution should be clear

**Step 2: Add monomers.**

1. Prepare a monomer mixture:
   - Butadiene: 30 grams (C₄H₆, MW = 54, so ~0.56 mol)
   - Styrene: 20 grams (C₈H₈, MW = 104, so ~0.19 mol)
   - Ratio: 60% butadiene, 40% styrene (typical for SBR)
2. Add the monomer mixture to the reactor slowly (over 30 minutes)
3. As monomers are added, they disperse in the water as tiny droplets, stabilized by the emulsifier

**Why emulsion polymerization?**
- Monomers are insoluble in water; emulsifier creates a stable dispersion
- Heat is removed more easily (water is a good heat conductor)
- Reaction rate is faster than bulk polymerization
- Product is a latex (colloidal suspension of polymer in water), easy to handle

**Step 3: Monitor polymerization.**

1. Temperature: maintain at 50–60°C (exothermic reaction)
   - Cooling jacket removes heat from the reaction
   - Too hot (over 70°C): side reactions, unwanted cross-linking
   - Too cold (under 40°C): slow reaction
2. Time: reaction typically takes 8–24 hours (longer than other polymers due to lower polymerization rate with butadiene)
3. Progress: the emulsion will become increasingly opaque (white) and more viscous as polymer particles form and grow

**Monitoring viscosity:**
- Early stage: emulsion flows easily (low viscosity)
- Middle stage: emulsion thickens (higher viscosity)
- Final stage: very thick, nearly solid (very high viscosity)

**Step 4: Stop polymerization.**

1. Add a small amount of inhibitor (e.g., hydroquinone, ~0.1 gram) to stop free radical generation
2. Cool the reactor to room temperature
3. The product is a latex: a colloidal suspension of SBR polymer (40–50% solids by weight) in water

**Step 5: Recover the polymer.**

Method 1 (Direct coagulation):
1. Add a coagulating salt solution (e.g., sodium chloride or sulfuric acid) to the latex
2. Polymer particles aggregate and settle
3. Drain the water; wash the polymer with water and ethanol
4. Dry at 80°C in a vacuum oven for 4–6 hours

Method 2 (Freeze coagulation):
1. Cool the latex to well below 0°C (freeze)
2. Polymer particles coagulate as ice crystals disrupt the emulsion
3. Thaw and drain water; wash and dry as above

**Final product:** White to off-white granular or powdered SBR rubber.

### Part 2: Vulcanization Process

Vulcanization is the transformation of raw rubber into finished rubber by creating cross-links between polymer chains. Sulfur is the most common cross-linking agent; accelerators speed the process.

**Step 1: Prepare rubber compound (mixing).**

1. Raw rubber (SBR or natural latex): 100 phr (parts per hundred rubber, by weight)
2. Sulfur: 2.5 phr
3. Zinc oxide: 5 phr (activator)
4. Stearic acid: 2 phr (activator; enhances accelerator efficiency)
5. Mercaptobenzothiazole (MBT) accelerator: 1.5 phr (speeds cross-linking)
6. Carbon black: 30–50 phr (reinforcement filler; increases strength and hardness)
7. Anti-oxidant (e.g., 6PPD): 2 phr (prevents oxidative degradation)

**Mixing procedure:**
1. Feed raw rubber into a heated mixing mill (open-frame rollers that knead and shear the material)
2. Add carbon black and mix for 3–5 minutes (thorough distribution)
3. Add remaining ingredients (sulfur, zinc oxide, accelerator, etc.)
4. Mix for another 2–3 minutes until all ingredients are uniformly distributed

**Appearance after mixing:** The rubber transitions from sticky/tacky to more cohesive; color darkens (due to carbon black).

**Step 2: Mold the rubber (before vulcanization).**

1. Remove mixed rubber from the mill
2. Place into a mold (the desired shape: tire, gasket, pad, etc.)
3. Close the mold tightly
4. Transfer to a vulcanizing press or autoclave

**Alternative:** Alternatively, the rubber compound can be extruded into sheets or strands and then pressed into shape in the vulcanizing mold.

**Step 3: Apply heat and pressure.**

1. Insert the closed mold into a heated press or autoclave
2. Raise temperature gradually to 150–160°C (for most SBR vulcanization)
   - Different rubber types and accelerators require different temperatures
   - Natural rubber typically vulcanizes at 140–160°C
   - Specialty rubbers may require 170–200°C
3. Apply moderate pressure (10–50 atm, depending on mold and product design) to:
   - Ensure good contact between rubber and mold surfaces
   - Reduce voids and air pockets in the final product

**Step 4: Monitor vulcanization (curing time).**

Vulcanization is a time-dependent chemical reaction. The optimal cure time depends on:
- Temperature: higher temperature = faster cure
- Accelerator type and amount: more accelerator = faster cure
- Rubber type: natural vs. synthetic
- Thickness of the part: thicker parts need longer time for heat to penetrate

**Typical curing times:**
- Thin parts (1–2 mm): 5–15 minutes at 150°C
- Medium parts (5–10 mm): 15–45 minutes at 150°C
- Thick parts (20+ mm): 45–120 minutes or longer

**Monitoring:**
- Use a rheometer (if available): measures the torque required to turn the rubber; as vulcanization progresses, torque increases (rubber stiffens)
- Visual inspection: cured rubber should be firm and elastic, not soft (under-cured) or hard/brittle (over-cured)

**Step 5: Cool and de-mold.**

1. Turn off heat; allow the mold to cool to room temperature (takes 15–60 minutes depending on size)
2. Open the mold
3. Remove the cured product
4. Post-cure (optional): heat the product at 70–100°C for 4–8 hours to further stabilize and improve properties

**Final product:** Cured rubber with excellent elastic properties, typically:
- Tensile strength: 10–30 MPa (megapascals)
- Elongation at break: 300–600%
- Shore A hardness: 50–70 (on a hardness scale)

### Part 3: Vulcanization Chemistry

Understanding the chemistry of vulcanization is important for controlling the process.

**Step 1: Sulfur cross-linking mechanism.**

Raw rubber (e.g., natural rubber or SBR) has long polymer chains. Sulfur atoms chemically bond between chains, creating cross-links that prevent chains from sliding past each other.

**Simple model:**
```
Polymer chain 1: —CH=CH—CH₂—CH₂—CH=CH—
Polymer chain 2: —CH=CH—CH₂—CH₂—CH=CH—
```

**After vulcanization with sulfur (S):**
```
Polymer chain 1: —CH=CH—CH₂—CH₂—CH=CH—S—S—S—
                                       |
Polymer chain 2: —CH=CH—CH₂—CH₂—CH=CH—S—S—S—
```

The double bonds (C=C) in the polymer are the reactive sites. Sulfur atoms insert between chains, usually as polysulfide bridges (—S—S—S—) of varying length, depending on temperature and accelerator.

**Step 2: Accelerator role.**

Without accelerators, sulfur vulcanization is very slow (days at 150°C). Accelerators speed the reaction by:
- Activating sulfur atoms (making them more reactive toward the C=C bonds)
- Facilitating sulfur insertion and cross-link formation

**Common accelerators:**
- Mercaptobenzothiazole (MBT): moderate speed, used in many applications
- Zinc diethyldithiocarbamate (ZDEC): fast, used for thin-walled products
- Thiuram (TMTD): very fast, used for thin rubber products requiring rapid cure

**Activation by zinc oxide:**
- Zinc oxide (ZnO) + stearic acid → complex that enhances accelerator efficiency
- Typically 5 phr ZnO and 2 phr stearic acid improve cure speed by 30–50%

**Step 3: Over-vulcanization and reversion.**

Over-vulcanization is the risk of curing too long. If rubber is heated too long at high temperature:
1. Initial vulcanization creates cross-links (good)
2. Continued heating breaks some cross-links (reversion)
3. Properties degrade: strength decreases, elasticity is lost

This is why monitoring cure time carefully is critical. Different rubber types have different susceptibility to reversion; natural rubber is more prone to reversion than SBR.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: SBR Copolymer Composition

You want to synthesize SBR with 70% butadiene and 30% styrene (common for high-performance tires).

Starting amount: 1 kg of total monomers

**Butadiene needed:** 1000 g × 0.70 = 700 g
- MW = 54 g/mol
- Moles = 700 g / 54 g/mol = 13 mol

**Styrene needed:** 1000 g × 0.30 = 300 g
- MW = 104 g/mol
- Moles = 300 g / 104 g/mol = 2.88 mol

**Verification:** Check that the copolymerization kinetics (polymerization rate of each monomer) produces the desired ratio. In emulsion polymerization, butadiene polymerizes faster than styrene, so you may need to adjust the initial ratio slightly to achieve the final desired composition.

### Example 2: Vulcanization Time Estimation

You're curing a rubber gasket at 150°C. Using a standard cure chart:
- At 150°C with 1.5 phr MBT, the time to 90% cure is approximately 20 minutes
- Time to maximum cure (100%) is approximately 30 minutes

**Question:** If you want 95% cure, what's the target time?

**Answer:** Approximately 25 minutes (interpolating between 90% at 20 min and 100% at 30 min).

**Note:** Higher temperatures cure faster (exponential relationship). At 160°C, the same 95% cure might take only 15 minutes; at 140°C, it might take 35–40 minutes.

### Example 3: Mixing Formulation for a Tire Tread

You need to prepare 100 kg of tire tread compound with the following recipe:

| Component | phr | Amount (kg) |
|-----------|-----|-------------|
| SBR rubber | 100 | 100 / 1.62 = 61.7 |
| Carbon black | 50 | 30.9 |
| Sulfur | 2.5 | 1.5 |
| ZnO | 5 | 3.1 |
| Stearic acid | 2 | 1.2 |
| MBT | 1.5 | 0.9 |
| Anti-oxidant | 2 | 1.2 |
| **Total phr** | **162.5** | **100 kg** |

**Calculation:** Total phr = 100 + 50 + 2.5 + 5 + 2 + 1.5 + 2 = 162.5 phr

To get 100 kg total:
- Divide each component by 1.625 to get the fraction needed
- SBR = 100 / 1.625 = 61.5 kg
- Carbon black = 50 / 1.625 = 30.8 kg
- And so on...

## Verification Criteria

Successful synthetic rubber and vulcanization is confirmed by:

1. **Appearance:** Uniformly colored (black if carbon-filled, otherwise translucent/white), no visible defects or air bubbles
2. **Elasticity test:** Stretch the cured rubber by 50%; it should return to original size in 1–2 seconds (good elasticity)
3. **Hardness test:** Measure Shore A hardness (should be 50–70 for tire tread, 40–60 for other applications)
4. **Tensile strength:** Pull until breaking; typical values are 15–25 MPa for tire compounds
5. **Chemical resistance:** Soak in motor oil for 24 hours; minimal swelling (less than 10% volume change)

## Common Mistakes

1. **Insufficient mixing of additives:** Uneven distribution of sulfur, accelerator, or fillers leads to inconsistent cure. **Fix:** Mix thoroughly for 3–5 minutes after adding all ingredients.

2. **Temperature control:** Temperature fluctuations cause inconsistent cure rates and poor properties. **Fix:** Use a precision temperature controller; monitor with thermometer.

3. **Over-vulcanization:** Curing too long degrades properties. **Fix:** Follow recommended cure times based on temperature; use rheometer if available to detect optimal cure.

4. **Wrong accelerator for application:** Using MBT (slow) for a product that needs fast cure, or ZDEC (very fast) for a thick product. **Fix:** Match accelerator type to desired cure time and part thickness.

5. **Contamination:** Water or other impurities interfere with vulcanization. **Fix:** Use clean molds; ensure rubber and additives are dry.

6. **Insufficient pressure in mold:** Voids remain in the final product, weakening it. **Fix:** Apply adequate pressure (20–50 atm typical) and ensure complete mold filling.
