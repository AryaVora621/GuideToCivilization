## Overview

Spinning is the process of twisting prepared fibers together to create thread or yarn strong enough for weaving and other textile work. Spinning is one of humanity's oldest crafts, requiring only simple tools: a spindle, fibers, and skill.

This chapter focuses on hand spinning—techniques that require minimal tools and can be performed by anyone with basic manual dexterity. Hand spinning is slower than mechanical spinning wheels but requires no infrastructure or fuel, making it ideal for post-collapse textile production.

Why this matters:
- **Strength:** Twisting fibers creates strength; untwisted fibers fall apart
- **Versatility:** Different twist amounts create different yarn types (fine, coarse, stretchy, etc.)
- **Texture:** Spinning creates the visual and tactile properties of finished textiles
- **Self-sufficiency:** Hand spinning enables textile production from raw fiber without external tools
- **Value:** Spun yarn is more valuable than raw fiber and can be traded or bartered

## Prerequisites

- Prepared fibers (carded plant fiber or carded animal fiber)
- Basic hand-eye coordination and ability to perform repetitive motions
- A spindle (can be improvised) or access to a spinning wheel
- Understanding of fiber processing (previous chapters)
- Patience: learning to spin takes practice (first quality yarn takes several hours)

## Materials & Tools

### Fiber Preparation

**Starting materials:**
- Carded fiber (from previous chapters) wound into rolags (small rolls) or kept in prepared form
- Mixed fibers (intentional blending): plant fiber with animal fiber for specific properties
- Specialty fibers: silk, fine cashmere, rare fibers for premium yarn

### Tools

**Spindle (minimum requirement):**
- Hand spindle: a weight on a stick (can be improvised)
- Commercial spindle: consists of a shaft and whorl (weight)
- Cost: can be improvised from a wooden dowel, stone or clay weight, and a stick

**Improvising a spindle:**
1. Shaft: wooden stick, 8-12 inches long
2. Whorl: round weight (stone, clay, wooden disk, or disk of any material)
3. Attach whorl near the bottom of shaft
4. Secure with simple support or twisted wire to prevent wobbling
5. Hook at top of shaft to catch yarn
6. Functional spindle can be made in under one hour

**Spinning wheel (optional but useful):**
- Mechanical tool that spins continuously when powered by foot pedal
- Faster than hand spinning but requires more infrastructure
- Not necessary for basic spinning; hand spindle is sufficient

### Additional Materials

- Ribbon or cord to tie rolag to spindle as starter fiber
- Water bowl (for moistening fingers; makes spinning easier)
- Container for storing prepared fiber
- Bobbin or spool to wind finished yarn

## Part 1: Understanding Yarn Structure

### What Is Yarn?

Yarn is a continuous length of twisted fibers that has strength, flexibility, and uniform structure. Yarn differs from raw fiber in critical ways:

**Raw fiber:**
- Individual fibers are separate
- No strength in pulling direction
- Fibers fall apart when disturbed

**Spun yarn:**
- Fibers are twisted together
- Twist creates friction between fibers
- Fibers cannot separate without untwisting
- Yarn is strong in pulling direction and flexible

### How Twist Creates Strength

Twist is the fundamental mechanism that creates yarn strength.

**Basic principle:**
1. When fibers are parallel (not twisted), they lie next to each other but are not bound together
2. Twisting fibers creates friction between them in all directions
3. A twisted bundle is much stronger than an untwisted bundle because fibers cannot slide past each other
4. Tightening twist increases strength up to a point; excessive twist makes yarn brittle

**Demonstration (without tools):**
1. Take a handful of carded fiber
2. Try to tear it apart by pulling straight: very easy (fibers separate immediately)
3. Roll the fiber between your hands, creating slight twist
4. Try to tear it again: much harder (twist holds fibers together)
5. Increase twist further; at some point, fibers are so tightly twisted that the bundle becomes brittle
6. The optimal twist is the point where strength is maximum and flexibility remains good

### Ply and Multiple Strands

**Single-ply yarn:** One twisted strand of fiber.

- Thin single-ply: fine fibers, light yarn (good for embroidery, lace, delicate knitting)
- Thick single-ply: coarser fibers, heavy yarn (good for blankets, rugs, cordage)

**Multi-ply yarn:** Multiple single-ply strands twisted together to create final yarn.

**Example: Two-ply yarn (most common)**
1. Spin two separate single-ply strands
2. Twist the two strands together in opposite direction to the original spin
3. Result: strong, balanced yarn with good texture and handling

**Benefits of multiple plies:**
- Increased strength (strength multiplies with ply count)
- Better elasticity (multiple strands compress and stretch together)
- More balanced twist (two plies canceling each other's tendencies)
- Visual texture (individual plies visible, creating interest)
- Structural strength for different purposes (2-ply for fine work, 4-ply for heavy wear)

### Yarn Count and Weight

**Yarn count:** The number of times a unit length of yarn is twisted.

- Fine yarn: many twists per inch (5-10 twists per inch)
- Medium yarn: moderate twists per inch (3-5 twists per inch)
- Coarse yarn: few twists per inch (1-3 twists per inch)

**Yarn weight (thickness):**
- Depends on both fiber thickness and number of fibers per strand
- Fine fibers can be twisted into thin yarn; coarser fibers into thicker yarn
- Multiple plies increase yarn weight

**Practical implication:**
- Fine yarn: requires more twist, takes longer to spin, produces delicate finished textile
- Coarse yarn: requires less twist, spins faster, produces sturdy finished textile

## Part 2: Hand Spinning Technique

### The Basic Spinning Process

Spinning converts loose prepared fibers into twisted yarn through a series of motions:

1. **Draw:** Pull fibers away from the prepared bundle to lengthen the strand
2. **Twist:** Rotate the growing yarn to twist the fibers together
3. **Wind:** Accumulate spun yarn by winding onto a spindle or bobbin

**Key skill:** Balancing these three actions to create consistent yarn.

### Setup for Hand Spindle Spinning

**Preparation:**
1. Obtain a working spindle (commercial or improvised)
2. Attach prepared fiber (rolag or draft of carded fiber) to spindle with a small amount of yarn (leader yarn) as a starter
3. Sit comfortably with spindle hanging freely or resting on a surface
4. Ensure good lighting to see the fiber clearly

### Step-by-Step Spinning with a Hand Spindle

**Step 1: Create the Leader**

1. Tie or attach prepared fiber to spindle using a small amount of yarn
2. Spin the spindle to twist the initial fiber
3. Allow twisted yarn to accumulate on the spindle shaft
4. This creates the leader—the first few inches of yarn that will guide subsequent spinning

**Step 2: Draft (Draw) the Fiber**

1. Hold prepared fiber (rolag) in one hand, loosely
2. Hold the growing yarn with the other hand at a point about 6-12 inches from the fiber source
3. Gently pull away from the fiber source, creating a thin strand of fibers
4. The distance between your hands is the draft zone—where fibers are being pulled thin
5. Moisture helps: dampen your fingers slightly to make drawing easier

**Step 3: Twist the Fiber**

1. Once fibers are drafted (pulled thin), they must be twisted together
2. Spin the spindle clockwise or counterclockwise (consistency matters; choose one direction)
3. As spindle rotates, twist travels from the spindle up into the drafted fibers
4. Twist binds the fibers together, creating yarn

**Step 4: Wind Onto Spindle**

1. As twist reaches the top of the drafted fibers, wind the finished yarn onto the spindle shaft
2. Wind in a spiral pattern, alternating direction to keep spindle balanced
3. Wind tightly enough that yarn does not slip, but not so tight that it compresses
4. Continue spinning and winding until spindle is full

**Step 5: Repeat**

1. When spindle is nearly full, stop spinning
2. Remove yarn from spindle and wind into a ball or skein
3. Clean spindle
4. Tie fresh fiber to spindle and continue

### Key Principles

**Consistent draw:**
- Pulling too hard creates thin spots (weak yarn)
- Pulling too lightly creates thick spots (uneven yarn)
- Goal: steady, even tension in drawn fibers

**Consistent twist:**
- Too much twist: yarn becomes brittle and difficult to work with
- Too little twist: yarn falls apart easily
- Optimal twist: holds fibers together while allowing flexibility

**Balanced tension:**
- Tension between the draft zone and the spindle determines spin speed
- Too much tension: yarn breaks during spinning
- Too little tension: unspun fibers accumulate without twisting
- Goal: smooth, continuous spinning with no breakage

### Common Problems and Solutions

**Yarn is too thick and breaks:**
- Problem: Not enough twist being applied; fibers are not holding together
- Solution: Increase spindle rotation speed; allow more twist to accumulate before winding

**Yarn is too thin in spots (weak yarn):**
- Problem: Drawing too much fiber at once; thin spots have insufficient twist
- Solution: Decrease draw rate; pull less fiber into the draft zone

**Yarn is too thick overall:**
- Problem: Not drawing fibers thin enough; excessive fiber in the draft zone
- Solution: Increase draw distance; pull fiber more thinly before twisting

**Yarn keeps breaking during spinning:**
- Problem: Excessive tension; fibers cannot move smoothly
- Solution: Reduce tension; relax grip on prepared fiber; allow spindle to spin more freely

**Spindle falls too slowly or stops spinning:**
- Problem: Spindle is too light or imbalanced; insufficient momentum
- Solution: Add weight to whorl; ensure spindle is balanced; spin with more force

## Part 3: Advanced Hand Spinning Techniques

### Plying (Creating Multiple-Ply Yarn)

Plying combines multiple single-ply strands into strong, balanced yarn.

**Two-Ply Spinning:**

1. Spin two separate single-ply strands (set aside on bobbins or small spools)
2. Set up the spindle with both strands attached as leaders
3. Hold both strands together in one hand (draft zone)
4. Spin the spindle in the opposite direction from which you originally spun (if you spun clockwise for single-ply, spin counterclockwise for ply)
5. The opposite spin direction balances the twist from the original singles
6. Result: balanced two-ply yarn that curls naturally and has good elasticity

**Four-Ply Spinning:**

1. Spin four separate single-ply strands
2. Ply them in stages: first ply two pairs together (creating two 2-ply strands), then ply the two 2-ply strands together
3. More complex but creates very strong, balanced yarn
4. Often used for premium textiles and special purposes

### Blending Fibers While Spinning

Different fibers can be combined while spinning to create custom yarn.

**Fiber blending technique:**
1. Prepare two different fiber types (e.g., wool and plant fiber)
2. Card them together in the initial fiber preparation (mixing fibers)
3. Spin as normal
4. Result: yarn that combines properties of both fibers

**Example blends:**
- Wool + plant fiber: combines warmth of wool with durability of plant fiber
- Fine fiber + coarse fiber: creates textured yarn with fine appearance and coarse durability
- Undyed fiber + dyed fiber: creates naturally variegated yarn (different colors blended)

### Thick vs. Thin Yarn Spinning

**Thin yarn (fine):**
- Requires finer fibers (wool, fine plant fiber)
- Requires more twist (5-10 twists per inch)
- Spins slowly (requires patience)
- Used for: delicate clothing, fine knitwear, embroidery
- Advantages: elegant appearance, good drape
- Disadvantages: time-consuming to spin, requires finer starting materials

**Thick yarn (coarse):**
- Can use coarser fibers (hemp, coarse animal hair)
- Requires less twist (1-3 twists per inch)
- Spins quickly
- Used for: blankets, rugs, cordage, heavy textiles
- Advantages: spins quickly, uses coarser materials, strong
- Disadvantages: less elegant appearance, does not drape as well

## Part 4: Using a Spinning Wheel

For production spinning (large quantities), a spinning wheel is much more efficient than hand spinning.

### Basic Spinning Wheel Operation

**Components:**
- Wheel: provides momentum to the spindle
- Spindle: twists the yarn
- Flyer: guides yarn onto the spindle
- Foot treadle: powers the wheel via foot pedaling

**Basic process:**
1. Sit at the wheel with prepared fiber
2. Attach fiber to spindle with leader yarn
3. Draft fibers with one hand while pedaling with feet
4. The wheel rotates the spindle, providing twist automatically
5. Wind yarn onto spindle by adjusting flyer position

**Advantages over hand spindle:**
- Faster production (10-20x faster than hand spindle)
- Less hand fatigue (wheel does the twisting; hands focus on drafting)
- More consistent yarn (wheel rotation is steady)
- Better for production of large quantities

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires infrastructure (wooden wheel, treadle, space)
- More complex to maintain and repair
- Requires learning curve (pedal coordination with hand drafting)
- Less portable than hand spindle

### Improvising a Spinning Wheel

A functional spinning wheel can be constructed with:
- Large wooden wheel (can be made or salvaged)
- Spindle mechanism (bearings or simple axle)
- Flyer system (improvised with hooks and guides)
- Treadle (foot-powered mechanism)
- Drive belt (rope or cord)

**Challenges:**
- Construction requires woodworking skill
- Alignment must be precise for smooth operation
- Initial investment in materials

For most situations, hand spindle spinning is sufficient and more practical.

## Part 5: Practical Application Examples

### Example 1: Spinning Fine Yarn for Clothing

**Scenario:** You have carded sheep's wool and want to spin fine yarn for a sweater.

**Process:**
1. Prepare rolag of fine sheep's wool
2. Spin single-ply yarn with 8-10 twists per inch
3. Spin two separate singles strands
4. Ply together to create balanced 2-ply yarn
5. Set twist by wetting and drying under slight tension

**Timeline:**
- Spinning single strands: 20-30 hours (for enough yarn for a sweater)
- Plying: 5-10 hours
- Setting twist: 1 day

**Result:** Fine, strong yarn suitable for knitting or weaving

### Example 2: Spinning Heavy Cordage from Hemp

**Scenario:** You have processed hemp fiber and need strong rope.

**Process:**
1. Prepare coarser hemp fiber
2. Spin thick single-ply with 2-3 twists per inch
3. Spin three or four strands
4. Ply three strands together to create heavy 3-ply cord
5. Set twist

**Timeline:**
- Spinning singles: 5-8 hours
- Plying: 2-3 hours
- Setting twist: 1 day

**Result:** Very strong cordage suitable for heavy-duty applications

### Example 3: Blended Fiber Yarn for Durability and Warmth

**Scenario:** You have both plant fiber and animal fiber. Goal: yarn combining both properties.

**Process:**
1. Card plant fiber and animal fiber together (blend them in the carder)
2. Spin blended fiber into single-ply yarn
3. Ply two strands together
4. Result: yarn with warmth of animal fiber and durability of plant fiber

**Properties:**
- More durable than pure animal fiber
- Warmer than pure plant fiber
- Less expensive than pure wool (plant fiber is less valuable)
- Suitable for everyday clothing and practical items

## Part 6: Yarn Finishing

### Twist Setting

Twist setting helps stabilize the yarn and prevent unraveling.

**Method:**
1. Wind finished yarn into a loose skein
2. Soak in warm water for 20-30 minutes
3. Gently wring out excess water
4. Hang under slight tension to dry (weights can provide tension)
5. Heat helps: dry in sun or near a fire (not too hot)

**Result:** Twist is permanently set; yarn is less likely to unravel during handling or use

### Plying Direction Balance

For multi-ply yarn, correct plying direction ensures balanced, even yarn.

- **S-twist singles:** Twisted in one direction (like the letter S)
- **Z-twist singles:** Twisted in the opposite direction (like the letter Z)
- **Balanced 2-ply:** One S-twist single and one Z-twist single, plied together (or both same twist, plied in opposite direction)

**Checking balance:**
- Hold finished 2-ply yarn loosely
- If it curls in one direction: ply direction was incorrect
- If it hangs straight: ply direction was correct (balanced)

## Common Mistakes

1. **Too much twist too quickly:** Excessive twist builds up, yarn becomes brittle, eventually breaks. Solution: apply twist gradually; check yarn periodically for over-twist.

2. **Spindle spinning too fast:** Fibers do not have time to draft; resulting yarn is thick and lumpy. Solution: spin slower; allow time to draw fibers before twist reaches them.

3. **Spindle spinning too slowly:** Insufficient twist; fibers do not hold together; yarn falls apart. Solution: spin faster or with more force; maintain consistent spindle momentum.

4. **Inconsistent drawing:** Some fibers drawn thin, others drawn thick; resulting yarn is uneven. Solution: maintain steady, consistent draw rate; practice to develop muscle memory.

5. **Plying in wrong direction:** Fails to balance twist from original singles; ply curls back on itself. Solution: ply in opposite direction from original spin; test small sample before committing to full plying.

6. **Wet fingers not reapplied:** Fingers dry out; drafting becomes difficult; spinner pulls too hard. Solution: dampen fingers frequently; keep water nearby.

7. **Spindle too heavy:** Excess weight makes spindle difficult to control; spins too fast. Solution: use lighter spindle; add weight gradually until spindle has adequate momentum without being unwieldy.

8. **Spindle wobbles or is imbalanced:** Creates inconsistent twist; yarn becomes uneven; spindle is difficult to control. Solution: ensure spindle rotates smoothly; check for bent shaft or unbalanced whorl.

9. **Skipping the twist-setting step:** Yarn unravels during use; twist loosens. Solution: always set twist by wetting and drying under tension.

10. **Attempting ply without adequate singles:** Attempts to ply before individual strands are spun; fibers are not twisted enough individually. Solution: spin thick, well-twisted singles; ensure singles can hold together without added ply.
