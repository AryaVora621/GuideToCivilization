## Overview

Animal fibers differ fundamentally from plant fibers. They are naturally warm, water-resistant, and durable. Wool from sheep, hair from goats and other animals, and silk from silkworms all require different processing approaches but yield superior textiles for clothing, blankets, and specialty items.

In a post-collapse world, animals provide not only meat and dairy but also fiber—a renewable resource that can be harvested annually without killing the animal. Understanding animal fiber processing transforms livestock from purely food sources into fiber sources, increasing the value and self-sufficiency of your settlement.

Why this matters:
- **Warmth:** Animal fibers insulate better than plant fibers, critical for survival in cold climates
- **Durability:** Animal fiber textiles last longer and withstand harder use
- **Value:** Wool and silk are premium trade goods
- **Self-sufficiency:** Raising fiber animals provides clothing material without external dependence
- **Efficiency:** Processing animal fiber is often faster than processing plant fiber

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of basic animal husbandry (care and management of livestock)
- Access to fiber-producing animals (sheep, goats, alpacas, rabbits) or silkworms
- Water for washing and processing
- Simple tools (combs, wooden implements, drying racks)
- Basic knowledge of plant fiber processing (many techniques overlap)
- Patience: animal fiber processing also takes time, though less than plant processing

## Materials & Tools

### Fiber Sources

**Wool (from sheep):**
- Finest and most valuable fiber
- Annual fleece yield per sheep: 4-15 lbs depending on breed
- Color range: white, cream, brown, black (natural dyes can extend this)
- Naturally water-resistant due to lanolin (natural oil)

**Goat hair (from goats and similar animals):**
- Coarser than sheep wool; typically longer
- Cashmere (from certain goat breeds) is extremely fine and valuable
- Mohair (from Angora goats) is silky and lustrous
- Annual yield: 3-8 lbs per animal

**Other animal hair:**
- Alpaca: fine, warm, similar quality to wool
- Rabbit: soft, warm, but short (often blended with other fibers)
- Camel: coarse, warm, valuable in arid regions
- Horse, cattle, human: coarser; used for specialty items

**Silk:**
- Produced by silkworm cocoons
- Finest and strongest animal fiber
- Natural production requires beekeeping-like management of silkworms
- Labor-intensive but yields small amount of extremely valuable fiber

### Processing Tools

**Minimum tools:**
- Hot water and large container (for washing)
- Combs or carders (can be improvised)
- Drying rack or clean surface
- Simple spindle (for converting fiber to thread)

**Useful additions:**
- Fine-tooth comb (for combing fibers)
- Carders (two brushes used to align fibers; similar to large hairbrushes)
- Drying frame
- Scales (to measure fiber weight)

## Part 1: Understanding Animal Fiber Structure

### What Is Animal Fiber?

Animal fibers are proteins (primarily keratin) formed into long, overlapping scale structures. Unlike plant fibers, which are hollow tubes, animal fibers are solid and flexible.

### Fiber Characteristics

**Wool structure:**
- Overlapping scales on fiber surface
- These scales cause fibers to cling together (felting) and create bulk
- Naturally contain lanolin (oils) for water resistance
- Naturally crimped (wavy), which provides insulation and elasticity

**Hair structure:**
- Similar to wool but often smoother (fewer scales)
- Less natural oil; may require treatment for water resistance
- Often straighter than wool, providing less bulk

**Silk structure:**
- Smooth protein fiber; no scales
- Produced as a continuous filament from silkworm cocoon
- Strongest animal fiber; reflects light, creating luster
- Natural shine and smooth feel

### Why This Matters

Understanding fiber structure explains processing requirements:

- **Washing:** Must remove lanolin and dirt without damaging scales
- **Carding:** Aligns scales in one direction; creates uniform fiber
- **Felting risk:** Excessive agitation or temperature change causes fibers to lock together (felt)
- **Spinning:** Animal fibers cling better than plant fibers; require less twist for strength

## Part 2: Washing and Preparation

### Fleece Preparation

**Shearing (harvesting fleece):**
1. Shear or pluck fleece from the animal
2. Remove obvious dirt, vegetable matter, and manure
3. Fleece should come off as a single sheet if properly shorn
4. Fleece with excessive vegetable matter should be separated before washing

**Sorting:**
1. Fleece quality varies by location on the animal (finest on sides, coarsest on legs)
2. Sort fibers by fineness and color if producing premium yarn
3. For general use, mixed quality is acceptable

### Washing and Scouring

The goal of washing is to remove lanolin (natural oil), dirt, sweat, and other impurities without felting the fiber.

**Safe Washing Method (Critical):**

1. Fill container with warm water (approximately 50-60°C / 120-140°F; hot enough that you can hold your hand in it briefly)
2. Add soap or detergent (soap flakes, lye water, or ash water; modern detergent works but is not necessary)
3. Submerge wool gently; DO NOT AGITATE or rub
4. Soak for 20-30 minutes
5. Lift fleece from water carefully; DO NOT WRING or squeeze
6. Drain water from fleece by gentle squeezing (hand-position support underneath)
7. Rinse in warm water (same temperature as wash water) three times; same gentle method
8. Final rinse should be clean water
9. Drain and squeeze gently
10. Spread on drying rack to dry (may take several days depending on humidity)

**CRITICAL WARNING:** 
- Never go from hot water to cold water suddenly (causes felting)
- Never agitate wet wool (causes felting)
- Never wring or squeeze hard (causes felting)
- Felting locks fibers together irreversibly; felted fiber is unsuitable for spinning

**Signs of proper washing:**
- Fiber is clean (no dirt visible)
- Fiber feels soft and lofty
- No lanolin smell (or greatly reduced)
- Fiber has not changed color (no yellowing or darkening from heat)

**Signs of felting (problem):**
- Fibers are matted or clumped
- Fiber is dense and does not fluff up
- Surface has a fuzzy, felted appearance
- If felting occurs, fiber is damaged and should be used for felt (not spinning)

### Hair Processing

Hair from goats and other animals often has coarser guard hairs mixed with softer undercoat. Processing requires separation.

**Sorting coarse and fine fibers:**
1. After washing and drying, comb or card the hair
2. Coarser fibers separate from finer fibers naturally
3. Reserve coarse fibers for cordage or coarse textiles
4. Fine fibers (cashmere, mohair) can be separated for premium use

## Part 3: Fiber Preparation (Carding)

Carding aligns fibers and removes remaining vegetable matter and debris. It prepares fibers for spinning.

### Understanding Carding

Carding uses two brushes with fine teeth to comb fibers. The process:
1. Transfers fiber from one card to the other
2. Aligns fibers in the same direction
3. Opens up compressed fiber, improving loft and warmth
4. Removes short fibers and debris

### Hand Carding Method

**Improvising carders (if not available):**
- Wooden paddles with nails driven through (coarse combs)
- Brushes (vegetable-fiber brushes work)
- Simple combs (fine-toothed)

**Carding process:**

1. Place a small handful of clean fleece on one carder (back carder)
2. Drag the other carder (front carder) over the fleece in one direction
3. Rotate the front carder away and back, carding the fibers
4. Continue carding until fibers are mostly aligned (30-50 strokes)
5. Transfer the carded fiber to the front carder by a gentle rolling motion
6. Repeat with fresh fiber on the back carder
7. Continue until the fiber is fluffy, aligned, and free of vegetable matter

**Post-carding:**
- Fibers are aligned and ready for spinning
- Carded fiber can be stored for extended periods if kept dry

### Machine Carding (If Available)

If you have access to a mechanical fiber carder (powered by hand crank or motor):
1. Feed clean, dried fleece into the carder
2. Crank (or run) the machine
3. Fibers emerge combed and aligned
4. Much faster than hand carding; requires initial investment or access to equipment

## Part 4: Processing Silk

Silk processing differs significantly from wool and hair processing due to the nature of silk production.

### Sericulture (Raising Silkworms)

**Silkworm rearing (basics):**

1. Silkworms (Bombyx mori) feed exclusively on mulberry leaves
2. After 4-5 weeks of feeding, silkworms spin cocoons
3. Each cocoon contains a single continuous silk filament (approximately 300-900 meters long)
4. Cocoons should be harvested before the moth emerges (which breaks the filament)
5. A single cocoon yields approximately 100 meters of usable silk filament

### Sericulture Challenges in Post-Collapse Settings

- Mulberry trees must be grown or found wild
- Silkworms require consistent temperature and feeding
- Infrastructure (baskets, frames) is needed to house silkworms
- Processing cocoons into usable fiber requires specific knowledge

### From Cocoon to Usable Fiber

**Stifling (killing pupae in cocoons without breaking filament):**
1. Cocoons are stifled (killed) by steam, heat, or submersion in hot water
2. This prevents the moth from emerging and breaking the filament
3. Timing is critical: pupae must be killed while the filament is intact

**Sericulture (extracting filament from cocoons):**
1. Stifled cocoons are soaked in hot water (70-80°C) to soften the sericin (gum holding the cocoon together)
2. The softened cocoon is gently unwound by rolling on a surface or combing with a fine brush
3. Multiple cocoons may be unwound simultaneously and their filaments twisted together (a single strand is too weak to work with)
4. Approximately 4-8 cocoons are needed to create a workable silk strand

**Degumming (removing remaining sericin):**
1. Raw silk filament still contains sericin, which makes it stiff
2. Boil silk in soap water to remove sericin
3. Rinse and dry
4. Result: smooth, lustrous silk ready for use

### Challenges and Alternatives

Silk production in post-collapse settings is labor-intensive and requires infrastructure. Alternatives:
- Focus on wool and hair as primary fiber sources
- Silk becomes a luxury item or trade good rather than everyday textile
- If silk production is attempted, start small (fewer silkworms) and scale up as expertise grows

## Part 5: Practical Application Examples

### Example 1: Processing Sheep Wool for Winter Clothing

**Scenario:** You have sheared a sheep in early summer. Goal: warm winter clothing.

**Timeline:**
1. Shearing and sorting: 2-3 hours
2. Washing and drying: 3-5 days (weather dependent)
3. Carding: 4-8 hours (depends on fiber amount and hand carding vs. machine)
4. Spinning: 10-20 hours (depends on desired yarn thickness)
5. Knitting or weaving: 20-40 hours (depends on garment size)

**Total:** Approximately 2-3 weeks from shearing to wearable garment

**Result:** A sweater or set of socks provides superior warmth compared to plant fiber alternatives

### Example 2: Processing Goat Hair for Rope

**Scenario:** You keep goats for dairy and fiber. Goal: strong rope for practical use.

**Process:**
1. Harvest hair by shearing or plucking
2. Wash carefully (goat hair is coarser and less prone to felting than wool; slightly hotter water is acceptable)
3. Dry thoroughly
4. Separate coarse guard hairs from fine undercoat
5. Use coarse fibers: comb or card lightly
6. Spin with minimal twist (2-3 turns per inch) to create a strong, slightly coarse yarn
7. Ply multiple strands together for rope

**Result:** Durable rope suitable for lifting heavy loads, securing cargo, or structural support

### Example 3: Processing Rabbit Fur for Soft Yarn

**Scenario:** You keep rabbits for food and fiber. Goal: soft, warm yarn for fine knitting.

**Process:**
1. Carefully pluck or comb out shed fur (pulling is less stressful than shearing)
2. Fibers are short (1-3 inches), requiring different handling than wool
3. Wash gently (rabbit fur is delicate)
4. Card thoroughly to align short fibers
5. Spin with 4-6 turns per inch to create cohesion
6. Blend with longer fibers (wool or plant fiber) to improve strength
7. Result: soft, warm, lofty yarn, though less durable than pure wool

**Yield:** A single rabbit yields 0.5-1 ounce of usable fiber; requires multiple rabbits for a garment

### Example 4: Producing Silk Thread from Silkworms

**Scenario:** You have cultivated silkworms and harvested cocoons. Goal: usable silk thread.

**Process:**
1. Stifle cocoons by steaming or hot water (15-20 minutes)
2. Cool completely
3. Soak in hot water to soften sericin
4. Gently unwind filaments from cocoons
5. Combine 4-6 filaments and twist together to create a workable strand
6. Boil in soap water to remove remaining sericin
7. Dry and ready for use

**Result:** Precious silk thread suitable for fine embroidery, specialty clothing, or high-value trade

## Part 6: Wool Finishing Techniques

After spinning, wool can be finished with heat and moisture to enhance properties.

### Setting the Twist

1. Wet finished yarn thoroughly with warm water
2. Allow to soak (30-60 minutes)
3. Gently squeeze out excess water (do not wring)
4. Hang yarn to dry under tension (use weights if necessary)
5. Heat helps set the twist: near a fire or in sun (warm, not hot)

**Result:** Twist is set permanently; yarn is less likely to unravel

### Fulling (Light Felting for Density)

Fulling is controlled felting to create denser, warmer fabric.

1. Wet finished cloth with warm water and soap
2. Gently agitate (rub fibers together) in controlled manner
3. Heat accelerates fulling (warm, not hot)
4. Monitor closely; stop before over-felting
5. Rinse and dry

**Result:** Fabric becomes denser, warmer, and more water-resistant

## Common Mistakes

1. **Hot to cold water shock:** Transitioning from hot wash water to cold rinse water causes immediate felting. Solution: all water should be approximately the same temperature.

2. **Agitating wool during washing:** Rubbing, wringing, or stirring wet wool causes felting. Solution: handle wool gently; support underneath to prevent stretching.

3. **Incomplete scouring:** Excess lanolin remaining in fiber makes finished yarn greasy and attracts dirt. Solution: wash with adequate soap and perform multiple rinses until water runs clear.

4. **Insufficient carding:** Uncarded fiber produces uneven, weak yarn with inconsistent feel. Solution: card thoroughly until fibers are fluffy and aligned.

5. **Over-carding:** Repeated carding can separate fibers too much, reducing cohesion. Solution: card until fibers are aligned; stop before fibers become sparse or thin.

6. **Attempting to process wet silk cocoons:** Wet cocoons rot before filaments can be extracted. Solution: stifle cocoons promptly and dry completely before storage; extract filaments from dry cocoons.

7. **Breaking silk filaments during extraction:** Silk is strong but breaks if pulled too hard or with sudden jerks. Solution: extract silk filaments with slow, steady pressure; do not jerk or pull suddenly.

8. **Mixing fiber types:** Different fibers (wool, hair, plant fiber) have different properties and should not be mixed unless intentionally blending for specific properties. Solution: process each fiber type separately; blend intentionally if desired effect is known.

9. **Poor drying conditions:** Fiber that dries too slowly develops mold or odor; fiber that dries too quickly in direct sun can yellow or degrade. Solution: dry in shade with good air circulation; aim for 3-5 days drying time.

10. **Assuming all cashmere or mohair can be easily processed:** Fine fibers are valuable but fragile. Rough handling or improper temperature control can destroy value. Solution: handle fine fibers with extra care; process separately from coarser fibers.
