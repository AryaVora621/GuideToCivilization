## Overview

Simple plain weave (alternating sheds with single-color warp and weft) produces serviceable cloth but limited visual interest. By varying the shed sequence, introducing colored threads, or using different yarn weights, weavers can create patterns ranging from simple geometric designs to complex decorative cloths.

Pattern weaving requires looms with multiple harnesses (four or more) and attention to detail. The reward is cloth that is both beautiful and structurally interesting, useful for special garments, trade goods, or items of ceremonial significance.

Why this matters:
- **Value:** Patterned cloth is more valuable than plain weave; trade-worthy
- **Function:** Patterns can be functional (different weave structures have different properties: tighter weaves for water resistance, more open weaves for breathability)
- **Distinction:** Special cloth marks occasions, status, or skill
- **Aesthetics:** Beautiful cloth improves quality of life and provides psychological benefit

## Prerequisites

- Skilled hand-weaving ability (can produce consistent plain weave)
- Access to a loom with at least 4 harnesses
- Understanding of warp threading (how warp connects to harnesses)
- Ability to plan ahead (pattern weaving requires advance planning)
- Patience and attention to detail (mistakes in threading affect the entire cloth)

## Materials & Tools

### Looms for Pattern Weaving

**Minimum requirements:**
- 4-harness floor loom or table loom (allows two-shaft patterns)
- 8-harness loom (allows complex patterns)
- Treadle linkages or hand levers that can combine harnesses (e.g., treadle 1 raises harnesses A+B)

**Tools:**
- Draft paper (for planning patterns)
- Pencil and ruler (for precise planning)
- Threading hook (for threading warp through heddles)
- Tie-on stick (for attaching warp to loom)
- Warp counter (to track threading progress)

### Fiber Selection for Patterns

**Solid color warp:**
- Single color for the background
- Pattern emerges from weft color or structural variation

**Multi-color warp:**
- Multiple colors arranged in stripes or patterns
- Creates visual richness even with plain weave weft

**Contrasting weft colors:**
- Weft color differs from warp
- Creates different visual effects on opposite sides of cloth

## Part 1: Understanding Pattern Structure

### How Patterns Work in Weaving

Pattern in weaving is created by:

1. **Shaft sequence:** The order in which harnesses raise and lower
2. **Color:** Different colored warp or weft threads
3. **Structure:** Different weave structures (plain, twill, satin, etc.) create different visual effects
4. **Yarn weight:** Alternating thick and thin yarns creates texture

### The Draft: Pattern Planning

A **draft** is a visual map showing:
- Which harnesses raise for each pick (weft pass)
- How warp is threaded through harnesses
- The resulting pattern

**Draft notation:**
- Rows represent picks (passes of weft)
- Columns represent warp threads
- Shaded vs. unshaded squares show which harnesses are raised
- Black = harness raised (warp thread goes over weft)
- White = harness lowered (warp thread goes under weft)

### Repeat Unit

Most patterns consist of a **repeat unit** that repeats horizontally and/or vertically.

**Example: Striped pattern**
- Repeat unit: 4 warp threads (threads 1-4 repeat along the width)
- Pattern: ABAB... (alternating two colors)

**Example: Checked pattern**
- Repeat unit: 8x8 squares
- Pattern: blocks of color create checkered appearance

Understanding the repeat unit allows you to:
1. Plan the threading precisely
2. Calculate total warp length needed
3. Predict the final pattern appearance

## Part 2: Basic Pattern Structures

### Plain Weave (Review)

**Threading:**
- Odd-numbered warp threads on harness 1
- Even-numbered warp threads on harness 2

**Treadling (shed sequence):**
- Pick 1: Raise harness 1
- Pick 2: Raise harness 2
- Repeat

**Result:** Balanced, sturdy cloth with no visible pattern (unless using colored threads)

### Twill Weave

Twill creates diagonal lines in the cloth. The characteristic diagonal pattern emerges from a staggered shed sequence.

**Threading for 4-harness twill:**
- Thread 1: Harness 1
- Thread 2: Harness 2
- Thread 3: Harness 3
- Thread 4: Harness 4
- Thread 5: Harness 1 (repeat)

**Treadling sequence:**
- Pick 1: Raise harnesses 1-2 (lowers 3-4)
- Pick 2: Raise harnesses 2-3 (lowers 1, 4)
- Pick 3: Raise harnesses 3-4 (lowers 1-2)
- Pick 4: Raise harnesses 4-1 (lowers 2-3)
- Repeat

**Result:** Diagonal lines running through the cloth at approximately 45 degrees

**Variations:**
- **2/2 twill:** Two threads up, two threads down (common, visible diagonal)
- **3/1 twill:** Three up, one down (steeper diagonal, more textured)
- **Herringbone twill:** Twill direction reverses at midpoint, creating V-shaped pattern

### Satin Weave

Satin creates a smooth, lustrous surface by floating yarn over multiple threads before binding down.

**Threading for 4-harness satin:**
- Thread 1: Harness 1
- Thread 2: Harness 2
- Thread 3: Harness 3
- Thread 4: Harness 4
- Thread 5: Harness 1 (repeat)

**Treadling sequence (5-pick repeat):**
- Pick 1: Raise harness 1 only (weft floats over 3 warp threads)
- Pick 2: Raise harness 2 only (offset by one thread)
- Pick 3: Raise harness 3 only (offset by one thread)
- Pick 4: Raise harness 4 only (offset by one thread)
- Pick 5: Raise harness 1 only (offset by one thread)
- Repeat

**Result:** Smooth, lustrous surface with float threads creating shine (similar to satin fabric)

**Note:** Satin requires longer floats than twill, making it less durable but more lustrous. Practical for special cloth, not everyday use.

## Part 3: Colored Warp Patterns

Colors introduce pattern without requiring complex shed sequences.

### Striped Warp

**Threading:**
- Threads 1-4: Color A
- Threads 5-8: Color B
- Threads 9-12: Color A
- Threads 13-16: Color B
- Repeat

**Treadling:** Standard plain weave shed sequence

**Result:** Vertical stripes in the cloth (warp color shows primary pattern)

**Width of stripes:** Determined by the number of threads of each color
- 4 threads = 4-thread-wide stripes
- 8 threads = 8-thread-wide stripes

### Checked Pattern (Gingham-like)

**Threading:**
- Combine horizontal and vertical color alternation
- Threads: AABB AABB... (4-thread repeat horizontally)
- Colors: Group threads by color, alternating along the width

**Treadling (with colored weft):**
- Picks 1-4: Weft color A
- Picks 5-8: Weft color B
- Repeat

**Result:** Checkered pattern where warp stripes intersect with weft stripes

**Complexity:** Easy to achieve, very visible pattern

### Multi-Color Warp Designs

Complex color effects achieved by:
1. Careful color selection (complementary colors create vibrant effects)
2. Precise thread counting (even small errors compound)
3. Regular tension monitoring (loose spots become visible)

## Part 4: Structural Patterns

Patterns created purely by manipulating the shed sequence without changing colors.

### Houndstooth Pattern

Houndstooth is a broken twill pattern creating a jagged line effect.

**Threading:**
- Standard 4-harness twill threading

**Treadling (8-pick repeat):**
- Picks 1-2: Twill sequence forward (diagonal climbs)
- Picks 3-4: Twill sequence backward (diagonal descends)
- Picks 5-6: Twill sequence forward
- Picks 7-8: Twill sequence backward
- Repeat

**Result:** Jagged diagonal lines creating characteristic houndstooth pattern

### Basket Weave

Basket weave creates a grid pattern with visible squares.

**Threading:**
- Threads 1-2: Harness 1
- Threads 3-4: Harness 2
- Threads 5-6: Harness 1
- Threads 7-8: Harness 2
- Repeat

**Treadling:**
- Picks 1-2: Raise harness 1
- Picks 3-4: Raise harness 2
- Repeat

**Result:** Visible grid of woven squares

**Variations:**
- 3x3 basket: Groups of 3 threads/picks create larger squares
- Irregular basket: Random group sizes create textured appearance

### Waffle Weave

Waffle weave creates a dimensional, textured surface.

**Threading:**
- Complex threading pattern that creates interlocking floats and bindings

**Treadling:**
- Sequence that creates raised and lowered areas

**Result:** Three-dimensional texture resembling waffle grid; good for towels and absorbent cloth

## Part 5: Designing Your Own Patterns

### Pattern Design Process

**Step 1: Choose the repeat unit**
- Decide how many warp threads the pattern repeats over (8, 16, 32, etc.)
- Decide how many picks the pattern repeats over
- Larger repeat = more complex pattern

**Step 2: Create a draft**
1. Draw a grid on paper (squares represent individual warp-weft intersections)
2. Shade in the pattern (shaded = harness raised)
3. The pattern should repeat at the edges (left edge matches right edge)

**Step 3: Determine harness requirements**
- Count the maximum number of harnesses needed
- Ensure your loom has at least that many

**Step 4: Thread the loom according to your draft**
- Carefully follow the threading sequence
- Use a warp counter to track progress
- Mistakes are difficult to correct after threading

**Step 5: Weave the pattern**
- Follow the treadling sequence precisely
- Each pick must match the draft or pattern is lost

### Design Principles

**Visual balance:**
- Pattern should be balanced (not too much of one color on one side)
- Patterns that are too busy are difficult to weave without errors

**Practicality:**
- Floats longer than 0.5 inch snag easily and wear poorly
- Patterns should be durable enough for intended use

**Repeat simplicity:**
- Patterns with small repeats are easier to weave without losing place
- 4x4 or 8x8 repeats are good for beginners

## Part 6: Practical Applications and Examples

### Example 1: Striped Kitchen Towels

**Scenario:** You want to produce patterned cloth for household use.

**Pattern choice:** Vertical stripes (simple color warp, plain weave)

**Process:**
1. Design: 4 threads each of two colors, repeating (AAAABBBBAAAABBBB...)
2. Thread: Follow the color sequence
3. Weave: Standard plain weave shed sequence
4. Result: Striped towels ready in 1-2 days of weaving

**Advantage:** Simple to execute; no complex shed sequences; significant visual improvement over plain cloth

**Yield:** Multiple yards of cloth from one threading

### Example 2: Geometric Twill Pattern Blanket

**Scenario:** You want to create special cloth for trade.

**Pattern choice:** 4-harness twill with herringbone pattern

**Process:**
1. Design: 8-pick twill sequence (4 picks forward, 4 picks backward)
2. Thread: Standard 4-harness twill threading
3. Weave: Follow herringbone treadling sequence carefully
4. Result: Beautiful geometric pattern

**Advantage:** Complex-looking pattern; actually simple execution once threading is complete

**Yield:** Significant trade value due to complexity and appearance

### Example 3: Multi-Color Checked Pattern

**Scenario:** Create decorative cloth combining color and structural complexity.

**Pattern choice:** Checked pattern with colored warp and weft

**Process:**
1. Design: 4x4 color blocks (4 threads each of two colors in warp; 4 picks each of two colors in weft)
2. Thread: Alternate colors precisely
3. Weave: Plain weave with alternating weft colors
4. Result: Gingham-like cloth with strong visual pattern

**Advantage:** Very visible pattern; valuable for trade

**Timeline:** 3-4 days weaving to produce several yards

## Part 7: Troubleshooting Pattern Weaving

### Common Problems and Solutions

**Pattern is distorted or shifted:**
- Problem: Threading mistake; warp is threaded incorrectly
- Solution: Check draft against actual loom; locate the error; correction requires re-threading (cannot be fixed by re-weaving)

**Some harnesses not raising/lowering properly:**
- Problem: Treadle linkage not set correctly; heddle tangled
- Solution: Check treadle connections; untangle any caught heddles; test with all harnesses before beginning important weaving

**Weft catches on warp threads:**
- Problem: Shed is not fully open; overlapping harnesses
- Solution: Check treadle timing; ensure only desired harnesses are raised; increase raise distance if possible

**Horizontal lines appear in pattern:**
- Problem: Inconsistent weft tension or thickness
- Solution: Maintain even weft tension; use consistent yarn size; beat evenly

**Pattern looks correct initially, then shifts:**
- Problem: Warp is shifting on beam; tension decreasing
- Solution: Tighten warp beam gradually; ensure even winding; monitor tension throughout weaving

**Color bleeding or mixing:**
- Problem: Inadequate washing before weaving; dyes are not set
- Solution: Wash and dry yarn before weaving; ensure dyes are set with appropriate mordants if using dyed fibers

## Part 8: Advanced Techniques

### Multiple Weft Colors

Instead of simple alternation, introduce weft colors in more complex sequences:

- **Graduated color:** Gradually transition from one color to another
- **Random color:** Introduce colors in unpredictable sequence
- **Pictorial weaving:** Use weft color intentionally to create images

**Challenges:**
- Requires careful planning
- Weft color changes are time-consuming
- Errors in color sequence are highly visible

### Supplementary Warp

Add extra warp threads (not all connected to harnesses) for special effects:

- Color accents (supplementary threads in contrasting color)
- Structural effects (supplementary threads create pattern without changing basic weave)
- Decorative elements (floating threads create lines)

**Advantage:** Adds complexity without requiring additional harnesses

### Tablet Weaving Integration

Combine tablet-woven trim with loom-woven cloth:

- Weave main cloth on loom
- Create decorative trim using tablet weaving
- Sew trim to cloth edges
- Result: Combined technique cloth with special detail

## Common Mistakes

1. **Thread counting errors:** Even one thread out of place distorts the entire pattern. Solution: Use a warp counter; recount frequently; verify after threading complete.

2. **Ignoring repeat unit boundaries:** Threading the repeat unit incorrectly at the pattern edges causes the pattern to break. Solution: Ensure the repeat aligns at both edges; practice with small repeats first.

3. **Treadle errors during weaving:** Pressing wrong treadle one time is visible in the cloth. Solution: Weave slowly; mark treadle sequence on paper; check each pick before starting the next.

4. **Mixing up similar patterns:** Twill, houndstooth, and herringbone look similar but require different treadle sequences. Solution: Write out exact treadle sequence; refer to it constantly; do not rely on memory.

5. **Inadequate float management:** Floats (long floats in satin or twill) snag during use. Solution: Keep floats under 0.5 inch; avoid patterns with very long floats for utilitarian cloth; satin suitable for special cloth only.

6. **Inconsistent tension leading to pattern distortion:** Looseness on one side of the cloth distorts pattern. Solution: Monitor warp tension throughout; adjust gradually; ensure even beating of weft.

7. **Color conflicts:** Colors chosen do not complement each other or are difficult to see against each other. Solution: Test color combinations before committing; use strong contrasts for visibility.

8. **Pattern too complex for skill level:** Attempting a pattern beyond your current ability leads to frustration and mistakes. Solution: Start with simple patterns (stripes, basic twill); advance to more complex patterns after gaining experience.

9. **Forgetting warp needs for pattern:** Pattern-weaving requires more warp than simple weaving (for error correction, testing, etc.). Solution: Calculate extra warp length; wind longer warp than for simple weaving.

10. **Not documenting the pattern:** Forgetting exact specifications after weaving. Solution: Keep detailed notes and draft samples; archive successful patterns for future reference.
