## Overview

Color transforms plain cloth into something valuable. In a post-collapse world, colored cloth becomes a mark of skill and resource access, making dyed textiles valuable trade goods.

Natural dyes have been used for thousands of years to create colors from plants, insects, and minerals. The process of dyeing involves chemistry—specifically, how to make dye molecules stick to fiber molecules permanently. Understanding this process enables any community to produce colored cloth without synthetic dyes.

This chapter covers natural dye sources, the chemistry of dye fixation, and practical dyeing techniques for fibers and finished cloth.

Why this matters:
- **Value:** Colored cloth is significantly more valuable than plain cloth
- **Function:** Colors can mark ownership, status, or use
- **Beauty:** Color improves quality of life and aesthetic satisfaction
- **Durability:** Properly fixed dyes don't wash out; poorly fixed dyes fade quickly
- **Availability:** Natural dyes come from plants grown locally; no external dependency

## Prerequisites

- Access to fiber or finished cloth to dye
- Knowledge of plant identification (to harvest dye plants)
- Basic chemistry understanding (mordants, pH, fastness)
- Large containers suitable for heating dye liquids
- Heat source (fire, sun, or stove)
- Patience: dyeing takes time; colors develop gradually

## Materials & Tools

### Dye Sources

**Yellow dyes:**
- Weld plant (entire plant; produces bright yellow)
- Goldenrod (flowers)
- St. John's Wort (plant)
- Turmeric (root; if available)
- Chamomile (flowers)
- Marigold (flowers)
- Tansy (flowers)

**Red and pink dyes:**
- Madder root (must be aged 2+ years; strongest red)
- Cochineal insects (if available; produces bright red)
- Redwoods/logwood (heartwood; produces red)
- Safflower petals (produces pink to red)
- Bedstraw root (produces pink to red)

**Blue dyes:**
- Woad plant (leaves; primary blue source; fermented indigo)
- Indigo plants (if available; strongest blue)
- Madder with iron modifier (produces blue-purple from red dye)

**Green dyes:**
- Produced by overdyeing yellow dye with blue dye
- Weld (yellow) + woad (blue) = green
- Iron-modified weld = olive green

**Brown and black dyes:**
- Walnut hulls (dark brown to black)
- Tannin-rich plants (oak galls, oak bark; combined with iron = black)
- Tea or coffee (tan to brown)

**Purple dyes:**
- Madder (red) + woad (blue) = purple
- Lichen (orchil; produces purple directly)

### Mordants (Fixatives)

Mordants allow dye molecules to bind permanently to fiber.

**Common mordants:**
- **Alum:** Most common, available from alum ore; creates bright colors
- **Iron (ferrous sulfate):** Creates darker, duller colors; modifies other dyes
- **Tannin:** From oak galls, tea, or other sources; necessary for some dyes
- **Salt (sodium chloride):** Aids dye fixation; sometimes used with other mordants
- **Vinegar (acetic acid):** Acidifies dye bath; helps fixation

**Obtaining mordants:**
- **Alum:** If ore is available locally, can be processed; often must be traded or salvaged
- **Iron:** From iron-rich soil, rust, or iron-containing minerals
- **Tannin:** Extract from oak galls by soaking in water
- **Salt:** From salt deposits, salt water evaporation, or trade
- **Vinegar:** Produce by fermentation of alcoholic liquids

### Tools

**Essential:**
- Large pot (suitable for heating water)
- Wooden stirring stick
- Measuring containers
- Strainers or cloths for filtering dye liquids
- Containers for storing dyes

**Useful:**
- Thermometer (for monitoring temperature)
- pH testing (can use vinegar/soil indicators)
- Weights and measures for consistent results

## Part 1: Understanding Dye Chemistry

### How Dyes Work

Dyes are organic molecules that:
1. Absorb certain colors of light (and reflect others)
2. Have the ability to chemically bond to fiber proteins (in animal fibers) or cellulose (in plant fibers)

Without a mordant, dyes sit on the fiber surface and wash out easily. With a mordant, dyes form stable chemical bonds.

### Fiber Receptivity

Different fibers take dye differently:

**Animal fibers (wool, silk, hair):**
- Naturally acidic; dyes bond readily
- Require less mordant than plant fibers
- Produce more vivid colors
- Preferred for fine dyeing

**Plant fibers (cotton, linen, plant fiber yarn):**
- Neutral pH; dyes don't bond as easily
- Require more mordant and more aggressive dyeing
- Colors are often duller than on animal fibers
- Require tannin pre-treatment for best results

### Mordanting Process

Mordanting prepares the fiber for dye uptake.

**Basic principle:**
1. The mordant chemically bonds to the fiber
2. The dye then bonds to the mordant
3. This creates a stable, permanent color

**Common mordant sequence:**
1. Wet the fiber
2. Apply mordant solution
3. Heat gently and soak (duration varies by mordant)
4. Remove fiber and cool
5. Dye the mordanted fiber (the mordant helps dye bond)

## Part 2: Preparing Dye Liquids

### Extracting Dye from Plants

**Method 1: Boiling (fastest)**

1. Chop dye plant material finely
2. Place in pot with water (ratio: 1 part plant to 8-10 parts water)
3. Boil for 1-3 hours depending on plant
4. Color of liquid indicates dye strength
   - Pale: weak dye; may need longer boiling or more plant material
   - Medium: usable dye
   - Dark: strong dye; may be concentrated
5. Strain through cloth to remove solid material
6. Save liquid; discard solids

**Results:**
- Yellow dyes (weld, goldenrod): bright yellow liquid
- Red dyes (madder, bedstraw): orange to brown liquid (color improves with mordanting)
- Blue dyes (woad, indigo): blue-green liquid

**Method 2: Fermentation (for woad; produces indigo)**

Woad leaves contain a precursor to indigo. Fermentation converts it:

1. Crush fresh woad leaves
2. Mix with water and some straw or other organic material
3. Cover loosely; allow to ferment in warm place for 1-2 weeks
4. The liquid becomes increasingly blue
5. Strain and use; or further processing to concentrate indigo

**Results:**
- Produces true blue color (indigo) from woad leaves
- More labor-intensive but produces stronger blue

**Method 3: Aging (for madder)**

Young madder root (less than 2 years old) produces weak red. Aged madder (2+ years) produces strong red.

1. Harvest madder roots
2. Dry completely
3. Store in cool, dry place for 2+ years
4. The dye compounds develop over time
5. Then extract using the boiling method

### Dye Liquid Storage

Dye liquids can be stored for later use:

- **Fresh:** Use within days; color is optimal
- **Dried:** Allow dye liquid to evaporate; redissolve in hot water when needed (stores for months to years)
- **Frozen:** If freezing is possible; freezing doesn't significantly degrade dye
- **Fermented:** Some dyes improve with storage (kept in sealed containers)

## Part 3: Mordanting Fibers

### Pre-mordanting (Before Dyeing)

**Basic alum mordant process (for animal fibers):**

1. Prepare fiber: wet thoroughly in clean water
2. Dissolve alum in hot water (ratio: 1 part alum to 20-30 parts water)
3. Add fiber to alum solution
4. Heat gently to approximately 65-80°C (warm but not boiling)
5. Maintain temperature for 30-60 minutes (longer for thicker fiber)
6. Cool slowly
7. Remove fiber and rinse lightly
8. Fiber is now mordanted; ready for dye

**Tannin pre-treatment (for plant fibers):**

1. Extract tannin: boil oak galls or oak bark; save the liquid
2. Add plant fiber to tannin solution
3. Heat gently; maintain temperature for 30-60 minutes
4. Cool
5. Now apply alum mordant (follow alum process above)
6. The combination of tannin + alum creates strong dye fixation

### Multiple Mordants

Some colors require multiple mordants in sequence:

1. Tannin mordant (if using plant fiber)
2. Alum mordant (primary mordant)
3. Optional iron mordant (adds depth, darkens color)

**Sequence:**
1. Tannin soak (plant fiber only)
2. Alum soak
3. Cool and rest (overnight or longer)
4. Iron soak (optional)
5. Cool thoroughly
6. Now apply dye

## Part 4: Dyeing Processes

### Basic Dyeing Method

**Equipment:**
- Pot (suitable for simmering, not boiling)
- Dye liquid (prepared from plants)
- Mordanted fiber or cloth

**Process:**

1. Prepare dye bath: add dye liquid to pot of water
   - Ratio: 1-2 parts dye liquid to 4-6 parts water
   - Adjust concentration for desired color strength
2. Warm slowly (do not boil; heat damages some dyes)
3. Add mordanted fiber
4. Maintain gentle heat (approximately 60-80°C) for 30-60 minutes
5. Stir occasionally to ensure even color uptake
6. Remove fiber and cool
7. Rinse gently in cool water
8. Dry fiber

**Color development:**
- Color appears immediately in the dye bath
- Actual color on fiber becomes apparent after drying
- Dried color is often more saturated than wet color

### Dyeing Finished Cloth

Dyeing finished cloth (after weaving) is similar to dyeing fiber:

1. Wet the cloth thoroughly
2. Add to warm dye bath
3. Heat gently; maintain temperature
4. Stir more frequently than fiber (to ensure even dyeing)
5. For uniform color: maintain constant gentle heat for 1+ hours
6. Cool and rinse
7. Dry: can be dried flat or hung

**Advantages of dyeing cloth:**
- Easier to see final color before completion
- Can dye already-finished items
- Good for over-dyeing (dyeing cloth a second or third color)

### Exhaust Dyeing (Using Dye Multiple Times)

When a fiber is removed from a dye bath, some dye remains. This can be used again:

1. First dyeing: use at full strength; remove fiber
2. Add more fiber to the same dye bath: it will dye a lighter shade
3. Repeat: with multiple rounds of dyeing, progressively lighter shades are possible

**Advantage:** Makes use of all available dye

**Example:**
- Deep red: first dyeing (full strength madder)
- Medium red: second dyeing (same dye bath)
- Light pink: third dyeing (same dye bath)

## Part 5: Creating Specific Colors

### Yellow

**Dye source:** Weld plant

**Process:**
1. Boil weld plant material (whole plant best; flowers second)
2. Strain dye liquid
3. Mordant fiber with alum
4. Add fiber to warm dye bath
5. Heat gently for 30-60 minutes
6. Cool and dry

**Color:** Bright, clear yellow; excellent color fastness

**Variations:**
- Goldenrod: slightly orange-yellow
- Weld + iron: green-yellow or olive
- Overdyed with blue: creates green

### Red

**Dye source:** Madder root (aged 2+ years)

**Process:**
1. Boil madder root in water (can use the same root multiple times for progressively weaker color)
2. Strain dye liquid
3. Mordant fiber with alum
4. Add fiber to warm dye bath (65-80°C; avoid boiling)
5. Maintain temperature for 60+ minutes (longer = deeper red)
6. Cool slowly
7. Rinse and dry

**Color:** Orange-red to deep red depending on concentration and duration

**Variations:**
- Madder + iron: purple-red to maroon
- Bedstraw: pink to red (weaker than madder)

### Blue (True Blue from Woad)

Woad produces true blue (indigo) through fermentation:

**Process:**

1. Harvest fresh woad leaves
2. Ferment leaves with straw (2-4 weeks; becomes increasingly blue)
3. Strain liquid; prepare for dyeing (or concentrate further)
4. Mordant fiber (alum only; no tannin needed)
5. Add fiber to woad dye bath (blue-green color)
6. Heat gently; maintain for 30-60 minutes
7. Remove and expose to air (oxidation turns fiber blue)
8. Repeat dipping and airing if deeper blue desired
9. Rinse and dry

**Color:** True blue; excellent color fastness; improves with wear

### Green (From Yellow + Blue)

Green is created by dyeing with yellow first, then blue (or vice versa):

**Process:**
1. Dye fiber yellow (with weld or goldenrod)
2. After drying, dye the same fiber blue (with woad)
3. Drying: fiber turns green
4. The exact shade depends on yellow:blue ratio

**Alternative:**
- Dye with weld, then modify with iron: produces olive green

### Purple (From Red + Blue)

Purple can be created by combining madder (red) with woad (blue):

**Method 1: Two-bath method**
1. Dye fiber red (with madder)
2. Then dye the same fiber blue (with woad)
3. Result: purple (shade depends on proportions)

**Method 2: Single-bath method**
1. Combine madder dye liquid with woad dye liquid
2. Dye fiber in the combined bath
3. Heat and cool
4. Result: purple in a single bath (less control)

### Brown and Black

**Brown:** Walnut hulls produce rich brown

1. Boil walnut hulls (outer covering of walnuts)
2. Strain to produce dark brown liquid
3. Mordant fiber with alum
4. Dye in walnut bath
5. Result: brown (shade varies by concentration)

**Black:** Requires iron and tannin

1. Dye fiber red (with madder)
2. Add iron mordant (ferrous sulfate solution)
3. The iron converts red to dark purple or black
4. Repeat if necessary for deeper black

Alternative: Heavy tannin + iron creates black without red base

## Part 6: Color Fastness and Durability

Fastness is the ability of a dye to resist fading. Not all dyes are equally fast.

### Fastness Ratings (Conceptual)

**Excellent fastness:** Colors that don't fade with repeated washing, sunlight exposure, or use
- Indigo (blue, woad)
- Madder (red)
- Weld (yellow)

**Good fastness:** Colors that fade somewhat but remain recognizable after years
- Cochineal (red; if available)
- Logwood (red; if available)
- Tannin + iron (brown, black)

**Poor fastness:** Colors that fade quickly
- Some yellow dyes (goldenrod, chamomile) without proper mordanting
- Some reds without adequate mordanting

### Improving Fastness

1. **Use proper mordant:** Alum + tannin produces better fastness than alum alone
2. **Adequate dyeing time:** Longer heating and soaking improve color saturation and fastness
3. **Quality dye source:** Aged madder produces better color than fresh madder
4. **Iron modifier:** Iron added after dyeing darkens color and often improves fastness
5. **Afterbath:** Brief soak in iron or tannin solution after dyeing can improve fastness

### Testing Fastness

To evaluate whether a color will hold:

1. Dye a small sample
2. Wash in warm soapy water (mimics actual use)
3. Compare color before and after washing
4. If color remains essentially unchanged: fastness is good
5. If color fades: more mordant or longer dyeing time needed in future

## Part 7: Practical Applications and Examples

### Example 1: Dyeing Wool Yarn Yellow for Trading Cloth

**Scenario:** You have spun wool yarn and want to create yellow yarn for trade.

**Process:**
1. Extract dye: boil weld plant (whole plants if available)
2. Mordant: soak yarn in alum solution (warm, 30 minutes)
3. Cool: allow to cool completely
4. Dye: add yarn to warm weld dye bath (60-80°C, 60 minutes)
5. Cool and dry

**Result:** Bright yellow yarn; excellent color fastness

**Timeline:** 1 day (not counting drying time)

### Example 2: Creating Red Cloth from Madder Root

**Scenario:** You have aged madder root and want to dye finished cloth red.

**Process:**
1. Extract dye: boil madder root 1-2 hours; strain
2. Mordant cloth: alum bath (warm, 30 minutes)
3. Cool
4. Dye: add cloth to warm madder dye bath (70-80°C, 60+ minutes)
5. For deeper red: maintain gentle heat longer
6. Cool slowly
7. Dry flat or hanging

**Result:** Red cloth suitable for special garments or trade

**Timeline:** 1-2 days

### Example 3: Creating Blue Woad Dye and Overdyeing for Green Cloth

**Scenario:** You want to create green cloth using available plants.

**Process:**
1. Extract yellow: boil goldenrod; strain
2. Extract blue: ferment woad leaves (2-4 weeks); strain
3. Dye cloth yellow: alum-mordanted cloth in goldenrod bath
4. Dry
5. Dye same cloth blue: add to woad bath; heat gently; remove and air-expose
6. Result: cloth changes from yellow to blue to green as it oxidizes
7. Dry

**Result:** Green cloth with excellent color fastness

**Timeline:** Several weeks (due to woad fermentation)

## Part 8: Troubleshooting Dye Problems

### Problem: Color is too light or pale

**Causes:** Weak dye, insufficient mordant, insufficient dyeing time

**Solutions:**
- Use stronger dye concentration
- Dye again in fresh dye bath
- Increase mordant in future
- Dye for longer time (heat to higher temperature, if dye permits)

### Problem: Color fades after washing

**Causes:** Inadequate mordanting, weak dye-fiber bond, poor color fastness

**Solutions:**
- Use alum + tannin mordanting for future dyes
- Add iron afterbath to fix color better
- Choose dyes with better natural fastness (madder, indigo, weld)

### Problem: Color is uneven or blotchy

**Causes:** Uneven mordanting, uneven heating in dye bath, fiber not fully wet before dyeing

**Solutions:**
- Wet fiber thoroughly before mordanting
- Wet fiber thoroughly before dyeing
- Stir frequently during dyeing
- Ensure even heat distribution

### Problem: Dye bath is the right color but fiber isn't taking it

**Causes:** Fiber not properly mordanted, fiber not hot enough, incompatible dye and fiber type

**Solutions:**
- Ensure proper mordanting before dyeing (alum at least)
- Heat dye bath to appropriate temperature (usually 60-80°C)
- Plant fibers need stronger mordanting than animal fibers

### Problem: Too much dye needed; not economical

**Causes:** Weak dye source, using too much water, trying to dye too much fiber

**Solutions:**
- Use higher concentration of plant material when extracting dye
- Reduce volume of dye bath (more concentrated color)
- Dye smaller quantities of fiber
- Multiple dyeing sessions (exhaust dyeing) with same dye liquid

## Common Mistakes

1. **Boiling dye bath:** High heat damages some dyes. Solution: Keep dye bath warm (60-80°C) but not boiling.

2. **Skipping mordanting:** Fiber takes dye weakly; color fades immediately. Solution: Always mordant before dyeing.

3. **Using young madder root:** Produces weak red. Solution: Age madder root for 2+ years before use.

4. **Adding dyed fiber to cold dye bath:** Color doesn't develop properly. Solution: Add fiber to warm dye bath and heat slowly.

5. **Not rinsing fiber adequately:** Excess dye stains other fibers or washes out later. Solution: Rinse in cool water until water is clear.

6. **Overloading dye bath:** Too much fiber in not enough dye liquid; resulting color is weak. Solution: Use adequate dye liquid relative to fiber (approximately 1 part dye liquid to 4-6 parts water).

7. **Incorrect iron addition:** Iron added at wrong point can damage dye color. Solution: Add iron as final afterbath, not in main dye bath.

8. **Not considering fiber type:** Plant fibers require much heavier mordanting than animal fibers. Solution: Use tannin + alum for plant fibers; alum alone for animal fibers.

9. **Inconsistent temperatures:** Fiber in parts of dye bath at different temperatures dyes unevenly. Solution: Stir frequently; maintain even heat.

10. **Assuming all plants with color contain useful dyes:** Not all colored plants produce stable dyes. Solution: Test dye source on small sample first.
