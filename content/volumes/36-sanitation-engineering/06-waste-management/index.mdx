## Overview

Post-collapse communities will generate waste: food scraps, worn-out tools, broken pottery, ash, and industrial byproducts. You must handle this waste without trucks, dumps, or sewage treatment. Your options are composting (organic), burial (inert), and isolation (hazardous).

Hazardous waste—batteries, heavy metals, chemical residues, certain minerals—poisons soil and water for centuries. Your job is to identify it, isolate it from water sources and agriculture, and prevent contamination.

## Materials & Tools

### For Composting

- Compost bin or pile area
- Carbon material (see Vol 10, Ch 6)
- Turning tools (fork, shovel)

### For Landfill/Burial

- Shovel and digging tools
- Location with good drainage and distance from water
- Stones or clay for capping

### For Hazardous Waste Isolation

- Containers (pottery, wood, or stone boxes)
- Heavy clay or stone to seal
- Isolated burial location away from water and agriculture
- Documentation (recording location for future generations)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Classifying Waste

**Step 1: Separate waste into three categories.**

**Category 1: Organic (Compostable)**
- Food scraps (except meat and fish, see step 2)
- Yard waste (leaves, grass, wood chips)
- Paper and cardboard (not bleached or heavily inked)
- Cloth (natural fiber: linen, wool, cotton)
- Straw, hay, sawdust

These decompose in weeks to months and produce soil amendment.

**Category 2: Inert (Landfillable)**
- Broken pottery and ceramic
- Glass
- Stone
- Uncontaminated soil
- Ash from wood fires (not coal or chemical ash)
- Bone (if not from infectious disease)

These do not decompose but also do not poison soil. They can be buried indefinitely.

**Category 3: Hazardous (Must be isolated)**
- Heavy metals: Lead, mercury, cadmium (from batteries, old paints, solder)
- Chemicals: Pesticides, herbicides, industrial solvents
- Asbestos (from old insulation or roofing)
- Coal ash or coal slag (contains heavy metals)
- Radioactive materials (extremely rare but possible)
- Contaminated soil (from industrial sites, mining)
- Medical waste (blood, infectious material)

These must be isolated from water and agriculture.

**Step 2: Special cases.**

**Meat and fish:** Do not compost raw meat or fish (attracts rats and predators, smells foul). Instead:
- Bury deep (30+ cm) away from water sources
- Or burn completely (cremate) and bury the ash
- Or preserve (dried, salted) for use

**Wood ash:** 
- Small quantities from wood fires: safe to compost or use on gardens
- Ash from treated wood (painted, stained, varnished): Hazardous (contains heavy metals) — isolate
- Coal ash or ash from synthetic materials: Hazardous — isolate

**Human waste:** See Vol 5, Ch 3 (Composting Toilets) and Vol 5, Ch 1 (Latrines).

### Part 2: Composting Organic Waste

**Step 1: Build or use a compost area.**

See Vol 10, Ch 6 for detailed composting. Key points:
- 1–2 meters from living areas
- Good drainage
- Covered to keep out rain and pests

**Step 2: Add waste in layers.**

Alternate layers of brown (carbon-rich) and green (nitrogen-rich) material:
- Brown: Leaves, sawdust, straw, shredded paper
- Green: Food scraps, grass clippings, manure

Ratio: 3–4 parts brown to 1 part green.

**Step 3: Monitor decomposition.**

- Compost pile heats up (>40°C) within days if ratio is correct
- Turns dark and crumbly within 3–6 months
- Ready for use on gardens after 6–12 months

**Step 4: Use finished compost.**

Spread on gardens, fields, or pasture. The organic matter improves soil structure and adds nutrients.

### Part 3: Landfill Management (Inert Waste)

**Step 1: Choose a landfill location.**

Requirements:
- At least 10 meters from any water source (well, stream, spring)
- On sloping land (allows drainage away from structures)
- Downhill from habitation and water sources
- Not in a flood zone

**Step 2: Excavate the landfill pit.**

Dig a pit 2–3 meters deep and 2–5 meters across (size depends on population and waste generation).

Excavate into stable soil (not clay with poor drainage or sandy soil that collapses). If the site has poor soil, line it with clay or stone.

**Step 3: Deposit waste in layers.**

- Coarse material (pottery shards, stones) on bottom (allows drainage)
- Ash and soil in middle
- Fine material on top

Keep a record: what waste, when, and how much.

**Step 4: Cap and close the landfill.**

When the pit is 1 meter from the top:
1. Cover with 30 cm of soil
2. Compact the soil (walk on it or tamp down)
3. Lay clay or stone on top for a water-resistant cap
4. Plant trees or grass on the cap to prevent erosion

Capped landfills settle over time (5–10 years) as material compacts. This is normal.

**Step 5: Know the lifespan.**

A properly capped inert landfill remains stable for centuries. Glass, pottery, and stone do not decay. The landfill becomes stable ground (you can build on it or plant on it after 10+ years).

### Part 4: Hazardous Waste Isolation

**Step 1: Identify hazardous waste.**

Before disposing, verify the material is hazardous:

**Lead sources:**
- Old paint (test by scraping paint into a container and soaking in vinegar; lead turns the vinegar blue)
- Solder from metal work
- Batteries (lead-acid)
- Some ceramic glazes

**Mercury:**
- Old thermometers (glass bulbs containing liquid mercury)
- Some old electrical switches

**Asbestos:**
- Old pipe insulation (white, friable)
- Roof shingles or tiles (older buildings)
- Brake pads (old vehicles)

**Pesticides and chemicals:**
- Any labeled container (often illegible after years, but assume hazardous if it came from industrial or agricultural use)

**Step 2: Contain hazardous material.**

Place hazardous items in a container:
- Sealed pottery jar, wooden box, or metal can
- Line the container with clay to prevent leaching
- Seal the container tightly

Keep hazardous material:
- Away from living areas
- Away from water sources
- In cool, dry storage if possible

**Step 3: Create an isolation burial site.**

Choose a location:
- At least 50 meters from any water source (stricter than inert landfill)
- Below the water table (water flows away from hazardous material)
- Not in a flood zone
- Downhill from any settlement

**Step 4: Bury hazardous waste.**

1. Dig a pit 1–2 meters deep
2. Place the sealed container in the pit
3. Cover with soil and clay (30 cm minimum)
4. Mark the location clearly (stake, rock pile, or signs warning "HAZARD — DO NOT DIG")
5. Record the location in writing (for future generations)

**Step 5: Document for the future.**

Write down:
- **Type of waste:** "Lead paint from old house, buried 2026"
- **Location:** "North side of settlement, 50 meters from the well, under the large oak tree"
- **Quantity:** "15 kg of paint chips"
- **Isolation method:** "Sealed in pottery jar, buried 1.5 meters deep"
- **Risk:** "Do not disturb. Leaching will poison the well."

Keep this record in a community archive (see Vol 20, Ch 8). Future generations must know what is buried and why.

**Step 6: Manage long-term hazard.**

Hazardous materials degrade on timescales of centuries to millennia:
- Lead: Stable for 1000+ years
- Mercury: Persists for centuries
- Asbestos: Essentially permanent
- Pesticides: Degrade over decades to centuries

Your responsibility is to ensure no one digs up or ingests these materials. Mark burial sites and maintain records.

## Verification

**Compost is working if:**
- Material decomposes (becomes unrecognizable)
- Temperature is >40°C (if measured)
- Finished product is dark, crumbly, soil-like

**Landfill is working if:**
- Material stays buried (no leaching, no animals digging it up)
- Drainage is away from water sources
- Cap prevents water infiltration
- No odor after 1–2 years

**Hazardous waste isolation is working if:**
- Container remains sealed (no rust, no leakage)
- Surrounding soil is stable (no collapse, no erosion)
- Water sources remain uncontaminated
- Records are maintained and accessible

## Common Mistakes

- **Burying hazardous waste near water sources.** Leaching contaminates groundwater. 50+ meters distance is essential.
- **Not sealing hazardous containers.** Unsealed waste leaches into soil. Use clay liners and tight seals.
- **Composting non-organic waste.** Pottery, glass, or metal in compost damages tools and soil. Separate carefully.
- **Landfill without capping.** Uncapped landfills allow water infiltration, releasing contaminants. Cap after 80% full.
- **Burning treated wood or painted materials.** Burning releases heavy metals into the air (inhalation hazard) and ash (contaminated). Bury instead.
- **Forgetting to record hazardous waste locations.** Future generations will dig and be poisoned. Document everything.
- **Confusing old ash with hazardous ash.** Wood ash is safe; coal ash is not. Know your fuel source.
- **Building on unsettled landfills.** Unsettled waste shifts and settles, causing structures to crack. Wait 10+ years before building.
