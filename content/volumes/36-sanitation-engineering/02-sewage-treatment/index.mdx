## Overview

A single latrine works for 5–20 people. Beyond that, you need a sewage treatment system. Sewage is the wastewater from toilets, baths, and food preparation combined. It contains bacteria, viruses, parasites, and organic matter.

Sewage treatment uses biology (bacteria and fungi) and filtration to:
1. Remove most of the organic matter (decomposition)
2. Kill most pathogens (time, temperature, predation)
3. Remove remaining solids (settling, filtration)
4. Produce water safe enough for reuse (irrigation) or return to environment

Two main systems work without central power: anaerobic baffled reactors (for small settlements) and constructed wetlands (for larger populations or longer processing).

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of latrines and waste containment (Vol 5, Ch 1)
- Basic knowledge of decomposition and carbon-nitrogen ratios (Vol 10, Ch 6)
- Population size of at least 20–30 people (smaller groups use latrines)

## Materials & Tools

### For an Anaerobic Baffled Reactor (ABR)

- **Excavation tools:** Shovel, pick, surveying instruments
- **Brick or stone:** For lining chambers (500–1000 bricks typical)
- **Mortar materials:** Clay or cement for sealing joints
- **Concrete:** For the floor and dividing baffles (500–1000 kg)
- **Pipes:** 50–100 mm diameter for inlet/outlet and gas venting
- **Cover materials:** Wooden or concrete slabs, soil

### For a Constructed Wetland

- **Excavation tools:** Shovel, pick, wheelbarrow
- **Plastic sheeting:** Heavy-duty HDPE (0.5–1 mm thick) to line the bed
- **Gravel:** Coarse sand and small gravel for filtration (several cubic meters)
- **Wetland plants:** Cattails, reeds, papyrus, or water-loving vegetation
- **Pipes:** For inlet/outlet (50–100 mm diameter)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Anaerobic Baffled Reactor (ABR) Design

An ABR treats sewage in stages. Sewage enters the first chamber, where bacteria break down organic matter anaerobically (without oxygen). Partially treated water flows into a second chamber, then a third. Each chamber has less bacterial activity and more settling. The final chamber releases cleaner water.

**Step 1: Calculate chamber size.**

The size depends on population and flow rate.

**Rule of thumb:**
- Each person produces approximately 40–60 liters of wastewater per day
- Total daily flow = population × 50 liters/person/day
- Residence time (how long waste stays in the reactor) = 10–20 days (longer is better)
- Total volume needed = daily flow × residence time

**Example:** 50 people × 50 liters/day × 15 days residence time = 37,500 liters = 37.5 cubic meters

Divide this volume into 3–4 chambers of roughly equal size.

**Step 2: Design the reactor layout.**

An ABR has three or four chambers in series:

**Chamber 1 (Inlet chamber):** Where raw sewage enters. Most solids settle. Bacteria begin to work.
- Volume: 1/3 of total volume
- Inlet submerged (pipe enters below water level)

**Chambers 2 & 3 (Middle chambers):** Partial treatment continues. Solids continue to settle.
- Volume: 1/3 of total volume each

**Chamber 4 (Outlet chamber, optional):** Final polishing. Water exits to either another treatment stage (wetland) or filtration.
- Volume: 1/3 of total volume
- Outlet: Slightly elevated above the bottom (to leave sediment behind)

**Baffles connecting chambers:**
- The baffle between chambers 1 and 2 reaches from near the bottom to near the top, forcing sewage to flow under (anaerobic zone) and around (aerobic zone near the surface)
- This creates mixing and multiple reaction zones

**Step 3: Excavate the chambers.**

Dig a long rectangular pit divided into sections:

**Pit dimensions (for 50 people × 37.5 m³):**
- Total length: 10 meters
- Width: 1.5 meters
- Depth: 2.5 meters
- Divide into four 2.5 m × 1.5 m × 2.5 m sections

**Excavation method:**
1. Mark the pit outline on the ground with rope or chalk
2. Remove topsoil
3. Dig the main pit to depth (2.5 meters), keeping sides as vertical as possible
4. Excavate any sediment or loose material that might collapse

**Step 4: Line the chambers.**

Lining prevents groundwater from entering and treated water from leaking out.

**Brick lining method:**
1. Lay a foundation of stones or brick on the pit bottom
2. Build brick walls (single layer, tightly joints with mortar) on all sides
3. Ensure bricks are laid level and plumb
4. Seal all joints with clay or cement mortar
5. Allow mortar to cure for 7 days before filling with water

**Concrete lining method (more durable but more labor):**
1. Mix concrete and pour a 10 cm floor layer on the pit bottom
2. Build wooden or stone forms for walls
3. Pour concrete walls 10–15 cm thick
4. Cure for 14 days before filling

**Step 5: Install baffles between chambers.**

Baffles divide the reactor and control flow:

**Baffle design:**
- **Height:** Extends from 0.3 meters above the bottom of the chamber to 0.3 meters below the water surface (so sewage can flow under and around the baffle)
- **Material:** Brick, stone, or concrete wall
- **Tightness:** Seal all joints with mortar to prevent bypass

**Installation:**
1. Build the baffle in place as you construct the chamber lining
2. Ensure it is vertical and stable
3. Leave small gaps (5 cm) at the top if desired (allows some gas exchange between chambers)

**Step 6: Install inlet and outlet pipes.**

**Inlet (from settlement or collection point):**
- Enters chamber 1
- Pipe diameter: 50–100 mm (depending on flow)
- **Submerged inlet:** The pipe extends below the water surface (prevents bypass of solids to the outlet)
- Position: Near the bottom, to allow mixing with existing sewage

**Outlet (to constructed wetland or final discharge):**
- Exits from chamber 4 (or chamber 3 if no chamber 4)
- Pipe diameter: 50–100 mm
- **Elevated outlet:** Positioned to leave at least 0.3 meters of settled material at the bottom
- Can be controlled with a valve (to manage water level) or an overflow pipe (to prevent backup)

**Step 7: Install a gas vent.**

Anaerobic decomposition produces biogas (methane, hydrogen sulfide). Venting prevents pressure buildup:

**Vent design:**
- Pipe diameter: 25–50 mm
- Material: PVC, bamboo, or metal
- Extends above the roof of the structure to 1–2 meters (so gas disperses)
- Can be left open (biogas escapes) or captured with a simple one-way valve (to prevent water from backing up into the vent)

**Gas safety note:** Biogas is flammable and potentially explosive in enclosed spaces. Ensure adequate ventilation and never install a lit flame or spark source near the reactor.

**Step 8: Cover the reactor.**

The reactor should be covered to:
- Prevent rain from diluting the sewage
- Keep insects out
- Reduce odor
- Protect from direct sunlight

**Cover options:**
- **Wooden structure:** Simple roof over the reactor, leaving access points for maintenance
- **Concrete slab:** Pour concrete over the reactor, leaving access holes for inspection and pumping
- **Buried:** Cover with plastic sheeting, then soil mounded over the top

Leave at least two access points (covers or lids) on the top:
- One for the inlet area (chamber 1)
- One or more for maintenance/inspection

**Step 9: Establish monitoring and maintenance schedule.**

Monitor the reactor:
- **Daily:** Check that inlet water is flowing and outlet water is flowing (if no flow, there may be a blockage)
- **Weekly:** Smell the outlet water (it should smell earthy, not strongly fecal; strong smell indicates treatment is failing)
- **Monthly:** Remove accumulated scum (floating material) from the top of chamber 1 with a scraper or bucket
- **Yearly:** Remove accumulated sludge (settled solids) from the bottom of chambers

**Sludge removal procedure:**
1. The sludge builds up over months to years
2. When sludge reaches 0.5 meters depth in any chamber, it is time to remove it
3. Use a pump or bucket to remove sludge and place it in a compost pile to cure
4. Sludge can be composted (see Vol 10, Ch 6) or used as a soil amendment after pathogen destruction (1–2 years of storage)

### Part 2: Constructed Wetland (Secondary or Tertiary Treatment)

A constructed wetland is a shallow, planted basin where sewage flows slowly through gravel and roots. Plants and soil microbes remove remaining pathogens and nutrients. Constructed wetlands work best following an ABR or as a secondary treatment for clarified sewage.

**Step 1: Design the wetland.**

**Wetland sizing:**
- Area needed ≈ 1–2 square meters per person
- Example: 50 people require 50–100 square meters of wetland
- Typical dimensions: 20 m × 3 m, or 10 m × 5 m, etc.

**Depth:**
- 0.5–1 meter deep (shallow enough to plant vegetation, deep enough for treatment)

**Slope:**
- Slight slope from inlet to outlet (minimum 0.5%, roughly 5 cm drop over 10 meters)
- Allows gravity flow without pumping

**Step 2: Excavate the wetland basin.**

**Excavation:**
1. Mark out the wetland dimensions with rope or chalk
2. Remove topsoil
3. Dig the basin to uniform depth (0.5–1 meter)
4. Ensure the bottom is level or slopes gently toward the outlet
5. Remove any large rocks or roots

**Step 3: Line the basin with plastic.**

A plastic liner prevents treated water from leaking into groundwater (and prevents groundwater from entering the system):

**Installation:**
1. Place a 5–10 cm layer of sand on the basin bottom (to protect the plastic)
2. Unroll heavy-duty HDPE plastic sheeting across the basin
3. Extend the plastic 0.5 meters up the sides and beyond
4. Overlap sheets by at least 1 meter if more than one piece is needed
5. Trim excess plastic once the basin is filled
6. Place sandbags or soil on top of the plastic edges to hold it in place

**Step 4: Add gravel and sand for filtration.**

The substrate provides surface area for biofilm growth and physical filtration:

**Substrate layers (from bottom to top):**
1. **Sand layer:** 5–10 cm of coarse sand (for plant roots and additional filtration)
2. **Gravel layer:** 20–30 cm of washed gravel (2–4 cm stones, for excellent drainage)
3. **Fine gravel layer:** 10–20 cm of smaller gravel (pea-sized, 0.5–1 cm stones)
4. **Top layer:** 5–10 cm of soil mixed with sand (for plant growth)

Total substrate depth: 40–60 cm

**Step 5: Plant the wetland.**

Select wetland plants that thrive in shallow, wet conditions:

**Good plant choices:**
- **Cattails:** Typha latifolia (most effective, absorbs nutrients)
- **Reeds:** Phragmites australis (tolerates poor water quality)
- **Papyrus:** Cyperus papyrus (if climate is warm)
- **Water hyacinth:** Eichhornia crassipes (floating plant, very effective but invasive)
- **Sedges:** Carex species (complement to cattails)

**Planting:**
1. Plant roots into the top soil layer at spacing of 30–50 cm apart
2. Plant densely (not sparse) to maximize plant coverage
3. Water the wetland gently at first, then to design depth over several days
4. Allow plants to establish (2–4 weeks) before introducing sewage

**Step 6: Introduce sewage gradually.**

Do not shock the system:

1. Run raw sewage from the ABR into the wetland inlet
2. Start with low flow (1/4 of design flow) for the first week
3. Increase to half flow for the second week
4. Increase to full design flow by the third week
5. Monitor plant health and outlet water quality as you increase flow

**Step 7: Monitor outlet water quality.**

Test treated water for pathogens and quality:

- **Smell:** Should smell earthy or like plants, not sewage
- **Color:** Should be clear or light brown, not dark brown
- **Floating debris:** Should be minimal
- **Microbial test (optional):** Count bacteria or test for coliforms using Vol 5, Ch 4 methods

**Treated water can be:**
- **Discharged to a stream or pond** (if adequately treated)
- **Used for irrigation** of non-food crops (trees, landscaping, not vegetables)
- **Returned to groundwater** via infiltration (recharge, described below)

## Verification

A working sewage treatment system shows:

- **ABR outlet water:** Clearer than inlet, earthy smell, few solids
- **Wetland growth:** Dense plant growth, no algae overgrowth, no dead sections
- **Outlet water:** Clear or light brown, no floating debris, no fecal smell
- **Downstream water quality:** If the treated water is discharged to a stream, the stream water should pass the coliform test from Vol 5, Ch 4

A failing system shows:

- **Strong smell:** Indicates inadequate treatment or anaerobic conditions
- **Dead plants:** Indicates toxic levels in treated water
- **Algae bloom in outlet:** Indicates high nutrient levels (nitrogen, phosphorus)
- **Backup or overflow:** Indicates blockage or hydraulic overload

## Common Mistakes

- **Oversizing the reactor:** Too much volume means slow treatment and pathogen survival. Size according to population, not guesswork.
- **Undersizing the reactor:** Too small means waste flows through too fast without treatment. Use the 10–20 day residence time rule.
- **Inadequate baffles:** Sewage bypasses the reactor and exits untreated. Install baffles correctly, sealing all joints.
- **Blocking inlet pipe:** Grease, hair, or solids can block the inlet. Install a simple screen upstream (a bucket with cloth) to catch large debris.
- **Not covering the reactor:** Rain dilutes the sewage, reducing treatment efficiency, and insects breed in the system. Always cover.
- **Overloading the wetland:** Too much flow causes water to short-circuit through the wetland without full contact with plants and soil. Stick to sizing rules.
- **Planting too sparsely:** Sparse vegetation is insufficient for nutrient removal. Plant densely.
- **Discharging untreated water:** Tempting to skip the ABR and go straight to the wetland, or skip the wetland and discharge ABR outlet directly. Always use both stages for full treatment.
