## Overview

You cannot see bacteria, but you can detect what they leave behind. You cannot measure pH with a meter, but you can use natural indicators. This chapter teaches field methods to assess water quality: simple tests that require only materials you can make or find.

Water safety is critical to survival. Contaminated water kills faster than hunger. But most contamination is testable without electronics or chemistry labs. By combining multiple simple tests, you can assess whether water is safe enough to drink, cook with, or use for irrigation.

This chapter covers three categories: biological hazard detection (bacteria, parasites), chemical hazard detection (toxic substances, acid-base balance), and physical hazard detection (sediment, color, odor).

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of bacterial growth and disease transmission (Vol 27, Ch 6 — Microbiology)
- Ability to make fire and boil water reliably
- Knowledge of pH and chemical indicators (Vol 28, Ch 3 — Acids and Bases)
- Patience: some tests take 24–48 hours

## Materials & Tools

**For biological testing:**
- Glass jars or bottles (clear, with lids)
- Incubation location (warm, dark place—near fire, under cloth, or buried in soil)
- Methylene blue dye (if available; see preparation below)
- Natural indicator plants (see Chemical Testing section)

**For chemical testing:**
- Natural pH indicators (red cabbage, turmeric, or other plants)
- Soap (to test for hard minerals)
- Salt (for conductivity test)
- Heat source (fire)
- Metal or ceramic vessels

**For physical testing:**
- Cloth or filter (to observe sediment)
- Sieve or strainer
- Simple tester glass (any clear container)

**For advanced testing (optional):**
- Alcohol (for certain reactions)
- Vinegar (acetic acid test)
- Plant materials with antibacterial properties (to compare water's ability to support bacterial growth)

## Field Tests for Biological Contamination

### Test 1: Turbidity and Visible Sediment

The presence of sediment or cloudiness indicates physical contamination and suggests biological contamination is also likely.

**Procedure:**
1. Fill a clear glass jar with water sample to a depth of 10 centimeters.
2. Hold the jar up to light and observe.
3. If you can see fine particles suspended in the water, sediment is present.
4. Look through the bottom of the jar at a light source. If the light is obscured, significant turbidity is present.

**Interpretation:**
- Clear water with no visible particles: low risk of physical contamination; may still have biological hazards
- Cloudy water or visible sediment: definite contamination; requires filtering and boiling before use
- Very dark or discolored water: possible chemical contamination; investigate further (see Color and Odor test below)

**Note:** Clear does not mean safe. Bacteria and viruses are invisible. Always boil water from uncertain sources.

### Test 2: Bacterial Growth Indicator (Methylene Blue Test)

Methylene blue is a dye that changes color in the presence of bacteria. Bacteria consume oxygen and create a reducing environment, which decolorizes the dye. This test indicates whether bacteria are present and viable.

**Preparation of methylene blue solution (if not already available):**
1. Obtain methylene blue dye (from pre-collapse supplies or trade for it).
2. Dissolve a small amount (a few crystals) in 100 milliliters of water to create a pale blue solution.
3. Store in a dark bottle.

**Procedure:**
1. Fill a clean glass jar with the water sample (about 50 milliliters).
2. Add 5 milliliters of methylene blue solution.
3. Seal the jar tightly.
4. Incubate in a warm, dark place (near fire, under cloth, or buried slightly in soil) for 24 hours.
5. Observe the color after 24 hours.

**Interpretation:**
- Color remains blue: few or no bacteria present; water is likely safe if boiled
- Color fades to colorless within 24 hours: many bacteria present; water is contaminated and requires aggressive treatment (boiling, chemical disinfection, or long solar UV exposure)
- Color fades partially: moderate contamination; boiling is recommended

**Notes:**
- This test does not identify specific pathogens; it only indicates bacterial presence
- Spore-forming bacteria (anthrax, botulism) may not affect this test; always boil unknown water sources regardless

### Test 3: Smell Test

Foul or unusual odors indicate chemical contamination or bacterial growth.

**Procedure:**
1. Fill a clean glass with water sample.
2. Smell at the mouth of the glass (do not inhale deeply).
3. Note the smell: fresh (normal), musty (algae or organic decay), sulfurous (hydrogen sulfide—indicator of anaerobic bacteria or mineral deposits), chemical (bleach-like, sweet, sour), or other unusual odors.

**Interpretation:**
- Fresh or no odor: water is likely safe from bacterial standpoint; chemical contamination still possible
- Musty odor: organic decay or algae; indicates stagnant water; boil before use
- Rotten egg (sulfurous) odor: hydrogen sulfide from anaerobic bacteria or mineral sources; water is contaminated or very hard; filter and boil
- Chemical odor: possible chemical contamination; investigate source and use alternative water if possible
- Sweet or fruity odor: possible fermentation or microbial activity; definitely boil

### Test 4: Fecal Indicator Organism Test (Coliforms)

Certain bacteria, especially E. coli, live in feces. Their presence indicates fecal contamination and suggests other disease-causing bacteria may be present. This test uses bacterial growth to detect coliform presence.

**Procedure (Lactose Fermentation Test):**
1. Boil water to sterilize it, then cool completely to room temperature.
2. Prepare a growth medium: dissolve 1 teaspoon of sugar in 100 milliliters of the cooled boiled water (this is your growth medium).
3. Add 10 milliliters of the test water sample to the growth medium.
4. Add a small bubble trap (see Bubble Trap below) to detect gas production.
5. Incubate in a warm, dark place for 24–48 hours.
6. Observe for gas bubbles or cloudiness in the growth medium.

**Bubble Trap Instructions (to detect gas):**
1. Fill a test tube with the growth medium solution.
2. Place an inverted smaller tube (like a shot glass) inside the test tube, trapping an air space.
3. If bacteria ferment lactose, they produce gas that collects in the inverted tube.
4. After 24–48 hours, if gas is present, the bubble trap will show clear gas pocket (foaming or bubbles).

**Interpretation:**
- No gas, no cloudiness: coliforms likely absent; water is probably fecal-contamination free
- Gas present or cloudiness: coliform bacteria present; water is contaminated with fecal matter; boil and disinfect aggressively

**Notes:**
- This test takes time and requires setup, but it is very reliable
- False positives can occur from non-fecal bacteria that also ferment lactose
- Always boil water from untested sources regardless of this test result

## Field Tests for Chemical Contamination

### Test 1: pH Testing with Natural Indicators

Water pH indicates acidity or alkalinity. Acidic water (pH < 7) can leach metals from pipes. Alkaline water (pH > 7) may have mineral content. Natural pH is around 7 (neutral).

**Method 1: Red Cabbage Indicator**

Red cabbage contains anthocyanins, which change color at different pH levels.

**Preparation:**
1. Chop red cabbage finely.
2. Boil in water for 5–10 minutes until water turns deep purple-red.
3. Filter through cloth to remove cabbage pieces.
4. Cool completely. This is your pH indicator.

**Procedure:**
1. Fill a clear glass with the water sample (about 50 milliliters).
2. Add 5–10 milliliters of red cabbage indicator.
3. Observe the color after 2–3 minutes.

**Color Interpretation:**
- Pink/Red color: acidic (pH 3–5)
- Purple/Blue color: neutral to slightly acidic (pH 6–7)
- Blue-green or green color: neutral to slightly alkaline (pH 8–9)
- Yellow-green color: alkaline (pH 10+)

**Interpretation:**
- Neutral (purple-blue): water is balanced; generally safe
- Acidic (pink): water may leach metals; consider adding chalk (calcium carbonate) to neutralize if available
- Very alkaline (yellow-green): water may have high mineral content; test for hardness (see Hardness Test below)

**Method 2: Turmeric Indicator**

Turmeric powder changes color in alkaline conditions.

**Procedure:**
1. Add a small pinch of turmeric powder to the water sample.
2. If water is significantly alkaline, the yellow turmeric turns red-brown within minutes.
3. If no color change occurs, water is neutral to acidic.

### Test 2: Water Hardness Test (Minerals)

Hard water contains dissolved minerals (calcium, magnesium). Hard water is not toxic but reduces soap effectiveness and can cause scale buildup in pipes and vessels.

**Soap Test (Simple):**
1. Fill a clear test tube or jar with water sample (about 100 milliliters).
2. Add 10 drops of liquid soap (if available; otherwise use water from boiled soap lye—see Vol 28, Ch 5: Making Soap).
3. Shake vigorously for 10 seconds.
4. Observe the foam that forms on top.

**Interpretation:**
- Abundant, stable foam (>5 centimeters high, lasting >1 minute): soft water; low mineral content
- Minimal foam or foam disappears quickly: hard water; high mineral content
- Cloud or sediment visible: very hard water; consider filtering or boiling to remove minerals

### Test 3: Conductivity Test (Mineral Content)

Water that conducts electricity contains dissolved salts and minerals. This is a rough indicator of mineral concentration.

**Simple Salt Bridge Test:**
1. Prepare a 9-volt battery (if available) or create one from copper and zinc electrodes (see Vol 37, Ch 2 — Batteries).
2. Insert two metal rods (or copper and zinc if battery unavailable) into the water sample, separated by 5 centimeters.
3. Connect them to the battery terminals.
4. Observe if the water conducts electricity (indicated by faint bubbles at the electrodes or a small LED light if available).

**Interpretation:**
- Water conducts electricity (bubbles form): high mineral content; water is hard or salty
- No conductivity: low mineral content; water is soft and may lack minerals

**Note:** This test requires electrical equipment which is advanced technology.

### Test 4: Taste and Feel Test (Advanced Use Only)

Taste and texture provide clues, but only do this with water you have already boiled and you suspect is contaminated with minerals (not pathogens).

**Procedure (boiled water only):**
1. Take a small sip (1 teaspoon) of boiled water sample.
2. Note the taste: fresh (neutral), salty, bitter, sweet, or metallic.
3. Note the feel: smooth, rough (feels like sediment), or slippery.

**Interpretation:**
- Fresh, neutral taste, smooth feel: water is good quality
- Salty taste: water contains salt; may be from saltwater infiltration or mineral deposits
- Bitter taste: possible presence of tannins, algae, or certain minerals
- Metallic taste: possible iron, copper, or other heavy metal presence; avoid using if possible
- Rough feel: sediment present despite apparent clarity; requires more aggressive filtering

### Test 5: Color Observation

Water color indicates the presence of various substances.

**Procedure:**
1. Fill a clear glass with water sample.
2. Hold it up to light and observe the color.
3. Compare to known-safe water side by side.

**Color Interpretation:**
- Clear/colorless: water is likely free of large particles and obvious contamination
- Yellow/brown: tannins from decaying vegetation; causes bad taste but not toxic if boiled
- Red/orange: iron compounds or clay; not toxic but indicates mineral content
- Green: algae growth; filter out before boiling
- Dark brown/black: heavy organic contamination; definitely requires boiling and filtration
- Gray/cloudy: sediment; requires filtering
- White/opaque: high sediment or mineral content; filter first, then boil

## Comprehensive Water Assessment Protocol

**Use this sequence to assess water safety:**

1. **Visual inspection** (5 minutes)
   - Observe color, clarity, sediment
   - Smell the water

2. **Biological rapid tests** (5–30 minutes)
   - Turbidity test (immediate)
   - Odor test (immediate)
   - Methylene blue test (24 hours if time permits)

3. **Chemical tests** (15–30 minutes)
   - pH test (red cabbage or turmeric)
   - Hardness test (soap test)

4. **Advanced tests** (24–48 hours if safety is critical)
   - Coliform test (fecal contamination)
   - Incubation test (bacterial growth observation)

## Treatment Decisions Based on Test Results

**Water is likely safe if:**
- Clear or light colored
- No foul odor
- pH is neutral (purple-blue with cabbage indicator)
- Soft water (abundant soap foam)
- No gas production in coliform test

**Treatment: Boil for 5–10 minutes at rolling boil. Cool before use.**

**Water is probably contaminated if:**
- Cloudy or discolored
- Foul odor (musty, sulfurous, chemical)
- Acidic (pink with cabbage) or very alkaline (yellow-green)
- Hard water (little soap foam)
- Gas production in coliform test
- Methylene blue decolorizes in 24 hours

**Treatment: Filter through cloth or sand, then boil for 20 minutes at rolling boil. Add chemical disinfectant (bleach, iodine) if available. Store in clean, sealed container. Consider long solar UV exposure (4–6 hours) as supplemental treatment.**

**Water is unsafe and should be abandoned if:**
- Very dark, opaque, or visibly contaminated with industrial refuse
- Severe chemical smell (industrial solvents, pesticides, oil)
- Extremely salty taste (saltwater source)
- Visible petroleum or chemical sheen on surface

**Treatment: Find alternative water source. Do not use for drinking or cooking. Boiling will not remove chemical toxins.**

## Storage of Test Samples

Keep water samples that test positive for contamination for 24–48 hours if possible to confirm results:

1. Store in sealed jars in a cool, dark place
2. Date and label each sample
3. Do not consume water from positive test samples
4. Use positive samples to practice testing methods

## Important Cautions

**Testing is not foolproof.** Some pathogens (certain viruses, Cryptosporidium cysts) may not be detected by these tests. Always boil water from unknown sources as a precaution.

**Coliform test false positives:** Non-fecal bacteria can ferment lactose; the test detects the enzyme but not specifically E. coli. It is an indicator, not a definitive test.

**Chemical tests have limits:** These field tests detect general characteristics but cannot identify specific toxins (arsenic, lead, pesticides). If chemical contamination is suspected, find an alternative water source.

**Always err on the side of caution.** When tests are ambiguous, treat water as contaminated and boil it.
