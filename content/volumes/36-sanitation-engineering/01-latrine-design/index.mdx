## Overview

A latrine is a toilet that separates human waste from the environment. Unlike a flush toilet (which requires water and sewage infrastructure), a latrine works by gravity and decomposition. Waste enters a pit or vault and stays contained. Bacteria and insects decompose the waste. No smell should escape (or minimal smell). Most importantly, the waste must not leak into groundwater.

There are three main types: pit latrines, vault latrines, and VIP (Ventilated Improved Pit) latrines. Your choice depends on soil type, water table depth, population, and climate.

## Prerequisites

- Knowledge of soil type (from Vol 9, Ch 1 or local digging)
- Ability to identify water table depth by digging test holes
- Understanding that shallow groundwater (less than 2 meters) requires special protection

## Materials & Tools

### For a Basic Pit Latrine

- **Digging tools:** Shovel, pick, crowbar
- **Lining materials (optional):** Stone, brick, or concrete rings
- **Covering materials:** Wooden or concrete slab with a hole
- **Superstructure:** Wooden or brick walls (1–2 meters high) to house the toilet seat
- **Roof material:** Thatch, corrugated iron, or wood
- **Ventilation pipe (for VIP system):** 100–150 mm diameter PVC or bamboo

### For a Vault Latrine (Water-Tight Container)

- **Container or sealed pit:** Brick-lined chamber or plastic-lined pit (alternative: concrete vault)
- **Pump or bucket system:** For removing full waste
- **Superstructure:** Same as pit latrine

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Site Selection and Assessment

**Step 1: Measure water table depth.**

Dig a test hole at least 2 meters deep at the proposed latrine location. If water appears before you reach 2 meters depth, you cannot use a pit latrine here — use a vault latrine instead.

A safe pit latrine requires:
- **Minimum distance to water source:** 10–30 meters horizontally (depending on soil type)
- **Minimum water table depth:** 2 meters below the pit bottom (in sandy soil, 3 meters preferred)
- **Soil type:** Not pure sand or gravel (waste will drain too fast and contaminate groundwater)

**Step 2: Evaluate soil type.**

Dig a soil profile pit (1 meter deep) at the latrine location:
- **Clay or silt:** Good (waste decomposes in place, little drainage to groundwater)
- **Sandy loam:** Acceptable (moderate drainage, slower contamination)
- **Pure sand or gravel:** Poor (very fast drainage, high groundwater contamination risk — use vault instead)

**Step 3: Choose location based on constraints.**

- Downslope from any water sources
- At least 10 meters from your main water source (well, spring)
- Not in a flood zone (if annual flooding occurs, the latrine floods)
- Accessible but not immediately visible from living areas (15+ meters is typical)
- On ground that slopes away from the pit (so rain does not pool around it)

### Part 2: Pit Latrine Design (For Favorable Conditions)

Use a pit latrine ONLY if water table is more than 2 meters deep and soil is loamy (clay or silt predominates).

**Step 1: Dig the pit.**

- **Diameter:** 1–2 meters (1.5 meters is typical for a family)
- **Depth:** 1.5–2 meters
- **Shape:** Roughly cylindrical

Digging method:
1. Mark a circle on the ground with chalk or rope
2. Remove the topsoil in rings, working inward and downward
3. Once you reach 1 meter depth, continue digging the cylinder
4. Stop when you reach 1.5–2 meters deep (or when the water table approaches)

**Step 2: Stabilize the pit (optional but recommended).**

If the pit walls are collapsing or sandy, line them with:
- **Stone:** Stack stones without mortar, leaving gaps for water drainage
- **Brick:** Lay bricks in a ring, staggered, without mortar (allows slow drainage but prevents wall collapse)
- **Concrete rings:** Pre-made concrete rings stacked on top of each other

Do NOT seal the pit completely — the bottom must allow slow drainage into deeper soil.

**Step 3: Install the slab and cover.**

The slab is a solid platform covering the pit (except for a hole for the toilet seat).

**Concrete slab method (most durable):**
1. Mix concrete (3 parts sand, 1 part cement, 1/2 part water)
2. Build a frame from wood or stones at ground level, level and slightly wider than the pit
3. Pour concrete 10–15 cm thick onto the frame
4. While wet, cut a hole (25–30 cm diameter) in the center
5. Smooth the edges and allow to cure for 7 days

**Wooden slab method (simpler, less durable):**
1. Cut a wooden plank (wood scraps, salvaged timber, or split logs)
2. Make it slightly wider than the pit diameter
3. Drill a hole 30 cm diameter in the center
4. Place it level on the pit rim (support with stones or soil mounded around the edge)

**Step 4: Install a superstructure (toilet booth).**

Build a simple structure around the slab to house the toilet:

**Simple booth design:**
1. Use wooden posts, bamboo, or salvaged materials
2. Build walls 1.5–2 meters high (tall enough to stand in)
3. Leave one side open as a door
4. Roof with thatch, corrugated iron, or salvaged materials
5. Ensure adequate ventilation (leave gaps in the walls to reduce smell)

**Step 5: Install a toilet seat.**

Options:
- Salvaged porcelain toilet seat fixed to the slab with bolts
- Wooden seat built to fit the hole
- Concrete seat poured on the slab
- Simple log or pipe fitted as a perch

Make it secure and comfortable at standard toilet height (40 cm from the seat top to the ground).

### Part 3: VIP Latrine Design (Improved Smell Control)

A VIP (Ventilated Improved Pit) latrine is a pit latrine with a ventilation pipe. The pipe removes gases and smells, and attracts flies into the pipe instead of the pit.

**Step 1: Dig and prepare the pit.**

Follow the same process as a pit latrine (Steps 1–3 above).

**Step 2: Install the ventilation pipe.**

**Pipe specifications:**
- **Diameter:** 100–150 mm (inner diameter)
- **Material:** PVC, galvanized steel, or bamboo
- **Length:** Extends 0.5–1 meter above the roof of the booth
- **Placement:** Attached to the edge of the slab, outside the booth

**Installation:**
1. Drill or cut a hole in the slab edge (slightly larger than the pipe diameter)
2. Insert the pipe through the hole into the pit space (above the waste level)
3. Extend the pipe vertically up the outside of the booth
4. Secure the pipe with straps or brackets
5. At the top of the pipe, fit a fly screen (plastic or brass mesh) to keep insects out

**Step 3: Ensure the pit is dark.**

The interior of the pit should be dark (no light entering). This keeps the waste material darker and less attractive to flies. Flies are attracted to light and try to exit through the vent pipe instead of staying in the waste.

**Step 4: Build the booth and superstructure as in Part 2.**

### Part 4: Vault Latrine Design (For High Water Tables or Rocky Soil)

Use a vault latrine if:
- Water table is shallow (less than 2 meters)
- Soil is pure sand, gravel, or bedrock (cannot dig a safe pit)
- Population is high (more than 20 people, pit fills too quickly)

A vault latrine is a water-tight container that holds waste and must be emptied periodically.

**Step 1: Prepare the location.**

Choose a site following the same rules as a pit latrine (downslope, away from water sources, accessible for emptying).

**Step 2: Build or install a sealed container.**

**Brick-lined vault method:**
1. Dig a hole slightly larger than your intended chamber (1.5 m × 1 m × 1 m typical)
2. Line the bottom and sides with brick or stone
3. Seal the interior with clay or concrete mortar
4. Ensure the bottom is sloped slightly toward a low point where a drain plug or outlet can be installed
5. Seal the top with a concrete slab (leaving a hole for the toilet)

**Plastic-lined pit method (faster):**
1. Dig the pit
2. Line it with heavy-duty plastic sheeting (1–2 mm thick HDPE preferred)
3. Create a simple frame on top to prevent the plastic from tearing
4. Make an outlet at the bottom for emptying (a simple drain plug sealed with a cork)

**Concrete vault method (most durable):**
1. Dig a hole shaped like your intended vault
2. Build a mold from wood or stone
3. Pour concrete 15–20 cm thick on all sides and the bottom
4. Cure for 14 days, then remove the mold
5. Pour a concrete slab on top with a hole for the toilet

**Step 3: Install an outlet for emptying.**

A vault must be emptied when full (roughly every 6–12 months for a family). Install an outlet:
- **Location:** At the lowest point of the vault floor
- **Design:** A simple pipe (2–3 cm diameter) with a threaded cap or wooden plug
- **Placement:** A small sump or dip where liquid can be drained before solid removal

**Step 4: Install a superstructure and ventilation.**

Build a booth around the vault following the pit latrine design (Part 2, Step 4). Add a vent pipe if possible to reduce smell.

**Step 5: Establish an emptying schedule.**

- **Frequency:** Every 6–12 months (depends on population and vault size)
- **Method:** Use a pump (hand pump or mechanical) to remove liquid first, then remove solid waste with buckets or a scoop
- **Disposal:** Solid waste can be composted (in a designated pit) or buried in an agricultural area at least 30 meters from any water source
- **Safety:** The person emptying must wear gloves and a mask; it is messy and odorous

## Verification

A working latrine shows these signs:

- **Odor:** Minimal (a properly ventilated latrine has almost no smell)
- **Structural integrity:** Walls are not collapsing; roof is weatherproof
- **Waste level:** For a pit latrine, waste decomposes and settles; the pit takes 3–5 years to fill (for a family)
- **No contamination:** Water sources remain clear and free of coliform bacteria (test with Vol 5, Ch 4)
- **Insect control:** For a VIP latrine, flies do not emerge from the pit (they are trapped in the vent pipe)

A failing latrine shows:
- Strong fecal smell (indicates inadequate ventilation or decomposition)
- Leaking liquid (indicates vault has failed or pit is oversaturated)
- Seepage into nearby water sources (test with boiled water smell, or discoloration)

## Common Mistakes

- **Digging too shallow:** A pit less than 1 meter deep will overflow and contaminate groundwater within months. Minimum 1.5 meters.
- **Placing near water source:** Latrine within 10 meters of a well or spring will contaminate it within 1–2 years. Double-check distances with a tape measure.
- **Inadequate ventilation:** A pit latrine without a vent pipe will smell. Add ventilation before opening the latrine to use.
- **Building on sandy soil:** Waste drains too quickly, contaminating groundwater. Vault is safer.
- **Not monitoring water table:** Digging in the rainy season may place a pit above the wet season water table. Always dig test holes to assess minimum water table depth.
- **Overpopulation:** More than 10–15 people per latrine pit causes it to fill quickly and may require emptying. Plan multiple latrines for larger groups.
- **Poor construction:** Walls that collapse bury the slab and make the latrine unusable. Invest in proper lining or brick support.
