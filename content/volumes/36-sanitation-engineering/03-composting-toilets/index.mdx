## Overview

A composting toilet is a toilet that does not use water. Instead, human waste is mixed with carbon-rich material (sawdust, dry leaves, straw) and allowed to decompose. Bacteria and fungi break down the waste. Heat from decomposition kills pathogens. The result, after 1–2 years, is soil-like material that can be used to amend agricultural land.

Composting toilets work in any climate, require no water connection, produce no sewage, and generate a valuable product. They are the most sustainable human waste management system for post-collapse scenarios.

## Materials & Tools

### For a Basic Composting Toilet

- **Toilet seat and frame:** Wooden or salvaged seat over a container or pit
- **Container:** Bucket, chamber, or pit lined with plastic (if available)
- **Carbon material:** Sawdust, dry leaves, shredded paper, straw (200–300 kg per year per person)
- **Mixing tool:** Fork or shovel
- **Covering material:** Tarp, plywood, or additional carbon to control smell
- **Ventilation:** Pipe to exhaust gases (optional but recommended)

### For a Two-Compartment System (Advanced)

- **Two separate chambers:** Allowing one to "cure" while the other is in use
- **Divider or outlet:** To move material from active chamber to curing chamber
- **Pipes:** For ventilation and drainage

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Single-Chamber Composting Toilet

**Step 1: Choose a location.**

- At least 10 meters from any water source (well, stream, spring)
- On high ground where drainage flows away
- Accessible for regular maintenance
- Protected from direct rain but with good air circulation
- Away from living areas (50+ meters if possible, though odor is minimal)

**Step 2: Build the toilet structure.**

The simplest design:
- Dig a pit 1–2 meters deep and 1–2 meters across, OR
- Use a large container (plastic bucket, barrel, or wooden box) placed over a pit
- Install a toilet seat at working height (standard toilet height is 40 cm)
- Ensure stability (the seat should not rock or collapse under weight)

**Step 3: Stock carbon material.**

Accumulate sawdust, dry leaves, shredded paper, or straw. You need 300–500 g per use (roughly a coffee can full).

Store carbon material:
- Dry (protected from rain)
- Near the toilet
- In quantities you can easily access

Sawdust is ideal (lightweight, good carbon-nitrogen ratio, readily available from mills). Dry leaves work but are bulkier. Straw works but can compact and reduce airflow.

**Step 4: Use the toilet.**

After each use (both urine and feces), add a handful of carbon material (200–300 g). This:
- Covers the waste visually
- Absorbs moisture
- Adds carbon to balance nitrogen in the waste
- Begins the decomposition process
- Reduces odor (a properly maintained toilet has minimal smell)

**Step 5: Monitor carbon-nitrogen ratio.**

The compost will succeed if you maintain roughly 25–30 parts carbon to 1 part nitrogen (by mass).

- Urine and feces are nitrogen-rich
- Carbon material is carbon-rich
- Adding carbon after each use keeps the ratio correct

If the pile becomes too wet and smells like ammonia, you have too much nitrogen — add more carbon.

If the pile does not decompose and looks like nothing is happening, you may have too much carbon — add a small amount of water to speed decomposition.

**Step 6: Monitor temperature (optional but helpful).**

A properly functioning composting toilet gets warm (40–60°C) from bacterial activity. This heat kills pathogens.

Temperature indicates success:
- **Warm (>40°C):** Good decomposition, pathogens are dying
- **Cool/cold:** Slow decomposition; add water or more nitrogen (urine)
- **Hot (>65°C):** Excellent, but may need more carbon to prevent smell

Use your hand to test temperature (carefully — it can be quite hot). If you have a compost thermometer, stick it in the pile.

**Step 7: Manage odor.**

A well-maintained composting toilet should have little to no odor. If it smells:

- **Ammonia smell:** Too much nitrogen, not enough carbon. Add more sawdust or dry leaves.
- **Fecal smell (like an outhouse):** Insufficient decomposition. Add water to speed bacterial activity, or increase temperature by adding more nitrogen-rich material.
- **Musty smell:** Normal for decomposition. Add a bit more carbon to reduce.

**Step 8: Know when to empty.**

A single-chamber toilet should be emptied once per year (or as the chamber fills). When the material reaches the level of the toilet seat, it is time to empty.

Emptying procedure:
1. Remove the toilet seat
2. Use a shovel to remove the compost material
3. Place it in a separate curing pile (see Part 2)
4. Replace the toilet seat
5. Resume use

### Part 2: Two-Compartment System (Allows Continuous Operation)

**Step 1: Build two chambers.**

Create two separate composting chambers, each capable of holding 1–2 years of waste from one person. The chambers can be:
- Two pits dug side by side
- Two large containers placed side by side
- Two buried barrels

**Step 2: Install a divider or valve.**

Connect the two chambers with a valve or door that allows you to switch between them. When one chamber is full, close the valve and begin using the other.

**Step 3: Use the active chamber.**

While the active chamber is in use, the full chamber is curing (decomposition continues, pathogens die, material stabilizes).

**Step 4: Curing timeline.**

After you switch chambers, let the full chamber sit for 1–2 years:
- At 6 months: Decomposition is well underway, but pathogens may remain
- At 1 year: Most pathogens are dead, material looks soil-like
- At 2 years: Complete pathogen death, material is safe to handle

**Step 5: Harvest the cured material.**

After 1–2 years, the material in the non-active chamber should be dark brown, crumbly, soil-like, and odorless. This is now safe to use on agricultural land.

### Part 3: Using Harvested Compost

**Step 1: Understand pathogen destruction.**

Composting toilets kill pathogens through:
- **Heat:** Thermophilic decomposition (50–65°C) kills most pathogens in weeks
- **Time:** Remaining pathogens die over 6–12 months as pH changes and competition for nutrients reduces pathogen survival
- **Competition:** Beneficial microbes outcompete pathogens

**Step 2: Verify safety before application.**

The safest approach:
- Wait 2 years before using the compost
- Never apply raw or partially composted human waste to food crops
- Apply only fully composted material (>1 year old) to food crops
- Consider applying only to non-food crops (ornamental plants, forests) if you are uncertain

**Step 3: Application rates.**

Apply composted human waste at:
- 5–10 tons per hectare per year for soil amendment
- 2–5 tons per hectare for nutrient provision
- Never apply more than 10 tons per hectare in a single year

These rates are conservative to ensure safety.

**Step 4: Crops to consider for application.**

Safe applications:
- **Ornamental plants:** Trees, shrubs, flowers (no food consumption risk)
- **Fiber crops:** Cotton, linen, hemp (not consumed)
- **Timber crops:** Trees grown for wood, not food
- **Pasture:** Grass for animal forage (animals digest pathogens)
- **Grain and legume crops:** Once material is >1 year old (more conservative: wait 2 years)
- **Vegetables:** Only if material is >2 years old and grown on managed land

Crops to avoid for the first 2 years:
- Leafy greens that are eaten raw (lettuce, spinach, cabbage)
- Root crops eaten raw (carrots, radishes)
- Fruits that touch soil (melons, strawberries)

**Step 5: Application method.**

- Incorporate the compost into the top 15–20 cm of soil by tilling or digging
- Do not apply to soil immediately before planting; allow 2–3 weeks for initial decomposition and settling
- Avoid applying during heavy rain (can leach contaminants)

## Verification

**Your composting toilet is working if:**
- Material decomposes (becomes brown, crumbly, unrecognizable)
- Temperature rises to >40°C (if measured)
- Odor is minimal or non-existent
- No pests (flies, rodents) colonize the system (good aeration and carbon coverage prevent this)
- Chamber fills at the expected rate (roughly 30–40 liters per person per year)

**Safety of harvested compost:**
- Material is dark brown to black
- Material crumbles easily (not solid chunks)
- Original waste is unrecognizable
- No ammonia smell (ammonia smell means decomposition is incomplete)

## Common Mistakes

- **Insufficient carbon.** Adding too little sawdust or leaves causes wet, smelly piles. Add more carbon than you think you need.
- **Using treated wood sawdust.** Sawdust from pressure-treated lumber contains arsenic and other toxins. Use only sawdust from untreated wood.
- **Not monitoring ratio.** A wet, anaerobic (oxygen-free) pile smells foul. Keep the pile aerobic by adding carbon and turning occasionally.
- **Too much water.** A composting toilet should not be flooded with water. If you use water to rinse (avoid this), use minimal amounts.
- **Applying immature compost to food crops.** Compost less than 1 year old may harbor pathogens. Wait 2 years to be safe, especially for crops eaten raw.
- **Ignoring ventilation.** A sealed composting toilet is anaerobic and smells terrible. Ensure air can circulate (but protect from rain).
- **Building the toilet too close to water sources.** Groundwater can be contaminated if the toilet leaches. Maintain 10+ meters distance.
- **Using the compost before it is fully decomposed.** Fresh human waste compost can burn plants and harbor pathogens. Patience is essential.
