## Overview

Greywater is water from sinks, showers, baths, and laundry. It is different from blackwater (toilet waste, which contains pathogens). Greywater is 80 percent of household wastewater and can be treated for reuse in irrigation, reducing freshwater demand by half.

Post-collapse, water is precious. Treating and reusing greywater extends survival in dry climates and improves food production. This chapter covers three greywater systems: simple infiltration trenches (easiest), constructed wetlands with plants (most robust), and direct reuse systems (highest value).

The goal is twofold: reduce freshwater demand and prevent pollution of groundwater and surface water from untreated waste.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of wastewater categories (greywater vs. blackwater) (Vol 5, Ch 1)
- Knowledge of soil permeability and water movement (Vol 5, Ch 1)
- Basic plant biology and irrigation needs (Vol 10: Agriculture)
- Ability to dig and construct simple systems
- Community coordination (systems are community-scale)

## Materials & Tools

**For infiltration trenches:**
- Gravel or stone (for filtration)
- Sand (for additional filtering and percolation)
- Soil (topsoil for final layer)
- Cloth or geotextile (to separate layers)
- Digging tools
- Pipes or channels to direct water

**For reed beds:**
- Aquatic plants (reeds, cattails, or rushes—species vary by climate)
- Gravel (for medium)
- Sand (for medium)
- Soil (topsoil)
- Container or dug basin (can be constructed or use existing depression)
- Piping for water input and overflow

**For reuse systems:**
- Filtration materials (cloth, sand, gravel, charcoal)
- Storage containers (if needed)
- Irrigation pipes or channels
- Valves or gates (to control flow)

## Part 1: Infiltration Trenches (Simplest Method)

Infiltration trenches allow greywater to percolate through soil layers, where bacteria, solids, and many chemicals are removed. The treated water recharges groundwater or feeds plant roots.

### Design Principles

**Depth:** 0.5–1.5 meters below ground surface (below frost line in cold climates, deep enough to reach plant root zones).

**Length and width:** Trenches run downslope or contour the land. Width is 0.5–1.5 meters; length depends on daily water volume (see sizing below).

**Slope:** Slight downslope (1–2 percent) to encourage flow and prevent ponding. For contour systems, level is acceptable if gravel permeability is good.

**Location:** At least 15 meters from wells or water sources; downslope from septic/toilet areas; in areas with good drainage (avoid heavy clay).

### Step 1: Size the System

Calculate daily greywater volume:
- Household of 4 people: approximately 80–120 liters per person per day from showers, laundry, sinks
- Total: 320–480 liters per day for family of 4

Sizing formula:
- Trench area (in square meters) = Daily volume (liters) / Percolation rate (liters per square meter per day)
- Typical percolation rate: 50–100 liters per square meter per day (sandy soil = faster; clay soil = slower)
- Example: 400 liters per day / 75 liters per square meter per day = 5.3 square meters of trench area

If trench is 1 meter wide:
- Length needed = 5.3 square meters / 1 meter = 5.3 meters

Add 20 percent margin: 6.4 meters long × 1 meter wide

### Step 2: Prepare the Excavation

1. **Dig the trench** to the target depth (0.5–1.5 meters), sloping slightly downhill
2. **Check the bottom:** Soil should be able to absorb water (test by digging a 30-centimeter hole, filling with water, observing how long it takes to drain)
   - Drains in less than 1 hour: good permeability (suitable)
   - Drains in 1–6 hours: acceptable
   - Does not drain in 6 hours: poor permeability (add sand layer or choose different location)

3. **Ensure compaction:** Remove large rocks and hard material; the bottom should be firm but permeable

### Step 3: Layer the Filtration Media

Build the trench in layers, from bottom to top:

**Layer 1 (Bottom): Large gravel (5–10 centimeters)**
- Improves drainage and prevents media from settling
- Use stones 5–10 centimeters in size

**Layer 2: Sand with drainage pipe (20–30 centimeters)**
- Provides additional filtration
- Install perforated drain pipe (or plastic pipe with holes drilled) along the bottom of the trench
- The pipe should slope slightly downhill (1–2 percent)
- Cover the pipe with sand
- Sand traps fine particles and additional bacteria

**Layer 3: Geotextile or cloth**
- Optional but valuable
- Separates sand from gravel (prevents sand migration downward)
- Use old cloth, burlap, or any permeable material

**Layer 4: Gravel (15–20 centimeters)**
- Provides additional support and drainage

**Layer 5: Topsoil with vegetation (10–15 centimeters)**
- Topsoil or native soil
- Plant with grasses, reeds, or drought-tolerant plants (roots help with final filtration and evapotranspiration)
- Plants also prevent erosion and make the system less visible

### Step 4: Direct Water into the Trench

**Input:** Install a pipe or channel to deliver greywater to the trench
- Pipe should enter near the top of the system (above the sand layer if possible)
- Use a distribution box (simple compartment) to divide flow evenly along the trench length
- Add a settling zone (simple tank or basin) upstream to remove large solids before entering the trench

### Step 5: Maintenance

- **Weekly:** Check for ponding or surface water (indicates overloading or clogging)
- **Monthly:** Remove large solids from settling tank
- **Annually:** Inspect the outlet (drain pipe) for sediment buildup; if clogged, flush with clean water from opposite end
- **Seasonal:** Trim vegetation to prevent overgrowth but maintain root system

**Signs of problems:**
- Water pools on surface: system is overloaded or clogged; reduce input volume or extend the trench
- Foul smell: indicates anaerobic conditions; aerate the system by allowing it to dry out between uses, or increase ventilation

## Part 2: Constructed Wetlands (Reed Beds)

Constructed wetlands use planted aquatic plants to filter greywater. Plants and associated microorganisms remove nutrients, bacteria, and some chemicals. This system is more robust than infiltration trenches and produces cleaner effluent.

### Understanding Wetland Processes

- **Physical filtration:** Gravel and sand trap solids
- **Biological uptake:** Plants absorb nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus)
- **Microbial decomposition:** Bacteria break down organic matter in an oxygen-rich environment (aerobic) and oxygen-poor environment (anaerobic)
- **Chemical adsorption:** Gravel and soil bind and remove some pollutants

### System Types

**Horizontal flow system (most practical):**
- Water flows horizontally through the wetland
- Plant roots and microorganisms are active throughout
- Water level stays below the surface
- Easy maintenance; less mosquito breeding

**Vertical flow system:**
- Water flows downward through the media
- Better aeration (more oxygen available)
- Less plant-based treatment but good solids removal
- More complex to construct

For post-collapse scenarios, horizontal flow is recommended.

### Design and Construction

#### Step 1: Determine System Size

Similar to infiltration trenches but typically smaller (plants are more efficient):

Sizing formula:
- Area (in square meters) = Daily greywater volume (liters) / Hydraulic loading rate (liters per square meter per day)
- Horizontal flow wetland loading rate: 5–10 liters per square meter per day (conservative)
- Example: 400 liters per day / 7.5 liters per square meter per day = 53 square meters

This is quite large. For small communities, plan multiple smaller wetlands or use a combination system (infiltration trench + wetland).

Alternatively, reduce source by reusing water directly for irrigation (see Part 3 below).

#### Step 2: Excavate and Prepare

1. **Excavate a shallow basin** 0.5–1 meter deep
2. **Create an impermeable lining** to prevent leakage:
   - Clay-lined: Compact 20–30 centimeters of clay on bottom and sides
   - Plastic membrane: Install geotextile or plastic liner (0.5 millimeters minimum thickness)
   - Natural lining: High clay content in soil (compact well)

3. **Test the lining:** Fill with water and check for leaks over 24 hours

#### Step 3: Install Inlet and Outlet

**Inlet:**
- Pipe or channel that distributes water evenly across the wetland width
- Should enter at one end, elevated slightly (15–20 centimeters)

**Outlet:**
- Drain pipe or channel at the opposite end
- Positioned to maintain water level at 15–30 centimeters depth
- Include overflow outlet for excess water (connects to infiltration trench or surface discharge)

#### Step 4: Add Filtration Media and Plants

**Layer 1 (Bottom):** Gravel (10 centimeters)
- Supports structure; provides drainage

**Layer 2:** Sand with roots (30 centimeters)
- Plant roots will grow through this layer
- Sand provides additional filtration

**Layer 3:** Topsoil (10 centimeters)
- Supports plant growth

**Plant selection:**
- Reeds (Phragmites australis): tall, efficient, spreads readily
- Cattails (Typha latifolia): similar to reeds, better in shallow water
- Rushes (Juncus species): shorter, grows in diverse conditions
- Papyrus (Cyperus papyrus): tropical regions, very productive

**Planting density:**
- Space plants 30–50 centimeters apart
- Plant in a grid pattern or rows
- Use rootstock (plant pieces with growing shoots) rather than seeds; they establish faster

#### Step 5: Commissioning and Operation

**First 2–4 weeks:**
- Add water slowly to allow settling and plant establishment
- System is not fully functional until plants are established (4–8 weeks)
- Bypass excess water to infiltration trench during this period

**Maintenance:**
- **Weekly:** Monitor water level (maintain 15–30 centimeters depth)
- **Monthly:** Remove any debris or floating matter
- **Annually:** In temperate climates, reeds die back in winter; clear dead vegetation in spring
- **Every 3–5 years:** Remove accumulated sediment from inlet and outlet areas

**Harvesting plants:**
- Cut reeds or cattails in late fall or early winter (after growth slows)
- Use for thatching, animal bedding, or compost
- Harvesting removes accumulated nutrients (nitrogen and phosphorus from the greywater)

## Part 3: Direct Reuse Systems (Irrigation)

If greywater is only lightly contaminated (from sinks and showers, not laundry), it can be used directly for irrigation after basic filtration. This provides the highest water conservation benefit.

### Water Suitability for Direct Reuse

**Suitable sources:**
- Sink and shower water (greywater)
- Laundry water (if using plant-based or biodegradable soaps)
- Vegetable washing water

**Not suitable:**
- Water with grease or oil (clogs soil; prevents water infiltration)
- Water with synthetic chemicals or pesticides
- Water with high salt content (laundry water from areas with hard water and salt-based softeners)

### Simple Filtration System

For direct use, implement a simple 2–3 stage filtration:

**Stage 1: Settling tank**
- Collection basin where water sits for 24 hours
- Solids settle to the bottom
- Skim any floating material
- Capacity: 1 day's greywater volume

**Stage 2: Gravel/Sand filter**
- Water from settling tank flows through a filter bed
- Layers: large gravel (10 centimeters) → sand (20 centimeters) → cloth → fine gravel (5 centimeters)
- Water exits through a spout at the bottom
- Capacity: 100–200 liters per hour (depends on sand quality)

**Stage 3: (Optional) Biochar or activated charcoal**
- Adsorbs some chemicals and odors
- Layer of 10 centimeters above the fine gravel
- Refreshed or replaced annually

### Direct Irrigation Methods

**Surface drip irrigation:**
- Lay porous hose or drip line across garden beds
- Water seeps slowly into soil near plant roots
- No water spray; minimizes contact with edible plant leaves
- Best for root vegetables, grains, trees

**Subsurface irrigation:**
- Bury porous hose 15–30 centimeters deep
- Water is delivered directly to root zone
- Highly efficient; minimizes evaporation
- Best for perennial crops

**Mulched areas:**
- Direct water into mulched garden beds
- Mulch filters additional sediment
- Soil biology (earthworms, bacteria) provides further treatment
- Suitable for non-edible plants (flowers, ornamentals) or when plants are fully cooked before eating

### Application Rates and Timing

**Application frequency:**
- Greywater is usually low in nutrients; supplement with compost
- Water 2–4 times per week, depending on plant needs and evaporation
- Avoid overwatering (standing water prevents oxygen availability to roots)

**Application volumes:**
- Most plants need 25–50 millimeters of water per week (250–500 liters per 100 square meters)
- A 4-person household generates 300–500 liters per day; this is sufficient for irrigating 0.5–1 hectare of garden

**Seasonal adjustments:**
- Summer: increase frequency and volume
- Winter: reduce or halt use (in cold climates) or use sparingly (in mild climates)

### Plants Suitable for Greywater Irrigation

**Best choices (can tolerate some salt and sediment):**
- Fruit trees (apples, pears, citrus)
- Grain crops (wheat, barley, rice)
- Root vegetables (carrots, potatoes, onions)
- Fodder crops (alfalfa, clover)
- Forest trees (eucalyptus, pine)

**Less ideal (prefer clean water):**
- Leafy greens (lettuce, spinach—if leaves will be eaten raw)
- Berries (strawberries, raspberries)
- Herbs used fresh

**Not recommended (risk of contamination or damage):**
- Vegetables eaten raw without washing (if greywater quality is uncertain)
- Aquatic crops (fish ponds, rice in water; greywater may contaminate)

### Maintenance and Safety

**Monthly tasks:**
- Clear filter media (sand clogs with suspended solids; replace or clean every 1–3 months)
- Empty settling tank (accumulated sludge; compost or dry for disposal)
- Check for leaks or overflow

**Health precautions:**
- Do not drink reused greywater
- Wash hands after handling greywater systems
- Boil any vegetables from greywater-irrigated plots if eaten raw
- Cook all produce thoroughly for maximum safety margin

## Hybrid Systems (Recommended for Most Communities)

Combine methods for robustness:

1. **Input greywater → Settling tank** (removes large solids)
2. **Settling tank → Infiltration trench or reed bed** (treatment)
3. **Overflow/excess → Reed bed or infiltration trench for non-reused water**
4. **Optional: Treated water → Direct reuse irrigation** (if quality is adequate)

**Advantages:**
- Flexibility: can reuse during dry seasons, discharge excess during wet seasons
- Robustness: if one component fails (e.g., reed bed drying out), the other still functions
- Scalability: add components as needed (start with infiltration, add reed bed later)

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: System smells foul**
- Cause: Anaerobic conditions (lack of oxygen)
- Solution: Allow to dry out between uses; increase aeration

**Problem: Water pools on surface**
- Cause: System overloaded or media clogged
- Solution: Increase system size; replace clogged media

**Problem: Plants are dying or not growing**
- Cause: Nutrient deficiency, inappropriate species for climate, or poor water quality
- Solution: Add compost; change plant species; assess water quality

**Problem: Filter clogs rapidly**
- Cause: High sediment load from upstream sources
- Solution: Add larger settling tank or pre-filter upstream
