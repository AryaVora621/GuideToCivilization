## Overview

Mosquitoes carry malaria, dengue, and yellow fever. Rats carry plague and typhus. Flies spread cholera and dysentery. More people die from vector-borne diseases than from direct injuries after collapse. But vector control does not require modern pesticides. It requires understanding where vectors breed, where they hide, and how to eliminate those places.

This chapter is about environmental management: removing breeding sites, creating barriers, and using biological control. These methods are sustainable, low-cost, and effective if applied consistently.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of mosquito, rat, and fly life cycles (Vol 27, Ch 6 — Microbiology and ecology sections)
- Knowledge of sanitation and waste management (Vol 5, Ch 1–2 — Latrine and Sewage)
- Ability to identify standing water, rodent entry points, and fly attractants
- Community coordination (vector control works best at scale)

## Section 1: Mosquito Control

Mosquitoes breed in standing water and transmit diseases during their blood-feeding behavior. Most species require water for larval development.

### Understanding Mosquito Life Cycle

- **Eggs:** Laid on water or damp surfaces (depends on species)
- **Larvae:** Aquatic; develop over 5–14 days; vulnerable to water disturbance or predation
- **Pupae:** Non-feeding aquatic stage; 1–4 days
- **Adults:** Emerge from pupae; live 1–4 weeks; females need blood meal to produce eggs

**Key point:** Eliminate standing water for 5–14 days and mosquitoes cannot complete their life cycle.

### Method 1: Source Reduction (Eliminate Breeding Sites)

This is the most effective control method.

#### Step 1: Identify Standing Water Sources

Walk your settlement daily:
- Rain-filled containers (barrels, buckets, discarded tires)
- Ditches, depressions, low-lying areas
- Puddles near water spills
- Bird baths, water troughs, plant saucers
- Swamps, marshes, very slow-moving water
- Blocked gutters, roof eaves
- Any object that collects water

Mark these locations and prioritize by:
- How frequently water is present
- How close to sleeping/living areas
- How difficult to eliminate

#### Step 2: Eliminate or Drain

**Eliminate completely:**
- Remove unused containers (barrels, buckets, tires, clay pots, old bottles)
- Fill in small depressions with soil
- Drain puddles by redirecting water flow or creating channels

**If elimination is not possible:**
- Drain every 3–5 days (before larvae mature to adults)
- Or cover the water surface completely (see below)

#### Step 3: Maintain Drainage

- Clear gutters and roof catchments
- Create slopes so water flows rather than pools
- Clean ditches and channels every week
- Maintain well-drained ground around living areas

#### Step 4: Community Approach

**Make vector control community work:**
- Weekly inspection by designated community member
- Mandatory container lids or removal
- Fine system: households with standing water must help drain neighboring sites (social pressure)
- Timing: drain all water sources on the same day each week to prevent spillover between households

### Method 2: Larval Control with Predators

If standing water cannot be eliminated (fish ponds, larger reservoirs), introduce predatory fish or insects.

#### Gambusia Fish (Mosquitofish)

These small fish (3–5 centimeters) eat thousands of mosquito larvae daily and survive in poor conditions.

**Care and breeding:**
- Introduce 3–5 fish to each water body
- Fish breed quickly in warm water (create separate nursery pools if needed)
- Provide minimal feeding (they survive on mosquito larvae and algae)
- Remove at least 20 percent of fish population annually to prevent overpopulation and starvation

**Effectiveness:** One fish can eliminate larvae in a 50-liter container over a week.

#### Dragonfly Nymphs

Dragonfly nymphs are also predatory and live in water. They eat mosquito larvae and pupae.

**Attraction:**
- Create vegetated shallow pools (aquatic plants attract dragonflies)
- Allow pools to exist undisturbed for several weeks
- Dragonflies lay eggs in vegetation; nymphs develop over 2–3 months
- Do not drain these pools during breeding season

**Limitation:** Slower than fish but requires no stocking effort.

### Method 3: Surface Film Barriers

If water cannot be drained or stocked with predators, cover the surface to block mosquito breathing and egg-laying.

#### Oil or Fat Film

A thin film of oil prevents larvae from reaching the water surface to breathe.

**Procedure:**
- Collect used cooking oil, animal fat, or plant oil
- Pour a thin film (just enough to cover) over water surface
- Reapply every 2–3 weeks as oil breaks down
- Use sparingly (avoid contaminating water sources for human use)

**Effectiveness:** Kills developing larvae within 24 hours. Also kills adult mosquitoes landing on water.

**Limitations:** Not suitable for drinking water sources. Potentially toxic to fish and other aquatic life.

#### Fine Netting

Cover water with mosquito netting (or fine cloth) to prevent adult access.

**Procedure:**
- Drape fine netting over container or water surface
- Weight edges down so mosquitoes cannot crawl underneath
- Secure so rain does not create pockets of standing water on top
- This blocks egg-laying but does not kill larvae already present (combine with draining)

### Method 4: Reduce Adult Mosquito Activity

Even if breeding is controlled, some adult mosquitoes arrive from elsewhere.

#### Resting Sites

Mosquitoes rest in dark, sheltered places during the day:
- Dense vegetation near houses
- Thick brush or tall grass
- Inside buildings in dark corners
- Under eaves and overhangs

**Control:**
- Clear vegetation within 10 meters of sleeping areas
- Trim low-hanging branches
- Ensure buildings are sealed (mosquitoes enter through small gaps)
- Use screens on windows and doors

#### Behavioral Control

- Sleep under bed nets (most effective personal method)
- Reduce outdoor activity at dawn and dusk (when most mosquitoes are active)
- Wear light-colored, long-sleeved clothing at peak hours
- Build smoke fires near sleeping areas (smoke repels mosquitoes)

## Section 2: Rat Control

Rats carry plague, typhus, and hantavirus. They contaminate food and water. A single pair of rats can produce 200+ offspring per year.

### Understanding Rat Behavior and Life Cycle

- **Gestation:** 21–23 days
- **Litter size:** 5–12 pups
- **Pups independent:** 3 weeks old
- **Lifespan:** 1–2 years (some live 3 years)
- **Activity:** Nocturnal (mostly); active year-round
- **Diet:** Omnivorous; need water and food within 20–30 meters of nest
- **Damage:** Chew through wood, drywall, stored grains, clothing; contaminate surfaces with feces and urine

**Key point:** Prevent entry and eliminate food sources; control populations through trapping and predators.

### Method 1: Rodent-Proof Construction

Prevent rats from entering buildings.

#### Seal Entry Points

**Identify entry paths:**
- Walk around building perimeter and look for holes, cracks, gaps
- Look under doors, around pipes, through ventilation openings
- Rats can squeeze through holes larger than their head (about 1 centimeter for small rats, 2 centimeters for larger rats)

**Seal with:**
- Metal mesh or hardware cloth (nailed or screwed over openings)
- Cement or mortar (sealed with wire mesh for strength)
- Raised door thresholds (at least 5 centimeters high)

**Priority sealing locations:**
- Under doors (most common entry)
- Around pipes and utilities
- Ventilation openings (cover with metal grating)
- Walls (especially where utilities enter)
- Roof eaves and overhangs

#### Elevation and Distance

- Keep stored food, grain, and supplies elevated at least 45 centimeters off the ground
- Store in sealed metal or glass containers (rats cannot chew through metal)
- Clear brush and debris within 3 meters of building (removes hiding places and nesting material)
- Avoid stacking wood, hay, or supplies directly against walls (creates shelter)

### Method 2: Eliminate Food and Water Sources

Rats require food and water; without these, they leave.

#### Food Management

- Store all food in sealed containers (metal boxes, glass jars with tight lids)
- Do not leave food scraps exposed overnight
- Compost at least 50 meters from buildings (buried scraps attract rats)
- Feed animals on a schedule; remove uneaten food
- Store animal feed in sealed containers elevated off ground
- Clean up grain spills immediately
- Close access to grain stores and food storage areas

#### Water Management

- Fix leaking pipes and dripping taps (rats need water daily)
- Remove standing water near buildings
- Do not leave food with liquid (soup, wet compost) accessible overnight

**Result:** Rats without accessible food and water will seek other locations within days.

### Method 3: Trapping

For existing rat populations, trapping is immediate and effective.

#### Setting Traps

**Trap placement:**
- Place along walls (rats follow edges; they rarely venture into open areas)
- Near entry points and exit routes
- In areas with rat droppings or sign
- Near food sources (grain stores, compost areas)
- Multiple traps: one trap catches 1–2 rats; 10 traps catch 10 rats

**Bait options:**
- Peanut butter (most effective)
- Nuts, seeds, dried fruit
- Chocolate
- Grain mixed with fat
- Meat scraps

**Setting:**
- Wear gloves (human scent can deter some rats)
- Set trigger very lightly (light-sensitive rats will avoid a heavy trigger)
- Place bait so rat must reach into trap to access it
- Place trap perpendicular to wall (not parallel to it)

#### Trap Types (if available)

**Snap traps (traditional):**
- Fast, humane if set properly
- Effective on small-to-medium rats
- Bait placement is critical
- Cannot be reused after firing (must reset completely)
- Cost: high initially, low per catch

**Box traps (improvised):**
- Rig a heavy container to fall when rat enters to eat bait
- Slower death but effective for larger populations
- Reusable
- Requires mechanical setup

**Deadfall traps (improvised):**
- Heavy weight suspended on a trigger
- Effective if weight is sufficient and mechanism is reliable
- Requires careful setup

**Disadvantages of poison:**
- Poisoned rats may die in inaccessible places (walls, under buildings), creating smell and decay
- Poison may persist in environment
- Secondary poisoning of predators and scavengers
- Avoid poison unless trap-resistant rats are present

### Method 4: Biological Control

Natural predators control rat populations.

#### Predators

- **Cats:** Hunt and kill rats; maintain population control; require food and shelter
- **Birds of prey:** Owls and hawks hunt rats; build perches and minimize hunting restrictions
- **Snakes:** Some species eat rats; provide suitable habitat
- **Dogs:** Ratting dogs (terriers) hunt rats effectively

**Maintaining predators:**
- Do not poison predators (they control rodents)
- Provide shelter (nest boxes for birds, areas for cats)
- Minimize threats (avoid traps, hunting pressure)
- Assess population control (if rat problems persist, predators may be insufficient)

### Method 5: Population Monitoring

Track rat populations to assess control effectiveness.

#### Monitoring Techniques

**Visual inspection:**
- Count fresh droppings daily in specific areas (increase = increasing population)
- Monitor bait consumption (how much bait is eaten without traps being triggered)
- Track trap catches per night

**Snap trap index:**
- Place 20 baited snap traps overnight
- Count how many are triggered
- Index = number triggered / 20
- Index > 0.3 (more than 6 triggered) indicates significant population

**Result:** If droppings decrease, traps catch fewer rats, and index drops below 0.1 over 2+ weeks, population is controlled.

## Section 3: Fly Control

Flies spread disease through their feet and mouthparts. They breed in feces, food waste, and decaying organic matter.

### Understanding Fly Life Cycle

- **Eggs:** Laid in organic material (feces, food, carrion); hatch in 12–24 hours
- **Larvae:** Develop in organic matter for 3–5 days; produce foul smell
- **Pupae:** Non-feeding stage; 3–5 days
- **Adults:** Emerge from pupae; live 15–30 days; very active feeders and reproducers

**Key point:** Eliminate breeding material; flies cannot reproduce without it.

### Method 1: Sanitation (Source Elimination)

The most effective fly control is preventing breeding sites.

#### Latrine Management

- Use properly designed latrines (see Vol 5, Ch 1 — Latrine Design)
- Ensure lids or covers are in place
- Empty or cover waste regularly
- If flies are visible, add ash or lime to latrine (smothers larvae, raises pH)
- For composting toilets, ensure proper cover and ventilation

#### Food and Waste Management

- Do not leave food or organic waste exposed overnight
- Store food in sealed containers or covered areas
- Cover garbage pits (use soil or cloth cover)
- Bury food scraps at least 30 centimeters deep, away from living areas
- Remove dead animals immediately (bury at least 1 meter deep)
- Clean up spilled food and grease immediately

#### Water Management

- Do not leave standing water (potential breeding in some fly species)
- Rinse containers daily
- Clean floors and surfaces to remove food particles

**Result:** Without accessible breeding material, fly populations decline within one week.

### Method 2: Physical Barriers

Prevent flies from reaching food and people.

#### Screens

- Screen all windows and doors
- Ensure gaps around screens are sealed
- Repair torn screens immediately
- Use fine mesh (1.6–1.8 millimeters to exclude small flies)

#### Covers

- Cover food during storage and preparation
- Use cloth, woven material, or fine mesh
- Ensure cover is weighted or tied so flies cannot crawl underneath

#### Mosquito Netting

- At eating areas or food prep zones, drape fine netting
- Create fly-free zones by completely enclosing spaces with netting

### Method 3: Traps and Baits

If flies are present despite sanitation, use traps to reduce population.

#### Sticky Traps

Create traps using sticky surfaces.

**Procedure:**
- Coat a strip of cloth or paper with a sticky substance (honey mixed with grease, rosin, or tree resin)
- Hang in areas with fly activity (near food, latrines, garbage areas)
- Flies land on the sticky surface and cannot escape
- Replace when covered with flies (every 1–3 days, depending on fly density)

**Effectiveness:** Reduces visible flies quickly; does not eliminate population entirely.

#### Bait Traps

Attract flies to a container and trap them.

**Procedure:**
- Fill a jar halfway with a bait (rotting fruit, feces, fish scraps)
- Cover the top with cloth or paper with a small hole (flies enter, cannot exit)
- Place in an area away from living/eating spaces
- Flies are drawn to bait, enter hole, and are trapped
- Kill trapped flies by adding boiling water or sealing the jar

**Effectiveness:** Good for monitoring population; also reduces numbers.

### Method 4: Biological Control

Introduce natural enemies of flies.

#### Parasitoid Wasps

Small wasps parasitize fly pupae, preventing emergence.

**Introduction (if available):**
- Obtain parasitoid wasp pupae
- Release near fly breeding sites
- Wasps lay eggs in fly pupae; wasp larvae consume fly pupa
- Wasps emerge instead of flies

**Limitations:** Wasps are not available post-collapse unless bred or imported; most effective in managed agriculture.

#### Predatory Insects

- Dragonflies eat adult flies
- Spiders and other insects eat flies

**Attraction:**
- Maintain areas of vegetation near latrines or compost areas
- Do not eliminate predatory insects
- Allow predators to establish populations naturally

## Integration: Community-Wide Vector Control

Individual efforts are limited. Community-scale control is much more effective.

### Phases of Implementation

**Phase 1: Awareness (Week 1)**
- Educate on disease transmission and vector life cycles
- Identify key breeding sites on maps
- Assess current conditions

**Phase 2: Targeted elimination (Weeks 2–4)**
- Community work days to remove standing water
- Seal building entry points
- Implement sanitation improvements
- Distribute bednets, screens, and traps

**Phase 3: Maintenance (Ongoing)**
- Weekly inspections
- Enforce source reduction rules
- Monitor populations (trap catches, droppings)
- Seasonal preparation (seasonal mosquito surges)

**Phase 4: Expansion (Months 2+)**
- Add biological controls (fish, predators)
- Implement community animal management
- Coordinate with neighboring settlements

### Success Measures

**Mosquitoes:**
- Standing water eliminated (target: zero sources within 20 meters of settlement)
- Trap catches decline from >5 per night to &lt;1 per night within 4 weeks
- Bednet usage >80 percent at night

**Rats:**
- Droppings scarce in traps and buildings (target: &lt;1 dropping per trap per week)
- Trap index < 0.1 (less than 2 traps triggered per 20 set)
- Food contamination ceases

**Flies:**
- Food remains clean during storage (no fly contamination)
- Sticky traps catch &lt;10 flies per day within 2 weeks
- Latrines show no adult flies or larvae
