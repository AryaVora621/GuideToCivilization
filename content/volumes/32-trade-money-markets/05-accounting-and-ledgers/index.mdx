Civilization runs on records of value. The moment trade grows beyond what one person can remember, someone must write down who paid, who owes, what was sold, and what remains. Accounting is the craft of keeping those records accurately. It sounds dry, but it is one of the oldest and most consequential technologies humans have ever invented: the earliest writing we have found is not poetry or law, it is accounting, clay tablets tallying grain and livestock. A society that cannot keep honest books cannot run a granary, fund an army, extend credit, or build anything that outlasts a single trader's memory.

This chapter teaches you to keep books. You will learn the difference between a simple list of transactions and the powerful double-entry system, how to record everyday dealings, how to check your work for errors, and how reliable accounts make trust possible between strangers. With these skills a market town can extend credit, a workshop can know whether it is profitable, and a community can manage shared resources without descending into disputes.

## Why Accounting Matters

Without records, three problems quickly cripple any economy beyond a single household:

1. **Memory fails and disputes erupt.** Did the smith pay for the iron, or still owe for it? Two honest people remember differently, and trade collapses into argument.
2. **You cannot tell profit from loss.** A trader who does not track costs and sales may work hard for a year and end up poorer without ever knowing why.
3. **Credit becomes impossible.** Lending and borrowing, the engine of investment, require trustworthy records of debts. No records, no credit, no growth.

Accounting solves all three. It turns fuzzy memory into firm, checkable fact.

## Prerequisites

- Reliable arithmetic: addition and subtraction, ideally with a written number system
- A stable unit of account (a money or a standard commodity to value things in)
- A durable writing surface and method (paper and ink, slate, or clay)
- Honesty and discipline, the true foundation of all bookkeeping

## Materials & Tools

**Essential:**
- A **ledger**: a bound book or stack of durable pages, used only for accounts
- A reliable writing instrument that does not easily fade or smudge
- A consistent **unit of account** in which all entries are recorded
- A **calendar or dating system** so every entry carries a date

**Helpful:**
- An **abacus or counting board** for fast, accurate sums
- A separate **daybook** or journal for recording transactions as they happen, before they are posted into the ledger
- Ruled columns to keep figures aligned and readable

## Part 1: The Core Concepts

Three ideas underlie all accounting.

- **Assets:** Things of value you own or are owed. Cash, grain, tools, land, and money other people owe you.
- **Liabilities:** What you owe to others. Debts, unpaid bills, borrowed goods.
- **Capital (or equity):** What is truly yours after subtracting what you owe. Capital equals assets minus liabilities.

This gives the master relationship of all bookkeeping:

> Assets equal Liabilities plus Capital.

Everything you own was either funded by your own capital or by money you owe to someone else. This equation must always balance. When it does not, you have made an error, and that self-checking property is what makes accounting trustworthy.

## Part 2: Single-Entry Bookkeeping (the simple start)

The simplest method is a single running list, like a personal cash record:

1. Rule a page with columns: **date**, **description**, **money in**, **money out**, **running balance**.
2. Record each transaction as one line.
3. After each line, update the running balance: previous balance, plus money in, minus money out.

A small example for a market trader:

| Date | Description | In | Out | Balance |
|------|-------------|----|----|---------|
| Day 1 | Start of month, cash on hand | 50 | 0 | 50 |
| Day 2 | Bought cloth to sell | 0 | 20 | 30 |
| Day 4 | Sold cloth | 35 | 0 | 65 |
| Day 5 | Paid stall rent | 0 | 5 | 60 |

**Strengths:** Easy to learn, fast, adequate for one person with simple dealings.

**Weaknesses:** It tracks only cash, not what you own or owe overall. It does not easily catch errors, and it cannot show whether a business is truly profitable once tools, stock, and debts are counted. For anything larger, you need double-entry.

## Part 3: Double-Entry Bookkeeping (the powerful method)

The great invention of medieval merchants is **double-entry**: every transaction is recorded twice, once as a **debit** and once as a **credit**, and the two must always be equal. The names are just labels for the two columns: by long convention, debit is the left column, credit is the right.

The rule that never changes: **for every transaction, total debits equal total credits.** Because of this, the books are self-checking. If at any time the debits and credits do not match, you know an error exists and roughly where to look.

### How Accounts Work

You keep a separate **account** (a section of the ledger) for each kind of value: a cash account, a stock account, an account for each major debtor and creditor, a sales account, an expenses account. Every transaction moves value between two accounts.

The directions take practice, but the everyday pattern is:

- When **cash comes in**, debit the cash account (cash increased) and credit the source (for example, sales).
- When **cash goes out**, credit the cash account (cash decreased) and debit the destination (for example, an expense or a purchase).

### A Worked Double-Entry Example

The same trader, recorded in double-entry:

1. **Bought cloth for 20 in cash.**
   - Debit Stock 20 (goods increased)
   - Credit Cash 20 (cash decreased)
2. **Sold the cloth for 35 in cash.**
   - Debit Cash 35 (cash increased)
   - Credit Sales 35 (a sale was made)
   - Debit Cost of goods 20 and Credit Stock 20 (the cloth left your stock)
3. **Paid stall rent of 5 in cash.**
   - Debit Rent expense 5
   - Credit Cash 5

At every step, the debits and credits for that transaction are equal. At month's end you can total the Sales account against the expense and cost accounts to see your true profit, something single-entry cannot reliably show.

## Part 4: The Bookkeeping Cycle

A disciplined set of books follows a repeating cycle:

1. **Record in the daybook (journal).** As each transaction happens, write it immediately in date order. This is the raw record.
2. **Post to the ledger.** Periodically copy each journal entry into the relevant accounts as debits and credits.
3. **Total each account.** Add up the debit and credit sides of every account to find its balance.
4. **Draw up a trial balance.** List every account's balance in two columns. The total of all debit balances must equal the total of all credit balances. If they match, your arithmetic is internally consistent; if not, hunt the error.
5. **Prepare summary statements.** From the balanced accounts, produce a statement of profit (income minus expenses) and a statement of position (assets, liabilities, and capital).

## Part 5: Keeping the Books Honest

Records are only as good as the discipline behind them.

- **Record promptly.** Memory fades within hours. Write transactions down at once, in the daybook.
- **Never erase; correct openly.** If you make a mistake, strike it through so the original is still readable and write the correction beside it. Erasures and overwrites are the hallmark of fraud and should be impossible in a trustworthy ledger.
- **Number and date every entry.** Gaps in numbering reveal tampering.
- **Reconcile regularly.** Count the actual cash and stock and compare it to what the books say. Differences must be investigated, not ignored.
- **Separate duties where you can.** In a larger operation, the person who handles cash should not be the only person who keeps its record. This makes theft far harder to hide.

## Verification Criteria

Your accounting is sound when:

- **Every transaction appears in the records** promptly and with a date.
- **In double-entry, debits equal credits** for every transaction and in the trial balance.
- **The accounting equation balances:** assets equal liabilities plus capital.
- **The books match reality:** counted cash and stock agree with the ledger balances.
- **Profit can be stated clearly** for any period: income minus all costs and expenses.
- **Corrections are visible**, never hidden, so the record can be trusted and audited.

## Common Mistakes

- **Delaying entries.** Transactions recorded from memory days later are wrong. Record at once.
- **Mixing personal and business funds.** Keep them in separate accounts or you can never tell whether the business is profitable.
- **Recording only cash.** Ignoring stock, tools, and debts hides the true picture. Track assets and liabilities, not just the cash drawer.
- **Erasing errors.** This destroys trust and conceals fraud. Strike through and correct in the open.
- **Skipping the trial balance.** Without this check, arithmetic errors accumulate unnoticed. Balance the books regularly.
- **Forgetting that voltage of trust comes from consistency.** A ledger kept in inconsistent units or with shifting rules is worthless; pick a unit and a method and hold to them.
