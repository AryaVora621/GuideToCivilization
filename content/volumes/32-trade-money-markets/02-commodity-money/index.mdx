## Overview

When a community grows beyond a handful of families, direct barter — trading goods for goods — breaks down. To swap your surplus grain for a neighbor's tools, you need that neighbor to want grain at the exact moment you want tools. This is the "double coincidence of wants," and it makes trade slow and clumsy. The solution that every civilization has discovered independently is money: a commonly accepted medium that everyone will take, because everyone knows everyone else will take it too.

The earliest money was not coins or paper. It was ordinary useful goods chosen because they had real, intrinsic value and convenient properties: salt, grain, cattle, lumps of metal, cacao beans, dried fish, cloth, and shells. This is **commodity money** — money that is itself a valuable commodity. A bar of salt is worth something whether you spend it or eat it. This intrinsic worth is what gives commodity money its strength and its trust, especially in a society without the institutions needed to back paper currency.

For a rebuilding civilization, commodity money is the natural first step toward a real economy. You do not need a government, a mint, or a banking system. You need only a community that agrees on a useful good to serve as a common measure and medium of exchange. This chapter explains why money emerges, what makes a good commodity money, and how to establish one in a young community.

## The Problem Money Solves

Imagine a village with a blacksmith, a farmer, a weaver, and a healer. The farmer has grain but needs a knife. The blacksmith has knives but does not need grain right now — he needs cloth. The weaver has cloth but wants medicine. The healer wants grain. Under pure barter, the farmer must arrange a chain of trades or wait until the blacksmith happens to want grain. Most trades simply never happen.

Money breaks this deadlock. If everyone accepts, say, salt, the farmer sells grain for salt, buys a knife with salt, and the chain dissolves. Each person trades their surplus for money, then money for what they want, whenever they want. Money serves three functions:

1. **Medium of exchange:** It is accepted in trade by everyone, so you never need a double coincidence of wants.
2. **Unit of account:** It provides a common measuring stick for value. Prices in "units of salt" let you compare the worth of a knife and a basket of grain directly.
3. **Store of value:** It holds worth over time, so you can sell now and buy later, saving the proceeds of your labor.

Commodity money performs all three, and because it has intrinsic value, people trust it even with no authority guaranteeing it.

## The Properties of Good Money

Not every commodity makes good money. Through long experience, humans converged on a set of properties that a good money should have. Judge any candidate against these:

### Durability

Money must not rot, rust, or spoil quickly. Grain is useful but can be eaten by pests and decays. Cattle die. Metal lasts for years. The more durable, the better it stores value over time.

### Portability

Money must be easy to carry and transfer. A cow is valuable but you cannot make small purchases with it, and moving it is hard. Salt, shells, or small metal pieces travel easily. High value in a small, light package is ideal.

### Divisibility

Money must be splittable into smaller units without losing value, so you can pay for small things and make change. A bar of metal can be cut; a cow cannot be divided without killing it. Grain and salt divide naturally by measure.

### Uniformity (Fungibility)

Each unit should be interchangeable with every other unit of the same kind. One measure of clean salt is as good as any other. This lets people trade by count or weight without inspecting each piece. Cattle are poor here — every animal is different.

### Scarcity (Limited Supply)

Money must be hard enough to come by that it holds value. If anyone can gather unlimited amounts cheaply, it becomes worthless (this is the root of inflation). Shells worked as money where they were rare; they collapsed as money when traders flooded a region with them. Metals are valued partly because they take real labor to mine and refine.

### Recognizability

People must be able to identify and verify it easily and trust it is genuine. A familiar shape, a known measure, or a tested metal is recognizable; a substance that is easy to fake is dangerous as money.

No commodity is perfect on every count, but the best moneys score well across most. This is exactly why metals — and eventually gold and silver in particular — won out over time: they are durable, divisible, uniform, scarce, and recognizable, lacking only the perfect portability of small everyday change.

## Historical Examples

- **Salt:** So valued that Roman soldiers were partly paid in it (the root of the word "salary"). Durable, divisible, useful, and scarce in many regions.
- **Cattle:** Among the oldest forms of wealth (the word "pecuniary" comes from the Latin for cattle). Valuable but indivisible and hard to transport.
- **Grain:** Used across the ancient Near East as a unit of account and payment. Divisible and useful but perishable.
- **Cowrie shells:** Used as money across Africa and Asia for centuries — portable, durable, hard to counterfeit, and naturally limited in supply.
- **Metal lumps and bars:** Copper, bronze, silver, and gold by weight, the direct ancestor of coinage. Durable, divisible, scarce, and recognizable.
- **Cacao beans, dried fish, cloth, tobacco:** Various societies used whatever combined value with the properties above.

## Establishing Commodity Money in a New Community

If you are helping a young community move beyond barter, here is a practical path:

### Step 1: Identify a Candidate Commodity

Look for a good that is widely useful, reasonably durable, divisible or countable, hard to fake, and limited in supply. In an agricultural community, salt or grain is natural. Where metalworking exists, small standardized metal pieces are stronger. Choose what fits local resources and what people already trust.

### Step 2: Establish a Standard Measure

Money needs a consistent unit. Define it clearly: a specific weight of salt, a fixed measure of grain, a standard size of metal piece. Agree on a trusted measuring tool (a balance scale, a standard cup, a known weight). Without a reliable standard, every transaction becomes an argument. This is why weights and measures (covered in a related chapter) are inseparable from money.

### Step 3: Let Prices Form

Do not try to dictate what everything costs. Let people quote prices in the chosen unit and trade. Through countless small exchanges, the community settles on rough values — so many measures of salt for a knife, so many for a day's labor. These prices carry real information about scarcity and demand.

### Step 4: Protect the Money's Integrity

Guard against debasement and counterfeiting. If the money is metal, watch for mixing in cheap metals. If it is measured goods, guard the standard measures against tampering. The trust that makes money work is fragile; protecting it is a community responsibility and eventually a role for trusted authorities.

## Worked Example: A Salt Economy

A village near a salt source decides to use salt as money.

1. **Standard:** They agree on a "measure" — a specific scoop, kept by a trusted elder, defining one unit of salt.
2. **Pricing:** Through trade, a knife settles at around 20 measures, a basket of grain at 5, a day's labor at 8.
3. **Trade:** The farmer sells grain for salt measures, then pays the blacksmith in salt for a knife. No double coincidence needed.
4. **Saving:** A craftsman stores salt measures in a dry pot, saving toward a larger purchase later — the store-of-value function at work.
5. **Risk:** When a trader arrives offering vast quantities of cheap salt, the village must decide whether to limit it, or watch their money lose value (inflation). Recognizing this threat is the beginning of monetary policy.

## Verification Criteria

You know a commodity money is working when:

- **Trades happen without barter chains.** People sell surplus for the money and buy what they need separately, without needing matched wants.
- **Prices are quoted in the money.** Goods are valued in units of the chosen commodity, showing it serves as a unit of account.
- **People save it.** Holding the money to spend later shows it stores value and is trusted.
- **A stable standard exists.** A recognized, protected measure or weight defines the unit, and disputes over quantity are rare.
- **The supply stays limited.** The money holds its value over time because its quantity cannot be cheaply expanded.

## Common Mistakes

- **Choosing a perishable money.** Grain and fish rot; their value as money erodes literally. Prefer durable goods for storing value.
- **No agreed standard.** Without a fixed measure or weight, every transaction becomes a negotiation over quantity. Set the standard first.
- **Ignoring supply.** Adopting something that can be gathered cheaply and without limit guarantees the money will inflate to worthlessness.
- **Fixing prices by decree.** Forcing prices away from what trade naturally sets creates shortages and black markets. Let prices form.
- **Failing to guard against fakes and debasement.** Money lives on trust; allow counterfeiting or dilution and the whole system collapses.

Commodity money is the foundation on which every later financial system is built. It needs no government, only a community that agrees on a useful, durable, scarce good to carry value between people and across time. Choose the commodity wisely, standardize its measure, protect its integrity, and let prices form freely, and you give a young society the single most powerful tool for cooperation ever invented: a common money.
