## Overview

You have precious metal—gold, silver, copper. How do you exchange it fairly?

**Barter with raw metal:**
1. Break off a piece
2. Buyer weighs it: is it really 5 grams?
3. Buyer tests purity: is this truly gold, or adulterated?
4. Negotiation: Is this fair for 10 loaves of bread?

This is slow and unreliable.

**Coinage solves this:**
1. A trusted authority stamps a coin with weight and purity marks
2. Buyer knows instantly: this coin is exactly 5 grams of gold
3. No negotiation needed—everyone accepts coins at face value
4. Trade becomes fast and scalable

This chapter teaches coinage: how to create coins, verify their value, and organize a monetary system around them.

## Materials & Tools

**For coin minting:**

- Precious metals (gold, silver, or copper in pure or alloy form)
- Furnace (capable of melting metals at 900–1200°C)
- Crucibles (ceramic or clay, heat-resistant)
- Casting molds or coin blanks (small discs approximately 2–3 cm diameter)
- Dies (metal stamps with relief images and text)
- Striking equipment (hammer and anvil, or mechanical press)
- Balance scale (for weighing coins accurately)
- Purity testing equipment (touchstone, chemical assay supplies)

**For quality control:**

- Weight standards (calibrated weights for verification)
- Measuring tools (calipers for diameter and thickness)
- Testing solutions (for chemical assay of purity)
- Record-keeping materials (paper, ink, ledgers)

## Part 1: Understanding Coin Value

### What Makes a Coin Trustworthy?

A coin's value rests on three factors:

**1. Material content:** The coin contains a known amount of precious metal.

**2. Weight:** The coin is the correct weight (verified by stamps on the surface).

**3. Purity:** The metal is genuine and not adulterated with cheaper metals.

### Determining Coin Value

A coin's value equals: **metal content × metal price + premium for coining**

**Example:**

A silver coin containing 10 grams of pure silver:
- Market price of silver: 0.5 units per gram
- Metal value: 10 grams × 0.5 = 5 units
- Coinage premium (3%): 5 × 0.03 = 0.15 units
- Total coin value: 5.15 units

The coinage premium compensates the mint for the work of minting, the government for administrative costs, and provides some profit.

### The Seigniorage Problem

**Seigniorage** is the profit made by minting coins.

**Example of dishonest coinage:**

A mint produces coins labeled "10 grams of silver" but uses only 9 grams of silver per coin (adulterating with copper). Coins circulate at their stated value (10 grams), but the mint keeps the extra silver.

**Consequences:**

1. **Inflation:** More coins in circulation dilute the purchasing power of each
2. **Loss of confidence:** When discovered, people distrust coins and demand heavier weights or higher purity
3. **Trade collapse:** Without trust in currency, complex commerce becomes impossible

Honest governments minimize seigniorage to maintain trust.

## Part 2: Minting Coins

### Metal Preparation

**Obtaining pure metal:**

1. **Mining:** Extract raw ore
2. **Smelting:** Heat ore to extract metal (from Vol 10, Ch 4)
3. **Refining:** Purify metal to desired purity (typically 90–99%)

For coinage, purity should be consistent—ideally at least 90% of the stated metal.

### Creating Blanks

**Method 1: Casting into molds**

1. Heat pure metal to melting temperature (varies: gold 1064°C, silver 962°C, copper 1085°C)
2. Pour into clay or stone molds in the shape of coins (flat discs)
3. Allow to cool and solidify
4. Remove blanks from molds
5. Trim excess and smooth edges

**Result:** Blank coins, uniform in shape, ready for striking.

**Method 2: Cutting from sheet**

1. Roll molten metal into flat sheets (approximately 3–5 mm thick)
2. Cut circular blanks using dies or shears
3. Trim edges smooth

### Striking Coins

**Die design:**

A coin die has two parts:

- **Obverse (front):** Image and text stamped on one side
- **Reverse (back):** Different image and text on the other side

Dies are carved or cast from steel or hardened bronze.

**Striking process:**

1. Place blank on a solid surface (anvil)
2. Position obverse die on top of blank
3. Strike die with hammer, impressing the image onto the blank
4. Flip coin and blank over
5. Position reverse die on the other side
6. Strike again, impressing the reverse side

**Result:** A coin with images and text on both sides.

### Quality Assurance

**Testing each coin:**

1. **Weight:** Place coin on balance scale, verify it matches the standard weight (within 0.1–0.5 grams tolerance)
2. **Diameter:** Measure with calipers, verify standard size
3. **Thickness:** Measure with calipers, verify uniformity
4. **Purity (optional):** For high-value coins, use chemical assay or touchstone testing (see below)

**Coins that fail inspection:**

- Remelted and recycled, or
- Marked as substandard and sold at discount, or
- Destroyed if severely defective

## Part 3: Verifying Coin Authenticity

### Touchstone Testing

A **touchstone** is a piece of dark stone (basalt or other stone with fine grain). It reveals the color of metals when rubbed against them.

**Procedure:**

1. Rub the coin to be tested on the touchstone, leaving a thin line of metal
2. Rub a known-genuine coin on the touchstone nearby, for comparison
3. Compare the color and intensity of the two lines
4. Genuine metal leaves a characteristic bright line; adulterated or base metal leaves a duller line
5. An experienced assayer can estimate purity to within 1–2%

**Limitation:** Detects major adulteration, but not precise purity.

### Chemical Assay

More precise testing using acid dissolution.

**Silver assay (simplified):**

1. Cut a small sample from the coin (approximately 1 gram)
2. Dissolve in nitric acid
3. The sample dissolves; silver remains visible
4. Test the dissolved portion against standards to measure purity

**Limitation:** Destructive (coin is damaged); done only for verification, not routine checking.

### Edge Inspection

Coins can be counterfeited or clipped (edges shaved to remove metal while keeping the face).

**Solutions:**

- **Milled edges:** Machine-cut vertical lines on coin edges (difficult to counterfeit)
- **Lettered edges:** Text stamped on edges (distinctive and difficult to replicate)
- **Rim design:** Raised edge on coin face (makes shaving obvious)

## Part 4: Organizing a Coinage System

### Standards

A government establishes monetary standards:

**Weight standard:** "A silver coin shall weigh 10 grams"

**Purity standard:** "A silver coin shall be 90% pure silver, 10% copper"

**Diameter/thickness:** Fixed dimensions ensure coins are recognizable

**Design:** Specific obverse and reverse designs prevent counterfeiting

### Minting Capacity

**Scale of operation:**

- Small mint: 10,000–50,000 coins per year (manual striking)
- Medium mint: 100,000–500,000 coins per year (with multiple dies and workers)
- Large mint: 1,000,000+ coins per year (mechanical presses, many dies)

**Factors limiting production:**

- Die wear (dies become dull after striking 10,000–50,000 coins, requiring replacement)
- Die creation time (carving a quality die takes weeks or months)
- Worker availability and skill

### Supply and Demand

**Maintaining monetary stability:**

Too many coins → inflation (prices rise as purchasing power decreases)
Too few coins → deflation (prices fall; economically painful)

A government must balance:
- Population growth (need more coins)
- Trade volume growth (need more coins)
- Metal availability (limits production)
- Inflation risk (too much coining causes problems)

Experienced governments mint coins equal to approximately 5–10% per year of the circulating supply.

## Worked Example: Establishing a Silver Coinage System

**Scenario:** A new kingdom wants to establish a standard silver coinage.

**Step 1 — Determine standards:**

1. Weight: 10 grams (easy to remember, practical for transactions)
2. Purity: 95% silver, 5% copper (adds durability, near-pure silver value)
3. Diameter: 25 mm
4. Thickness: 2.5 mm
5. Design: Obverse shows king's profile; reverse shows kingdom's symbol

**Step 2 — Create dies:**

1. Commission a master engraver to carve dies (timeline: 2–3 months)
2. Create 4 obverse dies and 4 reverse dies (allow for die wear)
3. Cost: approximately 100 grams of silver equivalent per die

**Step 3 — Establish mint facility:**

1. Build minting facility with furnace, anvils, and striking equipment
2. Hire mint master (experienced in coining)
3. Hire 10–20 workers (to strike coins)
4. Establish quality assurance procedures

**Step 4 — Initial minting:**

1. Produce first batch: 100,000 coins
2. Each coin contains 9.5 grams of pure silver (10g × 95%)
3. Total silver used: 950 kg
4. Time required: 2–3 months
5. Cost: 50 kg of silver for seigniorage (5% of total)

**Step 5 — Distribution and adoption:**

1. Distribute coins to government officials and merchants
2. Accept coins in payment of taxes
3. Official decree: coins are legal tender at face value
4. Merchants adopt coins for trade (10-gram silver coin = 4 units of value)

**Step 6 — Ongoing minting:**

1. Mint 50,000 coins per year to replace worn coins and support economic growth
2. Replace dies every 30,000 coins (die wear reduces image quality)
3. Regularly test coins to prevent counterfeiting and ensure standards

**Result:** A stable monetary system based on standardized silver coins. Trade becomes efficient; large commercial transactions become practical.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Excessive seigniorage.** Minting coins with less metal than claimed causes inflation and loss of trust. Keep seigniorage low (1–3%).

2. **Inconsistent weight.** Coins of the same denomination must have consistent weight. Variation causes confusion and distrust.

3. **Poor purity control.** If coins labeled "silver" contain significant copper, merchants will demand heavier coins or discount their value.

4. **Die wear neglected.** Dies wear out after approximately 30,000 strikes. Using worn dies produces unclear images, enabling counterfeiting.

5. **Over-minting.** Producing too many coins causes inflation. Limit annual minting to 5–10% of circulating supply.

6. **Under-minting.** Too few coins restricts trade and economic growth. Monitor demand and mint accordingly.

7. **Counterfeiting not addressed.** Without anti-counterfeiting measures (milled edges, distinctive designs), counterfeiters undermine the system.

8. **No quality assurance.** Without testing coins regularly, defects and counterfeits circulate unchecked.

9. **Poor die design.** Dies that are too simple or unclear don't prevent counterfeiting. Use detailed, distinctive designs.

10. **Mixing metals unpredictably.** If some coins are 90% pure and others are 80%, confusion results. Standardize all coins to the same purity.
