## Overview

Knowledge dies with the people who hold it. In the first months after collapse, you will lose access to digital libraries, the internet, and likely many educated people. Your settlement will collapse into illiteracy within 2–3 generations if you do not actively preserve the information in this guide. This is not hypothetical: in the Western Roman Empire's collapse (300–600 CE), literacy fell from 20% to 5%, and vast bodies of agricultural, engineering, and medical knowledge disappeared into oral tradition and were lost forever.

This chapter teaches you how to prevent that loss. You will learn to copy by hand efficiently, set up a teaching system that spreads knowledge across your population, and design archival systems that survive fire, water, rodents, and time. Your goal is simple: by year 5 post-collapse, at least 30% of your population can read, at least one person can read every major technical field, and critical knowledge exists in at least 3 independent copies in 3 separate locations.

## Materials & Tools

**For copying by hand:**
- Paper (or writing surface: bark, vellum, papyrus, ostrakon/pottery shards—see Vol 4: Language & Communication)
- Ink (soot mixed with animal fat or plant gum, or berry juice reduced over heat)
- Writing tools (quill, reed, charcoal stick—sharpen on stone)
- Ruler (any straight edge; a taut string for lines)
- Binding (twine, leather strips, wooden covers for durability)

**For long-term storage:**
- Sealed containers (ceramic jars with wax seals, wooden boxes lined with wax or pitch)
- Desiccant (charcoal, lime, salt to absorb moisture)
- Rodent-proof storage (elevated on stone or ceramic, away from walls)
- Multiple locations (at least 3 separate buildings, ideally different settlements)

**For teaching infrastructure:**
- Slate boards and chalk (alternative: sand table with stylus)
- Candles or oil lamps for evening teaching
- Paper for student notes

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Identify the Critical Knowledge to Preserve First (Weeks 1–2)

You cannot copy everything. Your time is limited. Copy in this order:

**Tier 1 (Copy in month 1–2):**
- Vol 3: Survival (water, fire, shelter, hunting, food preservation, first aid)
- Vol 4: Medicine (anatomy, wound care, fever management, herbal pharmacopoeia)
- Vol 5: Sanitation Engineering (latrines, water testing, disease control)
- Vol 6: Agriculture (crop selection, rotation, composting, pest control)
- Vol 8: Society & Governance (property, law, taxation, record-keeping)

**Tier 2 (Copy in months 3–6):**
- Remaining volumes in the 16-Step Progression: energy, manufacturing, transportation, mathematics, language.

**Tier 3 (Copy after year 1):**
- Specialized technical knowledge (advanced metallurgy, semiconductor technology, telecommunications).

Assign one person per tier-1 volume. They are your "knowledge custodian" for that subject. Their job is to create copies and teach others.

### Step 2: Hand-Copy Critical Chapters Efficiently (Ongoing)

Hand-copying is labor-intensive. Do not copy verbatim—copy with compression.

**Method A: Parallel condensed copying (fastest)**
- Read a chapter.
- Condense it to 60% of original length by removing examples and keeping only essential steps.
- Copy the condensed version in your clearest handwriting.
- Time per chapter: 8–12 hours for dense content, 3–5 hours for procedural content.
- Example: a 5,000-word medicine chapter becomes 3,000 words of copied steps.

**Method B: Full copying (most reliable)**
- Copy verbatim with perfect accuracy.
- Use for chapters with exact measurements (medicine dosages, fermentation salt ratios, alloy compositions).
- Time per chapter: 20–30 hours.
- Reserve this for 5–10 chapters per subject where precision is non-negotiable.

**Method C: Collective scribing (fastest for scale)**
- One person reads aloud while 5–10 scribes write.
- Reader pauses every 2–3 sentences to allow scribes to catch up.
- Accuracy is lower; use for less critical content (history, theory, background).
- Time per chapter: 4–6 hours for 10 scribes.

Rotate the copying task. Do not leave it to one person; knowledge custodian + one backup per volume.

**Paper conservation trick:** Use both sides of paper (front and back). Leave 1 cm margins. Use small, tight handwriting. Paper is precious; every surface counts.

### Step 3: Create Your Master Archive (Month 1)

**Location:**
- Choose a building that is dry, cool, and fire-resistant (stone church, basement, cave, stone tower).
- If not fire-resistant, build a stone or clay oven-like structure within it.
- Avoid ground level (flood risk) and top floor (heat, rain damage, vulnerability to collapse).
- Basement or internal room of a multi-story stone building is ideal.

**Storage setup:**
- Build wooden shelving with 5 cm spacing between shelves (air circulation).
- Use ceramic jars or wooden boxes with lids and wax seals.
- Place desiccant (charcoal) in sealed containers with documents.
- Elevate all boxes on stone or ceramic feet at least 15 cm above floor (rodent and water protection).
- Keep the room locked and temperature-stable.

**Contents of master archive (by end of year 1):**
- 3 full copies of each Tier 1 volume
- 1 copy of each Tier 2 volume
- 1 master index of all contents, kept separately
- Backup copies of the master index in 2 other locations

**Inspection schedule:**
- Monthly: open containers, check for moisture, mold, or pest damage. Replace desiccant.
- Yearly: assess condition of oldest copies. Replace any showing degradation.

### Step 4: Build Your Teaching Network (Months 2–12)

Knowledge preserved but not taught dies with time. Create a cascade of teaching.

**Teaching structure:**

**Level 1 (Months 2–3): Knowledge custodian trains 2–3 secondary teachers per volume**
- Weekly sessions, 2 hours each
- Teacher reads from the original guide and explains
- Secondary teachers take notes, ask questions, and identify confusing sections
- Goal: each secondary teacher can teach the material to others

**Level 2 (Months 4–6): Secondary teachers train apprentices (5–10 per teacher)**
- Apprentices are selected based on literacy (or literacy potential if you are starting from zero—see Vol 4: Language & Communication)
- Weekly sessions, 2 hours
- Apprentices learn by doing: practice copying, solve problems, teach each other

**Level 3 (Months 7–12): Apprentices teach the community**
- Monthly workshops, open to all
- Practical focus: everyone who attends learns one complete procedure (water purification, wound care, planting, etc.)
- Goal: 30% of the community has hands-on knowledge of at least one technical skill

**Example teaching cascade for medicine:**
- Month 2: Knowledge custodian (physician or medical trainee) trains 2 secondary teachers (you select motivated people with any medical background, or excellent memory)
- Month 4: Each secondary teacher trains 5 apprentices in specific modules (anatomy, wound care, fever management, etc.)
- Month 6: Apprentices lead weekly clinics teaching the community first aid
- Month 12: 30+ people in the settlement can treat wounds, recognize infections, and manage fevers

**Teaching format (all levels):**
1. Demonstration (show how)
2. Practice under supervision (students repeat)
3. Independent practice (students do it alone while teacher watches)
4. Teaching others (students explain to a peer—best way to cement learning)

### Step 5: Establish Redundant Archives in Other Locations (Months 3–6)

Do not keep all copies in one place. Fire, flood, invasion, or collapse of the main settlement will destroy everything.

**Secondary archive (settlement #2):**
- If your group has a second settlement within 20 km, place a complete copy of Tier 1 volumes there.
- Same storage protocol as master archive.
- Designate a custodian in settlement #2.
- Quarterly exchange: messengers between settlements check copies for damage and replace if needed.

**Tertiary archive (distributed backup):**
- If you have 5+ secondary teachers, give each a leather-bound, sealed copy of their subject area (medicine, agriculture, law, etc.) to keep at home.
- They are responsible for the copy and must show it to the master archive custodian annually for inspection.
- This protects against total loss of the main archive.

**Geographic dispersal (by year 2):**
- If your settlement grows to 5,000+ people or spawns new settlements, place one copy of all Tier 1 volumes in each settlement.
- This ensures knowledge survives regional collapse.

### Step 6: Create a Permanent Index and Update Protocol (Month 1)

**Master index (single document, ~5 pages):**
```
GUIDE TO CIVILIZATION — MASTER INDEX

Vol 1: Civilization Roadmap
  Ch 1: How to Use This Guide
  Ch 2: The 16-Step Progression
  Ch 3: Failure Modes
  Ch 4: Knowledge Preservation
  [Location in archive: Shelf A, Box 1]

Vol 3: Survival
  Ch 1: Water Purification
  Ch 2: Fire-Making
  ...
  [Location in archive: Shelf B, Boxes 3–5]

[Continue for all volumes]
```

**Update protocol:**
- Each time a new copy is made, add one line: "[Date] Copy #X of [volume] created by [name], stored in [location]"
- Once per year, the master custodian reviews and verifies all locations listed match reality.
- If a copy is damaged or lost, update the index and begin replacement copying.

### Step 7: Plan for Transmission Across Generations (Year 1+)

Knowledge preserved but not transmitted to the next generation is lost.

**Adult literacy (target: 50% of adults can read by year 3):**
- Begin teaching reading to all ages simultaneously (see Vol 7: Education)
- Use this guide as the primary textbook—people learn to read by reading survival-critical knowledge
- Creates incentive to learn and applies reading immediately

**Succession planning for knowledge custodians:**
- By year 2, each knowledge custodian has selected and trained a replacement
- By year 3, the original custodian mentors the replacement for 6 months
- By year 4, full handoff occurs; original custodian becomes advisor
- This prevents loss of tacit knowledge (the unwritten understanding of how to teach and curate)

**Oral tradition backup:**
- For the 3–4 most critical procedures (water purification, wound care, food preservation), train at least 5 people to teach by rote (memorized, not read).
- Teach mnemonic songs, rhymes, or visual stories to encode steps.
- Example: a 10-step wound-care sequence encoded as "10 fingers, 10 steps" with each step corresponding to finger techniques taught in ritual form.
- This survives paper loss and reaches people who cannot read.

## Verification

**Success looks like:**
- By month 3: Master archive exists with at least 2 copies of Tier 1 volumes, all in sealed storage
- By month 6: At least 3 secondary teachers exist per major subject area
- By month 12: 30%+ of the population has attended at least one teaching session and can execute one procedure (water purification, basic wound care, simple cooking technique)
- By year 2: 50%+ of adults are literate (can read this guide with assistance)
- By year 3: At least one secondary archive exists in another settlement or location

**Failure looks like:**
- Copies are made but stored carelessly, becoming water-damaged or moldy
- Teaching begins but attendance drops because no one enforces participation
- Knowledge custodians are selected but not relieved of other duties, so teaching never happens
- The guide is copied but not condensed; copying takes so long that only 20% of content is completed in year 1

## Common Mistakes

1. **"We will digitize everything"** — You have no electricity. USB drives degrade without power. Write on paper. Paper lasts 500+ years if stored dry. Digital storage fails in 10–20 years without active maintenance (power, format updates). Accept this and plan accordingly.

2. **"I will copy the whole guide myself"** — One person cannot copy 45 volumes. By year 1, you will have copied 2–3 volumes and burned out. Delegate. Train 10 people to copy in parallel.

3. **"We don't need archives if people know the material"** — People forget, people die, people leave. Archives are insurance. A single plague that kills 20% of your population will erase the knowledge of dozens of people. Archives survive plagues.

4. **"Let's wait on copying until we are more stable"** — Stability takes 1–2 years minimum. Start copying in month 1, in parallel with immediate survival needs. The copying IS the stabilization; it secures knowledge before you lose trained people to disease or departure.

5. **"We will teach when we have time"** — You will never have time. Teaching must be mandatory (1 hour per week per person) or it will not happen. Make it part of community rhythm—Sunday mornings, or after the evening meal.

6. **"Only the custodian needs to know each subject"** — If the custodian dies, you lose everything. Always train 2–3 backup teachers. Knowledge is not secure until at least 3 people can teach it.

7. **"Seal the archive and do not open it"** — Sealed archives accumulate moisture and mold over time. Open containers monthly, inspect, replace desiccant, air out documents. A "sealed" archive is a dying archive.
