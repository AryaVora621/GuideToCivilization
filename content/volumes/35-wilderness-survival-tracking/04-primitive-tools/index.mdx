## Overview

Flint is sharper than modern steel scalpels. A hand-sized piece of flint can become a knife, arrowhead, and scraper. Unlike bone tools which take time to carve, flint can be knapped (shaped by controlled fracturing) in minutes once you understand the technique.

This chapter teaches you to identify knappable stone, understand the mechanics of flint knapping, and produce functional cutting tools from raw stone. This skill separates good hunters from great ones.

## Prerequisites

- Vol 2, Ch 1 — Tracking and Sign (to understand hunting needs)
- Patience and practice — this skill improves with repetition

## Materials & Tools

### Raw Stone

- **Flint:** Most common; found in chalk deposits and gravels; light gray to dark brown
- **Chert:** Similar to flint; found in limestone regions; varies widely in color
- **Obsidian:** Volcanic glass; found near old volcanic areas; black, green, or red; sharper than flint but more brittle
- **Jasper and agate:** Acceptable but harder than flint; slower to work
- **Materials to avoid:** Granite (will not flake cleanly), sandstone (too soft)

### Knapping Tools

- **Hammer stone:** Fist-sized stone (granite, basalt) for striking; at least 1 kg
- **Pressure flaker:** Small stick or bone (10–15 cm) for fine pressure work
- **Soft hammer:** Antler or bone (500g); less powerful than stone
- **Striking platform edge:** Another flint piece (to prepare the surface)
- **Chipping away spot:** Protected area (place stones to catch flakes)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Testing Stone for Knappability

**Step 1: Find knappable stone.**

Look in river gravels, stream beds, or exposed stone faces. Strike a small piece with your hammer stone. Knappable stone fractures in a predictable cone shape (called Hertzian fracture) and produces a sharp edge. Non-knappable stone shatters into random chunks.

**Step 2: Collect raw nodules.**

Gather 2–3 fist-sized pieces. Not all will work, so collect more than you need.

**Step 3: Check for inclusions.**

Look inside the stone (you may need to break a test piece). Flint should be uniform in color. Visible crystals, clouds, or layering may mean the stone will not flake cleanly. Discard pieces with inclusions.

**Step 4: Test the edge.**

Strike a deliberate flake from the piece. The edge should be sharp enough to cut skin easily and should hold together when bent.

### Part 2: Preparing a Striking Platform

**Step 1: Select the core (the piece you will work).**

A fist-sized piece is ideal for a beginner. Larger cores take more skill.

**Step 2: Strike away an old weathered surface.**

The outside of many flints is weathered white. Strike this surface away first to reach fresh, knappable stone. Use your hammer stone and strike at a glancing angle.

**Step 3: Create a striking platform.**

You need a flat surface to strike flakes from. Look for a natural flat spot on the core. If there is none, strike deliberately to create one.

**Step 4: Prepare the edge.**

The edge of your striking platform should be slightly rounded (not a sharp 90° angle). If it is too sharp, it will shatter. Use your hammer stone to tap the edge gently to round it.

**Step 5: Test the platform.**

Strike a light blow on the platform edge. The flake should detach from the core cleanly. If it shatters, your striking platform is wrong — reposition or reprepare it.

### Part 3: Making Flakes

**Step 1: Position the core.**

Hold the core in one hand, wrapped in cloth or leather (flint is sharp). The striking platform should face away from your body. Rest the core on your leg or a stone.

**Step 2: Strike with the hammer stone.**

Strike the edge of the platform with the hammer stone at a 60–90° angle (almost perpendicular to the core's surface). The blow should be deliberate and swift, not a glancing tap.

**Step 3: Detach the flake.**

If the strike is correct, a flake will detach from the core. The flake will have a sharp edge. The core remains for the next flake.

**Step 4: Inspect the flake.**

The flake should have:
- One sharp edge (the edge farthest from the striking platform)
- A striking platform (the place you hit)
- Ripples radiating from the platform (called ripple marks — they show you the direction of force)

**Step 5: Repeat.**

Create a new striking platform on the core and repeat. Each flake is smaller but still useful.

### Part 4: Making a Sharp Cutting Edge (Hand Axe or Knife Blade)

**Step 1: Create a bifacial edge (sharp on two sides).**

Strike flakes from both sides of your core to create a double-sided cutting edge. Work from the edge toward the center. Your blade should thin gradually, not abruptly.

**Step 2: Use a soft hammer for final shaping.**

Antler or bone (softer than stone) produces smaller, finer flakes. Use the soft hammer for the last 5–10 strikes to refine the edge.

**Step 3: Use pressure flaking for extreme refinement.**

For very fine edges, use a pressure flaker (a small bone or stick). Position it on the edge you want to sharpen and press (do not strike) perpendicular to the blade. Small flakes will detach. This is the most advanced technique and requires practice.

**Step 4: Test the edge.**

Run your thumb (very carefully, moving away from the edge) along the blade. It should catch slightly on your skin. If it slides smoothly, it is dull.

### Part 5: Making an Arrowhead or Spear Point

**Step 1: Start with a small flake (2–3 cm).**

Select a flake with one sharp edge. The striking platform becomes the base; the sharp edge becomes the point.

**Step 2: Thin the base for hafting.**

You need to attach this point to a shaft. Thin the base (the striking platform end) by striking flakes parallel to the blade's length. The base should be 3–5 mm thick.

**Step 3: Create a notch or tang for hafting (optional but recommended).**

Using pressure flaking, create small notches or a narrower base (called a tang) for tying to the shaft. This is where sinew will bind the point.

**Step 4: Create a sharp point.**

Work both sides of the flake to thin it toward a point. Use a soft hammer or pressure flaker.

**Step 5: Test the point.**

Strike it gently against bone or leather. The point should penetrate without breaking.

### Part 6: Making a Scraper

**Step 1: Select a flake with one straight edge.**

The long edge becomes your scraping surface.

**Step 2: Create a scraping edge.**

Use your soft hammer or pressure flaker to work one side of the blade only, creating a steep angle (60–80° from the flake surface). This steep edge is the scraper.

**Step 3: Blunt the opposite edge for gripping.**

Work the opposite edge to create a blunt back. This is where your hand grips the scraper.

**Step 4: Test.**

Scrape it across hide or wood. It should remove a thin layer without catching. If it catches, the angle is too steep; rework it.

### Part 7: Recycling and Re-sharpening

**Step 1: Blunt edges can be re-flaked.**

If an edge becomes blunt, strike a fresh flake off it using your hammer stone.

**Step 2: Maintain the core.**

As you produce flakes, the core gets smaller. When it becomes a small hand-sized piece, you can switch to pressure flaking for finer tools or discard it.

**Step 3: Preserve your best tools.**

Wrap sharp edges in leather or cloth to protect them. A good flint blade is irreplaceable in survival situations.

## Verification

**Your flint knapping is successful if:**

- Each strike produces a predictable flake
- Flakes have one sharp edge and a clear striking platform
- You can produce 3–5 usable flakes per hour
- Your arrowheads do not shatter when struck against bone
- Your scrapers remove hide or wood without catching

**Test your tools:**
- Use an arrowhead to penetrate leather stretched over a log. A good point should go halfway through.
- Use a scraper to remove a thin curl of bark. If the tool catches, the angle is too steep.
- Use a blade to cut sinew or plant material. The edge should cut cleanly.

## Common Mistakes

- **Core too small or too large.** A fist-sized core is easiest for beginners. Smaller cores require more precision; larger cores require more strength.
- **Striking platform wrong angle.** The edge should be rounded and at 60–90° to the surface you strike. Too shallow, and the flake will not detach. Too steep, and the platform will shatter.
- **Striking too lightly.** Flint knapping requires decisive blows. Timid taps do not work. Strike with confidence and full force.
- **Reusing the same platform.** After 3–5 flakes from one platform, prepare a new one. Overused platforms produce poor flakes.
- **Working obsidian like flint.** Obsidian is sharper but more brittle. Use lighter blows and thicker pieces. Obsidian is excellent for blades but poor for hand axes.
- **Not testing the stone first.** Some flint looks perfect but will not flake cleanly. Always test a piece before committing hours of work.
- **Creating points that are too thin.** Thin points break easily. A functional arrowhead is 2–3 mm thick at the base, tapering to a sharp point. Not razor-thin.
- **Forgetting to protect sharp edges.** An unprotected flint blade cuts your hands and degrades quickly. Wrap all sharp edges in leather or cloth.
