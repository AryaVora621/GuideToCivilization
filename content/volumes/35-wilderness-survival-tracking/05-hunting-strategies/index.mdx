## Overview

Animals are predictable. They follow the same trails. They bed in the same thickets. They move at the same times. They flee from the same threats. Hunting is not about chance—it is about understanding the animal and positioning yourself where it cannot escape you.

This chapter teaches three hunting strategies that do not require guns or modern weapons: ambush (waiting in position), drive (forcing the animal toward a kill line), and snare placement (letting the trap do the work). Each strategy exploits animal behavior and the landscape. Each works best in specific terrain and for specific animals.

Success in hunting comes from knowledge: knowing the animal's daily movement, understanding the wind's direction, recognizing escape routes. A skilled hunter with a spear or bow will kill more game than an unskilled hunter with a rifle.

## Prerequisites

- Ability to read animal sign: tracks, scat, browse, bedding areas (Vol 2, Ch 1)
- Understanding of animal behavior: daily movement patterns, fear responses, social structure (from observation and chapter 1)
- Basic bow or spear-making skills (Vol 2, Ch 4 or Vol 3, Ch 9)
- Physical fitness to stalk, wait, or drive animals over distance
- Patience: hunting requires hours of stillness or waiting

## Materials & Tools

**For ambush hunting:**
- Bow or spear (made per Vol 2, Ch 4)
- Arrows or spear points (sharp; replacements)
- Camouflage clothing (neutral colors or animal skin)
- Wind indicator (grass, cloth, smoke) to read wind direction
- Water bottle
- Knife for dressing kill

**For drive hunting:**
- 3–8 people (smaller animals need fewer; elk or bison need more)
- Noisemakers (branches, stones, animal horns, drums)
- Barriers or funnel points (natural terrain or constructed)
- Ambush crew with spears or bows positioned at kill point
- Physical stamina and coordination

**For snare placement:**
- Strong cordage (vine, sinew, twisted plant fiber tested to breaking point >20 kg for large game)
- Stakes or anchors (iron if available; wood if not—must be driven deep)
- Trigger mechanism (deadfall weight, spring pole, or figure-four trigger)
- Knife or cutting tool
- Game trail knowledge

## Part 1: Ambush Hunting

Ambush exploits the animal's predictability. Every animal follows trails, drinks at water sources, beds in known places, and moves at consistent times. You intercept it by waiting in position.

### Step 1: Scout the Animal's Daily Movement

Spend 1–3 days observing without hunting:

1. Identify where the animal beds (usually in thick cover or high ground away from predators)
2. Identify where it feeds (open areas, vegetation, water source location)
3. Identify the trail it uses to connect these areas (the most worn path; check for fresh tracks daily)
4. Note the time it moves: most ungulates move at dawn and dusk (2–3 hours after sunrise; 2 hours before sunset)
5. Determine the wind direction during these movement times (wind should be in your face so it does not carry your scent downwind toward the animal)

**Note:** Fresh scat and track depth tell you if the animal passed today or days ago. Soft scat indicates recent feeding; dry scat indicates the animal has been away for hours or days.

### Step 2: Choose Your Ambush Position

Select a position that:

1. **Intercepts the animal's most-used trail:** Not all trails are equally used. The most worn path shows heaviest use.
2. **Has concealment:** You must be hidden from the animal's sight. Use terrain (rocks, ridges, fallen trees) or vegetation (dense brush, tall grass). The animal must not see you until it is within killing range (spear: less than 10 meters; bow: less than 30 meters, depending on arrow power).
3. **Is downwind:** The animal's sense of smell is its primary defense. Position yourself so the wind carries your scent away from the animal or perpendicular to it (never upwind).
4. **Allows escape routing:** Understand where the animal will flee if it spots you and misses. Do not ambush if it flees directly away from you and vanishes into forest.
5. **Is elevated when possible:** High ground gives you a better view of the animal's approach and better accuracy for spears or arrows.

### Step 3: Prepare the Ambush Position

1. **Arrive before the animal moves:** For dawn movement, arrive 1.5 hours before sunrise. For dusk movement, arrive 2 hours before sunset. The animal may move earlier or later than typical.
2. **Clear your line of sight:** Remove branches or vegetation that block your strike. Move slowly and make minimal sound.
3. **Prepare your weapon:** Have your spear or bow ready with an arrow nocked (an arrow ready to draw). A spear should be held in a position where you can strike quickly (low across your body or held vertically depending on the terrain).
4. **Test the wind:** Drop grass or dust and watch which direction it drifts. If the wind has shifted, relocate if possible.
5. **Sit and wait:** Movement gives away your position. Remain still. Breathe slowly. Do not fidget.

### Step 4: The Strike

When the animal is within killing range (and before it detects you):

1. **Draw and shoot or strike:** For a bow, draw smoothly and release toward the animal's vitals (chest cavity, heart-lung area). For a spear, thrust with full force into the same area.
2. **Vital zones by species:**
   - Ungulates (deer, elk): Heart-lung cavity is behind the front shoulder, approximately one-third up from the belly line. A hit there results in rapid incapacitation (30 seconds to 2 minutes).
   - Wild boar: Chest cavity, but boar thick skin and bone can deflect attacks. Aim for the chest or spine.
   - Small game (rabbits, hares): Head or spine. Quick kill.
3. **Follow up immediately:** After the strike, do not wait. If the animal is still moving, pursue and strike again. Do not let it flee far.

### Step 5: Dress and Transport

1. **Kill the animal completely:** If it is still alive, strike again. Do not let it suffer longer than necessary.
2. **Bleed it:** Cut the throat or chest to ensure rapid death and drain blood (meat without blood tastes worse and spoils faster).
3. **Open the body cavity:** Cut from sternum to pelvis, avoiding the gut contents (which spoil the meat if they spill). Remove the organs carefully.
4. **Cool the meat:** If it is warm outside, remove the internal organs quickly to cool the meat. Intestines generate heat.
5. **Transport:** Carry the meat back to your camp, or cache it if you cannot carry it all at once (wrap in leaves or place high in a tree away from scavengers).

## Part 2: Drive Hunting

Drive hunting uses a group to force an animal toward a prepared kill line. It is more efficient for larger animals in open or semi-open terrain.

### Understanding Drive Mechanics

An animal under attack retreats from noise and pressure. By creating noise and advancing as a coordinated group, you force the animal in a chosen direction. The kill team waits at the end of that escape route and intercepts.

This works for:
- Herds (bison, elk, wild horses): Create pressure on the herd's flanks and rear; it moves forward together.
- Solitary ungulates: Drive it toward a funnel (narrow terrain).
- Boar and larger predators: Not recommended for bears or big cats; too dangerous.

### Step 1: Identify the Terrain

Choose a location with:

1. **Natural funneling:** The terrain should naturally narrow (a canyon, a ridgeline with drop-offs on both sides, a narrow river valley). Animals instinctively follow terrain rather than cross open areas.
2. **Escape points to control:** Identify where the animal will try to escape. The kill team will position here.
3. **Approach areas:** The drive team needs clear approach routes to move through and make noise.

Example terrain:
- A canyon with a narrow entrance: the drive team pushes from the wide end; the kill team positions at the narrow entrance.
- A ridgeline: the drive team advances on both flanks, funneling the animal toward the ridge's end; the kill team waits there.

### Step 2: Prepare the Kill Line

1. **Position the kill team:** 4–8 people with spears or bows positioned at the funnel point or escape route. They must have clear sight lines.
2. **Construct barriers if needed:** If the terrain is not naturally funneling, construct a simple barrier (fence of branches, stacked rocks) to guide the animal into the kill zone.
3. **Brief the kill team:** Everyone should know:
   - Where the animal will appear
   - The signal to attack (a shout, a horn blast, a raised hand)
   - Where to strike (vital zones)
4. **Ensure all have weapons ready:** Spears should be held or placed within arm's reach. Bows should have arrows nocked.

### Step 3: Execute the Drive

1. **Position the drive team:** 3–6 people on the opposite side from the kill team, spread out along the animal's current location or bedding area.
2. **Advance slowly and make noise:** Start at a distance. Move forward steadily, making noise with branches, shouts, or drums. Do NOT run; controlled advance is more effective.
3. **Maintain pressure:** As the animal flees, keep advancing and making noise. Do not let it slow or circle back.
4. **Maintain a line:** The drive team should maintain a rough line (not too tightly bunched, not too spread out). This prevents the animal from slipping through gaps.

### Step 4: The Kill

When the animal reaches the kill zone:

1. **Attack simultaneously:** The kill team strikes as one, from multiple angles if possible. This prevents the animal from escaping (if one route is blocked, another is not).
2. **Target vital zones:** Same as ambush (heart-lung for ungulates; spine or head for smaller animals).
3. **Follow up:** Keep attacking until the animal stops moving.

### Step 5: Safety and Efficiency

**Dangers:**
- An animal cornered is desperate and dangerous. Boar and elk can gore or trample. Always have an escape route for the kill team.
- The drive team may injure themselves in terrain. Stay alert.
- Coordination failure (kill team attacks too early, before the animal enters the zone) results in wasted effort.

**Efficiency notes:**
- Drive hunting works best in fall (animals are moving more due to rut and migration) and less in summer (animals are more dispersed).
- Larger herds are easier to drive than solitary animals (herd animals follow each other; solitary animals are more unpredictable).
- Early morning or evening is best (animals are already moving).

## Part 3: Snare Placement

Snares work 24 hours. They do not require a skilled archer or spear-thrower. A snare placed on a frequently-used trail will eventually catch something.

### Understanding Snare Types

**Deadfall trap (weight falls on animal):**
- Used for: small to medium game (rabbits, hares, grouse, small carnivores)
- Mechanics: An animal trigger releases a heavy weight (rock, log) that crushes the animal
- Advantages: Quick kill; simple to construct; requires no cordage
- Disadvantages: Weight must be heavy enough; requires precise trigger

**Spring snare (cord attached to bent sapling):**
- Used for: medium game (hares, pheasants, small deer)
- Mechanics: A bent sapling is held in tension by a trigger. When the animal touches the trigger, the sapling springs up, hanging the animal by a loop around the neck
- Advantages: Quick kill; can catch multiple sizes of animals depending on sapling strength
- Disadvantages: Requires a suitable sapling; cordage must not break
- **Warning:** This method is inhumane and slow if the animal is too large (slow death by strangulation). Use deadfall for small animals, but avoid snaring animals so large that hanging kills them slowly.

**Pit trap (animal falls into hidden pit):**
- Used for: large game (elk, bison, boar)
- Mechanics: A concealed pit is dug across a game trail. An animal falls in and cannot escape.
- Advantages: Works for very large animals; communal kill (multiple people can finish the animal)
- Disadvantages: Requires significant labor (digging); animal is injured, not killed
- Use: Pit traps followed by a spear thrust to finish the animal

### Step 1: Scout the Snare Site

1. **Identify high-traffic game trails:** Walk the area and look for:
   - Worn earth (bare soil from repeated passage)
   - Scat on the trail
   - Bent or broken vegetation at animal height
   - Fresh tracks
2. **Test the trail:** Place a stick across it. If the stick is knocked over the next day, the trail is actively used.
3. **Identify the funnel point:** Game trails often narrow at valleys, between trees, or near water. Snare at the narrowest point.
4. **Determine what species uses the trail:** Examine scat and track size. Snare placement and size depends on the animal.

### Step 2: Construct a Snare (Spring or Deadfall)

**Spring snare construction:**

1. **Find a suitable sapling:** It should be 5–10 centimeters in diameter (thick enough to support the animal but not so thick it is hard to bend). It should be 3–4 meters from the trail.
2. **Bend the sapling:** Pull it down to ground level and hold it there with a trigger mechanism (see Trigger Mechanisms below). The sapling will be under tension.
3. **Attach the loop:** Tie the cordage to the bent sapling. The other end forms a loop 15–20 centimeters in diameter, positioned at animal head height (approximately 0.5 meters high for deer; 0.1 meters for hares).
4. **Position the loop:** It should hang just above the trail at head height. The animal walks through it and the loop tightens around its neck as it moves forward and the sapling is released.

**Deadfall construction:**

1. **Select a weight:** A rock (10–20 kg) or a heavy log (10–15 kg, depending on the animal size). Test its weight by holding it—can one person lift it? If yes, it is enough for small animals. If no, it is appropriate for medium animals.
2. **Build the trigger:** Use a figure-four trigger (see Trigger Mechanisms below) or a similar mechanism. The trigger must support the weight until the animal disturbs it.
3. **Place the weight:** Position it directly above the trigger, approximately 1 meter high. It must fall directly onto the animal.
4. **Bait (optional):** For carnivores, place fresh meat under the weight. For herbivores, place grain or browse.

### Step 3: Trigger Mechanisms

A trigger must be sensitive enough that an animal touching it releases the trap, but robust enough not to fail from wind or insects.

**Figure-four trigger (for deadfall):**

1. Cut three pieces of stick:
   - Horizontal piece (base): 15 cm, sharpened on one end
   - Vertical piece: 20 cm, with a notch carved near the top
   - Angled piece (bait stick): 10 cm, sharpened on one end
2. Assemble: The vertical stick has a notch. The horizontal stick's pointed end rests in the notch. The angled piece props against the horizontal stick and the vertical piece, creating a precarious balance. The bait is placed at the pointed end of the horizontal stick.
3. When the animal disturbs the bait, the horizontal stick pulls free, and the weight falls.

**Simple trigger (for spring snare):**

1. Tie a cord around the sapling at the top.
2. Attach a trigger stick (6 cm long, sharpened on both ends) to the cord.
3. Position the trigger stick horizontally across the trail at animal head height.
4. When the animal touches it, it dislodges, and the sapling snaps upward.

### Step 4: Position the Snare

1. **Clear the trail:** Remove debris and excessive vegetation so the animal walks naturally and does not divert around the snare.
2. **Mark the location:** Remember where it is. You will need to check it regularly.
3. **Set multiple snares:** A single snare may catch nothing for days. Place 5–10 snares on different trails and high-traffic areas.
4. **Cover your human scent:** Wash your hands and wear gloves. Avoid contaminating the snare with human smell (some animals will avoid a strong human scent).

### Step 5: Maintenance and Checking

1. **Check daily:** Walk the snare lines every morning. Fresh kills spoil quickly.
2. **Reset immediately:** If a snare has caught and killed an animal, reset it immediately after removing the kill. The animal's scent and blood on the trail actually attract other animals.
3. **Remove failed snares:** If a snare is disturbed but the animal escaped, rebuild or reposition it; it may have failed structurally.
4. **Maintain cordage:** Check that cord is still intact and not frayed. Replace if damaged.

## Verification

**Ambush hunting:**
- Success: The animal is killed with a single strike (quick kill) or dies within 2–3 minutes
- Failure: The animal is struck but escapes (your position was poor, the strike was off-target, or the weapon was weak)

**Drive hunting:**
- Success: The animal reaches the kill zone and is killed
- Failure: The animal evades the drive (escapes to the side) or the kill team's strike fails to land

**Snare hunting:**
- Success: The snare has caught the animal and it is dead or nearly dead
- Failure: The snare is empty, the trigger failed, or the animal escaped partially caught (missing a leg or with the cord around its body but not neck)

## Common Mistakes

1. **Ambushing upwind:** You ambush with the wind at your back. The animal smells you and bolts before you are within range. Always position yourself downwind.

2. **Choosing a poor ambush position:** The animal does not take the trail you are watching, or it approaches from a different angle. Scout thoroughly and be prepared to relocate if you see no movement for several hours.

3. **Being too impatient:** Ambush hunting requires hours of waiting. Nervous movement (shifting, adjusting, getting up to stretch) gives away your position. Commit to stillness.

4. **Drive terrain choice:** Attempting a drive in open terrain where the animal can run sideways and escape. Choose naturally funneling terrain (canyons, ridges, valleys) where the animal has limited escape routes.

5. **Drive team loses coordination:** Some team members fall behind or speed up, creating gaps in the line. The animal escapes through the gap. Brief the team beforehand and maintain verbal or visual contact.

6. **Snare trigger too heavy:** The trigger requires so much force to release that a light animal cannot trigger it. A hare cannot release a trigger designed for a boar. Build triggers appropriate to the target animal.

7. **Snare placement in wrong location:** Placing a snare on an unused trail is useless. Scout first and ensure the trail shows recent, frequent use before investing in snare construction.

8. **Snare exposed to wind:** A deadfall trigger may fail if wind shakes it loose. Position deadfalls in sheltered locations or secure the trigger against accidental disturbance with additional sticks.

9. **Forgetting to bleed the kill:** Meat that is not bled quickly spoils and tastes worse. Always open the throat or chest cavity immediately after killing to drain blood.

10. **Hunting during midday:** Most large game moves at dawn and dusk. Ambushing or driving at midday is inefficient. Schedule hunts around peak movement times.

## Comparison Table: When to Use Each Strategy

| Factor | Ambush | Drive | Snare |
|--------|--------|-------|-------|
| **Team size** | 1 person | 4–8 people | 1 person |
| **Time investment** | 4–8 hours per attempt | 6–12 hours per attempt | 15–30 min setup; 24h waiting |
| **Skill required** | High (positioning, weapon accuracy) | Medium (coordination, stamina) | Low (construction, placement) |
| **Best for** | Solitary animals, skilled hunters | Herds, open terrain | Multiple trails, passive hunting |
| **Kill speed** | Instant to 2 minutes | Instant to 5 minutes | Variable (30 seconds to slow) |
| **Energy cost** | Medium (stillness fatigue) | High (running, noise) | Low (passive) |
| **Risk to hunter** | Low | Medium (animal may charge) | Low |
| **Scalability** | Works for all game sizes | Works for all game sizes | Best for small to medium |
