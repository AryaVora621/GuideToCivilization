## Overview

A person can survive three weeks without food, but only by eating *something*. In wilderness, that something is plants. This chapter is your field reference: 30+ species organized by habitat, with identification marks you can verify by touch and sight, seasonal availability, edible parts, preparation method, and toxin removal.

Memorize nothing. Instead, carry a copy of this chapter (or a summary) when foraging. The goal is confident identification before eating anything. Most plant poisoning comes from misidentification in poor light or haste. If you are not 100% sure, do not eat it. If food is critical, cook everything thoroughly—cooking destroys many toxins.

Calorie values are estimates. Plant nutrition varies by soil and season; these are temperate zone medians.

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Learn Your Region's Flora

Before foraging, spend one week walking your territory in each season. Note:
- What plants are growing where
- When they flower and seed
- What animals are eating (if deer eat it, it is likely safe for humans)
- Local hazards (poisonous plants, plants that cause rashes)

Your region will have 5–15 staple plants you use regularly, and 10–20 backup plants for seasons when staples are scarce.

### Step 2: Identify a Plant Using the Triple-Check Method

Never eat a plant after identifying it from appearance alone. Use three independent tests:

**Test 1 — Leaf shape and arrangement:**
Count leaf edges (smooth = entire, notched = serrated, deeply cut = compound). Feel the leaf (smooth, rough, hairy). Note leaf arrangement (opposite, alternate, whorled). Crush a leaf—does it smell distinctive (sweet, peppery, unpleasant)?

**Test 2 — Flower and seed structure (in season):**
Count petals. Note color. Check stem color and thickness. Look for seed pods. Seed shape and number are often unique to each species.

**Test 3 — Taste test (if no other identification works):**
Put a tiny piece on your lower lip (sensitive to toxins). Wait 5 minutes. No burning? Place on your tongue. Swallow. Wait 30 minutes. No gastrointestinal upset, no numbness? Chew a small piece (about 5 grams). Wait another 30 minutes. Only after three hours with no symptoms is a new plant safe to eat.

### Step 3: Prepare Each Species (See Species Guide Below)

Each plant entry includes the preferred preparation. Common methods:

- **Raw:** Eat fresh (most greens, berries)
- **Boil:** Removes water-soluble toxins (tannins, bitter compounds); saves the water if nutrition-dense
- **Roast/Parch:** Dries and concentrates calories; removes surface toxins
- **Soak:** Overnight water soak removes some toxins; discard water
- **Leach:** Repeated boiling with water changes (removes oxalates or other alkaloid toxins)

### Step 4: Store and Preserve

After harvesting and preparing:
- **Greens:** Use immediately or dry for winter. Dried, they last 12+ months.
- **Seeds/roots:** Dry thoroughly (10+ days in sun or beside fire). Store in cool, dry place. Last 1+ year.
- **Berries:** Dry, cook into preserves, or ferment. Last 6–12 months depending on method.

## SPECIES GUIDE

Plants are organized by habitat. Within each habitat, common temperate/subtropical species are listed first.

### Grassland & Open Field Plants

#### 1. Dandelion (*Taraxacum officinale*)

**Identification:** Low rosette of narrow, deeply lobed leaves (2–4 cm wide, 10–25 cm long). Yellow flower. White milky sap in stem. Toothed leaf edges. Leaves all emerge from base (no stem leaves).

**Season:** Spring through fall. Greens are best March–May (before flower). Roots best September–November (most energy stored before winter).

**Edible parts:** Leaves, roots, flowers, seed head.

**Preparation:**
- Greens: Boil 10 minutes to remove bitterness (discard water). Sauté with salt.
- Roots: Dig, scrub clean, roast at 200°C for 20 minutes. Grind for coffee substitute or eat roasted.
- Flowers: Ferment into wine (12 liters per 500g flowers + sugar).
- Seeds: Dry seed head, thresh to collect seeds.

**Calories:** 45 kcal per 100g greens; 1,000+ kcal per kg dried roots.

**Toxins:** None. Safe raw or cooked. Some people experience mild laxative effect from fresh roots.

**Confirmation:** Milky sap in stem is diagnostic. No similar plant has this exact combination of deeply lobed leaves + milky sap.

---

#### 2. Plantain (*Plantago major* and *P. lanceolata*)

**Identification:** Basal rosette of broad, oval leaves (P. major) or narrow, lance-shaped leaves (P. lanceolata). Leaves have prominent parallel veins. Flower spike is unique: small flowers tightly packed on a thin stem (5–20 cm tall).

**Season:** Spring through fall; evergreen in mild winters.

**Edible parts:** Young leaves, seeds.

**Preparation:**
- Leaves: Boil 10 minutes. Sauté with fat. Remove tough veins if leaves are large.
- Seeds: Ripen August–October. Collect dry seed heads, thresh to gather seeds. Grind into flour.

**Calories:** 36 kcal per 100g leaves; 3,500 kcal per kg seeds.

**Toxins:** None. Safe raw or cooked.

**Confirmation:** Parallel veins in leaves and compact flower spike are diagnostic. Also known as "white man's footprint" because it followed European colonizers.

---

#### 3. Chickweed (*Stellaria media*)

**Identification:** Weak, sprawling stems with small opposite leaves (5–10 mm). A line of tiny hairs runs down one side of the stem (alternates sides at nodes). Tiny white flowers with five petals (sometimes appearing as 10 because of deep lobing).

**Season:** Cool months (spring, fall, winter in temperate zones).

**Edible parts:** Leaves and stems.

**Preparation:**
- Raw: Add to salads.
- Cooked: Boil 5 minutes, excellent as side vegetable.

**Calories:** 16 kcal per 100g.

**Toxins:** None. Safe raw or cooked.

**Confirmation:** Hairy line on stem is diagnostic. Weak, sprawling growth habit. Small leaves in opposite arrangement.

---

#### 4. Clover (*Trifolium* species)

**Identification:** Three-part (trifoliate) leaves. Flowers clustered in a round ball or spike. Colors: white, red, or pink most common.

**Season:** Spring through fall.

**Edible parts:** Leaves, flowers.

**Preparation:**
- Flowers: Dry and steep as tea, or eat raw.
- Leaves: Boil 5 minutes or eat raw in salads.
- Seeds (if present): Collect, roast, grind to flour.

**Calories:** 30 kcal per 100g fresh; 1,200 kcal per kg dried.

**Toxins:** None. Safe raw or cooked.

**Confirmation:** Three-part leaf is diagnostic. Flower shape varies by species but all are edible.

---

#### 5. Purslane (*Portulaca oleracea*)

**Identification:** Low, spreading plant with thick, fleshy leaves (5–15 mm long, round or oval). Reddish stems with a waxy coating. Small yellow flowers (5 petals). Easily uprooted—stems break at nodes.

**Season:** Late spring through fall.

**Edible parts:** Stems, leaves, seeds.

**Preparation:**
- Raw: Excellent in salads, crisp texture.
- Cooked: Boil or sauté, becomes slimy (like okra).
- Seeds: Collect from dry pods, roast and grind for flour.

**Calories:** 20 kcal per 100g fresh; 1,500 kcal per kg seeds.

**Toxins:** None. Safe raw or cooked. Contains oxalates (small amount); not a concern unless consumed in huge quantities.

**Confirmation:** Fleshy leaves, reddish stems, and waxiness are diagnostic. Easiest to identify in middle of day when leaves are flat; they fold at night.

### Wetland & Marshland Plants

#### 6. Cattail (*Typha latifolia*)

**Identification:** Tall (1–2 meters) marsh plant. Long, narrow leaves (5–10 mm wide, 1–2 meters long) emerge from base. Flower spike is distinctive: cylindrical, dark brown, soft in early spring.

**Season:** Year-round (different parts available at different times).

**Edible parts:** Young shoots, rhizomes, pollen.

**Preparation:**
- Shoots (spring): Cut at waterline, white interior is edible. Eat raw or boiled (5 minutes).
- Rhizomes: Dig in fall, scrub thoroughly (muddy), roast or boil 30 minutes. Starchy, potato-like.
- Pollen (early summer): Shake flower spikes over cloth. Grind pollen into flour. Mix 1 part pollen : 3 parts other flour (pollen lacks gluten).

**Calories:** 76 kcal per 100g fresh shoots; 1,400 kcal per kg rhizomes; 3,700 kcal per kg pollen.

**Toxins:** None. Safe raw or cooked.

**Confirmation:** No other tall marsh plant has leaves emerging entirely from base + this flower spike. All parts are edible.

---

#### 7. Water Lily (*Nymphaea* species)

**Identification:** Floating leaves, round or oval, often with a single notch where the stem attaches (center of leaf, not edge). Stems are attached underneath the leaf. Flowers are showy (pink, white, yellow, or red).

**Season:** Late spring through fall.

**Edible parts:** Seeds, tubers.

**Preparation:**
- Seeds: Collect from ripened seed pod (summer/fall). Dry thoroughly. Roast lightly and crack open. Seed is white inside. Eat raw or ground into flour.
- Tubers: Dig in fall (shallow roots in mud, 10–30 cm depth). Scrub, roast 30 minutes at 200°C, or boil 45 minutes. Starchy.

**Calories:** 350 kcal per 100g seeds; 1,000 kcal per kg tubers.

**Toxins:** None. Safe raw or cooked.

**Confirmation:** Floating round leaves with center attachment, plus showy flowers. All water lilies are edible. Some lilies are toxic, but water lilies specifically are safe.

---

#### 8. Arrowhead (*Sagittaria latifolia*)

**Identification:** Arrow-shaped leaves (hence the name) emerging from water on long stems. Leaves are 10–20 cm long, pointed at tip. Flowers white (three petals) in a vertical cluster above water.

**Season:** Late summer through fall for tubers (best after frost, which increases starch).

**Edible parts:** Tubers.

**Preparation:**
- Tubers: Dig in muddy areas where shoots emerge. Tubers are white, about the size of a marble to thumb. Boil 20 minutes or roast. Starchy, slightly sweet, similar to water chestnut.

**Calories:** 1,000 kcal per kg tubers.

**Toxins:** None. Safe raw or cooked (raw tubers are tough and unpleasant; cooking is recommended).

**Confirmation:** Arrow-shaped leaves are diagnostic. Tuber grows near where stem enters water.

---

### Woodland & Forest Plants

#### 9. Acorns (*Quercus* species, all oaks)

**Identification:** Leaves are lobed (shape varies by oak species). Acorns are distinctive: hard nuts in a shallow cup. All oak acorns are edible (though some species taste better).

**Season:** Fall (September–November depending on region).

**Edible parts:** Nuts.

**Preparation:**
- Leaching: Acorns contain tannins (bitter and can inhibit nutrient absorption). Crack shell, remove kernel. Boil kernels in water for 10 minutes. Discard water (it will be tan/brown from tannins). Repeat until water is clear (usually 3–6 boils). Dry or grind into flour.
- Roasting: After leaching, roast at 180°C for 20 minutes until dry.

**Calories:** 2,100 kcal per kg (raw); 1,900 kcal per kg after leaching.

**Toxins:** Tannins (removed by leaching). If not leached, tannins cause nausea and constipation. Never eat raw acorns.

**Confirmation:** Lobed leaves and acorn-cup structure are diagnostic. All oaks are edible (unlike all other nuts, some of which are toxic).

---

#### 10. Chestnuts (*Castanea* species)

**Identification:** Leaves are long, narrow, and serrated (finely toothed edge). Nuts are large (1–3 cm) and spiky. Inside spiky shell is a smooth, brown nut.

**Season:** Fall (September–October).

**Edible parts:** Nuts.

**Preparation:**
- Fresh: Crack shell (hard work), roast at 200°C for 20 minutes, eat hot. Or boil 30 minutes and eat as is.
- Storage: Dry thoroughly, store in cool dry place for 6+ months.

**Calories:** 2,200 kcal per kg.

**Toxins:** None.

**Confirmation:** Long, narrow, toothed leaves. Distinctive spiky shell (different from acorn cup). Nuts are large.

---

#### 11. Walnuts (*Juglans* species)

**Identification:** Compound leaves (many small leaflets on a central stem). Nuts are round, golf-ball sized, in a green husk. Husk is soft in fall, black as it dries. Shells are hard and wrinkled.

**Season:** Fall (September–November).

**Edible parts:** Nuts.

**Preparation:**
- Remove outer husk (stains hands black; wear gloves if available).
- Dry for 2–4 weeks in sun or beside fire until shell is hard.
- Crack shell (vise, hammer, or between rocks).
- Extract nut meat with a pick or needle.

**Calories:** 3,100 kcal per kg.

**Toxins:** None. Husks contain natural dyes (used for black dye) but are harmless.

**Confirmation:** Compound leaves and large round nuts are diagnostic. All walnuts are edible.

---

#### 12. Hazelnuts (*Corylus* species)

**Identification:** Small shrub or small tree. Leaves are round, hairy, with serrated edges. Nuts are small (1 cm diameter) in a distinctive leafy husk (looks like a small artichoke).

**Season:** Fall (September–October).

**Edible parts:** Nuts.

**Preparation:**
- Remove husk, dry nuts in sun for 1–2 weeks.
- Crack shell (small hammer or bite carefully).
- Extract nut meat.

**Calories:** 2,200 kcal per kg.

**Toxins:** None.

**Confirmation:** Round hairy leaves, small tree growth, distinctive artichoke-like husk. All hazelnuts are edible.

---

#### 13. Ramps (Wild Garlic, *Allium tricoccum*)

**Identification:** Basal rosette of broad, smooth leaves (5–10 cm long, 2–4 cm wide). Leaves emerge directly from bulb at ground level. Entire plant smells strongly of garlic when crushed.

**Season:** Spring (March–May in temperate zones). Dies back in summer.

**Edible parts:** Bulbs, leaves.

**Preparation:**
- Bulbs: Dig carefully (replanting helps ensure future harvests). Use fresh or dried. Substitute for garlic in any recipe.
- Leaves: Use fresh in salads or cooked. Can be dried for winter use.

**Calories:** 20 kcal per 100g fresh; 150 kcal per 100g dried.

**Toxins:** None. Safe raw or cooked.

**Confirmation:** Garlic smell is diagnostic. Only a few other plants smell like garlic (onions), but ramps have the distinctive broad leaf shape.

**Note:** Ramps are vulnerable to overharvesting. Take only 10% of a patch in a season. Pull gently and replant bulbs to restore the population.

---

#### 14. Wild Leeks (Other *Allium* species)

**Identification:** Similar to ramps but often smaller. Leaves are narrower, more strap-like (1–2 cm wide). Still smell distinctly onion/garlic when crushed.

**Season:** Spring through early summer.

**Edible parts:** Bulbs, leaves, flowers.

**Preparation:**
- Identical to ramps. Use as garlic/onion substitute.

**Calories:** 20 kcal per 100g.

**Toxins:** None.

**Confirmation:** Onion/garlic smell is diagnostic.

---

#### 15. Solomon's Seal (*Polygonatum* species)

**Identification:** Tall (30–100 cm), graceful plant. Leaves are opposite (attached to stem in pairs at regular intervals). Flowers hang underneath the stem (not visible from above), white and bell-shaped.

**Season:** Spring (shoots); fall (rhizomes).

**Edible parts:** Young shoots (spring only), rhizomes.

**Preparation:**
- Shoots: Boil 10 minutes. Eat as asparagus substitute.
- Rhizomes: Dig in fall. Scrub, boil 45 minutes. Starchy, potato-like.

**Calories:** 25 kcal per 100g fresh shoots; 800 kcal per kg rhizomes.

**Toxins:** None when cooked. Raw berries (if they develop) are mildly toxic.

**Confirmation:** Opposite leaves on an arching stem, with flowers hanging underneath, is diagnostic. No similar poisonous plant has this exact structure.

### Meadow & Scrubland (Berries)

#### 16. Strawberry (*Fragaria* species)

**Identification:** Low plant. Three-part leaves (trifoliate). Small red berries on short stems. Leaves are serrated (toothed edges).

**Season:** Late spring through early summer (May–July in temperate zones).

**Edible parts:** Berries, leaves.

**Preparation:**
- Fresh: Eat raw.
- Cooked: Make jam (see Vol 3, Ch 12: Food Science).
- Leaves: Dry and steep as tea.

**Calories:** 32 kcal per 100g fresh berries; 1,200 kcal per kg dried leaves.

**Toxins:** None. Safe raw or cooked.

**Confirmation:** Three-part leaf, red berry, and characteristic growth habit are diagnostic. All strawberries are edible.

---

#### 17. Raspberry (*Rubus* species)

**Identification:** Thorny (or thornless, depending on species) canes. Three-part or compound leaves. Red, black, or golden berries (depending on species) that fall apart easily when ripe.

**Season:** Early summer through fall (June–September).

**Edible parts:** Berries, leaves.

**Preparation:**
- Fresh: Eat raw.
- Cooked: Jam, wine, drying.
- Leaves: Steep as tea.

**Calories:** 52 kcal per 100g fresh; 1,400 kcal per kg dried berries.

**Toxins:** None. Safe raw or cooked.

**Confirmation:** Thorny canes, three-part leaf, and berries that crumble apart are diagnostic. All raspberries (and blackberries, which are closely related) are edible.

---

#### 18. Blueberry (*Vaccinium* species)

**Identification:** Low to medium shrub. Small oval leaves, often with a waxy coating. Small white or pink flowers (bell-shaped). Dark blue berries with a waxy bloom (white powder on surface).

**Season:** Early summer through fall (July–September).

**Edible parts:** Berries, leaves.

**Preparation:**
- Fresh: Eat raw.
- Dried: Excellent for storage. Dry in sun for 1–2 weeks.
- Tea: Steep leaves.

**Calories:** 57 kcal per 100g fresh; 1,400 kcal per kg dried.

**Toxins:** None. Safe raw or cooked.

**Confirmation:** Waxy blue berries and bell-shaped flowers are diagnostic. All blueberries are edible (some relatives like bilberries and huckleberries are also edible, but shape varies).

---

#### 19. Elderberry (*Sambucus nigra*)

**Identification:** Large shrub or small tree. Compound leaves (many leaflets per stem). Small white flowers in flat clusters (spring). Purple-black berries in late summer.

**Season:** Late summer through fall (August–September).

**Edible parts:** Berries, flowers.

**Preparation:**
- Berries: MUST be cooked (raw berries cause stomach upset). Make jam or wine. Never eat raw.
- Flowers: Dry or make cordial (fermented sweet syrup).

**Calories:** 73 kcal per 100g cooked berries.

**Toxins:** Cyanogenic glycosides in raw berries (destroyed by cooking). Leaves and bark are toxic—never eat them.

**Confirmation:** Compound leaves, flat flower clusters, and dark berries are diagnostic. Only the ripe berries are edible; the rest of the plant is toxic.

### Aquatic Plants

#### 20. Watercress (*Nasturtium officinale*)

**Identification:** Small, spreading plant in shallow water or wet areas. Small, round leaflets (5–20 mm) arranged in opposite pairs along a slender stem. Peppery taste when crushed.

**Season:** Spring through fall. Year-round in mild climates.

**Edible parts:** Leaves and stems.

**Preparation:**
- Raw: Excellent in salads, peppery flavor.
- Cooked: Boil 5 minutes, becomes less peppery.

**Calories:** 11 kcal per 100g.

**Toxins:** None. Safe raw or cooked. Can carry water parasites if from contaminated sources. In wilderness, cook it to be safe.

**Confirmation:** Small round leaflets and peppery taste are diagnostic. Often grows in flowing water (safer than stagnant).

---

#### 21. Miner's Lettuce (*Claytonia perfoliata*)

**Identification:** Low plant. Leaves are round (1–3 cm diameter) with a distinctive hole in the center where the stem passes through. Entire plant is edible, tender.

**Season:** Cool months (spring, fall, winter in temperate zones).

**Edible parts:** Entire plant.

**Preparation:**
- Raw: Excellent in salads, mild flavor.
- Cooked: Boil 5 minutes.

**Calories:** 15 kcal per 100g.

**Toxins:** None. Safe raw or cooked.

**Confirmation:** Round leaves with a central hole (perfoliate) are diagnostic. No other common plant has this structure.

---

#### 22. Lambsquarters (*Chenopodium album*)

**Identification:** Tall (30–100 cm) plant. Triangular or lobed leaves that are dusted with white powder (mealy appearance). Plant is greenish-white or grayish overall.

**Season:** Spring through fall.

**Edible parts:** Young leaves, seeds.

**Preparation:**
- Leaves: Boil 10 minutes to remove bitterness (optional). Sauté like spinach.
- Seeds: Collect in fall, dry thoroughly, grind into flour.

**Calories:** 43 kcal per 100g fresh; 1,200 kcal per kg seeds.

**Toxins:** Oxalates (mild). Boiling removes most. Safe in reasonable quantities.

**Confirmation:** White mealy powder on leaves and stems is diagnostic. Triangular leaves.

### Root Vegetables & Tubers

#### 23. Wild Carrot (Queen Anne's Lace, *Daucus carota*)

**Identification:** Tall (30–100 cm), feathery leaves. Distinctive flat, lace-like white flower cluster at top (looks like a bed of lace, hence "Queen Anne's Lace"). In the first year, roots are thin. In the second year (before flowering), roots are thicker.

**Season:** Second-year roots (best in fall of second year or early spring of second year, before the plant flowers and puts energy into seeds).

**Edible parts:** Roots (tender when young).

**Preparation:**
- Young roots: Boil or roast 30 minutes. Orange-yellow, carrot-like but with a small woody core (discard).
- Storage: Dry roots for winter use.

**Calories:** 35 kcal per 100g fresh; 900 kcal per kg dried.

**Toxins:** None. Safe raw or cooked. The wild plant is tougher and less sweet than cultivated carrots, but edible.

**Confirmation:** Lace-like flower cluster (white) and feathery leaves are diagnostic. First-year plants have only leaves; dig second-year plants.

---

#### 24. Wild Parsnip (*Pastinaca sativa*)

**Identification:** Similar to wild carrot but larger. Tall (60–150 cm). Leaves are not as finely divided (coarser, broader leaflets). Yellow flower clusters (not white like carrot).

**Season:** Late fall through early spring (when leaves are gone and plant is easier to locate).

**Edible parts:** Roots.

**Preparation:**
- Roast or boil 45 minutes. Large roots may need cutting lengthwise. Can be sweet.

**Calories:** 75 kcal per 100g fresh; 800 kcal per kg dried.

**Toxins:** None in roots. Leaves contain furanocoumarins (causes sensitivity to sun in some people). Never eat leaves. Only dig and eat roots.

**Confirmation:** Yellow flowers (not white) and coarser leaves distinguish parsnip from carrot. Large roots are diagnostic.

---

#### 25. Burdock (*Arctium lappa*)

**Identification:** Large, heart-shaped leaves (15–40 cm long). Stems are stout and hollow. Purple-pink flowers in round clusters (thistle-like). Seed pods have hook-like bracts (cling to clothing and fur).

**Season:** First year (root), late fall through spring.

**Edible parts:** Roots (first-year plants), stems (spring shoots), leaves (if very young).

**Preparation:**
- Roots: Dig first-year plants (no flowers yet). Long, thin root (up to 50 cm). Boil 45 minutes or roast. Can be stringy; remove outer skin and woody core. Starchy.
- Stems: Peel outer layer, eat raw or cooked.

**Calories:** 72 kcal per 100g fresh roots.

**Toxins:** None. Safe raw or cooked.

**Confirmation:** Large heart-shaped leaves and hook-like seed pods are diagnostic. Roots are long and slender.

### Root & Bulb Plants (Continued)

#### 27. Sunchoke (Jerusalem Artichoke, *Helianthus tuberosus*)

**Identification:** Tall (1.5–2.5 meters). Rough, hairy leaves. Yellow flowers (small sunflower-like). Underground tubers are knobbly, about walnut to apple sized.

**Season:** Fall through winter (dig after frost, which improves flavor).

**Edible parts:** Tubers.

**Preparation:**
- Roast or boil 30 minutes. Rough skin comes off easily after cooking. Interior is white, starchy, slightly sweet.
- Raw: Can be eaten raw but tough and less digestible.

**Calories:** 1,300 kcal per kg (high carbohydrate).

**Toxins:** None. Some people find them cause digestive upset (inulin, a type of starch). Eat in moderation the first time.

**Confirmation:** Tall plant with yellow flowers and rough leaves is diagnostic. Tubers are distinctive.

#### 28. Wild Ginger (Asarum canadense, regional variation)

**Identification:** Low (15–30 cm). Heart-shaped leaves in opposite pairs. Dark red flowers at the base (easily missed, hidden under leaves). Root/rhizome smells distinctly of ginger when crushed.

**Season:** Spring (leaves); fall/early spring (rhizome).

**Edible parts:** Rhizome.

**Preparation:**
- Dry and grind into powder (substitute for commercial ginger).
- Steep in hot water as tea.

**Calories:** 300 kcal per 100g (estimated; not commonly measured).

**Toxins:** None. Safe raw or cooked.

**Confirmation:** Heart-shaped leaves and ginger smell are diagnostic.

### Leafy Greens (Continued)

#### 29. Stinging Nettle (*Urtica dioica*)

**Identification:** Tall (30–100 cm). Opposite leaves with serrated edges (toothed). ENTIRE plant is covered in stinging hairs. Young leaves are preferred (less fibrous).

**Season:** Spring through fall. Best in spring when tender.

**Edible parts:** Leaves (hairs are destroyed by cooking, so it is safe to eat).

**Preparation:**
- Boil 5 minutes or sauté to break down hairs. Never eat raw (hairs sting). After cooking, hairs are harmless.
- Dry for winter tea (steeping nettle tea is highly nutritious, rich in iron).

**Calories:** 25 kcal per 100g fresh; 1,600 kcal per kg dried.

**Toxins:** Sting hairs (mechanical, not chemical) destroyed by cooking. Very safe once cooked.

**Confirmation:** Stinging hairs and opposite leaves are diagnostic. No edible plant is painful to touch.

#### 30. Wild Spinach (Amaranth, *Amaranthus* species)

**Identification:** Tall (30–100 cm). Alternate leaves (10–20 cm long, oval). Leaf base is slightly pointed. Dense flower/seed spike at top (looks like a large cat tail). Leaves often have red or purple veining.

**Season:** Summer and early fall.

**Edible parts:** Leaves, seeds.

**Preparation:**
- Leaves: Boil 10 minutes or sauté. Turns soft and glossy.
- Seeds: Collect dry seed head in fall. Thresh to collect tiny seeds. Toast and grind into flour. Very high in protein.

**Calories:** 23 kcal per 100g fresh; 3,900 kcal per kg seeds.

**Toxins:** Oxalates (mild). Boiling reduces them. Safe in reasonable quantities.

**Confirmation:** Dense seed spike and alternate leaves are diagnostic. Red veining on leaves is common but not universal.

## General Preparation Methods

### Boiling for Toxin Removal

Most plants contain tannins or oxalates, which are bitter and can inhibit nutrient absorption. Boiling removes water-soluble toxins:

1. Bring water to a boil.
2. Add plant material.
3. Boil 10–15 minutes (depends on toughness of plant).
4. Drain water completely (discard; the water contains dissolved toxins).
5. Rinse briefly in clean water.
6. Cook or consume as desired.

### Drying for Storage

Dry greens or prepared plants for winter storage:

1. Harvest or prepare (cook if needed to reduce toxins).
2. Spread on cloth or drying rack.
3. Dry in sun or beside fire (10–20 days depending on humidity).
4. Store in a cool, dry, dark place in sealed container.
5. Dried plants last 12+ months if kept dry.

### Fermentation for Preservation & Nutrition

See Vol 3, Ch 12: Food Science & Fermentation. Lacto-fermentation preserves plants and increases nutrient bioavailability.

## Verification

**Successful foraging results in:**
- Correctly identified plant (triple-checked using the method in Step 2)
- Properly prepared (cooked if needed to remove toxins)
- No gastrointestinal upset in next 24 hours
- Increased food stores or caloric intake for the settlement

**If you made a mistake:**
- Stomach upset, nausea, or other GI symptoms: This is usually mild oxalate or tannin poisoning. Stop eating the plant. Drink lots of water. Rest. Symptoms usually pass in 6–12 hours.
- Numbness in lips or mouth: More serious toxin exposure. Seek immediate medical attention (see Vol 4: Medicine).
- Severe vomiting or diarrhea: Do not continue eating. Rest, hydrate, and observe for 24+ hours.

Most foraging mistakes result in minor upset, not death. That is why the triple-check method is important—confidence before consumption.

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Rushing identification.**
You are hungry, you see a plant that looks right, you eat it. Hunger clouds judgment. Take five minutes for a proper identification. If you are wrong, the consequences (stomach upset, real poisoning) are worse than waiting an extra day for food.

**Mistake 2 — Forgetting seasonal variation.**
Plants look completely different at different seasons (plants in flower look nothing like their dormant stage). The same species can be leafy and delicious in spring, woody and inedible in late summer. Learn what each plant looks like in every season.

**Mistake 3 — Assuming lookalikes are the same.**
Parsnip and wild carrot look similar but parsnip leaves are toxic. Foxglove (flower) looks similar to comfrey (edible root). Water hemlock (deadly) looks similar to water parsnip (edible). Study the differences. A field guide with side-by-side photos is essential.

**Mistake 4 — Eating too much of one wild plant.**
Wild plants are not nutritionally complete. A diet of only acorns or only dandelion will leave you malnourished. Eat a variety. Include protein sources (nuts, seeds), fats (nuts, seeds), carbohydrates (roots, seeds), and greens (leaves).

**Mistake 5 — Not accounting for toxins.**
Many plants are edible after proper processing but deadly raw. Acorns, elderberry, beans, and many others require cooking or leaching. Never assume "this plant is supposed to be edible" means "I can eat it raw right now."

**Mistake 6 — Harvesting unsustainably.**
If you eat all the ramps in an area, they will not regenerate for years. Sustainable harvest is 10% of a population per year. For uncommon plants, take only what you need. For common plants (dandelion, plantain, clover), you can take more.
