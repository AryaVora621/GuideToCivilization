## Overview

A track is a story. The prints tell you what animal passed, when, how fast it was moving, what it was doing, and where it was going. Scat (droppings) shows what it ate. Browse marks show where it feeds. Runs (trails) show habitual pathways.

This chapter teaches you to read the forest like a book. Within hours of practice, you will spot details that reveal an animal's size, speed, species, and behavior. This skill is the foundation of successful hunting and wilderness travel.

## Prerequisites

None. This chapter has no prerequisites. Learn this before hunting.

## Materials & Tools

- **Eyes and attention** (only tool required)
- **Optional tracking aids:** Pencil and paper (to sketch tracks), magnifying glass (to see fine details)
- **Optional equipment:** A tracking stick (a forked branch) to measure stride and pace

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Part 1: Reading Tracks

**Step 1: Identify the print type.**

Animal prints fall into families based on the number of toes:

- **Five toes:** Raccoon, badger, bear (front print shows all five)
- **Four toes:** Most carnivores (dog, fox, wolf, cat) and most hoofed animals (deer, elk, sheep)
- **Two toes:** Cloven-hoofed animals (deer, elk, goats, pigs)
- **One toe (or nail):** Horses, donkeys
- **Three toes:** Some birds, ostriches
- **Soft prints (webbing):** Beaver, otter, duck, goose

**Step 2: Measure the print.**

- **Length** (front to back of the print, not including claws or dew claws)
- **Width** (side to side)
- **Distance between prints** (stride: distance from one print to the next; pace: distance from left to right)

Record these if you can. A deer track is 5–8 cm long. A wolf track is 8–12 cm. A human track is 20–25 cm.

**Step 3: Look for claw marks.**

- **Cats (including lynx):** Retractable claws; claw marks absent or barely visible
- **Canines (fox, wolf, dog):** Claw marks clearly visible, pointing forward
- **Bears:** Long claw marks, 5–10 cm ahead of the toe prints

**Step 4: Note the asymmetry or symmetry.**

- **Walking:** Prints form a nearly straight line (direct register)
- **Trotting:** Prints form a slightly offset line, left-right alternating
- **Galloping:** Prints are scattered, indicating high speed
- **Bounding:** Two prints close together (back feet), then a gap, then two more (front feet) — rabbits and squirrels bound; foxes and cats sometimes gallop

**Step 5: Examine the substrate for depth and clarity.**

- **Deep prints in soft ground:** Heavy animal, moving slowly or cautiously
- **Shallow prints:** Light animal or fast movement
- **Crisp edges:** Print made recently (hours old)
- **Blurred or weathered edges:** Print made hours or days ago

### Part 2: Aging Tracks

**Step 1: Fresh tracks (0–6 hours).**

- Sharp, crisp edges
- Fine details visible (toe pads, claw scratches, individual hoof points)
- No debris fallen into the print
- In soft ground, moisture may still be visible in the print
- Sand or soil in the print is undisturbed

**Step 2: Recent tracks (6–24 hours).**

- Edges are less sharp but still distinct
- Small bits of debris (leaves, sand) have fallen into the track
- In sand, the print may show slight smoothing from wind
- Moisture has evaporated

**Step 3: Old tracks (1–3 days).**

- Edges are blurred or rounded
- Debris fills half or more of the print
- The print may be partially obscured by windblown sand or duff (dead leaves)
- In dry conditions, the print is fading fast
- In mud, the print is hardening or cracking

**Step 4: Very old tracks (3+ days).**

- Edges are nearly gone
- The print is filled with debris
- The outline is faint or invisible
- In mud, the print has hardened to cement-like consistency with visible cracks

**Practice aging:** Find multiple tracks from the same animal at different distances from water or recent disturbance. Aged tracks tend to collect debris and lose definition.

### Part 3: Reading Scat (Droppings)

**Step 1: Identify the animal by scat shape and contents.**

- **Deer:** Small pellets, round or slightly oblong, 1–2 cm diameter, often clustered
- **Rabbit:** Small round pellets, 0.5–1 cm diameter, may contain plant fibers
- **Wolf or wild dog:** Larger, twisted or tapered, 2–3 cm diameter, often contains bone, hair, or vegetable matter
- **Fox:** Similar to wolf but smaller and more tapered; often contains feathers or fur
- **Bear:** Highly variable — may be fibrous (plant) or chunky (meat); large, 3–5 cm diameter; black or brown
- **Badger or wolverine:** Chunky, contains bone and fur; 2–3 cm diameter; foul odor
- **Raccoon:** Chunky or fibrous; often contains seeds or insects; 1–2 cm diameter
- **Rodent (squirrel, porcupine):** Small, 0.3–1 cm; rodent scat shows as scattered pellets
- **Bird (hawk, owl):** White with bone or fur; scat is splattered, not pelleted
- **Fish-eater (otter, mink):** Often contains fish scales, bones, or shells; oily appearance

**Step 2: Determine freshness.**

- **Fresh (hours old):** Scat is moist, glossy, or still warm; strong odor
- **Recent (1–2 days):** Surface is drying, no odor
- **Old (3+ days):** Hardened, dull, cracked

**Step 3: Note location.**

- Scat in the middle of a trail means the animal was moving casually
- Scat on a rise or prominent rock means the animal was marking territory
- Scat in a latrine (pile of scat in one spot) means the animal visits regularly

### Part 4: Reading Browse and Feeding Signs

**Step 1: Identify browse type.**

- **Deer browse:** Leaves are cleanly torn at a 45° angle (no upper teeth; the lower jaw presses against a hard palate)
- **Elk browse:** Similar to deer but rougher; may strip bark from saplings
- **Rabbit browse:** Vegetation is cut cleanly at a 45° angle at about knee height
- **Porcupine browse:** Bark is stripped vertically from tree trunks; inner bark is exposed; branches are gnawed
- **Beaver browse:** Trees are cut down at the base, leaving a characteristic pencil-point stump; inner bark is eaten
- **Bear browse:** Fruit trees may be torn apart; berry bushes may be flattened; ants' nests are torn open; logs are overturned

**Step 2: Judge the age of browse.**

- **Fresh (hours old):** Torn leaves show white, juicy tissue
- **Recent (1–2 days):** Exposed surfaces are drying but not brown
- **Old (3+ days):** Exposed surfaces are brown or black from oxidation

**Step 3: Map the feeding area.**

Abundant browse in one area tells you the animal was feeding steadily. Scattered browse indicates the animal was traveling and stopping briefly to eat.

### Part 5: Reading Runs and Trails

**Step 1: Distinguish animal trails from human trails.**

- **Animal trails:** Narrow (wildlife uses the exact same path repeatedly), low vegetation, winding to avoid obstacles
- **Human trails:** Wider, straight where possible, vegetation pushed aside

**Step 2: Identify the animal by trail characteristics.**

- **Deer trail:** Narrow (10–15 cm wide), clear, usually along ridges or between cover and feeding areas
- **Elk trail:** Wider (20–30 cm), more visible, often following stream courses
- **Bear trail:** Very wide (30–50 cm), vegetation is crushed, prints are visible if muddy; trails connect berry patches and salmon streams
- **Predator trails (wolf, mountain lion):** Narrow, often follow the same path for days; prints may show direct register (almost a straight line)
- **Rabbit or hare trails:** Very narrow (5 cm wide), faint, may have many branches
- **Beaver trail:** Muddy or wet, wide at water's edge, leading from water to feeding trees

**Step 3: Determine trail use.**

- **Heavy use (daily):** Worn-down vegetation, visible prints even in dry ground, clear path
- **Light use (weekly):** Grass is bent but not worn; prints visible only in soft ground
- **Abandoned (unused for weeks):** Vegetation growing back into the trail; no prints visible

**Step 4: Predict animal movement.**

A well-used trail from a water source toward a feeding area tells you the animal is there at specific times:
- Early morning: animals feed
- Mid-day: animals rest in shade
- Late afternoon: animals move to water and feed again
- Night: nocturnal animals (raccoon, badger) hunt

### Part 6: Putting It Together — Reading a Scene

**Step 1: Start wide and work inward.**

Stand back and look at the overall scene. Where is the water? The food? The shelter (dense trees)? Trails connect these three things.

**Step 2: Identify the main trails.**

Follow the worn paths. Note their direction and what they connect.

**Step 3: Find tracks.**

Look in soft ground (mud, sand, dust) along trails. Measure and identify them.

**Step 4: Look for scat and browse.**

Scat on the trail or near water tells you the animal was drinking. Browse tells you when the animal was feeding.

**Step 5: Determine the story.**

Put the evidence together: "A deer came down this trail 6 hours ago (fresh track, sharp edges), walked to the stream to drink (scat near water), and fed on this shrub (fresh browse with white tissue) before moving into the forest."

## Verification

**Your tracking is accurate if:**
- You identify the animal correctly (match the track to the right species)
- You estimate the animal's age correctly (judge whether the track is fresh or old)
- You predict where the animal will be next (follow the trail and find more evidence)

**Test yourself:** Find a set of tracks or scat. Make a prediction about the animal (species, size, when it passed). Return to the same spot 24 hours later and look for fresh evidence. If you see more prints from the same animal in the expected direction, your reading was correct.

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing young animals with different species.** A young deer's track looks smaller and might resemble a small canine track. Look for other signs: deer trails are narrower, and deer browse shows 45° tears.
- **Assuming scat age based on appearance alone.** Scat can look fresh or old depending on weather. Combine appearance with location: fresh scat is rarely more than 100 meters from the animal.
- **Not noting the full trail context.** A single track is meaningless. Look for multiple tracks, the trail's direction, and what the trail connects.
- **Aging tracks incorrectly.** The biggest mistake is thinking a track is fresher than it is. When in doubt, assume it is older. Old tracks are safer (the animal is farther away).
- **Ignoring substrate.** Sand ages tracks much faster than mud. A track in sand may look 3 days old when it is only 6 hours old. Learn your local soil.
- **Assuming all prints from one animal are the same.** Front and back prints differ. A deer's front track is rounder; the back track is more pointed. Count prints carefully.
- **Not checking for multiple animals.** A trail may show two different track sizes. Two animals traveled the same path or at different times.
