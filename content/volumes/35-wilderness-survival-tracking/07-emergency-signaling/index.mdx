## Overview

A signal is meaningless unless someone is looking for it. Your goal is to make yourself visible to searchers from the air, from a distance, and at night. Mirror flashes are visible 50+ kilometers. Smoke rises above terrain. Ground signals are visible from aircraft. Sound carries farther than you think.

This chapter teaches you to signal effectively, choose methods by situation, and understand what rescue personnel actually look for.

## Prerequisites

- Vol 2, Ch 2 — Terrain Navigation (for understanding visibility and high-ground positioning)

## Materials & Tools

### For Mirror Signaling

- **Mirror:** Any reflective surface (steel, aluminum, compact mirror, polished metal, water in a cup, broken glass)
- **Aiming guide (optional):** A notch carved in a stick, or your hand with two fingers forming a V

### For Smoke Signals

- **Fire:** See Vol 3, Ch 2 (Fire Making)
- **Green materials:** Fresh leaves, branches, damp grass (makes white smoke)
- **Dark materials (optional):** Plastic, rubber, fabric (makes black smoke) — visible but less preferred
- **Fuel:** Dry wood (burns hotter = better rise)

### For Ground Signals

- **Materials:** Rocks, wood, or vegetation to form shapes
- **Contrasting material:** Light rocks on dark earth, or dark material on light ground

### For Sound Signals

- **Whistle:** 3 blasts is the universal distress signal
- **Rock or log:** To bang on trees or ground
- **Voice:** Yelling (though not effective beyond 1 km in open terrain)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Method 1: Mirror Signaling (Most Effective)

**Step 1: Understand mirror signaling range.**

A mirror signal is visible to aircraft up to 50+ km away in clear conditions. It is the fastest long-range distress signal. Aircraft crews scan for flashes.

**Step 2: Prepare your mirror.**

Any reflective surface works:
- Compact mirror, steel pan, or polished metal
- Broken mirror or glass (even a piece 5 × 5 cm works)
- Water in a steel cup (reflect the sun into the cup)
- Polished rock (harder to use but effective)

**Step 3: Find the sun.**

Your back should be to the sun. The mirror should reflect the sun toward your target (an aircraft, distant location, or a wide area).

**Step 4: Aim the mirror (aiming method 1 — hand sighting).**

Make a V with your thumb and forefinger. Hold the mirror at arm's length. Place the target (aircraft or location you want to signal) in the V. Move the mirror until the sun's reflection appears in the V, aimed at the target.

**Step 5: Aim the mirror (aiming method 2 — using a notch).**

Carve a small notch in a stick. Sight the target through the notch. Hold the mirror so the sun reflects toward the notch. The reflection goes toward the target.

**Step 6: Flash the signal.**

Once aimed, tilt the mirror slightly side to side to create a flashing motion. Flashes are more noticeable than steady light. Flash in groups of three: three flashes, pause, three flashes.

**Step 7: Know limitations.**

Mirror signaling works only:
- In daylight
- When the sun is behind you
- When there is clear line of sight to the target
- In clear, not cloudy, conditions

**Step 8: Carry a backup mirror.**

Keep a small mirror in any survival kit. It is lightweight and invaluable.

### Method 2: Smoke Signals

**Step 1: Choose your location.**

Smoke is visible from up to 15 km away depending on visibility and air movement. Choose high ground where smoke will rise above terrain and be visible from aircraft.

**Step 2: Build a fire.**

See Vol 3, Ch 2 for fire-making details. Use dry wood to create a hot fire that will sustain smoke.

**Step 3: Gather green materials.**

Collect fresh branches, green leaves, damp grass, or pine boughs. These create white smoke when added to the fire — white smoke is visible against both sky and ground.

**Step 4: Add smoke-making material in waves.**

Do NOT add all green material at once; the fire will go out. Add a large handful every 30 seconds to 1 minute. The fire should flare between additions. This creates visible pulsing smoke.

**Step 5: Create the pattern.**

Three columns of smoke is the standard distress signal. Space three fires 10 meters apart, or create three separate burns at the same fire by adding different materials in sequence.

**Step 6: Know the limitations.**

Smoke signals work only:
- In daylight
- When there is air movement to carry the smoke upward (still air = smoke drifts weakly)
- When the sun is high enough to illuminate the smoke (early morning or late evening smoke is harder to see)

**Step 7: Combine with other signals.**

Do not rely on smoke alone. Smoke takes time to gather materials and build the fire. Use mirrors first (faster) and smoke second.

### Method 3: Ground Signals

**Step 3: Create a large X or SOS.**

Ground signals are visible from aircraft at 500+ meters altitude. Create large, high-contrast shapes using rocks, wood, or ground disturbance.

**Step 1: Choose your location.**

An open area (cleared field, beach, rocky plain) where the signal will be visible from above.

**Step 2: Choose your signal.**

- **SOS:** Three dots, three dashes, three dots, using rocks or trenches. Each letter is 3 × 3 meters minimum.
- **X:** Two lines of rocks crossing. 10+ meters on each side.
- **Arrow:** Points to your location; useful if you have a specific shelter or landmark.
- **Large V or straight line:** Any high-contrast feature visible from altitude.

**Step 2: Gather materials.**

- Rocks (most durable)
- Wood logs or branches (visible as long as not covered)
- Cleared earth (dig trenches or kick away vegetation to show dark soil)
- Colored fabric or clothing laid out in a pattern

**Step 4: Space and contrast are critical.**

The signal must be:
- Large (minimum 10 meters on any side)
- High-contrast (light rocks on dark ground, or dark earth on light ground)
- Simple (avoid complex shapes — X and SOS are best)

**Step 5: Maintain the signal.**

Check weekly. Wind, rain, and animals can displace rocks. Remove any debris that might obscure the signal.

**Step 6: Mark your location with a shelter.**

If you build a shelter (see Vol 3, Ch 3), place it near the signal so rescuers can see both the ground signal and your shelter.

### Method 4: Sound Signals

**Step 1: Use a whistle (most effective).**

A whistle is audible up to 1 km in open terrain and up to 500 meters in forested terrain. It is louder than your voice and carries farther.

**Step 2: Learn the distress signal.**

Three blasts of the whistle, pause, three blasts, pause, three blasts. This is the universal distress signal and recognized worldwide.

**Step 3: Carry a whistle.**

A small plastic or metal whistle weighs almost nothing and is invaluable. Any whistle works (even a homemade one from bone or wood).

**Step 4: Use bells or banging as backup.**

If you have no whistle:
- Bang on a log or rock with a stick, creating three loud impacts
- Ring a bell or metal object three times
- Shout (less effective; your voice tires, and sound does not carry as far)

**Step 5: Know when sound signals work.**

Sound signals work:
- At any time of day
- In any weather (though rain and wind reduce range)
- When line of sight is not required
- When rescuers are within 1 km (in open terrain)

**Step 6: Use sound signals in forests.**

In dense forest, sound carries farther than visual signals. Bang on trees or the ground regularly — searchers will hear and investigate.

### Method 5: Combining Signals (Best Practice)

**Step 1: Use all available signals together.**

A multi-method approach increases the chance of rescue:
- Morning: Use mirrors (sun angle is good)
- Midday: Use ground signals and smoke if visible
- Evening: Use sound signals
- Night: Use a fire or flashlight (if you have one)

**Step 2: Position signals to overlap.**

Place your mirror signaling location near your ground signals, or within sight of your shelter. Rescuers will investigate multiple signals.

**Step 3: Maintain readiness.**

Keep mirrors, whistle, and fire materials prepared at all times. Do not wait until you see an aircraft to start signaling.

**Step 4: Establish a pattern rescuers recognize.**

Change signals regularly (every few hours) so rescuers know you are still alive and active. Stationary signals suggest abandonment.

## Verification

**Mirror signaling is effective if:**
- You create a steady flash visible at arm's length
- You can direct the reflection toward a target
- The signal can be seen by an observer 1 km away on the ground (test by having someone stand in the distance and confirm they see flashes)

**Smoke signals are effective if:**
- The fire burns hot and sustains smoke for 10+ minutes
- Smoke rises at least 50 meters above ground (visible from aircraft)
- The signal is visible from 1 km away

**Ground signals are effective if:**
- The shapes are large (10+ meters minimum dimension)
- Contrast is high (light on dark or dark on light)
- The signal is visible from a helicopter hovering at 500 meters altitude

**Sound signals are effective if:**
- Three whistle blasts are audible at 200+ meters away
- Rescuers within hearing distance (1 km) can identify the signal as intentional (pattern of three)

**Signs of successful signaling:**
- An aircraft or helicopter circles overhead
- Searchers arrive at your location
- You hear response signals (shouting, horn blasts)

## Common Mistakes

- **Using a mirror in the wrong direction.** Your back must be to the sun. If the sun is in front of you, the mirror will not work.
- **Building a signal fire that goes out.** Green materials kill fires. Add them gradually, not all at once. Keep the core fire hot with dry wood.
- **Creating signals that are too small.** From aircraft at 1000 meters, an X made of car-sized rocks is barely visible. Make signals 10+ meters on each side.
- **Assuming a single signal is enough.** Use all methods. Aircraft may miss a mirror flash but spot smoke. Rescuers on foot may not see ground signals but will hear a whistle.
- **Signaling only when you see rescuers.** Rescuers may be watching for signals. Signal continuously and regularly (e.g., mirror signals every few hours during daylight).
- **Ignoring wind direction for smoke.** Wind carries smoke away from your target. Position fires upwind of where you expect rescuers to approach.
- **Using black smoke as your only signal.** Black smoke (from plastic or rubber) is visible but less preferred. White smoke (green materials) is better.
- **Exhausting yourself with sound signals.** A whistle does not tire you. Shouting tires the voice. Carry a whistle.
