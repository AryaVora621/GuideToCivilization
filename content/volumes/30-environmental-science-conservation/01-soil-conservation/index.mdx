## Overview

Soil is the foundation of agriculture. Loss of 1 cm soil takes 100 years to form naturally. Post-collapse, soil conservation determines survival.

This chapter covers:
1. **Erosion:** Wind and water; prevention.
2. **Structure:** Maintaining soil health (porosity, aggregate stability).
3. **Loss prevention:** Terracing, contours, vegetation.
4. **Restoration:** Rebuilding degraded soil.

## Prerequisites

Understand soil science (Vol 10, Ch 1). Know crop management (Vol 10, Ch 5).

## Part 1: Erosion Mechanisms and Prevention

### Water erosion

**Cause:** Rain falls; water flows downhill; carries soil.

**Types:**
- Sheet erosion: Thin layer of soil removed uniformly (hard to detect until severe).
- Rill erosion: Water concentrates; cuts small channels (visible; precedes severe loss).
- Gully erosion: Deep channels formed (major soil loss; fields destroyed).

**Prevention:**
1. **Contour farming:** Plant rows along hillside contours (perpendicular to slope), not up-and-down (reduces water flow velocity).
2. **Terracing:** Cut horizontal benches on steep slopes; plant on terraces (eliminates water running downhill).
3. **Cover crops:** Plant vegetation that covers soil (roots hold soil; vegetation breaks rainfall impact).
4. **Mulch:** Straw or plant material on soil surface (protects from rain impact; slows water flow).
5. **Windbreaks:** Trees or hedge rows slow water runoff (also prevent wind erosion).

### Wind erosion

**Cause:** Wind blows soil particles away (especially fine particles in cultivated fields).

**Risk factors:**
- Dry soil (moister soil is less erodible).
- Bare ground (no vegetation).
- Flat terrain (no natural barriers).
- High wind speed.

**Prevention:**
1. **Windbreaks:** Plant trees or shrubs perpendicular to prevailing wind (reduce wind speed 50%+ downwind).
2. **Keep soil moist:** Irrigation or rain; wet soil does not blow away.
3. **Vegetation:** Permanent grass, cover crops; living roots hold soil.
4. **Stubble retention:** Don't plow under crop residue immediately; leave stubble 1-2 years (holds soil; breaks wind).

### River/stream erosion

**Cause:** Flowing water erodes banks (especially when water volume increases in flood).

**Prevention:**
1. **Riparian buffer:** Keep trees and vegetation along stream banks (root systems stabilize banks; shade prevents temperature extremes).
2. **No cultivation near water:** Avoid planting right to water's edge (let natural vegetation stabilize).
3. **Gravel bars:** Deposition areas that absorb energy; prevent bank erosion upstream.

## Part 2: Soil Structure Maintenance

### Why structure matters

**Good structure:**
- Porous (holds water; drains excess).
- Aggregated (stable; resists compaction and erosion).
- Allows root penetration.
- Supports beneficial soil organisms.

**Poor structure:**
- Compacted (impermeable; poor drainage).
- Crusted (water runs off; prevents seedling emergence).
- Susceptible to erosion.

### Practices maintaining structure

**Minimize tillage:** Plowing destroys aggregate structure; compacts soil below plow layer.
- Post-collapse: Hand-digging disturbs less than plowing.
- Perennial crops (trees, berries) eliminate yearly tillage.

**Add organic matter:** Compost and manure improve structure; bind soil particles.
- Application rate: 2-5 cm annually (see Vol 10, Ch 6).
- Effect builds over years (persistent improvement).

**Diverse crop rotations:** Different root depths, plant types improve soil structure.
- Legumes fix nitrogen; deep-rooted plants break compaction; grasses rebuild aggregates.

**Avoid compaction:** Heavy machinery (post-collapse: hand labor prevents this).
- Work on raised beds (concentrates fertility; minimizes compaction).

### Water holding capacity

**Objective:** Soil retains water during dry periods; drains excess in wet periods.

**Improvement:**
1. Add organic matter (increases pore space; holds water).
2. Improve structure (aggregates create pores).
3. Minimize compaction.

**Result:** Deeper rooting; more drought-resistant crops; sustained yield.

## Part 2b: Soil Testing and Monitoring

### Visual assessment

**Erosion severity:**
- None visible: Good management.
- Light sheet erosion (top layer lost; slight texture change): Early warning; implement preventive measures.
- Rills visible (small channels): Moderate erosion; terrace or contour required.
- Gullies forming (deep channels): Severe erosion; requires immediate intervention; field may be unproductive.

**Structure assessment:**
- Good: Crumbles easily; aggregates visible; porous.
- Fair: Some clumping; moderate porosity.
- Poor: Compacted; crusted; dust or mud only; no aggregates.

**Organic matter:**
- Visual: Dark color (more organic matter); light color (depleted).
- Feel: Moist, crumbly (good organic matter); dry, hard (depleted).

### Practical monitoring

**Soil pit:** Dig to 30 cm depth (twice yearly or annually).
- Assess color, structure, compaction.
- Look for layers (layering indicates compaction).
- Check root penetration (depth of visible roots).

**Erosion pins:** Drive nail partway into soil; measure exposure over months.
- If soil level drops below nail head, erosion is occurring.
- Quantify erosion rate (cm per year).

**Cover assessment:** Percentage of ground covered by vegetation.
- &lt;30%: High erosion risk.
- 30-70%: Moderate risk; implement improvements.
- &gt;70%: Low erosion risk; sustainable.

## Part 3: Contour and Terraced Farming

### Contour farming

**Method:** Plant rows along hillside contours (not up-slope).

**Benefit:** Water flows laterally; doesn't accumulate and erode.

**Implementation:**
1. Identify contour lines (same elevation).
2. Mark contours with chalk or stakes.
3. Plant rows, furrows, terraces along contours.
4. Maintain contours every year (re-establish if eroded).

**Effectiveness:** Reduces erosion 30-50%; simple to implement.

**Slope limit:** Works well up to 15% slope; steeper requires terracing.

### Terracing

**Method:** Cut horizontal benches on steep slopes; plant on benches.

**Benefit:** Eliminates vertical slope; water doesn't run downhill.

**Labor requirement:** Intensive (weeks of work for small area); but permanent (lasts decades).

**Types:**
1. **Cut-and-fill terraces:** Cut into hillside; pile soil on lower edge (creates bench).
2. **Stone-faced terraces:** Dry-stack stone walls support terraces (very durable; requires skill).
3. **Vegetated terraces:** Planted vegetation supports terrace edges.

**Post-collapse suitability:** Feasible for small fields; requires high labor.

## Part 4: Cover Crops and Vegetation

### Winter cover crops

**Purpose:** Prevent erosion during off-season; add organic matter; fix nitrogen.

**Types:**
- Legumes (clover, vetch): Fix nitrogen; valuable for next crop.
- Grasses (rye, oats): Protect soil; extensive root system prevents erosion.
- Mix of both: Balanced benefits.

**Seeding:** After harvest of main crop; allow 2-3 months growth before spring plowing.

**Incorporation:** Plow under in spring (returns organic matter).

### Perennial vegetation

**Hedgerows and windbreaks:** Trees and shrubs planted along field edges.
- Provide wind protection; prevent erosion.
- Produce fruit/nuts (food production).
- Habitat for beneficial insects and wildlife.

**Permanent pasture:** Never plowed; continuous grass cover prevents erosion.
- Stocking with livestock adds manure (fertilizer).
- Sustainable indefinitely with proper management.

### Buffer strips

**Riparian buffers:** Strip of grass, shrubs, trees along water.
- Width: 10-30 meters wide (wider = better filtration).
- Prevents erosion; filters runoff; improves water quality.

## Part 5: Settlement-Scale Implementation

### Farmland assessment

**First step:** Walk your settlement farmland and classify:
- Flat land (&lt;5% slope): Low erosion risk; focus on organic matter.
- Moderate slope (5-15%): Medium risk; implement contour farming.
- Steep slope (&gt;15%): High risk; requires terracing or permanent vegetation.
- Near water: Riparian buffer required; no cultivation.

### Year 1 implementation plan

**All slopes:**
- Plant cover crops (winter); incorporate spring.
- Add compost (2-3 cm); work into top layer.
- Minimize tillage (hand-dig only if necessary).

**Moderate slopes (5-15%):**
- Mark contour lines (string lines; chalk marks).
- Plant next season along contours.

**Steep slopes (&gt;15%):**
- Plant cover crop; do not cultivate.
- Begin terrace construction (heavy labor; Year 2-3 project).
- Or convert to permanent pasture or tree crops (no annual tillage = no erosion).

**Water edges:**
- Plant riparian vegetation (shrubs, trees); let naturalize.
- Remove annual crops from within 10m of water.

### Year 2+ management

**Maintain contours:** Repair if rain erodes them.

**Monitor:** Check erosion pins quarterly; visual assessment twice yearly.

**Continue organic matter:** 2-3 cm compost/manure annually (builds structure permanently).

**Rotate crops:** Include legumes and deep-rooted crops (improve structure).

## Part 6: Soil Restoration

### Degraded soil recovery

**Timeline:** 1-10 years to see significant improvement.

**Steps:**
1. **Stop erosion:** Implement contour, terracing, vegetation (prevents further loss).
2. **Add organic matter:** 5-10 cm per year (accelerates recovery).
3. **Minimize disturbance:** Reduce tillage; allow structure to develop.
4. **Improve hydration:** Cover crops or irrigation; wet soil resists erosion.
5. **Monitor:** Test annually (soil depth, organic matter, water-holding capacity).

### Abandoned land restoration

**Severely degraded land (gullies, compaction):**
1. Terrace or contour (stabilize).
2. Plant cover crops (stabilize and improve).
3. Add compost/manure heavily (rebuild).
4. Wait 2-3 years before cropping; allow vegetation to stabilize.
5. Gradual cropping (start with tolerant crops; progress to sensitive crops).

**Timeline:** 5-10 years to restore productive soil.

## Part 7: Economics and Community Benefits

### Cost-benefit of conservation

**Investment:** 100-200 person-hours per 5-hectare farm (contour marking, terrace construction, management).

**Return:** Sustained productivity for decades (vs. degraded soil 3-5 years without conservation; food insecurity).

**Payback period:** 1-2 years (productivity gain from improved soil structure and erosion prevention).

**Long-term value:** Each hectare conserved can feed 1-2 people indefinitely (if properly managed; this is the foundation of civilization survival).

### Community coordination

**Soil surveys:** Designate one person to assess all settlement farmland (categorize by slope/risk; create conservation priorities).

**Work parties:** Terrace construction and major soil work requires 20+ people (organize seasonal work parties; share labor across farms).

**Seed supply:** Collect and store cover crop seeds (clover, vetch, rye); distribute to farmers.

**Compost/manure:** Coordinate production (Vol 10, Ch 6); distribute to priority areas (worst-degraded soil first).

**Monitoring:** Assign rotation monitor (check erosion pins, visual assessment monthly); report to settlement leadership quarterly.

## Verification

Your soil conservation skill is adequate if:
1. **Assessment:** You can identify erosion types and severity.
2. **Contour:** You can identify and establish contour lines on slopes.
3. **Practices:** You implement cover crops, mulch, and minimize tillage.
4. **Restoration:** You understand timeline and steps for soil recovery.
5. **Scaling:** You apply practices to settlement farms (5+ hectares).

## Common Mistakes

**Plowing up-slope.** Speeds erosion; destroys soil structure. Always contour or terrace.

**Bare ground in off-season.** Exposes soil to rain and wind. Plant cover crops.

**Compaction from heavy traffic.** Vehicle compaction is permanent (years to recover). Minimize travel on fields.

**Insufficient organic matter.** Single applications don't improve degraded soil. Apply annually, continuously.

**Ignoring early erosion signs.** Sheet erosion is hard to detect; by the time visible, major loss occurred. Monitor constantly.

**Not protecting water sources.** Water erosion is primary soil loss vector. Keep vegetation along streams.

**Expecting quick results.** Soil restoration takes 5-10 years. Plant cover crops and compost immediately; expect visible improvement in Year 2-3; full recovery in 5+ years.

**Farming on land unsuitable for crops.** Very steep slopes (&gt;25%) should be permanent forest or pasture (not cultivated). Accept limitations; use appropriate land for appropriate purposes.

## Part 8: Seasonal Soil Conservation Calendar

### Spring (March-May)

- Assess over-wintering cover crops; incorporate into soil.
- Apply compost/manure (2-3 cm); work into top layer.
- Plant crops along established contours.
- Monitor for spring erosion (heavy rains).

### Summer (June-August)

- Monitor crops; maintain cover (minimize bare ground).
- Check erosion pins for erosion progress.
- Maintain contours if rain erodes them.
- Prepare cover crop seeds for fall planting.

### Fall (September-November)

- Harvest main crop; leave stubble (holds winter soil).
- Plant cover crops immediately after harvest.
- Begin terrace construction if planned (takes weeks/months).
- Assess year's erosion; plan next year improvements.

### Winter (December-February)

- Minimize field travel (compacts soil).
- Cover crops growing; protecting soil.
- Plan next year's soil conservation work.
- Repair contours damaged by winter erosion.

## Summary

Soil conservation is not a luxury; it is the foundation of civilization. Every farm must implement contour farming, cover crops, and organic matter addition as baseline practices. Without conservation, productive soil becomes desert within 10 years. With conservation, soil improves and sustains civilization for generations. The choice is binary: conserve, or collapse.
