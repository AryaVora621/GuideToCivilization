## Overview

Carrying capacity is the maximum population a territory can sustainably support. Post-collapse civilization succeeds if settlements respect land limits; fails if populations exceed carrying capacity.

This chapter covers:
1. **Definitions:** Carrying capacity for food, water, fuel.
2. **Calculations:** How much land per person.
3. **Integration:** Combining all resource types.
4. **Reality check:** Accounting for inefficiency and failure.
5. **Adjustment:** Strategies if land is limited.

## Prerequisites

Understand agriculture yields (Vol 10). Know environmental systems (Vol 13, Ch 1-3).

## Part 1: Carrying Capacity Fundamentals

### Definition

**Carrying capacity:** Maximum population sustainable indefinitely given:
- Available land (hectares).
- Resource production per hectare (food, water, timber, forage).
- Per-capita consumption (calories, water, fuel).

**Formula:** Carrying Capacity = Total Production / Per-Capita Consumption

### Why it matters

**Exceed capacity:** Population starves, social collapse, resource depletion, conflict.

**Within capacity:** Sustainable; stable; surplus for contingency.

**Well below capacity:** Buffer for crop failure, animal loss, population growth.

**Post-collapse reality:** Most settled areas dramatically exceed carrying capacity (industrial agriculture creates fake surplus). When infrastructure collapses, land-based capacity becomes the hard limit.

## Part 2: Agricultural Carrying Capacity

### Caloric needs

**Per person per day:** 2000-2500 kcal (depends on activity level, climate).

**Post-collapse agriculture:** ~2000 kcal/day is baseline (limited variety; less activity than industrial society).

**Annual need per person:** 2000 kcal/day × 365 = 730,000 kcal/year.

### Crop yield calculations

**Staple grains (wheat, barley, rye):**
- Yield: 1-2 tons per hectare (post-collapse, manual agriculture).
- Calories per ton: ~3.5 million kcal.
- Per hectare production: 3.5-7 million kcal/year.

**Example:** 1 hectare grain = 3.5-7 million kcal = support 4.8-9.6 people (at 730,000 kcal/person/year).

**Legumes (beans, lentils):**
- Yield: 0.8-1.5 tons per hectare.
- Calories per ton: ~3.3 million kcal (plus protein, which is valuable).
- Per hectare production: 2.6-5 million kcal/year.

**Root crops (potatoes, turnips, carrots):**
- Yield: 5-15 tons per hectare (high volume).
- Calories per ton: ~0.8-1 million kcal (lower calorie density than grain; higher water content).
- Per hectare production: 4-15 million kcal/year.

**Example:** 1 hectare potatoes = 5-15 million kcal = support 7-21 people (higher end if land-rich; lower end if land-poor).

### Balanced diet requirement

**Calories alone insufficient:** Need protein, fats, vegetables, minerals.

**Practical mix:**
- 50% staple grain (main calories).
- 20% legumes (protein).
- 20% vegetables/root crops (vitamins, minerals).
- 10% fats (oil, meat, dairy).

**Land allocation example (100 people):**
- Grains: 15 hectares (provides ~45,000 tons/year calories).
- Legumes: 6 hectares (provides protein + rotates soil).
- Vegetables/roots: 6 hectares (provides nutrition + variety).
- Fallow/pasture: 5 hectares (allows rotation, livestock).
- **Total: 32 hectares for food for 100 people.**

## Part 3: Livestock Carrying Capacity

### Feed requirement per animal per year

**Cattle (1000 kg animal):**
- Forage need: 3-5 tons dry grass/forage per year.
- Per hectare productivity: 1-2 tons dry forage/year (depending on climate, management).
- Hectares per cow: 2-5 hectares (depending on quality of pasture).

**Sheep/goats (50 kg animal):**
- Forage need: 200-300 kg dry grass/forage per year.
- Multiple can fit per hectare: 5-10 sheep/goats per hectare.

**Pigs (100-150 kg):**
- Feed: 500-800 kg grain or mixed feed per year (can use crop waste + supplemental grain).
- Post-collapse: Fed on food waste, crop residue, foraged plants.

**Poultry (chickens, ducks, geese):**
- Feed: 50-100 kg grain per bird per year.
- Can be integrated with crop production (eat insects, crop waste).

### Livestock productivity per capita

**Meat production per animal per year:**
- Cattle: 150-200 kg meat (annual slaughter of portion of herd).
- Pigs: 50-100 kg meat (higher reproduction allows faster turnover).
- Sheep/goats: 15-30 kg meat.
- Chickens: 1-2 kg meat per bird per year.

**Post-collapse diet (100 people):**
- Meat requirement: 20-30 kg per person per year (supplementary, not primary calories).
- Translates to: 2,000-3,000 kg meat per year for settlement.
- Could be met by: 15-20 cattle, or 80-100 pigs, or 100+ sheep/goats, or 500+ chickens (mixed herd realistic).

**Land for livestock (100 people):**
- 20 cattle at 3 hectares per cow = 60 hectares.
- OR 10 cattle + 50 sheep = 30 + 5 = 35 hectares.
- OR 30 pigs + 200 chickens + 20 sheep = 5 + 0 + 2 = 7 hectares (grain-fed; grain comes from crop land).

**Realistic mixed herd:** 10 cattle (dairy/meat) + 30 sheep (meat/dairy) + 20 pigs (meat) + 100 chickens (eggs/meat) on 15-20 hectares pasture (plus grain supplement from crop land).

## Part 4: Forest Carrying Capacity

### Fuelwood requirement

**Per person per year:** 1-2 cords wood (post-collapse: efficient heating, cooking).

**100 people:** 100-200 cords = 240-480 cubic meters.

**Forest productivity:** 2-5 tons dry wood per hectare annually (varies by species, climate, management).

**Hectares needed:** 15-30 hectares (provides fuelwood only; no other uses).

### Timber carrying capacity

**Post-collapse structural needs:** Building, repairs, tools (occasional; every 5-10 years per building).

**Settlement-level estimate:** 1-2 buildings per person (homes, storage, workshops).

**Per building lifetime:** 20-30 cubic meters timber (rough estimate over 50-year lifespan).

**Sustainable timber yield:** 1-2 cubic meters per hectare per year (harvest every 60-80 years; yield = total volume / rotation time).

**Hectares needed:** 10-20 hectares (combined with fuelwood production; same land can provide both).

### Combined forest productivity

**Integrated forest management (15-20 hectares per 100 people):**
- Fuelwood: 2-5 tons/year × 15 hectares = 30-75 tons (sufficient for 100 people).
- Timber: 1 ton/year × 15 hectares = 15 tons (in rotation; provides long-term supply).
- Nuts/fruits: 100-200 kg per hectare (supplements diet).

## Part 5: Water Carrying Capacity

### Daily water requirement

**Per person:** 2-3 liters drinking/cooking.

**100 people:** 200-300 liters/day = 70,000-110,000 liters/year.

**Livestock:** Cattle 40-50 liters/day; sheep 2-5 liters/day (see Vol 10, Ch 8).

**Irrigation (if needed):** 5-10 mm per day during growing season = 50,000-100,000 liters/hectare for growing season (see Vol 13, Ch 2).

### Water source sustainability

**Rainfall:** Depends on climate.
- Wet climate (1000+ mm/year): Water abundant; can support high population density.
- Moderate (500-1000 mm/year): Water manageable; seasonal scarcity.
- Dry (&lt;500 mm/year): Water limiting; low population density necessary.

**Spring/well:** If consistent year-round, very reliable (10-50 liters/hour sustainable).

**Pond:** 100-500 cubic meters storage = 100,000-500,000 liters; sufficient for 3-15 months depending on use.

**Carrying capacity assessment:**
- Abundant water (spring + pond + 1000+ mm rain): No limitation.
- Moderate water (pond + 500-1000 mm rain): Sustainable for 100-200 people.
- Limited water (&lt;500 mm rain, no reliable spring): Sustainable for 20-50 people.

## Part 6: Integration: Total Carrying Capacity

### Combining all resources

**Typical settlement (100 people) land requirements:**

| Resource | Hectares | Notes |
|----------|----------|-------|
| Crops (food) | 30 | Grains, legumes, vegetables |
| Pasture (livestock feed) | 15 | Cattle, sheep, pigs |
| Forest (fuelwood, timber) | 15 | Sustainable yield |
| Infrastructure (paths, buildings, waste) | 2 | Roads, settlements, composting areas |
| Buffer (crop failure, contingency) | 8 | 20% surplus |
| **Total** | **70 hectares** | |

**Rule of thumb:** 0.7 hectares per person for self-sufficient settlement (crops, livestock, forest, contingency).

### Land quality adjustment

**Fertile land (good soil, water, climate):**
- Can support 2-3 people per hectare.
- Total land need for 100: 33-50 hectares.

**Marginal land (poor soil, dry, cold):**
- Can support 0.3-0.5 people per hectare.
- Total land need for 100: 200-300 hectares.

**Realistic post-collapse:** Most settlements will have mixed-quality land; assume 0.5-1.0 people per hectare (100-150 hectares for 100 people).

## Part 7: Calculation Tool and Examples

### Step-by-step carrying capacity assessment

**1. Measure available land:**
- Total territory claimed by settlement: ___ hectares.
- Usable for agriculture: ___ hectares.
- Usable for pasture: ___ hectares.
- Usable for forest: ___ hectares.

**2. Calculate agricultural production:**
- Grain: ___ hectares × 5 million kcal/hectare = ___ kcal.
- Legumes: ___ hectares × 3 million kcal/hectare = ___ kcal.
- Vegetables: ___ hectares × 8 million kcal/hectare = ___ kcal.
- Total food calories: ___ kcal / year.

**3. Divide by per-capita need:**
- ___ kcal / 730,000 kcal per person = ___ people (food capacity).

**4. Calculate livestock capacity:**
- Pasture hectares / hectares per animal × animals per settlement = population support from meat/dairy.

**5. Calculate forest capacity:**
- Forest hectares × tons/hectare = fuelwood tons/year.
- Check against 1-2 tons per person per year (100 people = 100-200 tons needed).

**6. Adjust for reality:**
- Subtract 20% for inefficiency, failure, waste.
- Subtract for infrastructure, paths, unusable land.
- Result = realistic sustainable population.

### Example: 100-hectare settlement

**Assumed composition:**
- 35 hectares grain + legumes.
- 20 hectares vegetables/roots.
- 20 hectares pasture.
- 20 hectares forest.
- 5 hectares infrastructure/waste.

**Calculations:**
- Grains (25 ha): 25 × 5 million = 125 million kcal.
- Legumes (10 ha): 10 × 3 million = 30 million kcal.
- Vegetables (20 ha): 20 × 8 million = 160 million kcal.
- Total: 315 million kcal / 730,000 per person = **432 people** (food alone).

**Livestock check:**
- 20 hectares pasture / 3 hectares per cow = 6-7 cattle.
- Plus 30+ sheep = meat/dairy for ~50 people.

**Forest check:**
- 20 hectares forest × 3 tons/hectare = 60 tons fuelwood/year.
- 60 tons / 1.5 tons per person = **40 people** capacity (forest limiting).

**Carrying capacity:** Limited by forest; realistically supports **40-50 people** with balanced resources.

## Part 8: Exceeding Capacity: Reality Check

### Warning signs

**During good years (surplus):** Population grows; complacency increases.

**Pressure points:**
- Fuelwood becomes scarce (forests cut faster than growth).
- Pasture degrades (livestock numbers exceed forage production).
- Soil degradation (crop yields decline; need more land).
- Water stress (dry season; conflict over water allocation).

**Before collapse:** 2-5 year warning period (production lags demand).

### Consequences of exceeding capacity

**Year 1-2:** Surplus draws down; storage depletes.

**Year 3-4:** Rationing begins; social tension increases.

**Year 5+:** Starvation, disease, conflict; collapse likely.

**Post-collapse migration:** Refugees abandon settlement; survivors face resource wars.

## Part 9: Adjustment Strategies (If Land Is Limited)

### If too little land

**Option 1: Reduce population.**
- Most direct; least popular.
- Post-collapse reality: Starvation or emigration forces this eventually.

**Option 2: Intensify production.**
- Increase crop yields per hectare (better soil, irrigation, crop selection).
- Reduce livestock (prioritize efficient animals: poultry, rabbits, fish).
- Maximize agroforestry (multiple uses per hectare).
- Potential: Support 1.5-2x baseline population if well-managed.

**Option 3: Specialize and trade.**
- Settle in fertile niche (river valley, coastal); specialize in surplus production.
- Trade surplus for forest products, timber, metals from other settlements.
- Works post-collapse only if strong inter-settlement network exists.

**Option 4: Extended supply network.**
- Settlements cluster; share resources.
- One settlement focuses on grain; another on forest; another on livestock.
- Requires stable alliances; vulnerable to conflict.

### Realistic post-collapse scenario

**Most settlements will be land-rich, population-poor** (depopulation from industrial collapse).

**Opportunity:** Recover degraded land; restore forests; build agroforestry; establish carrying capacity well below maximum.

**Long-term stability:** Population grows to 50-70% of carrying capacity (buffer for contingency, social growth).

## Part 10: Planning for Contingency

### Build safety margin

**Target: Sustainably support X people at 70% of capacity.**

**Benefits:**
- 30% surplus for population growth.
- Buffer for crop failure (1-2 year storage).
- Ability to support refugees/visitors.
- Margin for inefficiency and learning.

**Example:** If land can support 100 people, plan for 70; surplus can feed extra 20+ for short term.

### Diversification as buffer

**Multiple food sources:** If grain fails, legumes + roots + livestock still feed settlement.

**Multiple fuel sources:** Forest + crop residue + peat (if available) + animal dung (biogas).

**Multiple water sources:** Spring + well + pond (three independent systems; failure of one doesn't catastrophe).

**Multiple livestock:** If cattle disease, sheep/pigs/poultry still produce meat.

## Verification

Your carrying capacity understanding is adequate if:
1. **Measurement:** You can measure your settlement's usable land.
2. **Calculation:** You can estimate crop, livestock, and forest yields.
3. **Integration:** You can combine yields into total population support estimate.
4. **Reality check:** You account for inefficiency and failure modes.
5. **Adjustment:** You understand what happens if exceeding capacity and options to address.
6. **Planning:** You plan for contingency and growth within limits.

## Common Mistakes

**Assuming industrial efficiency post-collapse.** Crop yields drop 50%; livestock efficiency drops; losses increase. Plan for 50% of what industrial society achieved.

**Ignoring water limits.** Carrying capacity is bottlenecked by scarcest resource (usually water in dry climates, land in wet).

**Not accounting for fallow.** Land needs rest (rotation); actual usable area is 60-70% of total (not 100%).

**Underestimating livestock land requirements.** 5 hectares per cow is realistic; underestimating means land conflict.

**Assuming trade continues.** Post-collapse, assume zero inter-settlement trade (most settlements fail). Plan for complete self-sufficiency.

**Expecting population control.** Post-collapse births exceed deaths; population grows. Plan for growth; ensure land can sustain 2x initial population.
