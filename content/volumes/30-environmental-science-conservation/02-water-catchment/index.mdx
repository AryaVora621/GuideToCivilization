## Overview

Water is critical for agriculture, drinking, sanitation. Post-collapse, reliable water supply determines survival. Catchment systems capture rainfall; store it for use during dry periods.

This chapter covers:
1. **Rainfall capture:** Roofs, catchment surfaces.
2. **Storage:** Cisterns, ponds, tanks.
3. **Springs and wells:** Underground water access.
4. **Scaling:** Settlement water security.

## Prerequisites

Understand soil conservation (Vol 13, Ch 1). Know water systems (Vol 5).

## Part 1: Rainfall Catchment

### Roof catchment (simplest)

**Method:** Gutters capture water from building roofs; direct to storage.

**Calculation:**
- Roof area × rainfall = water captured.
- Example: 100 sq meter roof × 50 mm rain = 5000 liters captured.

**Post-collapse materials:**
- Gutters: Wood, bamboo, carved channels (any material directing water).
- Downspouts: Bamboo pipes, wooden chutes.
- Storage: Barrels, cisterns, tanks.

**Quality:** First-flush diverts dusty water; clean water fills cistern.

### Ground catchment (larger scale)

**Method:** Pavement, compacted earth, or plastic sheeting directs runoff to storage.

**Scale:** 500+ sq meters catches significant water per storm.

**Construction:** Slope toward central collection point; minimal slope (1% gradient).

### Spring protection (upgrade existing)

**If natural spring exists:**
1. Box spring (capture source).
2. Channel to storage (gravity-fed).
3. Overflow outlet (prevents contamination).
4. Screen inlet (prevents debris).

**Result:** Reliable water year-round if spring is perennial.

## Part 2: Storage Systems

### Cisterns (above ground)

**Materials:**
- Barrels (50-200 liters; portable).
- Tanks (500-5000 liters; require support structure).
- Constructed tanks (brick, stone, concrete).

**Construction (simple tank):**
1. Dig level pit.
2. Build walls (stone, wood framing, brick).
3. Seal interior (clay, plaster, or plastic lining).
4. Add roof (prevents contamination, algae).
5. Install overflow outlet.

**Pros:** Accessible; easy to monitor.

**Cons:** Algae growth in sunlight; freezing in winter (exposed).

**Maintenance:** Clean yearly; drain and refill (removes sediment).

### Ponds (larger scale)

**Purpose:** Store runoff; water livestock; gravity-feed irrigation; supporting ecosystem.

**Site selection:**
- Low point in landscape (water naturally flows there).
- Clay soil (impermeable; holds water); avoid sandy soil (drains quickly).
- Proximity to farmland (reduces pumping/gravity distance).
- Avoid contamination sources (uphill from animals, composting areas).

**Construction:**
1. Choose depression or create one (dig and pile soil on downstream side; creates dam).
2. Line with clay (if available) or plastic sheet (if available); or rely on soil compaction.
3. Size: 10+ meters long; 2-3 meters deep (volume = length × width × average depth).
4. Overflow outlet (PVC pipe or constructed channel; prevents overtopping and dam failure).
5. Shallow inlet (allows sediment to settle; reduces silt entry into pond).
6. Spillway (secondary overflow in extreme rainfall).

**Volume calculation examples:**
- 10m × 10m × 2m = 200,000 liters (200 tons; enough for 100 people ~2 days).
- 20m × 20m × 2.5m = 1,000,000 liters (1000 tons; enough for 100 people ~10 days).
- 30m × 30m × 3m = 2,700,000 liters (enough for 100 people ~30 days).

**Sealing options:**
- Natural clay: Most effective if available; compact layers; hardens with use.
- Plastic sheeting: Vulnerable to puncture; requires UV protection; expensive post-collapse.
- Bentonite (clay powder): Mix into soil; creates seal (intermediate cost/effectiveness).
- Compaction alone: Works if frequent; reduces evaporation and percolation.

**Maintenance:**
- Yearly: Remove sediment buildup; check dam integrity; clear overflow.
- Every 5-10 years: Drain; dredge sediment; repair any damage.

**Seasonal management:**
- Spring/summer: Fills with rain; use for irrigation.
- Fall: Top off before winter.
- Winter: May freeze (ice insulates; water stays liquid underneath); access may be difficult.
- Dry season: Ration strictly; conservation critical.

**Seasonal:** Refill with spring/summer rains; use during dry season.

### Underground storage (tanks, cisterns in cellars)

**Advantages:**
- Protected from sun (no algae; slower evaporation).
- Protected from freezing (insulated by earth).
- Gravity-fed to distribution system.

**Construction:** Dig below frost line; build cistern; cover with removable lid.

## Part 3: Wells and Groundwater

### Hand-dug wells

**Purpose:** Access groundwater below surface (deeper than springs; available year-round).

**Digging process:**
1. Choose location (downhill from contamination sources).
2. Dig to water table (depth varies; 2-20 meters typically).
3. Line well (stone, brick, or wood) to prevent collapse.
4. Install hand pump or rope-and-bucket system.

**Depth finding:** Dig until water appears; let settle 24-48 hours; water level stabilizes.

**Testing:** Well water may have minerals (whitish tint = safe; brown = iron); boil before drinking initially.

### Yield calculation

**Daily yield:** Depends on aquifer recharge rate (how fast groundwater replenishes).
- Good aquifer: 50+ liters/hour hand pumping (sustainable daily).
- Poor aquifer: 10-20 liters/hour (enough for drinking but not irrigation).

**Seasonal:** Yield drops in dry season (water table drops).

**Post-collapse:** Wells are backup; not primary (require physical labor to pump).

## Part 4: Water Quality and Treatment

### Testing (post-collapse)

**Visual:** Color (clear = good; brown = sediment; green = algae); smell (no smell = good; musty = stagnant).

**Basic treatment:**
1. Settle (let sediment drop; takes days).
2. Skim debris from surface.
3. Boil (kills pathogens).
4. Filter through cloth/sand (removes particles).

### Contamination prevention

**Storage:**
- Cover cisterns/ponds (prevents insects, debris).
- Screen inlet (filters leaves, debris).
- Regular cleaning (removes settled sediment).

**Use:**
- Don't drink untreated water from surface sources.
- Always boil water from ponds/catchment for drinking.
- Use for irrigation untreated (safe for plants).

## Part 4: Calculating Water Needs

### Daily consumption

**Person:** 2-3 liters drinking/cooking per day.

**Livestock:** Cattle 40-50 liters/day; sheep/goats 2-5 liters/day.

**Irrigation:** Depends on crops (see Vol 10, Ch 4; 2-10 mm daily during growth).

**Example (100 people + 10 cattle + 5 hectares crops):**
- Drinking/cooking: 100 × 2.5 = 250 liters/day.
- Cattle: 10 × 45 = 450 liters/day.
- Irrigation (5mm): 5 hectares × 5,000 liters per mm = 25,000 liters/day.
- **Total: ~25,700 liters/day.**

### Annual rainfall and storage

**Climate-dependent:**
- Wet climate (1000+ mm/year): Can rely on regular catchment; moderate storage.
- Moderate climate (500-1000 mm/year): Need significant storage; capture all rainfall.
- Dry climate (&lt;500 mm/year): Must store rainwater for entire year; requires very large cistern/pond.

**Example (moderate climate, 750 mm/year):**
- Annual rainfall: 750 mm.
- Dry season (6 months): No rainfall; must store 25,700 × 180 days = 4.6 million liters.
- Roof catchment (1000 sq meters): 1000 sq m × 750 mm = 750,000 liters/year (insufficient).
- **Need:** Larger catchment (5+ hectares) or supplement with wells/springs.

## Part 5: Spring Management

### Finding springs

**Signs:** Wet ground, lush vegetation, water flowing year-round.

**Testing:** Observe through dry season (if water flows, it's perennial).

### Spring protection

**Capture:**
1. Dig small box around spring source.
2. Install overflow outlet (prevents overflow).
3. Install tap/spigot for collection.

**Quality:**
- Spring water is usually clean (filtered through soil).
- Test for safety (boil before drinking initially).

**Reliability:** Perennial springs provide consistent water.

## Part 5b: Water Distribution Systems

### Gravity-fed systems (ideal post-collapse)

**Principle:** Store water at high elevation; gravity flows downhill to users (no pumping needed).

**Setup:**
1. Locate cistern/pond at highest elevation (hilltop if possible).
2. Lay pipes/channels downhill to distribution points.
3. Minimal slope (1-2%) for flow; avoid steep (causes pressure damage).
4. Taps/valves at distribution points (homes, fields, livestock).

**Advantages:**
- No pumping (reduces labor).
- Reliable (gravity always works).
- Scalable (one cistern serves multiple buildings).

**Disadvantage:** Requires elevation difference (works poorly in flat terrain).

### Hand-pump systems

**When needed:** High elevation not available; water stored below natural elevation.

**Equipment:** Hand pump (requires assembly; post-collapse: rebuild from available materials).

**Maintenance:** Pumps require regular greasing; parts wear (plan for repair/replacement).

**Labor:** Hand pumping is labor-intensive (1 liter per minute = 1000 liters takes ~17 hours continuous).

### Water distribution schedule

**Emergency rationing (if storage is low):**
- Drinking/cooking: 2 liters per person per day (non-negotiable).
- Cooking: 1 liter per person daily (included above).
- Hygiene: 1 liter per person daily (if available; wash hands, face).
- Livestock: Minimum to maintain health (cattle 20 liters/day; 50% normal).
- Irrigation: Stopped (no non-essential crops watered).

**Adequate supply (normal operations):**
- All needs met; no restrictions.

**Contingency:** If water drops below 5 days of supply, implement emergency rationing.

## Part 6: Settlement-Scale Water System

### Integrated approach

**Components:**
1. Roofs (100-200 sq m): Catch 5,000-10,000 liters per 50 mm rain.
2. Ponds/cisterns (50,000-200,000 liters): Store seasonal rainfall.
3. Spring (if available): Perennial water supply.
4. Wells (if available): Deep groundwater backup.

**Gravity flow:** Higher elevation storage flows downhill to homes/fields (no pumping needed).

**Seasonal management:**
- Spring/summer: Collect and fill cistern; use for irrigation.
- Fall: Prepare for winter (clean cistern; ensure storage full).
- Winter: Use stored water; monitor consumption.
- Dry season: Strictly ration if storage low.

### Redundancy

**Three-system approach:** Spring + pond + cistern = security.
- If spring fails, pond provides water.
- If pond runs dry, cistern provides emergency supply.
- If all fail, well provides backup (requires hand pump; labor-intensive).

## Verification

Your water catchment system is adequate if:
1. **Calculation:** You know your settlement's daily water need.
2. **Catchment:** You capture rainwater from roofs and/or ground.
3. **Storage:** You have 10+ days of water in storage.
4. **Spring:** You've tested for perennial water if available.
5. **Quality:** You understand basic treatment (boiling, settling).
6. **Scaling:** You can support 100+ people for a full year.

## Common Mistakes

**Underestimating water need.** Calculate accurately; assume 3 liters per person daily (including irrigation for subsistence).

**Single water source.** Rely on one source = risk of failure. Pursue three sources (rain, spring, well).

**Uncovered storage.** Algae and contamination in exposed cisterns. Cover all storage.

**No overflow outlet.** Overflow drowns nearby land; causes erosion. Manage overflow.

**Relying on dry wells.** Wells fail in drought; maintain spring/cistern as primary.

## Part 7: Monitoring and Maintenance Practices

### Daily monitoring

**Cistern/pond level:** Check daily; gauge or mark stick shows water height.
- If dropping faster than expected, investigate (leak, unauthorized use, high evaporation).
- Calculate expected usage; flag anomalies.

**Water quality:** Visual inspection (color, smell).
- Algae (green water): Cistern was uncovered; recover and let settle.
- Sediment (brown): Pond needs dredging; use for irrigation only until cleared.
- Smell: Stagnation; drain, refill, or add flow-through (spring).

### Seasonal maintenance

**Spring (March-May):**
- Clean cisterns (remove sediment buildup).
- Test spring flows (check for year-round reliability).
- Inspect pond dam (look for cracks, erosion).

**Summer (June-August):**
- Monitor level (high use season).
- Maintain catchment surfaces (clear debris).
- Watch for leaks (if level drops rapidly).

**Fall (September-November):**
- Top off pond (prepare for winter).
- Drain and inspect cisterns.
- Prepare for reduced rainfall season.

**Winter (December-February):**
- Manage ice (if applicable; access may be blocked).
- Plan spring maintenance.
- Monitor rationing (is supply adequate?).

### Record-keeping

**Track:**
- Monthly water level (gauge readings).
- Rainfall (for future planning).
- Usage (helps estimate future needs).
- Problems (leaks, contamination, shortages).

**Use:** Identify seasonal patterns; predict future shortages; plan infrastructure improvements.

## Summary: Water Security Strategy

Post-collapse water security has three layers:

**Layer 1 (Primary): Seasonal storage**
- Pond or large cistern (100,000+ liters).
- Captures rainfall during wet season.
- Provides water for dry season (3-6 months).

**Layer 2 (Backup): Spring or well**
- Perennial water supply (year-round flow).
- Lower volume than seasonal storage (adequate for drinking + minimal irrigation).
- Requires development (protection, capture infrastructure).

**Layer 3 (Emergency): Rainwater catchment**
- Roof gutters and cisterns.
- Quick-response water capture (useful in immediate crisis).
- Low volume; supplements other sources.

A settlement with all three layers survives droughts indefinitely. With only one layer, drought means water stress (rationing, conflict). Water is non-negotiable: three layers is minimal post-collapse infrastructure.
