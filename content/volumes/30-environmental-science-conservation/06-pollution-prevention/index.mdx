## Overview

Pollution contaminates water, soil, and food; causes illness and death. Post-collapse, there is no EPA; no environmental cleanup. Pollution prevention is the only option.

This chapter covers:
1. **Water pollution:** Sources and prevention.
2. **Soil pollution:** Chemical, heavy metal, organic contamination.
3. **Air pollution:** Smoke and emissions (limited post-collapse).
4. **Waste management:** Human waste, food waste, industrial residue.
5. **Detection:** Identifying contamination.
6. **Response:** Emergency containment if pollution occurs.

## Prerequisites

Understand sanitation (Vol 5, Ch 6). Know environmental systems (Vol 13, Ch 1-3).

## Part 1: Water Pollution Sources and Prevention

### Primary pollution sources

**Human waste (sewage):**
- Bacteria (E. coli, Salmonella, Vibrio).
- Viruses (hepatitis A, gastroenteritis viruses).
- Parasites (Giardia, cryptosporidium).
- Nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus; cause algae blooms).

**Animal waste:**
- Similar pathogens as human waste; concentrated (livestock at one site).
- Post-collapse: Major risk if livestock upstream of water intake.

**Agricultural runoff:**
- Soil sediment (clogs gills, reduces light for aquatic plants).
- Nutrients (excess nitrogen/phosphorus causes algae blooms).
- Pesticides (post-collapse: unavailable; prior industrial agriculture left residue).

**Industrial/chemical:**
- Heavy metals (lead, mercury, cadmium; accumulate in organisms).
- Organic chemicals (solvents, fuels; persist in environment).
- Post-collapse concern: Abandoned industrial sites, stored chemicals.

**Natural sources (less controllable):**
- Algae blooms (from excess nutrients or warm water).
- Acid mine drainage (from exposed ore; rare but severe).

### Prevention strategy by water source

**Drinking water sources (wells, springs, cisterns):**
1. **Locate uphill from all contamination:** Well/spring upstream; livestock, compost, outhouse downstream.
2. **Protect from surface contamination:** Cap well; screen spring; prevent runoff entering.
3. **Monitor water quality:** Test for clarity, smell, taste; boil if any doubt.
4. **Backup source:** If primary contaminated, have alternative (well or pond).

**Irrigation water (pond, stream):**
- Can tolerate more contamination than drinking water (water boiled before eating).
- Still: Prevent fecal contamination (E. coli harms crop workers; can make food unsafe).
- Practice: Don't irrigate with water visibly contaminated by human/animal waste.

**Livestock water:**
- More robust than humans; can tolerate some contamination.
- Risk: If water has high pathogen load, livestock become vector (spreading disease via meat/milk).
- Practice: Provide clean water; accept some contamination only if no alternative.

## Part 2: Soil Pollution Prevention

### Heavy metal sources (post-collapse concern from prior industry)

**Lead:**
- Old batteries, paint, ammunition.
- Accumulates in soil; bioaccumulates in plants, then humans.
- Exposure causes learning disability, organ damage.

**Mercury:**
- Old thermometers, dental amalgam, fungicides.
- Bioaccumulates; toxic at low levels.

**Cadmium, zinc, copper:**
- Industrial runoff, metal plating, mining.

**Prevention:**
1. Identify contaminated sites (old factories, smelters, mines).
2. Avoid growing food on heavily contaminated soil (relocate crops).
3. Test soil if available (look for discoloration, odd odors).
4. Add compost/organic matter (dilutes contaminant; reduces bioavailability).
5. Plant deep-rooted cover crops (draw down metal; prevent erosion).

### Organic contaminants (post-industrial residue)

**Petroleum products, solvents, pesticides:**
- Break down slowly; persist for decades.
- Some bioaccumulate; some degrade with aeration and microbial action.

**Prevention:**
1. Avoid contaminated areas (old landfills, service stations).
2. Aerate soil (compost, tilling; accelerates breakdown).
3. Add organic matter (microbes break down contaminants).
4. Plant phytoremediation crops (some plants accumulate toxins; harvest and remove).

### Biological contamination of soil

**Pathogenic bacteria in compost (if manure not properly heated):**
- Fecal pathogens survive in cool compost; infect food crops.

**Prevention:**
1. Hot compost: Temperature &gt;55°C for 2 weeks (kills pathogens).
2. Or: Age compost 6+ months before using on food crops.
3. Don't compost human feces (too dangerous; use separate system - see Vol 5).

## Part 3: Food Contamination Prevention

### Pathways to food contamination

**At production (field):**
- Contaminated irrigation water (wash produce with pathogenic water).
- Contaminated soil (root crops absorb heavy metals; leafy greens get soil splash).
- Worker hygiene (unwashed hands contaminate during harvest).

**At storage:**
- Contaminated storage area (mold spores, insect droppings, rodent feces).
- Cross-contamination (raw meat touching vegetables).

**At preparation:**
- Unwashed hands/utensils.
- Inadequate cooking temperature.
- Prolonged room-temperature storage.

### Prevention practices

**Production:**
1. Use clean irrigation water (protect drinking water source; use pond/well for irrigation).
2. Practice crop rotation (alternates disease/pest buildup).
3. Harvest with clean hands; clean equipment.
4. Don't harvest leafy crops with visible fecal contamination nearby.

**Storage:**
1. Keep produce and meat separate (prevent cross-contamination).
2. Store in clean location (sealed from insects, rodents).
3. Monitor for mold/decay; remove affected items immediately.

**Preparation:**
1. Wash hands before food handling.
2. Wash all vegetables/fruits (removes soil and some pathogens).
3. Cook meat to safe temperature (&gt;65°C internal temperature).
4. Refrigerate or ferment cooked food within 2 hours.

**Sanitation:**
1. Wash utensils, cutting boards in clean water (boil if possible).
2. Don't share cutting surfaces between raw meat and vegetables.

## Part 4: Waste Management as Pollution Prevention

### Human waste (fecal waste)

**Must be managed to prevent water contamination:**
- See Vol 5, Ch 6 (comprehensive sanitation guidance).
- Briefly: Pit toilet, composting toilet, or contained septic system; never allow runoff to water.

### Food waste and compost

**Acceptable food waste management:**
1. Compost (hot compost; kills pathogens; becomes fertilizer).
2. Animal feed (cooked scraps safe for livestock; raw scraps risk disease).
3. Burial (if no livestock around; deep burial; prevents scavenging).

**Never:**
- Dump raw food waste near water (attracts animals; contaminates water).
- Compost with animal manure without heating (pathogens survive).

### Industrial/chemical waste (post-collapse)

**Post-collapse reality:** Few industrial chemicals produced; main concern is legacy contamination.

**Management:**
1. Identify sources (old factory, storage area, abandoned vehicle).
2. Contain (if liquid, dig pit and line with clay; prevent seeping).
3. Isolate (mark contaminated area; keep livestock/people away).
4. Accept (if not affecting water/food, accept presence; focus effort elsewhere).

### Ash and burned waste

**Wood ash (generally safe):**
- Mineral content; slightly alkaline.
- Can use on soil (adds minerals; raises pH).
- Practical limit: Don't add &gt;5% by weight (excess raises pH too much).

**Plastic/synthetic burn products (contaminating):**
- Never burn plastic (releases toxic chemicals).
- Metals, glass: Don't burn (melts; contaminates ash).

## Part 5: Detecting Pollution

### Visual indicators

**Water:**
- Color (clear = good; brown = sediment; green = algae; grey = chemical).
- Clarity (transparent = good; opaque = contaminated).
- Smell (no smell = good; musty = algae; foul = sewage).
- Film/foam (algae or chemical; indicates pollution).

**Soil:**
- Discoloration (unusually dark or bright color = concern).
- Smell (rotten = anaerobic; chemical smell = organic contamination).
- Vegetation (stunted or dead plants = toxicity).

**Food:**
- Mold (discard; contamination likely internal).
- Slime/sticky surface (bacterial growth; discard).
- Odd smell or taste (never eat; trust senses).

### Testing (post-collapse, limited options)

**Water taste test (risky; spit out if unusual):**
- Salt taste: Salt contamination.
- Metallic taste: Heavy metals.
- Foul taste: Pathogens or decay.

**pH test (if pH paper available):**
- Very alkaline (pH &gt;8): Heavy metal or chemical contamination.
- Acidic (pH &lt;6): Acid drainage or decomposition.

**Boiling test:**
- Boil water; if cloudiness increases, sediment or minerals.
- If smell worsens, volatile organic compounds.

**Observation of aquatic life:**
- Fish present = generally safe (fish die if toxins present).
- Insects/invertebrates abundant = good sign (water oxygenated).
- Absence of life = concern (chemical contamination or low oxygen).

**Settlement testing protocol:**
- Designate one person to check water daily (taste, smell, color).
- Keep log: Record observations; note any changes.
- If any change, switch to alternative source immediately.
- Boil suspect water before use.
- Test seasonal changes (algae blooms in summer; stagnation in winter).

## Part 6: Emergency Response to Contamination

### If primary water source contaminated

**Immediate actions:**
1. **Stop using contaminated source** (for drinking/cooking).
2. **Switch to backup source** (well, pond, storage).
3. **Boil all water** from contaminated source (if needed for other uses).
4. **Identify cause** (upstream contamination? Animal die-off? Algae bloom?).
5. **Quarantine area** (keep livestock away; mark for humans).

**Recovery:**
- Wait 2-4 weeks (some contamination resolves naturally; algae dies; sediment settles).
- Monitor: Taste, smell, clarity; if improved, resume use (with boiling).
- If persistent: Must develop alternative source.

### If food suspected contaminated

**Decision tree:**
1. **Obvious mold/decay:** Discard immediately.
2. **Suspicious but no visible sign:** Boil (if cookable); discard if raw.
3. **Large batch contaminated:** Discard; accept loss; move on (hoarding contaminated food risks settlement illness).

### If soil contaminated (known)

**Actions:**
1. **Relocate production:** Move crop areas to clean soil.
2. **Isolate contaminated area:** Fence; prevent access.
3. **Phytoremediation:** Plant accumulator plants; harvest and remove over years.
4. **Time:** Some contamination improves naturally; metals immobilized over decades.

## Part 7: Settlement-Scale Pollution Prevention Plan

### Infrastructure planning

**Water system (separation of sources):**
- Drinking: Protected well or spring (uphill from everything).
- Irrigation: Pond or stream (downstream of any potential contamination).
- Livestock: Separate trough (can use slightly contaminated water; reduces demand on clean sources).

**Waste management locations:**
- Human toilet: Downhill from water sources; &gt;30 meters from well.
- Compost: Downhill; &gt;50 meters from well.
- Livestock enclosure: &gt;30 meters from well; enclosed (no runoff).
- Food storage: Pest-proof; cool; away from waste areas.

### Routine maintenance

**Water source monitoring:**
- Daily: Visual check (color, clarity, smell).
- Monthly: Taste test (boil and taste).
- Seasonal: Check for contamination sources upstream (dead animals, new construction).

**Soil monitoring:**
- Annual: Soil pit assessment; note color, smell, vegetation response.
- If unusual, move crops to different area (rotate to test new soil).

**Waste management:**
- Weekly: Check compost temperature (hot = working; cool = problem).
- Monthly: Clean storage areas; remove rodent droppings, mold.

## Part 8: Post-Collapse Pollution Reality

### What's controllable

**Human waste:** Can be managed completely with proper sanitation.

**Agricultural runoff:** Can reduce through conservation practices (contours, cover crops).

**Livestock waste:** Can contain with proper enclosure.

### What's harder to control

**Industrial legacy:** Contaminated sites from prior industry; limited remediation options.

**Accidental spills:** If someone spills chemical, can contain but not remove completely.

**Diffuse pollution:** Atmospheric deposition, weathering of contaminated rock; unavoidable.

### Practical philosophy

**Post-collapse pollution prevention is about:**
1. Not creating new pollution (sanitation, waste management).
2. Avoiding known contamination (don't farm on polluted sites).
3. Protecting water (only controllable resource; most critical).
4. Accepting legacy pollution (focus effort on water; accept soil/air contamination if unavoidable).

**Allocation of effort:** 80% to water protection; 15% to food safety; 5% to other (soil, air, waste).

## Verification

Your pollution prevention skill is adequate if:
1. **Sources:** You identify major pollution sources in your area.
2. **Water:** You have plan to protect drinking water source.
3. **Food:** You prevent contamination at production and preparation.
4. **Waste:** You have system for human/animal waste and food waste.
5. **Detection:** You can identify contamination by sight, smell, taste.
6. **Response:** You know what to do if contamination occurs.
7. **Scaling:** You can design settlement-scale pollution prevention.

## Common Mistakes

**Underestimating human waste.** Pit toilets far downstream of well (30+ meters) but uphill = eventual seepage to well. Locate downhill AND far away.

**Compost near water.** Hot compost kills pathogens; cool compost doesn't. Locate cool compost far from water (50+ meters).

**Assuming water "looks clean."** Pathogens invisible. Always boil water unless from protected spring.

**Cross-contamination in food prep.** Raw meat touching vegetables = pathogens on vegetables. Separate utensils/areas.

**Overconfidence in storage.** Assume rodents and insects WILL find food. Must secure storage; seal completely.

**Accepting persistent odor.** Foul smell = contamination. Move away; find cause; resolve.

**Farming on industrial sites.** Heavy metals, petroleum, solvents persist decades. Avoid growing food on former factories, service stations, mines.
