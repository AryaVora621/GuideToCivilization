## Overview

Concrete is made by chemically bonding stone together using cement. The result is a material stronger than stone itself, capable of spanning greater distances, and able to be cast into any desired shape.

In the ancient world, Roman concrete (using volcanic ash as cement) built structures that stand today, 2000 years later. Modern concrete, made from Portland cement, has similar durability. Understanding how to produce cement and concrete enables the creation of permanent structures that outlast wood, brick, and most other materials.

This chapter covers producing cement from limestone, mixing concrete, and casting concrete structures.

Why this matters:
- **Durability:** Concrete structures last centuries or millennia
- **Strength:** Concrete is strong enough for large-span structures
- **Flexibility:** Concrete can be cast into any shape
- **Weather resistance:** Concrete resists decay from moisture, wind, and temperature
- **Self-sufficiency:** Cement production requires only limestone and heat

## Prerequisites

- Access to limestone (or seashells; calcium carbonate sources)
- Heat source for calcining limestone (hot kiln or similar)
- Aggregate (sand, gravel, small stones)
- Water
- Tools for mixing and casting concrete
- Basic understanding of chemistry and material properties

## Materials & Tools

### Raw Materials for Cement

**Limestone (primary ingredient):**
- Pure calcium carbonate (CaCO₃)
- Chalk or seashells work if limestone unavailable
- Purity: higher purity produces better cement

**Silica and alumina (in limestone or added separately):**
- Present naturally in most limestone
- If limestone is very pure, may need to add clay (source of silica and alumina)
- Ratio: approximately 75-80% calcium carbonate, 20-25% silica/alumina

**Gypsum (optional; for controlling setting time):**
- Added after initial cement production
- Small amount (3-5%)
- Slows hydration rate; allows more working time

### Aggregate (the bulk of concrete)

**Sand:**
- Fine aggregate
- Size: particles less than 0.25 inches
- Cleanliness: wash to remove clay and organic matter
- Quantity: approximately 40% of concrete volume

**Gravel or crushed stone:**
- Coarse aggregate
- Size: 0.25 to 1.5 inches
- Clean and free of clay
- Quantity: approximately 40% of concrete volume

**Cement (binder):**
- Produced from limestone (see below)
- Quantity: approximately 15-20% of concrete volume
- Water: approximately 10-15% of concrete volume

### Tools

**Essential:**
- Kiln (for calcining limestone)
- Grinding tools (for finishing cement)
- Mixing containers
- Shovels and mixing tools
- Measuring containers
- Molds (wooden forms for casting)

**Useful additions:**
- Thermometer (for monitoring kiln temperature)
- Scales (for precise mixing ratios)
- Wheelbarrow (for transporting materials)

## Part 1: Understanding Cement and Concrete

### Portland Cement (Modern Cement)

Portland cement is a carefully controlled chemical compound made by heating limestone (calcium carbonate) with silica and alumina, then grinding to powder. The "Portland" name comes from its resemblance to Portland stone used in England.

**Chemical composition:**
- Calcium silicates (the cementing agents)
- Calcium aluminate
- Calcium sulfate (gypsum; controls setting time)

**Key properties:**
- **Hydration:** When mixed with water, cement undergoes a chemical reaction that produces strength
- **Exothermic:** The reaction releases heat (why concrete gets warm during curing)
- **Progressive strength:** Strength increases with time (rapid first week, continues for months/years)

### Concrete (Composite Material)

Concrete is cement + aggregate + water. The cement chemically bonds the aggregate particles together.

**Why aggregate is necessary:**
- Provides bulk (cheaper than cement)
- Improves strength through interlocking of particles
- Reduces shrinkage (pure cement shrinks; aggregate resists)
- Improves durability (aggregate particles provide internal strength)

**Ratio importance:**
- Too much cement: expensive, excessive shrinkage, poor durability
- Too little cement: weak concrete, poor binding
- Optimal ratio: carefully balanced for strength and economy

## Part 2: Producing Cement

### Calcining Limestone (Converting CaCO₃ to CaO)

Calcining is heating limestone to approximately 900-1100°C, at which point it decomposes:

**Chemical reaction:**
CaCO₃ (calcium carbonate) → CaO (calcium oxide) + CO₂ (carbon dioxide)

**Process:**

1. **Quarrying:** Excavate limestone (breaking into manageable chunks)
2. **Crushing (optional):** Break into smaller pieces (faster heating)
3. **Loading kiln:** Stack limestone in a kiln designed for calcining
4. **Heating:** Heat to 900-1100°C
   - Duration: 6-8 hours for small batches
   - Temperature must be maintained (not allowed to cool significantly)
5. **Oxidation:** During heating, the limestone breaks down and carbon dioxide escapes
6. **Cooling:** Allow kiln to cool (several hours to days depending on size)
7. **Unloading:** Remove the quicklime (calcium oxide)

**Result:** Quicklime (calcium oxide; white, caustic powder)

### Slaking the Quicklime (Adding Water)

Quicklime reacts vigorously with water:

**Process:**

1. Place quicklime in a pit or container
2. Add water carefully (reaction is vigorous and produces heat)
   - Approximately equal weight of water to quicklime
   - Add water slowly; avoid splashing
3. The reaction produces calcium hydroxide (slaked lime) and heat
4. Allow reaction to complete (mixture becomes hot, then cools)
5. The result is slaked lime (calcium hydroxide paste)

**Note:** This step can be combined with the next step if desired

### Creating Portland Cement (Advanced)

True Portland cement requires:

1. **Clinker production:** Heat slaked lime (calcium hydroxide) with silica and alumina to very high temperature (1450°C), producing Portland cement clinker
2. **Clinker cooling:** Allow clinker to cool
3. **Grinding:** Grind clinker to fine powder (cement)
4. **Gypsum addition:** Add approximately 3-5% gypsum (controls setting time)
5. **Final grinding:** Grind to uniform powder

**Challenge:** Achieving the precise temperature and composition for true Portland cement is difficult without modern equipment.

### Simplified Cement (Roman/Natural Cement)

For post-collapse purposes, simplified cement can be made:

**Method (producing "natural" cement):**

1. Burn limestone (as described above)
2. Slake the quicklime with water
3. Grind to powder (skip the complex Portland clinker production)
4. The result is calcium hydroxide, which can bind aggregate through carbonation

**Binding mechanism:**
- Calcium hydroxide + Carbon dioxide (from air) → Calcium carbonate (the binding agent)
- Slower than Portland cement but still effective
- Roman concrete still stands after 2000 years using this principle

**Advantages:**
- Simpler production (no need for 1450°C kiln)
- Still produces durable concrete
- Used successfully for millennia

## Part 3: Concrete Mixing

### Proportions (By Volume)

**Standard concrete mix:**
- Cement: 1 part
- Sand: 2-3 parts
- Gravel: 3-4 parts
- Water: enough to make workable paste (approximately 0.5 parts)

**Variations:**
- For more strength: increase cement ratio (1:2:3 instead of 1:2.5:4)
- For economy: increase aggregate (1:3:5)
- For durability: proper ratio (1:2:3) with good finishing

### Mixing Process

**Manual mixing (small batches):**

1. **Combine dry materials:** Mix cement, sand, and gravel together
   - Shovel back and forth until uniform color
   - Takes 5-10 minutes of vigorous mixing
   - Ensure all materials are evenly distributed

2. **Add water:** Gradually add water while mixing
   - Water should make the mixture workable but not soupy
   - Consistency: stiff paste that barely slumps
   - Too much water: weak concrete
   - Too little water: doesn't bond well

3. **Continue mixing:** Ensure all particles are coated with cement paste
   - Takes 3-5 minutes after water is added
   - Should appear uniform (no dry spots)

**Animal or mechanical mixing (larger batches):**
- Use animals (cattle, horses) to walk on concrete pile (traditional method)
- Or construct mechanical mixer (rotating drum with paddles)

### Workability

Concrete should be stiff enough to hold shape but workable enough to pour and settle:

- **Too stiff:** Difficult to pour; traps air pockets; results in weak concrete
- **Too wet:** Runs away; settles poorly; weak from excess water
- **Correct:** Forms a ball when squeezed; slumps slightly

## Part 4: Casting Concrete

### Forms (Molds for Concrete)

Forms hold concrete in shape while it cures:

**Materials for forms:**
- Wood (boards nailed together)
- Metal (steel forms; reusable)
- Existing materials (earth banks for some applications)

**Requirements:**
- Rigid (don't flex under concrete weight)
- Watertight (prevent cement from washing out)
- Smooth interior surface (produces smooth concrete finish)
- Easily removable (after concrete cures)

### Casting Process

**Simple example: pouring a concrete pad**

1. **Prepare subgrade:** Clear and level the ground
2. **Create forms:** Nail wooden boards around the perimeter
   - Height defines concrete thickness
   - Ensure level and square
3. **Dampen forms:** Wet the forms and soil
   - Prevents rapid water absorption from concrete
4. **Place concrete:** Pour mixed concrete into forms
   - Start in one corner
   - Spread evenly with shovel
5. **Consolidate:** Remove trapped air
   - Tap sides of forms
   - Tamp concrete with tool
   - Air pockets create weak spots
6. **Strike off excess:** Level concrete with top of forms
   - Use a straight board (screed)
   - Drag board across top; fill low spots; level high spots
7. **Finish surface:**
   - For smooth finish: trowel with flat tool
   - For non-skid finish: broom (drag broom across surface)
   - Allow to set slightly before finishing

### Curing (Allowing Concrete to Harden)

Concrete strength develops over time as cement hydrates:

**Timeline:**
- 24 hours: concrete is handle-able; forms can sometimes be removed
- 7 days: concrete reaches approximately 70% final strength
- 28 days: concrete reaches approximately 90% final strength
- 3-6 months: concrete approaches full strength
- 1+ years: concrete continues to strengthen slowly

**Curing conditions:**

**Moisture:** Concrete needs moisture to cure properly
- Keep concrete damp (cover with plastic, burlap, or wet sand)
- Prevents rapid drying which causes cracking
- Critical first week; important for first month

**Temperature:** Warm temperatures speed curing; cold slows it
- Ideal: 50-80°F (10-25°C)
- Freezing: concrete should not freeze before it hardens (takes days to weeks)
- Very hot: covers with shade cloth (prevents too-rapid drying)

**Protection:** Protect curing concrete from:
- Traffic (weight before full strength)
- Impact
- Vibration
- Freezing (if not fully hardened)

### Stripping Forms

Forms can be removed after concrete hardens:

**Timeline:**
- Light traffic: 7 days
- Significant loading: 14-28 days (conservative)
- Depends on temperature (warmer = faster)

**Process:**
1. Carefully remove form fasteners
2. Gently pry or tap forms away
3. Support concrete if necessary (may not be fully hardened)
4. Clean up concrete edges

## Part 5: Reinforced Concrete (Adding Strength)

Plain concrete is strong in compression but weak in tension. Adding reinforcement (typically iron or steel rods) dramatically increases strength and allows larger spans.

### Reinforcement Materials

**Options:**
- Iron or steel rods (if available; strong; durable)
- Bamboo (if available; weaker but functional)
- Metal bars from salvage

### Placing Reinforcement

**Process:**

1. **Position in forms:** Place reinforcement in forms before pouring
   - Should be spaced to provide strength in multiple directions
   - Common spacing: 12-18 inches on center
   - Elevation: approximately 2 inches from bottom (protects from corrosion)

2. **Pour concrete:** Pour concrete around reinforcement
   - Vibrate or consolidate to ensure concrete fully surrounds reinforcement
   - Air pockets allow rust; must be minimized

3. **Cover thickness:** Minimum 2 inches of concrete over reinforcement
   - Protects from weather and corrosion
   - Thicker cover improves durability

### Improving Reinforced Concrete

**Compression members (columns, footings):**
- Vertical reinforcement (every 12 inches around perimeter)
- Ties (horizontal bands holding vertical bars in place)

**Tension members (beams, floor slabs):**
- Reinforcement concentrated where tension is greatest
- More reinforcement near supports, less at midspan

## Part 6: Practical Applications and Examples

### Example 1: Concrete Foundation for a Building

**Scenario:** Building structure needs solid foundation

**Process:**

1. **Excavate:** Dig footing trench below frost line (typically 2-4 feet deep)
2. **Forms:** Set wooden forms for concrete height (typically 12-24 inches above grade)
3. **Reinforcement:** Place rods or mesh in footing
4. **Pour concrete:** Mix and pour concrete into forms
5. **Cure:** Allow concrete to cure for 7-14 days
6. **Remove forms:** Carefully remove forms
7. **Backfill:** Fill around foundation with soil

**Result:** Solid, durable foundation resistant to settling and frost heave

### Example 2: Concrete Floor Slab

**Scenario:** Permanent shelter needs durable floor

**Process:**

1. **Prepare subgrade:** Level ground; compact soil
2. **Forms:** Build wooden perimeter forms at desired level
3. **Pour:** Mix and pour concrete; level with screed board
4. **Finish:** Smooth with trowel for desired surface
5. **Cure:** Keep damp for 7 days; avoid traffic for 28 days

**Result:** Durable floor resistant to rot, pests, and weather

### Example 3: Concrete Pad for Equipment

**Scenario:** Equipment or structures need level, durable pad

**Process:**

1. **Design:** Determine size and reinforcement needed
2. **Forms:** Simple wooden box forms
3. **Mix and pour:** Standard concrete, leveled and finished
4. **Cure:** Standard 7-28 day cure

**Result:** Durable mounting surface for equipment, animals, or structures

## Part 7: Troubleshooting Concrete Problems

### Problem: Concrete cracks while curing

**Causes:** 
- Rapid drying (especially in hot sun or wind)
- Excessive water in mix
- Reinforcement movement
- Freezing before curing complete

**Solutions:**
- Cover concrete with plastic or keep damp
- Use proper water ratio (not too wet)
- Properly consolidate to minimize air pockets
- Protect from frost

### Problem: Concrete is weak or crumbles easily

**Causes:** 
- Too much water
- Insufficient cement
- Poor consolidation (trapped air)
- Inadequate curing time

**Solutions:**
- Use correct proportions (1 part cement : 2 sand : 3 gravel)
- Measure water carefully
- Consolidate thoroughly (tap forms, tamp surface)
- Allow adequate curing (minimum 7 days)

### Problem: Surface finish is rough or porous

**Causes:** 
- Form texture (texture reproduces on concrete)
- Air pockets (from inadequate consolidation)
- Rapid drying

**Solutions:**
- Smooth forms before use
- Consolidate well while pouring
- Keep damp during curing
- Ground surface can be smoothed after curing

### Problem: Concrete doesn't bind to reinforcement

**Causes:** 
- Oil or contamination on reinforcement
- Reinforcement too smooth (insufficient bond)
- Poor consolidation around reinforcement

**Solutions:**
- Clean reinforcement before use
- Rough reinforcement surface improves bond
- Consolidate thoroughly around bars

## Common Mistakes

1. **Incorrect proportions:** Too much water or too little cement creates weak concrete. Solution: Measure carefully; use standard proportions until experienced.

2. **Inadequate mixing:** Dry materials not fully combined; cement doesn't coat aggregate uniformly. Solution: Extend mixing time; ensure uniform color and texture.

3. **Too much water:** Concrete is weak; shrinks excessively; cracks. Solution: Add only enough water for workability; concrete should be stiff, not soupy.

4. **Insufficient consolidation:** Air pockets create weak spots; concrete is porous. Solution: Tap forms; tamp surface; vibrate if possible; ensure concrete is fully settled.

5. **Rapid drying:** Concrete cracks from differential drying. Solution: Cover concrete; keep damp for 7 days; prevent wind and sun exposure initially.

6. **Freezing before cure:** Concrete never reaches design strength if frozen before hardening. Solution: Protect from frost; don't use in winter or provide insulation.

7. **Early loading:** Concrete is not strong enough for load. Solution: Wait 7 days minimum for light traffic; 28 days for significant loading.

8. **Poor form construction:** Forms leak cement; are out of level; flex under load. Solution: Build robust forms; seal gaps; brace adequately.

9. **Contaminated aggregate:** Clay or organics prevent good bonding. Solution: Wash sand and gravel; remove fine particles and debris.

10. **Improper reinforcement placement:** Reinforcement corrodes from exposure; doesn't prevent cracks effectively. Solution: Maintain 2 inches cover minimum; position bars where tension occurs; consolidate concrete around bars.
