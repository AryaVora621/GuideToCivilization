## Overview

Wood is abundant, beautiful, and endlessly useful. In a world without plastic, metal, or glass, wood becomes the primary material for tools, furniture, shelter, and decoration. Woodworking skill is among the most valuable capabilities in a post-collapse community.

Woodworking is not mysterious. It requires patience, attention to detail, and practice—but not special talents. Anyone can learn to craft fine wooden objects through deliberate practice and proper technique.

This chapter focuses on practical woodworking: selecting wood, using hand tools effectively, creating strong joints, and finishing work for durability and beauty.

Why this matters:
- **Self-sufficiency:** Wooden items (furniture, tools, containers) are created on demand rather than imported
- **Durability:** Well-crafted wooden objects last generations
- **Value:** Crafted wooden items are valuable trade goods
- **Beauty:** Well-made wooden items improve quality of life and provide aesthetic satisfaction
- **Resilience:** Woodworking requires no electricity, fuel, or scarce materials

## Prerequisites

- Access to wood and basic tools (saw, hammer, chisel, plane)
- Physical capability for sustained manual labor
- Patience: good woodworking cannot be rushed
- Attention to detail and measurement
- Basic mathematical ability (measuring, calculating dimensions)

## Materials & Tools

### Wood Selection

**Wood properties important for woodworking:**

- **Hardness:** Ability to resist denting and damage
- **Grain:** Direction of wood fibers; affects how wood cuts and splits
- **Workability:** How easily wood can be shaped
- **Stability:** Resistance to warping or splitting as it dries
- **Durability:** How long the wood resists decay (depends on species and environment)

**Common woods for post-collapse woodworking:**

**Softwoods (easy to work; less durable):**
- Pine: very easy to work; acceptable durability; common
- Fir: moderate workability; good durability
- Spruce: moderate workability; moderate durability

**Hardwoods (harder to work; more durable):**
- Oak: difficult to work; very durable; beautiful grain
- Maple: difficult to work; extremely hard; very durable
- Birch: moderate difficulty; durable; attractive grain
- Walnut: moderate difficulty; beautiful; moderate durability
- Ash: moderate difficulty; flexible and strong; good for tool handles

**Practical selection:**
- For learning: softwoods (faster, more forgiving)
- For tools: hardwoods (durability and hardness)
- For furniture: hardwoods or mixed (durability; oak or maple are traditional)

### Tools Required

**Hand tools (minimum set):**

1. **Saw:** Hand saw for crosscutting and ripping
   - Crosscut saw: cuts across grain (80-90 teeth per foot)
   - Rip saw: cuts along grain (4-6 teeth per inch)
2. **Hammer:** For driving nails and other striking work
3. **Chisel:** For removing waste wood and cleaning joints
4. **Plane:** For smoothing surfaces and reducing thickness
5. **Measuring tools:** Ruler, measuring tape, square
6. **Marking tools:** Pencil, marking gauge

**Useful additions:**
- Vise or clamps (to hold work)
- Block plane (for small smoothing work)
- Combination square (for marking and checking angles)
- Hand drill (for boring holes)
- Screwdriver (if screws are used)

### Finishing Materials

- **Oils:** Linseed oil, walnut oil, or rendered animal fat for water protection
- **Waxes:** Beeswax for protection and polish
- **Stains:** Plant-based dyes (from textile dyeing chapter)
- **Soap/cleaners:** Plant-based soaps for cleaning before finishing

## Part 1: Understanding Wood

### Wood Structure

Wood is composed of:

1. **Fibers:** Long, tube-like cells running along the tree's length
2. **Rays:** Shorter cells running perpendicular to fibers (radiate from tree center)
3. **Pith:** Soft tissue at tree center (weak; avoid for woodworking)
4. **Bark:** Outer protective layer (remove before woodworking)

### Grain Direction

**Grain** is the direction of wood fibers:

- **Straight grain:** Fibers run parallel to edges (easiest to work)
- **Cross grain:** Fibers change direction (difficult to plane; prone to splitting)
- **Figured grain:** Grain twists or curves (beautiful but difficult to work)

**Understanding grain prevents problems:**
- **With the grain:** Tools cut smoothly; surfaces are clean
- **Against the grain:** Tools tear fibers; surfaces are rough
- **Always look:** Before sawing or planing, identify grain direction and adjust accordingly

### Wood Movement

As wood dries or absorbs moisture, it moves:

- **Shrinkage:** Wood shrinks as it dries (more across grain than along grain)
- **Swelling:** Wood swells as it absorbs moisture (reversal of shrinkage)
- **Warping:** Uneven drying causes wood to curve or twist

**Implications for woodworking:**
- Use dry wood (or account for shrinkage)
- Design joints to allow wood movement
- Avoid constraining large areas (causes warping)
- Finish wood to minimize moisture absorption

## Part 2: Preparing Lumber

### Selecting Lumber

**Visual inspection:**

1. Check for straight edges (avoid twisted or bowed lumber)
2. Look for cracks (especially at ends)
3. Identify knots (affect strength and appearance)
4. Check grain direction
5. Avoid sapwood (outer, softer layer) for structural work

**Moisture:**

- **Green wood (freshly cut):** Easier to work but will shrink; needs time to dry
- **Air-dried wood:** Partially dry; better than green; still may move slightly
- **Fully seasoned wood:** Completely dry; minimal movement; ideal for fine woodworking

### Dimensioning Lumber (Making It Usable)

Lumber from a sawmill is rough and not uniform. Dimensioning prepares it:

**Step 1: Face jointing (flatten one surface)**
1. Place rough lumber on a jointer plane or hand plane
2. Plane one broad surface flat
3. This becomes the reference surface

**Step 2: Edge jointing (straighten one edge)**
1. Plane one edge straight and square to the face
2. This edge becomes the reference edge

**Step 3: Planing to thickness**
1. Plane the opposite face parallel to the first
2. Reduces thickness to desired dimension

**Step 4: Ripping to width**
1. Saw along the reference edge to width
2. Creates uniform width

**Result:** Four-square lumber ready for accurate woodworking

### Marking for Cutting

Clear, accurate marking prevents mistakes:

1. **Identify grain direction:** Look at wood surface; trace fiber direction
2. **Measure carefully:** Use accurate measurements and a marking tool
3. **Mark cut lines:** Use a sharp pencil or knife; avoid wide lines (accuracy is lost)
4. **Mark grain direction:** Arrow showing how to cut with grain
5. **Double-check:** Measure twice; cut once

## Part 3: Basic Woodworking Techniques

### Sawing

Sawing separates pieces of wood with the minimum of waste.

**Crosscutting (cutting across grain):**
1. Mark the cut line clearly
2. Position wood securely (vise or clamps)
3. Start the saw with light upward strokes (kerf—the cut made by the saw)
4. Once the kerf is established, use full strokes
5. Let the saw do the work (don't force it)
6. As you near the end, support the falling piece to prevent splitting

**Ripping (cutting along grain):**
1. Mark the cut line
2. Secure wood firmly
3. Use a rip saw (larger teeth than crosscut saw)
4. Saw at a steeper angle (approximately 45 degrees)
5. Full strokes; let the saw work
6. Support the falling piece

**Key principles:**
- Sharp saws cut faster and easier than dull saws
- Let the saw's weight do the work; don't force
- Support wood to prevent splitting
- Wear safety consideration: keep fingers away from the saw line

### Planing

Planing smooths surfaces and reduces thickness.

**Using a hand plane:**
1. Position wood in a vise (or secure it firmly)
2. Identify grain direction
3. Adjust plane blade (iron) for thin shavings
4. Cut with the grain (plane moves in direction of grain)
5. Long, smooth strokes
6. Support the wood near the cut to prevent tearout

**Preventing tearout (rough surface):**
- Always cut with the grain
- Use a sharp blade
- Take thin shavings
- If grain is difficult, plane from the opposite end
- Or plane at an angle (skewing)

**Achieving flatness:**
- Plane diagonally across the surface first
- Then plane lengthwise with the grain
- Check frequently with a straightedge
- Stop when surface is flat and smooth

### Chiseling

Chisels remove waste wood and clean joints.

**Basic technique:**
1. Secure wood firmly
2. Position chisel with bevel down (for pushing cuts)
3. Strike the chisel with a mallet (not the hands)
4. Strikes should be controlled and decisive
5. Remove thin slices, not large chunks

**Preventing splitting:**
- Work with the grain direction
- Make cuts from both sides of the grain toward the middle
- Take thin cuts

**Sharpening chisels:**
- Dull tools are dangerous and inefficient
- Sharpen by stropping on leather or whetstones
- A sharp chisel cuts smoothly; a dull chisel requires excessive force

## Part 4: Creating Joints

Joints connect two pieces of wood. Strong, well-fitted joints are the foundation of quality woodworking.

### Butt Joints (Simplest)

A butt joint is two pieces of wood joined end-to-end or edge-to-edge with no interlocking:

**Advantages:**
- Simple to make
- Fast
- Requires minimal skill

**Disadvantages:**
- Weak in tension (joints pull apart under stress)
- Large glue surface not always available
- Requires nails or screws for strength

**Construction:**
1. Prepare both pieces flat and square
2. Apply glue to both surfaces
3. Press together firmly
4. Add nails or wooden pegs for strength
5. Allow glue to cure fully

**Use:** Interior shelving, non-structural items, where simplicity is valued

### Mortise and Tenon (Strong, Traditional)

A mortise is a hole; a tenon is a projection that fits into the hole:

**Advantages:**
- Very strong (primarily in shear and bending)
- Traditional (used for centuries)
- No hardware required

**Disadvantages:**
- Complex to execute well
- Requires accurate measuring and careful chiseling
- Takes time

**Construction:**

**Step 1: Create the tenon** (on the piece that protrudes)
1. Mark the dimensions of the tenon on all four sides
2. Saw the tenon: remove waste on two opposite sides
3. Saw the sides: remove waste on the other two sides
4. Result: a rectangular projection protruding from one piece

**Step 2: Create the mortise** (hole in the other piece)
1. Mark the mortise on both sides of the wood
2. Drill a series of holes slightly smaller than the tenon width
3. Chisel out the waste between holes
4. Create a clean rectangular hole
5. Test fit the tenon; adjust if necessary

**Step 3: Assembly**
1. Apply glue to the tenon and mortise walls
2. Press firmly together
3. Use wooden pegs or wedges for additional strength (optional)
4. Allow glue to cure fully

**Use:** Furniture legs and rails, door frames, structural elements

### Lap Joints (Moderate Strength)

Two pieces overlap; they are notched so they sit flush:

**Advantages:**
- Moderate strength
- Can be simple to execute
- Good for T-shaped or cross joints

**Disadvantages:**
- More complex than butt joint
- Less strong than mortise and tenon

**Construction:**

1. Mark where the two pieces overlap
2. Cut a notch in each piece equal to half the thickness of that piece
3. The notches interlock
4. Glue and clamp together
5. Optional: add pegs or nails for strength

**Use:** Frame corners, X-braced elements, where moderate strength suffices

### Dovetail Joints (Complex, Very Strong)

Interlocking angled projections create this joint:

**Advantages:**
- Extremely strong
- Very attractive (shows craftsmanship)
- Traditional (used for fine furniture)

**Disadvantages:**
- Complex; requires practice
- Takes significant time
- Requires accuracy

**Construction:**

1. Layout: Mark the dovetail pattern (angled projections and notches)
2. Saw: Cut the projections on one piece
3. Chisel: Remove waste carefully
4. Trace: Use the first piece as a template for the second piece
5. Cut: Saw and chisel the notches on the second piece
6. Test: Fit the two pieces together (should be snug but not forced)
7. Assemble: Glue and clamp (dovetails provide mechanical lock)

**Use:** Fine furniture, boxes, where strength and appearance matter

## Part 5: Finishing and Protection

### Surface Preparation

Before finishing, the wood must be clean and smooth:

1. **Planing:** Use a plane to create a smooth surface
2. **Sanding:** Use fine abrasive (sandstone or fine saw dust) to remove plane marks
3. **Scraping:** A cabinet scraper (flat metal blade) can remove fine marks
4. **Cleaning:** Wipe away dust with a damp cloth

**Goal:** Smooth, clean surface ready to receive finish

### Oil Finishing

Oil penetrates wood and provides modest water protection:

**Process:**
1. Apply oil liberally to wood surface
2. Wipe off excess; oil soaks into wood
3. Allow to dry (24-48 hours)
4. Apply additional coats (3-5 coats typical)
5. Final coat can be wiped off for a satin appearance or left for a glossy finish

**Oils suitable for woodworking:**
- Linseed oil: traditional; hardens somewhat as it cures
- Walnut oil: attractive darkening; cures similar to linseed
- Rendered animal fat (tallow): available; effective but may be sticky

**Advantages:**
- Easy to apply
- Enhances wood grain
- Repairable (additional oil soaks into worn spots)
- Pleasant smell

**Disadvantages:**
- Not waterproof (provides modest water resistance)
- Requires multiple coats
- Takes time to fully harden

### Wax Finishing

Wax provides protection and a pleasant feel:

**Process:**
1. Prepare wood with oil first (if desired)
2. Melt beeswax (heat gently; do not allow to smoke)
3. Apply warm wax with a cloth or brush
4. Buff when cooled (several hours later)
5. Repeat for additional protection

**Advantages:**
- Pleasant appearance (satin to glossy)
- Nice to touch (smooth, warm feel)
- Fragrant (beeswax smell)
- Repairable

**Disadvantages:**
- Labor-intensive (requires regular buffing)
- Not highly protective (mainly aesthetic)
- Softer than oil (wax can be dented)

### Combining Finishes

For maximum protection and appearance, combine techniques:

1. **Oil + wax:** Oil for protection, wax for appearance and feel
2. **Multiple oils:** Several light coats build protection better than one heavy coat
3. **Oil + dye:** Dye the wood first (using plant-based dyes), then protect with oil

## Part 6: Practical Applications and Examples

### Example 1: Making a Simple Wooden Box

**Scenario:** You need a storage container.

**Process:**
1. Select wood (pine or similar softwood suitable for learning)
2. Cut four side pieces (identical dimensions)
3. Cut two end pieces (smaller dimension for depth)
4. Cut a bottom piece
5. Create joints (butt joints with glue and nails are simplest)
6. Assemble sides and ends
7. Insert and glue bottom piece
8. Plane and sand smooth
9. Finish with oil

**Timeline:** 4-6 hours (not counting drying)

**Result:** Functional storage box

### Example 2: Crafting a Wooden Handle for a Tool

**Scenario:** A tool needs a handle.

**Process:**
1. Select appropriate hardwood (ash is excellent for handles)
2. Determine handle dimensions
3. Rough shape with an axe or saw (octagonal shape is easier than round)
4. Refine shape with a plane and chisel
5. Sand smooth with progressively finer abrasives
6. Finish with oil or oil + wax

**Timeline:** 4-8 hours (plus drying time)

**Result:** Comfortable, durable tool handle

### Example 3: Building Furniture (Table or Bench)

**Scenario:** You want a table for work or eating.

**Process:**
1. Design the table (simple rectangular top supported by four legs)
2. Select wood (hardwood for durability)
3. Prepare lumber to consistent dimensions
4. Create leg frame using mortise and tenon joints
5. Attach top using buttons or clips that allow for wood movement
6. Plane and sand surfaces
7. Finish with oil and wax

**Timeline:** 20-40 hours (depending on complexity)

**Result:** Durable table suitable for long service

## Part 7: Tool Sharpening and Maintenance

Sharp tools are essential for good woodworking.

### Maintaining Saws

- **Cleaning:** Wipe blade clean after use
- **Storage:** Hang vertically or protect blade; avoid rust
- **Sharpening:** Requires special files and skill; may be delegated to specialists

### Maintaining Chisels and Planes

**Sharpening:**
1. Use a whetstone or stropping leather
2. Maintain the bevel angle
3. Create a sharp edge (back of the tool should be flat)
4. Stropping on leather maintains sharpness between full sharpenings

**Storage:**
- Protect blades from damage
- Store in a dry location (avoid rust)
- Keep handles in good condition

### Maintaining Wooden Tools

- Clean after use
- Oil wooden handles periodically
- Repair or replace damaged handles
- Avoid excessive moisture (causes swelling and warping)

## Common Mistakes

1. **Dull tools:** Cause poor results and frustration. Solution: Maintain sharp tools regularly; learning to sharpen is essential.

2. **Working against the grain:** Produces rough, torn surfaces. Solution: Always identify grain direction; plane with the grain.

3. **Measuring once, cutting once:** Assumes accuracy; wastes wood on mistakes. Solution: Measure carefully; use measuring tools; verify measurements.

4. **Forcing joints:** Joints should fit properly without force. Solution: If a joint is too tight, remove it carefully and adjust; never force joints together.

5. **Inadequate clamping:** Joints don't close properly. Solution: Use clamps; apply proper pressure; verify joint fits before glue sets.

6. **Wood movement not considered:** Finished piece warps or joints crack. Solution: Design to allow wood movement; leave gaps for wood to move; use appropriate joinery.

7. **Uneven surface preparation:** Planing in one direction creates slight unevenness. Solution: Plane diagonally, then lengthwise; use a straightedge to verify flatness.

8. **Finishing applied to dirty wood:** Dust and impurities trapped under finish. Solution: Clean wood thoroughly; wipe with damp cloth; allow to dry completely before finishing.

9. **Joints not glued properly:** Joints separate after drying. Solution: Ensure surfaces are clean and dry; apply adequate glue; clamp firmly; allow proper cure time.

10. **Trying complex joinery without practice:** Results in frustration and poor joints. Solution: Practice simple joints first; advance to complex joints after gaining skill and confidence.
