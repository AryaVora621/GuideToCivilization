## Overview

A brick is a simple object: a block of clay hardened by heat. Yet this humble material has enabled construction of civilizations. Bricks are durable (lasting centuries), attractive, and can be made anywhere clay is available.

In a post-collapse context, the ability to produce bricks transforms temporary shelter into permanent structures. Communities that can make their own bricks become independent of external supply chains for building materials.

This chapter focuses on practical brickmaking: finding and preparing clay, forming bricks by hand, and firing them in simple kilns.

Why this matters:
- **Durability:** Fired bricks last hundreds of years
- **Permanence:** Brick structures are far more durable than wood or temporary materials
- **Independence:** Clay is common; brickmaking requires no manufactured inputs
- **Efficiency:** Once a kiln is built, large quantities of bricks can be produced regularly
- **Value:** Bricks are valuable trade goods

## Prerequisites

- Access to clay soil (test to determine suitability)
- Heat source (fire) for firing bricks
- Basic tools (shovels, wooden molds, pressing boards)
- Physical capability for sustained manual labor
- Workspace (drying area, kiln area)
- Patience: brickmaking is slow (drying takes weeks; firing takes days)

## Materials & Tools

### Finding Clay

**Where to find clay:**
- Near streams, rivers, or lakes (bottom sediment)
- Excavation sites or eroded banks
- Areas with clay soil (identified by color: reddish, gray, or yellowish)
- Test: Take a handful of soil and form into a ball; if it holds its shape and is sticky when wet, clay is present

**Testing clay suitability:**

1. Make a simple brick from found clay
2. Dry thoroughly (1-2 weeks)
3. Attempt to fire (in a kiln or carefully in a fire)
4. Successful firing: brick becomes hard; does not crumble
5. Unsuccessful: clay contains too much sand or impurities; try a different location

**Clay quality:**
- **Pure clay:** Ideal; forms strong bricks; rare
- **Clay with sand:** Common; acceptable if sand content is not excessive (up to 30%)
- **Clay with stone:** Undesirable; stones interfere with forming and firing
- **Silty clay:** Acceptable; silt burns away during firing

### Brick-Making Tools

**Essential tools:**
- Shovel or digging tool
- Large container or basin for mixing clay
- Wooden mold (defines brick size and shape)
- Striking board (wooden board to level brick in mold)
- Drying rack or ground area (for bricks to dry)

**Improvising molds:**
- Simple wooden frame (four boards forming rectangle)
- Internal dimensions: standard brick size (approximately 4"W x 8"L x 2.5"H, though size varies)
- Hinged mold (one side opens) makes brick removal easier
- Smooth interior surfaces (prevent clay from sticking)

### Heat Source for Firing

**Kiln types:**
- **Open fire:** Simple; least efficient; labor-intensive
- **Clamp kiln:** Bricks stacked in specific pattern; fire burns through the pile
- **Kiln structure:** Permanent or semi-permanent structure with fuel chamber and brick chamber

## Part 1: Understanding Bricks and Clay

### Brick Composition

A brick consists of:
1. **Clay minerals:** Primary binder; provides strength when fired
2. **Silica (sand):** Fills space; reduces shrinkage
3. **Impurities:** Iron (causes reddish color), lime, organic matter

### Firing Process (Chemistry)

When clay is heated above 900°C (1650°F):

1. **Dehydration:** Water is driven out
2. **Chemical change:** Clay minerals transform into harder, more stable forms
3. **Sintering:** Clay particles begin to fuse together (creates strength)
4. **Vitrification:** At very high temperatures, clay becomes glass-like (maximum strength)

Properly fired bricks achieve hardness and durability that last centuries.

### Brick Strength Factors

- **Clay composition:** Correct balance of clay and sand
- **Moisture content:** Proper forming and drying prevents cracks
- **Firing temperature:** Adequate heat (900-1100°C typical) creates strong bricks
- **Firing duration:** Sufficient time at temperature ensures uniform hardness

## Part 2: Preparing Clay

### Extraction

1. Locate clay-bearing soil (by testing)
2. Excavate: remove topsoil; expose clay layer
3. Collect: gather clay into a pile; remove rocks and large stones by hand
4. Transport: move clay to work area

### Initial Cleaning

**Removing impurities:**

1. Spread clay on ground
2. Allow to dry partially (1-2 days)
3. Break dried clay into smaller pieces
4. Hand-pick large stones and debris
5. Screen through cloth or mesh to remove small stones
6. Repeat if necessary

### Tempering (Mixing)

Tempering prepares clay for brick forming:

**Basic process:**

1. **Moisture adjustment:** Add water to dry clay until it reaches workable consistency
   - Too dry: cracks during forming and drying
   - Too wet: sticks to mold; difficult to shape
   - Correct: moldable but maintains shape

2. **Mixing:** Combine clay thoroughly
   - **Foot treading:** Traditional method; walk on clay repeatedly to mix
   - **Chopping:** Use a tool to chop and turn clay; mixes while breaking up lumps
   - **Animal power:** Cattle or horses can be driven over clay to mix (if available)
   - Duration: mix until clay is uniform (no dry spots or lumps)

3. **Sand addition (if needed):** If clay is too pure (too much shrinkage)
   - Add sand (up to 20-30% by volume)
   - Mix thoroughly
   - Sand prevents excessive shrinkage cracks

**Checking temper:**
- Form a small ball
- Squeeze: clay should hold shape but not be sticky
- Bend: should bend without cracking
- Drop: should not shatter on impact

## Part 3: Forming Bricks

### Step 1: Preparing the Mold

1. Clean mold thoroughly (remove clay from previous bricks)
2. Dampen interior surface (prevents sticking)
3. Place on flat, firm surface
4. Ensure mold is level

### Step 2: Filling the Mold

1. Press tempered clay firmly into mold
2. Fill completely; remove air pockets
3. Overfill slightly (approximately 0.5 inch above top)
4. Use hands or a tool to pack clay firmly

**Removing air pockets:**
- Pat and press vigorously
- Tap mold bottom on ground
- Press harder in corners
- Air pockets create weak spots that fail during firing

### Step 3: Striking Off

**Striking off** means removing excess clay level with the mold edge:

1. Use a wooden board (striking board) or flat tool
2. Press the board firmly against the mold edge
3. Drag the board across the top of the mold
4. Remove excess clay with one smooth motion
5. The brick should be flush with the mold edges

### Step 4: Removing from Mold

**Hinged mold (easier):**
1. Open the hinged side
2. The brick falls out
3. Minimal handling required

**Fixed mold:**
1. Invert the mold quickly and firmly (in one motion)
2. The brick should come free with the inversion
3. If stuck: tap mold edges to loosen
4. Place brick on drying rack

### Consistency

**Making multiple bricks:**
- Consistent handling produces uniform bricks
- Uniform bricks dry evenly; fire evenly
- Develop a rhythm and pattern
- Production: experienced workers can form 200-400 bricks per day

## Part 4: Drying

Drying is critical; improper drying leads to cracked, ruined bricks.

### Drying Environment

**Ideal conditions:**
- Warm (promotes water evaporation)
- Dry air (low humidity)
- Shaded or partially shaded (prevents uneven drying)
- Protected from rain

**Drying time:** 2-4 weeks depending on:
- Climate (warm, dry climates = faster)
- Brick size (thicker bricks = longer)
- Humidity (low humidity = faster)
- Temperature (cold weather = much slower)

### Drying Process

**Week 1:**
- Bricks release surface water
- Should not be moved or disturbed
- Place on drying rack with good air circulation
- Stack bricks with spaces (air must flow between bricks)

**Week 2-3:**
- Bricks continue to dry
- Interior water gradually evaporates
- Can be moved carefully if necessary
- Should not be stacked directly on top of each other (weight + moisture = compression damage)

**Week 4:**
- Bricks should be mostly dry
- Check by weight (fully dry bricks are noticeably lighter)
- Check by color (fully dry bricks are lighter in color)
- Sound: tap brick; should sound hollow

### Preventing Cracks

**Causes of cracking:**
1. **Uneven drying:** Outside of brick dries faster than inside; surface cracks
2. **Wind drying:** Rapid water loss from surface while interior retains moisture
3. **Sun exposure:** Uneven heating causes uneven drying
4. **Stacking:** Weight from stacked bricks compresses drying bricks below

**Prevention:**
- Dry in shade or filtered sunlight
- Avoid wind exposure initially (partial protection)
- Do not stack bricks during drying
- Allow 2-4 weeks without rushing
- Space bricks for air circulation

## Part 5: Firing

Firing hardens clay into durable brick through heat.

### Firing Temperatures

- **Low temperature (700-900°C):** Clay loses water but remains porous; sun-dried bricks
- **Proper firing (900-1100°C):** Clay becomes hard; good durability
- **High temperature (1100-1200°C):** Maximum hardness; some clay can vitrify (glass-like)
- **Over-firing (>1200°C):** Some clay becomes too brittle; bricks can warp or fuse together

**Target temperature:** 950-1050°C produces strong, durable bricks

### Simple Kiln (Clamp Kiln)

A clamp kiln burns fuel mixed with the bricks to fire them:

**Construction:**
1. Layer bricks in a specific pattern
2. Green (unfired) bricks form the walls and interior
3. Fuel (wood, coal, or dried clay) is mixed into the pile
4. The fuel burns as the kiln fires, heating the bricks

**Classic clamp pattern:**

1. Base: layer of burned bricks or stone (insulation)
2. Load: arrange unfired bricks with fuel mixed throughout
   - Fuel: approximately 20% of volume
   - Spacing: fuel needs air to burn
3. Cap: top layer of brick, sometimes sealed
4. Flue hole: single opening where fire starts and gases escape

**Firing process:**

1. Light fire at flue hole (or fire channel from outside)
2. Initially: cool air is drawn in; green bricks dry and warm
3. After several hours: fire reaches the brick mass
4. Over several days: fire gradually progresses through the kiln
5. Duration: 7-14 days for complete firing
6. Cool-down: after fire burns out, kiln cools gradually (several weeks)

**Monitoring:**
- Temperature can be estimated by observing firing progress (clay color changes)
- Early firing: bricks remain pale
- Mid-firing: bricks darken
- Good firing: deep color; bricks ring when tapped
- Over-firing: bricks darken excessively; can fuse together

### Permanent Kiln Structure

For large-scale production, a permanent kiln increases efficiency:

**Simple beehive kiln:**
1. Dome-shaped structure (can be made from bricks)
2. Permanent firebox below the dome
3. Bricks stacked inside dome
4. Heat circulates around bricks; exits through top vent

**Advantages:**
- Reusable structure (fired brick becomes kiln material)
- Better heat distribution (more even firing)
- More fuel-efficient (heated structure retains heat)
- Faster production cycles

**Challenges:**
- Requires initial brick production to construct
- Complex design and execution
- Takes time to build

## Part 6: Post-Firing

### Unloading and Inspection

**After kiln cools (2-3 weeks):**

1. Remove bricks from kiln carefully
2. Inspect each brick:
   - **Good brick:** Hard, rings when tapped, uniform color
   - **Weak brick:** Crumbles or breaks easily; soft
   - **Deformed brick:** Warped or cracked during firing

3. Sort:
   - Grade A: Best bricks; use for visible or structural work
   - Grade B: Acceptable bricks; use for backing or less visible areas
   - Rejects: Damaged or failed bricks; can be recycled (crushed for fill or temper in new bricks)

### Storage

- Stack in organized piles
- Support from ground (prevents moisture absorption)
- Cover top to prevent rain (but allow air circulation)
- Protect from damage until use

### Recycling Failures

Failed bricks can be salvaged:

1. Crush failed bricks
2. Mix crushed brick with new clay (as temper)
3. Or use crushed brick as fill material

## Part 7: Building With Bricks

### Bonding Patterns

**Bonding** is the pattern of overlapping bricks for strength:

**Running bond (most common):**
- Horizontal courses alternate: one course has bricks lengthwise; next course staggers joints
- Very strong; requires minimal cutting
- Most efficient pattern

**Flemish bond:**
- Alternates stretchers (long face) and headers (short face)
- Very strong; attractive appearance
- More labor-intensive (more cutting)

**Stack bond:**
- Bricks stacked directly on top of each other
- Weaker; not recommended for structural walls
- Simpler but inferior

### Mortar

Mortar is the material between bricks (provides bond and seals):

**Traditional mortar composition:**
- Lime: binder (approximately 1 part)
- Sand: filler (approximately 3-4 parts)
- Water: activator
- Optional: clay (adds stickiness)

**Process:**
1. Mix lime and sand
2. Add water gradually; mix until workable
3. Consistency: should hold its shape but be spreadable

### Laying Bricks

**Basic technique:**

1. Spread mortar on course below (bed joint)
2. Place brick on mortar
3. Press down gently; some mortar extrudes
4. Apply mortar to end of new brick (head joint)
5. Place next brick in course
6. Repeat
7. Strike joints: remove excess mortar; compress remaining mortar

**Checking plumb and level:**
- Use plumb line (weight on string) for vertical
- Use level tool for horizontal
- Adjust as you work
- Walls must be plumb (vertical) for strength and appearance

## Part 8: Practical Applications and Examples

### Example 1: Small Permanent Kiln for Regular Production

**Scenario:** You want to produce bricks regularly for community building projects.

**Process:**
1. Design simple kiln (dome or rectangular chamber)
2. Fire initial bricks (production for kiln material)
3. Build kiln structure from fired bricks
4. Subsequent firings produce additional bricks
5. Establish regular production schedule

**Timeline:** 
- Kiln construction: 1-2 weeks
- First firing cycle: 3-4 weeks (forming + drying + firing)
- Subsequent cycles: 3-4 weeks per batch

**Capacity:** 500-2000 bricks per firing cycle

### Example 2: Building a Permanent Shelter

**Scenario:** Your community wants to upgrade from temporary shelter to permanent structure.

**Process:**
1. Estimate brick requirements (based on wall dimensions)
2. Produce bricks in batches (multiple firing cycles)
3. Prepare foundation
4. Build walls using running bond pattern
5. Install roof
6. Finish interior

**Timeline:** 6-12 months (dependent on scale and firing schedule)

**Result:** Permanent, durable structure lasting centuries

### Example 3: Making Water-Resistant Bricks for Special Use

**Scenario:** You need bricks for structures exposed to water (wells, cisterns).

**Process:**
1. Select clay with appropriate composition (dense, low-porosity)
2. Fire to higher temperature (1000-1100°C) for maximum hardness
3. Use bricks for water-contact structures
4. Seal with lime-based wash if needed

**Result:** Long-lasting structures resistant to water damage

## Common Mistakes

1. **Inadequate clay preparation:** Lumps and impurities cause weak bricks. Solution: Mix clay thoroughly; remove debris carefully.

2. **Forming bricks with insufficient pressure:** Air pockets cause weak spots. Solution: Pack clay firmly into mold; tap to remove air; press vigorously.

3. **Drying too fast:** Uneven drying causes cracks. Solution: Protect bricks from direct sun and wind; allow 2-4 weeks; don't rush drying.

4. **Stacking bricks during drying:** Weight compresses drying bricks below. Solution: Keep bricks separate during drying; stack only after fully dry.

5. **Under-firing:** Bricks remain soft and friable. Solution: Maintain firing temperature long enough; monitor firing progress; ensure adequate heat penetration.

6. **Over-firing:** Bricks warp or fuse together. Solution: Monitor firing carefully; know the clay's optimal firing temperature; stop before over-firing.

7. **Inconsistent brick dimensions:** Walls become uneven; joints are difficult to mortar. Solution: Maintain consistent mold design; form bricks uniformly.

8. **Poor mortar composition:** Joints fail; water penetrates; walls separate. Solution: Use proper ratio of lime to sand; add water gradually; compress joints fully.

9. **Walls out of plumb:** Appear crooked; are structurally weak. Solution: Check frequently with plumb line; adjust during laying; rebuild if necessary.

10. **Failing to allow adequate cooling:** Hot kiln still cooling can damage bricks if opened too quickly. Solution: Allow kiln to cool completely before unloading (2-3 weeks typical).
