## Overview

Casting is the most direct way to turn raw metal into a useful shape: melt it, pour it into a hollow mold of the form you want, and let it solidify. The principle is ancient — older than written history — yet it remains one of the most important manufacturing processes in any civilization, because it can produce shapes that no other method can. A complex engine block, a curved bell, a toothed gear blank, a statue, a cooking pot: all are routinely cast, often in a single pour, where carving or forging them would be impossibly slow or simply impossible.

What makes casting so powerful is that liquid metal flows into every corner of a mold, taking up intricate internal and external detail at once. You make the mold once and can often reuse it or remake it cheaply; each pour reproduces the shape. For producing many copies of a complicated part, or any part with internal cavities, casting is frequently the only practical route. It is also the gateway process for a metalworking civilization: master melting and pouring, and you can make the tools, machine parts, and vessels that everything else depends on.

This chapter teaches the essentials: how molds and patterns work, how to melt and pour safely, what happens as metal solidifies, and how to recognize and prevent the defects that turn a casting into scrap.

## The Basic Idea

Every casting process has the same skeleton:

1. **Make a mold** — a heat-resistant hollow cavity shaped like the part you want, with channels for the metal to enter and for air to escape.
2. **Melt the metal** to a pourable liquid.
3. **Pour** the molten metal into the mold cavity, filling it completely.
4. **Let it solidify and cool** until the metal is solid and strong.
5. **Remove the casting** from the mold and clean it up — cutting off the channels, removing surface roughness.

The art lies in the details of each step: making a mold that survives the heat and gives good detail, melting cleanly without contamination, pouring fully without trapping air, and cooling without cracks or voids.

## Patterns and Molds

To make a mold cavity, you usually start with a **pattern** — a model of the part, around which the mold is formed. The pattern is slightly **larger** than the final part, because metal shrinks as it cools (the "shrinkage allowance"), and its surfaces are slightly tapered (the "draft") so it can be withdrawn without tearing the mold. A good pattern is the foundation of a good casting; errors in the pattern reproduce in every part.

The main mold types, from simplest to most refined:

- **Sand casting** is the workhorse. The mold is made of damp, packed sand bonded with clay, formed around the pattern in two halves (a split box called a flask). The pattern is removed, leaving a cavity; the halves are reassembled and the metal poured. Sand is cheap, tolerates high temperatures, and is broken away after each pour and reused. Sand casting suits everything from small parts to huge ones, but gives a rough surface and modest detail.
- **Investment casting ("lost wax")** gives fine detail. Make the pattern in **wax**, coat it in a fine ceramic slurry that hardens into a shell, then **melt the wax out**, leaving a precise cavity. Pour metal in, then break the ceramic away. Each pattern is destroyed, but the detail and accuracy are excellent — ideal for jewelry, fine tools, and intricate parts.
- **Permanent-mold casting** uses a reusable metal mold for many identical parts. The mold is expensive to make but pays off over a long run, giving good surface and repeatability. It suits metals that melt well below the mold's own melting point.

Critically, every mold needs more than just the cavity: a **sprue and runners** (channels guiding metal in), a **gate** (the entry to the cavity), **risers** (reservoirs of extra molten metal that feed the casting as it shrinks), and **vents** (escape paths for air and gas). Without these, the metal will not fill cleanly and the casting will be flawed.

## Melting the Metal

Melting demands serious, controlled heat — far above what cooking or heating needs. A **furnace** does the job: a crucible furnace (metal held in a clay-graphite pot heated by fuel or induction) for smaller work, or a larger reverberatory or cupola furnace for volume. The essentials:

- **Reach the metal's melting point with margin.** Pour temperature must be somewhat above the melting point so the metal stays liquid long enough to fill the mold before it freezes ("superheat").
- **Protect the melt from contamination.** Molten metal greedily absorbs gases and oxidizes in air; impurities and dissolved gas ruin castings. Fluxes float impurities to the top as a removable slag, and covers limit oxidation.
- **Skim the slag** off the top before pouring, so it does not flow into the mold.

Different metals demand different temperatures: low-melting metals like lead and tin are easy; copper alloys (bronze, brass) need more; cast iron and steel need a great deal. Match your furnace and crucible to the metal.

## Pouring and Solidification

Pouring is the dramatic, dangerous moment. The metal is poured steadily into the sprue, flowing through the runners and gates to fill the cavity from the bottom up, pushing air out through the vents and risers. Pour **steadily and fully** — too slow and the metal freezes before filling; too turbulent and it traps air and oxide.

As the metal cools, it **solidifies from the mold walls inward**, because the mold draws heat away first. Two facts dominate what happens next:

- **Metal shrinks as it solidifies and cools.** This is why patterns are oversized and why **risers** exist: the riser stays liquid longest and feeds extra metal into the casting as the part shrinks, preventing internal voids. A casting without adequate feeding develops **shrinkage cavities** as it starves for metal in its last-freezing regions.
- **Cooling rate sets the structure.** Fast cooling gives fine grain and hardness but risks cracking from stress; slow cooling gives coarser, softer, tougher metal. Control the cooling to get the properties you want, and cool evenly to avoid the internal stresses that crack a casting.

## Step-by-Step: A Sand Casting

1. **Make the pattern,** slightly oversized (for shrinkage) and tapered (for draft).
2. **Pack the mold:** ram damp bonded sand around the pattern in both halves of the flask; cut the sprue, runners, gate, risers, and vents.
3. **Remove the pattern** carefully, leaving a clean cavity, and reassemble the flask.
4. **Melt the metal** in the furnace to pouring temperature; flux and skim the slag.
5. **Pour** steadily into the sprue until the cavity and risers are full.
6. **Let it solidify and cool** fully — do not rush it open.
7. **Shake out** the casting from the sand.
8. **Finish:** cut off the sprue, runners, and risers; grind away the gate marks and surface roughness; inspect.

## Verification Criteria

A sound casting:

- **Fills completely** — every feature of the part is present, with no missing or rounded-off detail from metal freezing early ("misrun" or "cold shut").
- **Is free of cracks** on the surface and (where you can check) inside.
- **Has no large shrinkage voids** in thick or last-freezing sections — verified by weight, by sectioning a sample, or by tapping for a solid ring.
- **Matches dimensions** within tolerance after shrinkage; measure against the intended part, not the oversized pattern.
- **Has a clean surface** free of trapped sand, slag inclusions, or gas-hole pitting.

## Common Mistakes

- **Pouring too cold or too slow,** so the metal freezes before filling — giving incomplete castings (misruns) or weak seams where two cold streams met (cold shuts). Pour hot enough and steadily.
- **Inadequate risers and feeding,** leaving shrinkage cavities in thick sections. Every casting must be fed as it shrinks.
- **No venting,** trapping air and gas as blowholes. Give the air a way out.
- **Forgetting shrinkage allowance,** producing parts that come out undersized. The pattern must be larger than the final part.
- **No draft on the pattern,** so it tears the mold on removal. Taper every vertical surface.
- **Pouring contaminated metal** full of slag and dissolved gas, giving porous, weak, inclusion-riddled castings. Flux, skim, and protect the melt.
- **Uneven or too-fast cooling,** cracking the casting from internal stress. Cool evenly and not too quickly for crack-prone metals.

## Safety

Casting is among the most dangerous shop processes. Molten metal causes catastrophic burns and **explodes violently on contact with water or moisture** — even a damp mold or tool can flash trapped water to steam and hurl metal. Keep everything bone dry, never pour onto or near water, wear heat protection and a face shield, keep a clear path away from the pour, and ensure good ventilation against furnace fumes. Treat molten metal with the respect it demands; carelessness here maims.
