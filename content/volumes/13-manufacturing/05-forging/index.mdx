Forging is the craft at the root of the toolmaking world. Almost every metal tool a civilization needs, from nails and hinges to axes, plough shares, knives, chisels, and the very hammers and tongs of the smith's own trade, begins as a glowing bar beaten into shape on an anvil. The blacksmith was for thousands of years one of the most important people in any settlement, because without forged metal there are no good tools, and without good tools there is no agriculture, no building, no further manufacturing. Master forging and you can make or repair nearly every metal object your community depends on.

This chapter teaches the fundamentals of working hot metal by force. You will learn to build and run a forge, to recognize working temperatures by color, to wield the hammer for the basic operations of drawing, upsetting, bending, and punching, and to make real tools from raw stock. It builds on iron production and the workshop, and it unlocks heat treatment, toolmaking, and the making of edged tools.

## Why Forging Works

Metal, when hot enough, becomes soft and plastic without melting. In this state its internal structure can be pushed, squeezed, and rearranged by force, and it holds the new shape when it cools. Forging exploits this in a way that casting cannot match: hammering does not just shape the metal, it **improves** it. The blows compress the grain, close internal voids, and align the metal's structure along the flow of the work, producing a piece stronger and tougher than a cast or simply cut part of the same metal. A forged tool survives shocks that would crack a cast one.

Three ideas govern the craft:

- **Heat makes metal workable.** The hotter (without burning), the softer and easier to move, but there are limits.
- **Force shapes it.** Hammer blows, pressure, and bending move the hot metal where you want it.
- **The metal hardens as it cools.** You have only a short window while the piece glows; work fast, reheat often.

## Prerequisites

- A source of iron or steel stock (bar, rod, or salvaged metal)
- The ability to make and sustain a hot fire with the right fuel
- Strength and stamina, and a safe, fireproof working space
- Patience to learn hammer control, which takes much practice

## Materials & Tools

**The forge and fire:**
- A **forge**: a hearth that concentrates fuel and forces air through it for high heat
- **Fuel**: charcoal or coal (coke), which burn hot and clean enough to heat metal to forging temperature
- A **bellows** or blower to force air into the fire, raising its temperature

**The smith's core tools:**
- An **anvil**: a heavy block of hard iron or steel with a flat face, a horn for curves, and holes for tools. The mass matters; a heavy anvil returns the hammer's energy into the work.
- **Hammers** of various weights for striking
- **Tongs** shaped to grip different stock, to hold hot metal safely
- A **vise** to clamp work
- **Punches and chisels** (hardies) for cutting and making holes
- A **slack tub** of water for quenching and cooling

## Part 1: Building and Running the Forge

The forge's job is to heat metal to a workable temperature, which for iron and steel is a bright orange to yellow heat, far hotter than an ordinary fire.

1. **Build the hearth:** a fireproof bed (clay, brick, or stone) to hold the burning fuel, with an air inlet (the tuyere) low in the fire.
2. **Force air through it:** a bellows or blower drives air through the tuyere into the heart of the fuel. This forced air is what raises the fire from ordinary cooking heat to metal-working heat. Without forced air you cannot forge.
3. **Build a deep fire:** the metal is buried in the hot zone within the glowing fuel, not laid on top. Coal forms a domed, caked fire; charcoal burns faster and needs frequent feeding.
4. **Manage the fire:** keep the hot zone fed and free of clinker (fused ash), and place the work where the fuel glows brightest.

## Part 2: Reading the Heat by Color

A smith without a thermometer judges temperature by the color of the glowing metal, and this skill is central to the craft:

- **Dull red:** too cold to forge most steel; the metal resists and may crack if struck hard.
- **Bright red to orange:** the general working range for shaping iron and mild steel.
- **Yellow:** very hot, soft, and easy to move; good for heavy shaping and welding-adjacent work.
- **White and sparking:** too hot; the metal is burning, throwing sparks, and being ruined. Pull it out at once.

The rule: heat to the working color, shape quickly while it glows, and reheat the moment it dulls toward dark red. Working metal that is too cold wastes effort and damages both the piece and your tools.

## Part 3: The Four Core Operations

Nearly all forging is built from a handful of basic moves.

### Drawing Out (making longer and thinner)

Hammering a bar across its width stretches it longer and thinner. Strike with overlapping blows, turning the work, to taper a point, lengthen a bar, or thin a section. This is the most common operation: pointing a nail, tapering a chisel, lengthening a rod.

### Upsetting (making shorter and thicker)

The reverse of drawing: hammer on the end of a heated bar (or drop it end-down) to compress and thicken it. Used to make a bolt head, thicken a section that must bear load, or gather material before shaping.

### Bending

Hot metal bends easily over the anvil's edge or horn, or held in the vise. Bend over the rounded horn for curves, over a sharp edge for angles. Heat only the part to be bent so the rest stays straight.

### Punching and Drifting (making holes)

Drive a punch through the hot metal to start a hole, working from both sides to avoid cracking, then enlarge and shape it by driving a tapered **drift** through. This makes the eye of a hammer or axe, or holes for rivets and bolts.

To these add **cutting** (with a chisel or hardy, while hot or cold) and **welding** (joining two pieces by hammering them together at near-melting heat, an advanced skill).

## Part 4: A First Project, Making a Cold Chisel

A cold chisel is a classic first forging and teaches drawing, shaping, and the beginnings of heat treatment.

1. **Choose stock:** a piece of medium or high carbon steel (an old file or coil spring works), which can be hardened.
2. **Heat** the end to bright orange.
3. **Draw out the cutting end** into a flattened wedge by hammering both faces, turning often, forming a symmetrical taper.
4. **Shape the striking end** if needed, slightly rounding the corners so it does not mushroom dangerously.
5. **Refine the edge** at a lower heat with lighter blows.
6. **Harden and temper** (covered fully in the heat treatment chapter): heat to the hardening color and quench, then gently reheat to draw back some hardness so the edge is hard but not brittle.
7. **Sharpen** the edge on a stone.

The same sequence, scaled and varied, makes punches, screwdrivers, scribes, and many other tools.

## Part 5: Safety in the Forge

Forging is hot, heavy, and hazardous, and a careful smith respects it:

- **Everything near the fire is hot,** and hot metal looks identical to cold metal. Assume any piece you did not just see cool is hot, and handle with tongs.
- **Wear eye protection** against flying scale and sparks, and stout clothing and footwear that resist heat.
- **Beware burning metal:** white, sparking heat means the piece is being destroyed; remove it.
- **Quench carefully:** plunging hot metal into water throws steam and can crack the piece; do it deliberately.
- **Keep the floor clear** of trip hazards and the area free of anything that catches fire.
- **Anchor the anvil** so it cannot shift, and keep hammer faces and handles sound.

## Worked Example: Forging a Wall Hook

1. **Stock:** A length of square iron bar.
2. **Point one end:** heat to orange, draw out to a taper for driving into wood or masonry.
3. **Bend the hook:** reheat the middle, bend over the anvil horn into a smooth curve.
4. **Form the holding end:** flatten and curl the far end so objects rest on it.
5. **Finish:** let cool, then clean off the scale. The hook is stronger than a cast one because the grain follows the bends.
6. **Lesson:** every household and workshop fitting, hooks, brackets, latches, handles, comes from these same few operations repeated and combined.

## Verification Criteria

Your forging is competent when:

- **You sustain a working heat:** the fire brings iron and steel to bright orange or yellow with forced air.
- **You read color correctly:** working in the orange-yellow range, never letting the piece burn white.
- **Your hammer blows land true and overlapping**, moving metal predictably without deep dents or off-center strikes.
- **You can draw out, upset, bend, and punch** to produce a piece close to your intended shape.
- **Forged pieces are sound:** no cracks from cold-working or overheating, with the grain following the form.

## Common Mistakes

- **Working metal too cold.** It resists, cracks, and ruins tools. Reheat the moment it dulls.
- **Overheating to white, sparking heat.** The metal burns and crumbles, ruined beyond saving. Pull it before it sparks.
- **Weak or no forced air.** An ordinary fire never reaches forging heat. Build and use a bellows or blower.
- **A light, loose anvil.** It absorbs the blow instead of returning it, doubling your effort. Use a heavy, well-anchored anvil.
- **Poor hammer control.** Off-center, uneven blows produce ugly, weak work. This comes only with practice; start with light, accurate strikes.
- **Treating hot metal as cool.** A constant, serious burn risk. Assume every uncertain piece is hot, and record working ranges in plain words such as "quench when below dull red," never a broken-tag form, so notes copy cleanly.
