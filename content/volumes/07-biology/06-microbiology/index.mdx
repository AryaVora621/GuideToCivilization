## Overview

You cannot see them, but they are everywhere. Bacteria live in soil, water, and your body. Viruses infect cells. Fungi decompose dead matter. Archaea thrive in extreme environments. These microorganisms are the foundation of life—they cycle nutrients, produce food (fermentation), and cause disease.

Understanding microorganisms helps you:

1. **Prevent disease:** Know how pathogens spread and how to block transmission
2. **Preserve food:** Understand how microorganisms spoil food and how to prevent it
3. **Produce food:** Use fermentation (controlled microbial growth) to preserve vegetables, dairy, and grains
4. **Manage health:** Recognize when microorganisms are causing illness and respond appropriately

This chapter teaches practical microbiology for post-collapse survival.

## Part 1: Bacterial Cells

Bacteria are single-celled organisms that are prokaryotic (no nucleus). They are the most abundant organisms on Earth and the smallest that are alive.

### Structure of a Bacterial Cell

A bacterial cell contains:

1. **Cell wall:** A rigid structure surrounding the cell membrane, giving bacteria their shape
2. **Cell membrane:** A boundary controlling what enters and exits
3. **Cytoplasm:** The interior jelly-like substance containing water and chemicals
4. **DNA (nucleoid):** The genetic material (not in a nucleus, just loose in the cytoplasm)
5. **Ribosomes:** Structures that make proteins
6. **Flagella:** Whip-like structures that allow some bacteria to move

**Size:** Bacteria are approximately 0.5–5 micrometers (0.0005–0.005 millimeters). Invisible without magnification of 1000x or more.

### How Bacteria Reproduce

Most bacteria reproduce asexually by binary fission (splitting in two):

1. **DNA replication:** The bacterial DNA copies itself
2. **Cell division:** The cell grows and the copied DNA attaches to the cell membrane at different locations
3. **Septum formation:** A protein ring forms at the cell equator, pinching the cell
4. **Separation:** The ring tightens until the cell divides completely, creating two identical daughter cells

**Reproduction rate:** Under ideal conditions (food, water, temperature ~37°C), bacteria divide every 20 minutes. One bacterium becomes 2 million in 10 hours.

### Bacterial Diversity

Bacteria are incredibly diverse:

**Beneficial bacteria:**
- Soil bacteria that fix nitrogen (make atmospheric nitrogen available to plants)
- Gut bacteria that digest food and produce vitamins
- Fermentation bacteria (lactobacillus in yogurt and sauerkraut)
- Photosynthetic bacteria (some produce oxygen)

**Pathogenic bacteria:**
- Streptococcus (causes strep throat)
- Staphylococcus (causes skin infections)
- Vibrio (causes cholera)
- Mycobacterium (causes tuberculosis)
- Clostridium (causes tetanus, botulism)

**Environmental bacteria:**
- Thermophilic bacteria (thrive in heat)
- Halophilic bacteria (thrive in salt)
- Anaerobic bacteria (live without oxygen)

### Bacterial Growth and Death

Bacterial growth depends on:

1. **Temperature:** Each species has an optimal temperature
   - Mesophiles: 37°C (human pathogens)
   - Thermophiles: 50–80°C (hot springs)
   - Psychrophiles: cold water
2. **pH:** Most prefer neutral pH (7). Some prefer acid (yogurt bacteria). Some prefer alkaline.
3. **Oxygen:** Aerobes need oxygen. Anaerobes do not (some are inhibited by oxygen).
4. **Food:** Bacteria need organic carbon and nitrogen to grow.
5. **Water:** Most bacteria need water (though spores can survive without it).

Bacteria die when:
- Temperature becomes too extreme (boiling kills most)
- pH becomes too extreme (very acidic or alkaline)
- Oxygen is removed (for obligate aerobes) or added (for obligate anaerobes)
- Food runs out
- Toxins are introduced (disinfectants, salt, acids)

## Part 2: Viruses

Viruses are not cells. They are packages of genetic material (DNA or RNA) surrounded by a protein coat. They are smaller than bacteria (20–300 nanometers) and cannot reproduce without a host cell.

### Structure of a Virus

A virus consists of:

1. **Genetic material (DNA or RNA):** Instructions for making more viruses
2. **Protein coat (capsid):** Protects the genetic material; also allows the virus to recognize and enter host cells
3. **Envelope (in some viruses):** A lipid membrane surrounding the protein coat (gives the virus flexibility)

**Size:** Viruses are 20–300 nanometers (invisible without electron microscopy).

### How Viruses Reproduce

Viruses cannot reproduce on their own. They must hijack a host cell:

1. **Attachment:** The virus recognizes and binds to a specific cell receptor
2. **Entry:** The virus enters the cell (by injection or membrane fusion)
3. **Takeover:** The virus's genetic material directs the host cell to make viral proteins and replicate viral DNA
4. **Assembly:** New viral particles are assembled inside the host cell
5. **Release:** The host cell breaks open (lysis), releasing hundreds of new viruses to infect other cells

**Result:** One virus becomes hundreds in minutes to hours, depending on the virus.

### Viral Diversity

Viruses infect all types of cells:

**Animal viruses:**
- Influenza (respiratory infection)
- Measles (systemic infection)
- Polio (nerve infection)
- Coronavirus (respiratory/systemic)
- HIV (immune cell destruction)

**Plant viruses:**
- Tobacco mosaic virus (causes disease in tobacco, tomatoes, peppers)
- Potato virus (causes crop damage)

**Bacterial viruses (bacteriophages):**
- Infect bacteria only
- Used in fermentation and agriculture (some are beneficial)

### Viral Transmission

Viruses spread from person to person or animal to animal through:

1. **Respiratory droplets:** Coughing, sneezing (influenza, measles, coronavirus)
2. **Direct contact:** Touching infected tissue (herpes, warts)
3. **Bodily fluids:** Blood, saliva, sexual contact (HIV, hepatitis)
4. **Vectors:** Mosquitoes (dengue, yellow fever), ticks (Lyme disease)
5. **Food/water:** Contaminated food (rotavirus, hepatitis A)

Prevention depends on understanding transmission. Respiratory viruses are blocked by masks and distance. Waterborne viruses are blocked by clean water. Vector-borne viruses are blocked by insect control.

## Part 3: Other Microorganisms

### Fungi

Fungi are eukaryotic (have a nucleus) and can be single-celled or multicellular:

**Single-celled fungi (yeast):**
- Saccharomyces cerevisiae (bread and wine fermentation)
- Candida (causes yeast infections)

**Multicellular fungi (molds, mushrooms):**
- Penicillium (produces penicillin antibiotic)
- Aspergillus (spoils food, causes infections in immunocompromised people)
- Edible mushrooms (cultivated for food)

**Fungal reproduction:** Fungi produce spores (asexual reproduction) or fruiting bodies (sexual reproduction). Spores are lightweight and travel long distances.

**Fungal growth:** Fungi grow slower than bacteria (days vs. hours) but are effective decomposers and fermenters.

### Archaea

Archaea are prokaryotic (no nucleus) but genetically distinct from bacteria. Most live in extreme environments:

**Methanogens:** Produce methane from organic matter (used in biogas digesters)
**Halophiles:** Thrive in salt (used in salt preservation)
**Thermophiles:** Thrive in heat (used in industrial processes)

**For survival:** Archaea are less relevant than bacteria for disease or food production but are important for biogas and fermentation in extreme conditions.

### Protozoa

Protozoans are single-celled eukaryotes, larger than bacteria (10–50 micrometers):

**Pathogenic protozoans:**
- Plasmodium (causes malaria, transmitted by mosquitoes)
- Giardia (causes diarrheal disease, in contaminated water)
- Trypanosoma (causes sleeping sickness, transmitted by tsetse flies)
- Entamoeba (causes dysentery, in contaminated food/water)

**Environmental protozoans:**
- Feed on bacteria and help filter water
- Indicate water quality (presence suggests biological activity)

## Part 4: Microorganism Applications for Survival

### Application 1: Food Preservation (Preventing Microbial Growth)

Food spoils when microorganisms grow in it. Understanding microbial growth helps you preserve food.

**Methods to prevent microbial growth:**

1. **Heat:** Boiling kills most microorganisms. Canning uses heat to sterilize food and create an airtight seal.

2. **Cold:** Refrigeration (below 5°C) slows bacterial growth. Freezing stops bacterial growth (though does not kill bacteria).

3. **Salt:** High salt concentration (more than 20%) kills most bacteria by osmotic pressure (water leaves the cell).

4. **Acid:** Acidic pH (below 4.5, like vinegar or lemon juice) inhibits bacterial growth.

5. **Oxygen depletion:** Anaerobic conditions (no air) prevent aerobic bacteria from growing. Fermentation uses beneficial bacteria that do not need oxygen.

6. **Drying:** Removing water prevents most bacteria from growing (though spores survive).

7. **Smoking:** Smoke contains antimicrobial compounds that inhibit growth.

**Combination methods:** Using multiple methods together is more effective than one alone. Salt + acid (fermentation) creates an environment hostile to pathogens but suitable for beneficial bacteria.

### Application 2: Food Production (Using Fermentation)

Fermentation is controlled microbial growth that produces food and preserves it:

**Lactate fermentation (yogurt, sauerkraut, kimchi):**
1. Lactobacillus bacteria consume sugars and produce lactic acid
2. The acid environment prevents pathogenic bacteria from growing
3. The food is preserved and develops flavor

**Alcoholic fermentation (beer, wine):**
1. Yeast consumes sugars and produces ethanol and carbon dioxide
2. The ethanol environment prevents pathogenic bacteria from growing
3. The beverage is preserved and becomes intoxicating

**Acetic acid fermentation (vinegar):**
1. Acetobacter bacteria convert ethanol to acetic acid
2. The high acid prevents pathogenic bacteria from growing
3. The vinegar is preserved and develops flavor

**Mold fermentation (tempeh, some cheeses):**
1. Mold grows on soybeans or curds
2. The mold produces enzymes that break down proteins and fats
3. The food becomes digestible and develops flavor

### Application 3: Disease Prevention and Management

Understanding how pathogens spread helps you prevent disease:

**Transmission blocking:**
1. **Respiratory pathogens:** Distance, masks, ventilation
2. **Waterborne pathogens:** Clean water, boiling, filtration
3. **Foodborne pathogens:** Proper cooking, sanitation, safe storage
4. **Vector-borne pathogens:** Insect control, protective clothing

**Immune system support:**
1. **Nutrition:** Adequate protein, vitamins, minerals
2. **Hygiene:** Handwashing, wound care
3. **Sleep and stress reduction:** Support immune function
4. **Isolation (if infected):** Prevent transmission to others

**Infection management:**
1. **Heat (fever):** Your body raises temperature to slow bacterial growth; avoid lowering fever below 39°C
2. **Immune cells:** White blood cells attack pathogens; rest and hydration support this
3. **Drainage:** Allow infected wounds to drain (pus is your immune system working)
4. **Antibiotics (if available):** Use strategically; overuse creates resistance

## Part 5: Recognizing Microbial Infection

### Signs of Bacterial Infection

1. **Fever:** Temperature above 37°C (98.6°F). Higher fevers suggest more severe infection.
2. **Pus:** Yellow-white drainage from wounds. This is dead white blood cells and bacteria.
3. **Redness and swelling:** Immune response to infection.
4. **Pain:** Localized to the infected area.
5. **Systemic signs:** Fatigue, chills, loss of appetite.

### Signs of Viral Infection

1. **Fever:** Often high and sudden.
2. **Body aches and fatigue:** Systemic response.
3. **Respiratory symptoms:** Cough, sore throat, runny nose (for respiratory viruses).
4. **No pus:** Viral infections typically do not produce pus (no bacterial discharge).

### Distinguishing Bacterial from Viral

This matters because antibiotics kill bacteria but not viruses.

- **Bacterial:** Localized infection, pus, responds to antibiotics, slower onset
- **Viral:** Systemic symptoms, no pus, no antibiotic response, rapid onset

If you have fever and pus (especially from a wound), suspect bacterial infection. If you have fever and body aches but no pus, suspect viral infection.

## Verification

**Understanding microbiology means:**

1. You can predict how quickly a bacterial infection will spread (exponential growth in hours)
2. You understand why boiling water prevents disease (kills pathogens)
3. You can set up a fermentation (control microbial growth for food preservation)
4. You understand why food spoils (microorganism growth) and how to prevent it (heat, salt, acid, drying)
5. You can distinguish bacterial from viral infections (pus vs. systemic symptoms)
6. You understand disease transmission and can predict prevention methods

If you can reason through these examples using microbiology principles, you understand the concept.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Thinking all bacteria are harmful:** Most bacteria are beneficial or neutral. Only a tiny fraction cause disease.

2. **Underestimating bacterial growth rate:** Bacteria divide every 20 minutes under ideal conditions. A single bacterium becomes billions in one day.

3. **Assuming viruses can be treated with antibiotics:** Antibiotics kill bacteria. Viruses are not affected. Antiviral drugs are rare and require medical infrastructure.

4. **Thinking sterilization is always necessary:** Sterilization kills all microorganisms, which is overkill for most food preservation. Fermentation uses beneficial bacteria to prevent pathogens.

5. **Forgetting that fermentation requires the right conditions:** Fermentation works only if salt or acid levels are correct. Wrong conditions allow pathogens to grow.

6. **Assuming water is safe if it looks clear:** Clear water can contain invisible bacteria, viruses, or protozoans. Always boil questionable water.

7. **Thinking sick people cannot spread disease once fever breaks:** Viruses can be contagious for days after fever ends. Isolation should continue until recovery is confirmed.

8. **Underestimating the importance of hygiene:** Handwashing and wound care prevent more infections than any medicine in a post-collapse scenario.
