## Overview

You are not alone in your environment. Plants provide food. Animals hunt and are hunted. Decomposers break down the dead. Microorganisms cycle nutrients. All of these organisms interact as an ecosystem.

Ecology is the study of how organisms interact with each other and their environment. Understanding ecology helps you:

1. **Produce food sustainably:** Understanding food chains helps you know how much food you can extract from an ecosystem without depleting it.
2. **Predict population crashes:** Understanding predator-prey dynamics helps you anticipate when a food source will become scarce.
3. **Manage ecosystems:** Understanding succession (how environments change over time) helps you manage land for food production or other goals.
4. **Recognize limits:** Understanding carrying capacity (the maximum population an ecosystem can support) helps you avoid overexploiting resources.

In a post-collapse world, you depend on local ecosystems for survival. Understanding them is essential.

## Part 1: Ecosystems and Energy Flow

### What Is an Ecosystem?

An ecosystem is a community of organisms (plants, animals, microorganisms) plus the physical environment they live in (soil, water, air, temperature). Everything in an ecosystem is connected. Energy flows through the ecosystem. Nutrients cycle.

**Key players in an ecosystem:**

1. **Producers (plants):** Use light energy to create chemical energy (glucose) through photosynthesis
2. **Primary consumers (herbivores):** Eat plants, converting plant energy into animal energy
3. **Secondary consumers (carnivores):** Eat herbivores
4. **Tertiary consumers (top predators):** Eat carnivores
5. **Decomposers (bacteria, fungi):** Break down dead organisms and waste, returning nutrients to soil

Every organism plays a role. Remove one, and the system changes.

### Energy Flow Through an Ecosystem

Energy enters an ecosystem as sunlight. Producers capture it. It flows through consumers. At each step, energy is lost.

**Energy flow in an ecosystem:**

1. **Sunlight:** 10,000 units of energy per square meter per day (approximate)
2. **Producers:** Plants capture about 1% of sunlight (100 units) through photosynthesis. The rest is reflected or absorbed as heat.
3. **Primary consumers (herbivores):** Eat plants. They gain about 10% of plant energy (10 units). The rest is lost as heat, movement, and waste.
4. **Secondary consumers (carnivores):** Eat herbivores. They gain about 10% of herbivore energy (1 unit). The rest is lost.
5. **Tertiary consumers (top predators):** Eat carnivores. They gain about 10% of carnivore energy (0.1 units).

**The 10% rule:** At each trophic level (step in the food chain), about 90% of energy is lost and 10% is transferred to the next level.

### Why Energy Transfers Are Inefficient

Energy is lost at each step because:

1. **Heat:** Organisms generate heat as they move, think, and maintain body temperature. This energy is released to the environment and cannot be used by the next organism.
2. **Waste:** Organisms do not eat 100% of what they kill. Some is inedible (bones, shells, skin). Some is excreted.
3. **Growth and repair:** Organisms use energy to grow and repair themselves. This energy is not transferred to predators that eat them.

**Consequence:** An ecosystem can support far fewer top predators than plants. For every 1 lion, there must be ~10 zebras, and ~100 plants providing food.

### Food Chains and Food Webs

A food chain is a linear sequence of organisms, each eating the one before it:

Plant → Herbivore → Carnivore → Top Predator

A food web is a complex network of food chains, showing all the connections:

Plants are eaten by herbivores, which are eaten by multiple carnivores. Carnivores eat multiple herbivores. Decomposers break down all of them.

Real ecosystems are food webs, not chains. Removing one organism affects many others through multiple pathways.

### Example: A Grassland Ecosystem

**Producers:** Grasses capture sunlight and create chemical energy (glucose).

**Primary consumers:** Grasshoppers eat grass. Mice eat grass seeds.

**Secondary consumers:** Birds eat grasshoppers and mice. Snakes eat mice.

**Tertiary consumers:** Hawks eat birds and snakes.

**Decomposers:** Bacteria and fungi break down dead organisms and return nutrients to soil.

**Energy flow:** Sunlight → Grass (1% captured) → Grasshoppers and mice (10% of grass energy) → Birds and snakes (10% of herbivore energy) → Hawks (10% of carnivore energy)

**Nutrient cycling:** When organisms die, decomposers break them down. Nitrogen, phosphorus, and other nutrients return to soil. New plants grow using these nutrients.

## Part 2: Populations and Population Dynamics

A population is a group of organisms of the same species living in the same area. Populations grow, stabilize, or decline based on available resources.

### Carrying Capacity

The carrying capacity is the maximum population size an environment can support indefinitely. It is determined by available resources (food, water, shelter, space).

**Example:** A forest can support 100 deer if there is enough vegetation to feed them year-round. That is the carrying capacity. If the population grows to 150 deer, there is not enough food. Deer starve, and the population declines back toward 100.

Carrying capacity changes with environmental conditions:
- A wet year increases plant growth, increasing carrying capacity
- A drought decreases plant growth, decreasing carrying capacity
- Predators control herbivore populations, effectively reducing carrying capacity for that species

### Logistic Growth

When a population starts small and resources are abundant, it grows exponentially (doubling regularly). As it approaches carrying capacity, growth slows. At carrying capacity, growth stops.

**Growth phases:**

1. **Lag phase:** Small population, slow growth (organisms are adapting to the environment)
2. **Exponential phase:** Population doubles regularly (plenty of resources, minimal competition)
3. **Stationary phase:** Population reaches carrying capacity, growth stops
4. **Death phase:** If resources become severely limited, population declines (starvation, disease)

Understanding these phases helps you predict population change.

### Predator-Prey Dynamics

Predators and prey populations are linked:

1. **Prey population increases:** With abundant food (plants), the prey population grows
2. **Predator population increases:** With abundant prey, the predator population grows
3. **Prey population crashes:** Predators eat prey faster than prey reproduces; prey becomes scarce
4. **Predator population crashes:** Without prey, predators starve
5. **Prey population increases again:** With fewer predators, prey recovers

This creates a cycle:

**Year 1:** Prey abundant → Predators increase
**Year 2:** Predators peak → Prey crashes
**Year 3:** Predators starve → Prey recovers
**Year 4:** Cycle repeats

The cycle is typically 3–4 years for most predator-prey systems.

### Example: Lynx and Hare Populations

In Canada, lynx (predators) and hares (prey) populations have been tracked for over 200 years:

- **Hare peaks:** Abundant plants support many hares
- **Lynx increase:** One year later, lynx increase (delayed response as lynx raise young)
- **Hare crash:** Lynx hunting reduces hare population
- **Lynx starvation:** Without prey, lynx starve and population declines
- **Hare recovery:** Without predators, hares rebound
- **Cycle repeats:** Every ~10 years

This is a natural cycle. Understanding it helps you predict when game animals will be abundant or scarce.

### What Limits Population Growth?

Several factors limit population growth:

**Density-dependent factors** (affect populations more as density increases):
- Food scarcity (more organisms compete for the same food)
- Space limitation (crowding limits reproduction)
- Disease (spreads faster in dense populations)
- Waste accumulation (toxins build up in dense populations)

**Density-independent factors** (affect populations regardless of density):
- Extreme weather (drought, harsh winter)
- Natural disaster (floods, wildfires)
- Seasonal changes

Post-collapse survival requires understanding both. Drought affects all populations equally. Overcrowding affects food-limited populations more.

## Part 3: Succession — How Ecosystems Change Over Time

Succession is the process by which an ecosystem changes over time. Understanding it helps you know what will happen to land over years or decades.

### Primary Succession: From Bare Ground

Primary succession happens on bare ground (volcanic landscape, cleared land, abandoned building site).

**Stage 1: Pioneer community (0–10 years)**
- Fast-growing, hardy plants colonize (lichens, grasses, mosses)
- Few animals
- Organic matter builds up slowly
- Soil begins to develop

**Stage 2: Early successional community (10–50 years)**
- Shrubs arrive, outcompeting early plants
- Herbivores and insect communities expand
- Soil deepens and enriches
- Trees begin to establish

**Stage 3: Mid-successional community (50–200 years)**
- Trees dominate
- Forest canopy develops
- Shade-tolerant understory plants arrive
- Full diversity of animals present

**Stage 4: Climax community (200+ years)**
- Stable forest ecosystem
- Dominated by shade-tolerant species
- Slow change thereafter
- Maximum biodiversity and productivity

### Secondary Succession: Recovery After Disturbance

Secondary succession happens when an existing ecosystem is disturbed (forest fire, farm abandonment, logging).

**Stages are similar to primary succession but faster:**
- Pioneer shrubs and trees arrive within years (not decades)
- Seed sources are nearby, so diversity recovers faster
- Soil is already developed, so growth is faster
- Climax community is reached in 50–100 years (not 200+)

### How Succession Affects Food Availability

Early succession (pioneer and early communities) is often more productive than climax communities:
- Pioneer plants grow fast and put energy into reproduction (many seeds = food)
- Early animals are diverse and abundant (many species means abundant game)
- Logging or fire creates early successional conditions, temporarily increasing food

As succession progresses:
- Forest matures, large herbivores decline
- Food becomes more concentrated in trees (nuts, fruits) rather than herbaceous plants
- Human populations that depend on early successional habitats must manage land (burn, clear, graze) to maintain food production

### Example: Forest After Abandonment

A farm is abandoned in temperate forest:

**Years 0–5:** Bare field. Fast-growing grasses and weeds colonize. Seeds are easy to harvest. Small herbivores are abundant (mice, rabbits).

**Years 5–20:** Shrubs arrive (brambles, saplings). Fruit production increases. Medium herbivores increase (deer). Large open areas are gone.

**Years 20–50:** Trees shade out shrubs. Herbaceous plants decline. Large herbivores (deer, elk) move in to eat remaining shrubs and browse lower branches. Nut-bearing trees begin producing.

**Years 50+:** Mature forest. Shrub layer disappears. Herbivores decline. Food becomes concentrated in acorns, hickory nuts, and forest edges.

Understanding this helps you know when a field will be productive for crops (first 10 years), when game will be abundant (years 5–30), and when the forest will need management to remain productive for humans.

## Part 4: Nutrient Cycling

Energy flows through ecosystems (one-way), but nutrients cycle (round and round).

### The Carbon Cycle

Carbon cycles between the atmosphere, organisms, and soil:

1. **Photosynthesis:** Plants take CO2 from air and convert it to glucose (plant matter)
2. **Eating:** Animals eat plants, incorporating carbon into animal tissue
3. **Respiration:** Plants and animals respire, releasing CO2 back to the air
4. **Death and decomposition:** Dead organisms are broken down, returning carbon to soil
5. **Fossilization (slow cycle):** Some carbon is buried and becomes fossil fuel

**For survival:** Carbon cycling means plants grow from atmospheric CO2. Cutting trees removes carbon from the ecosystem temporarily (until new trees grow). Burning biomass releases CO2 quickly; slow decomposition releases it slowly.

### The Nitrogen Cycle

Nitrogen is essential for proteins and DNA. It cycles between the air, soil, organisms, and back:

1. **Nitrogen fixation:** Bacteria convert atmospheric nitrogen (N2) into ammonia (NH3), a form plants can use
2. **Uptake:** Plants absorb ammonia and produce proteins
3. **Eating:** Animals eat plants and accumulate nitrogen
4. **Excretion and death:** Animals excrete nitrogen-rich waste or die, returning nitrogen to soil
5. **Decomposition:** Bacteria break down waste and dead matter, releasing nitrogen
6. **Denitrification:** Some bacteria convert ammonia back to atmospheric nitrogen

**For survival:** Nitrogen-fixing bacteria (in legume root nodules) enrich soil. Rotating legumes with other crops maintains soil nitrogen without artificial fertilizers. Animal manure recycles nitrogen. Understanding nitrogen cycling helps you maintain soil fertility.

### The Phosphorus Cycle

Phosphorus is essential for bones, teeth, and DNA. It has a simpler cycle than nitrogen:

1. **Weathering:** Rocks containing phosphorus break down, releasing phosphorus to soil
2. **Uptake:** Plants absorb phosphorus and accumulate it in leaves and seeds
3. **Eating:** Animals eat plants and accumulate phosphorus in bones
4. **Decomposition:** Dead organisms and manure return phosphorus to soil
5. **Runoff:** Phosphorus washes into rivers and eventually ocean (a slow loss from land)

**For survival:** Phosphorus is often limiting (in shortest supply). Recycling manure and preserving bone-rich waste helps maintain phosphorus. Understanding phosphorus helps you manage soil fertility.

## Ecology in Practice: Three Survival Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Sustainable Fishing

A river has fish. You want to harvest them sustainably (forever, not just one season).

**Without understanding ecology:** Fish every day, catch as many as possible. Year 1: abundant fish. Year 2: fewer fish. Year 3: fish population crashes. No more fish.

**With understanding ecology:**
1. Estimate the population size (fishing experiments, counting)
2. Estimate the growth rate (what percentage of the population reproduces each year?)
3. Harvest only the surplus (about 20–30% of the population, depending on growth rate)
4. Monitor: if catches decline, reduce harvest
5. The population stabilizes at maximum sustainable yield

Example: If fish population grows 30% per year and you harvest 30%, the population remains stable.

### Scenario 2: Predicting Game Availability

Predator-prey cycles mean game animals are sometimes abundant and sometimes scarce.

**Without understanding ecology:** Hunt heavily when game is abundant. When game crashes, you have nothing to eat.

**With understanding ecology:**
1. Track game populations over years (notes from hunters, direct observation)
2. Recognize the pattern (peaks every 3–4 years usually)
3. Prepare during abundant years (preserve meat, save energy)
4. Reduce hunting during crash years
5. Plan food sources accordingly (agriculture, storage)

This helps you smooth out the boom-bust cycle.

### Scenario 3: Managing Land for Food Production

You have land. You want to know how to manage it for maximum food.

**Short-term (years 0–10):**
- Clear land and farm (early succession produces abundant crops)
- Rotate crops (maintains soil fertility)
- Leave some areas wild (wildlife refuge provides hunting)

**Medium-term (years 10–50):**
- As forest regrows, shift to hunting and nuts (oak, hickory)
- Manage forest (selective cutting, burning) to maintain productive early succession
- Perennial crops (fruit trees, nut trees) become productive

**Long-term (years 50+):**
- Established forest (climax community) provides stable but lower food production
- Maintain openings (fields, meadows) through management to keep early successional habitat productive

Understanding succession helps you plan land use for decades.

## Verification

**Understanding ecology means:**

1. You can draw a food chain and explain energy loss at each step
2. You understand carrying capacity and can estimate whether your area can support your population
3. You can predict predator-prey cycles and plan accordingly
4. You understand nutrient cycling and can manage soil fertility
5. You understand succession and can predict what will happen to land over time
6. You can estimate sustainable harvest rates (fishing, hunting, logging)

If you can reason through these examples using ecology principles, you understand the concept.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Assuming energy flows one direction, not nutrients:** Energy enters as sunlight and leaves as heat. Nutrients cycle. Do not confuse the two.

2. **Thinking an ecosystem can support unlimited population:** Every ecosystem has a carrying capacity. Exceed it and the population crashes.

3. **Underestimating energy loss in food chains:** 90% of energy is lost at each step. Top predators require huge areas (low density naturally).

4. **Forgetting that ecosystems change:** Succession is inevitable. Managing land means controlling succession (burning, cutting) to maintain desired stages.

5. **Assuming all species are equally important:** Keystone species (apex predators, nutrient cyclers) have disproportionate impact. Remove them and the ecosystem collapses.

6. **Thinking predators are only enemies:** Predators regulate prey populations, preventing overexploitation. Without predators, prey often overgrazed and crash.

7. **Underestimating time scales:** Forest recovery takes decades to centuries. Planning must account for this.

8. **Assuming human-managed ecosystems are more stable:** Monocultures (single crop) are fragile. Diverse ecosystems are resilient. Complexity provides stability.
