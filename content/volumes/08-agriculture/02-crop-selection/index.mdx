## Overview

Crop selection is a strategic decision, not a romantic one. You are not growing crops for enjoyment; you are growing crops to keep your settlement alive.

This chapter teaches you to:
1. **Prioritize calories.** A settlement starves if it lacks calories, not if it lacks interesting flavors.
2. **Match crops to climate and soil.** Growing wheat in a rainforest will fail. Growing rice in a desert will fail.
3. **Plan for multi-year survival.** Some crops feed you this year; others store for next winter.
4. **Diversify strategically.** Multiple crops reduce risk (if one crop fails, others sustain you).

## Prerequisites

Understand your soil type (Vol 10, Ch 1). Know your climate (temperature, rainfall, frost dates).

## Part 1: Calorie Priority

### Why Calories Matter

A human needs roughly 2000-2500 calories per day to survive. A settlement of 200 people needs 400,000-500,000 calories per day (146-182 million calories per year).

Growing food that looks nice but provides few calories is a luxury. Post-collapse, luxury is a risk.

### High-Calorie Crops

These crops provide the most calories per unit of land:

**Grains (highest calorie density):**
- **Wheat:** 1200-1600 calories per 500g (uncooked). Grows in temperate climates. Stores for years if kept dry.
- **Maize (corn):** 1500-1800 calories per 500g. Grows in warm climates. Stores well. Requires more water than wheat.
- **Rice:** 1800-2000 calories per 500g (cooked). Grows in wet climates. Labor-intensive (flooding, harvesting). Stores well.
- **Barley:** 1200-1400 calories per 500g. Hardier than wheat; grows in cooler climates.
- **Rye:** 1200-1400 calories per 500g. Very hardy; grows in poor soil and cold climates.
- **Oats:** 1200-1400 calories per 500g. Nutritious; grows in cool, wet climates.

**Legumes (protein + calories):**
- **Beans (dried):** 900-1100 calories per 500g. Fixes nitrogen. Stores for years. Essential protein source.
- **Lentils:** 1000-1200 calories per 500g. Stores well. Less labor than beans.
- **Peas (dried):** 900-1100 calories per 500g. Fixes nitrogen. Stores well.

**Tubers and roots (reliable, storable):**
- **Potatoes:** 400-500 calories per 500g (cooked). Extremely high yield per area. Stores 6+ months. Requires cool storage.
- **Cassava:** 800-900 calories per 500g (cooked). Grows in poor soil. Drought-tolerant. Stores reasonably if kept dry.
- **Turnips:** 200-300 calories per 500g. Very hardy. Stores well in root cellars.

**Calorie comparison (per acre per year):**
- Wheat: 10-12 million calories
- Maize: 14-16 million calories
- Rice (wet): 16-20 million calories
- Potatoes: 16-20 million calories
- Beans: 6-8 million calories (but provides protein, which grain does not)

**Strategic composition:** A settlement should plant:
- 60-70% grains (wheat, barley, maize, or rice depending on climate).
- 20-30% legumes (beans, lentils, peas) for protein and nitrogen fixation.
- 10-20% tubers (potatoes, cassava) for storage and diversification.

### Low-Calorie Crops (Prioritize Later)

These are nutritious but calorie-poor:
- Leafy greens (lettuce, kale, spinach): 20-50 calories per 500g. Valuable for vitamins but not calories.
- Squash and pumpkins: 150-200 calories per 500g. Some value but low yield.
- Carrots and beets: 150-200 calories per 500g. Good for storage but low yield.
- Fruit: 200-400 calories per 500g (varies). Valuable for nutrition but not essential for survival.

**When to grow these:** After you have secured 3+ years of grain storage and your settlement is stable.

## Part 2: Matching Crops to Climate

### Climate Zones and Crop Selection

**Cold temperate (frost every winter, &lt;0°C):**
- **Best:** Rye, barley, oats, wheat, potatoes, turnips, beans (short-season varieties).
- **Avoid:** Maize (needs longer growing season), cassava (frost kills it).

**Temperate (frost some winters, 0-10°C average winter):**
- **Best:** Wheat, barley, oats, potatoes, beans, lentils, peas.
- **Also good:** Maize (if 120+ frost-free days).

**Warm temperate (rare frost, 10-20°C average winter):**
- **Best:** Wheat, maize, beans, peas, potatoes, cassava.
- **Also good:** Rice (if you can create paddies).

**Subtropical (no frost, 15-25°C average winter):**
- **Best:** Maize, rice, cassava, peanuts, sweet potatoes, beans.
- **Also good:** Wheat (winter crop), barley.

**Tropical (no frost ever, warm year-round):**
- **Best:** Rice, maize, cassava, yams, sweet potatoes, coconuts, bananas (calories), legumes.
- **Challenge:** Year-round growing but also year-round pests and disease.

### Determining Your Frost Date

Your **frost date** determines what you can grow. Post-collapse, you may not have precise data, but you can estimate:

1. Observe: When does the first hard frost arrive in fall? When does the last frost occur in spring?
2. Record: Track this for 2-3 years to establish a pattern.
3. Calculate: Growing season = days between last spring frost and first fall frost.

**Growing season requirements:**
- Winter wheat: Plant in fall, harvest in summer (9+ months).
- Spring wheat, barley: 3-4 months of frost-free weather.
- Maize: 4-5 months (120-150 frost-free days minimum).
- Beans: 3-4 months.
- Potatoes: 3-4 months.
- Rice: 4-6 months.

If your frost-free season is only 3 months, maize (needing 4-5 months) will not mature.

### Rainfall and Water Requirements

**High rainfall (1000+ mm/year):**
- Rice thrives.
- Wheat can grow but may develop mold (use resistant varieties).
- Crops prone to fungal disease (monitor closely).

**Moderate rainfall (500-1000 mm/year):**
- Wheat, barley, oats ideal.
- Maize doable if rainfall is 600+ mm/year.
- Beans, peas, lentils do well.

**Low rainfall (&lt;500 mm/year):**
- Drought-tolerant crops only: barley, rye, cassava, sorghum (if available).
- Require irrigation if you want high-yield crops.
- Fallowing (leaving land unplanted) is necessary to conserve soil moisture.

**Extreme rainfall (&gt;1500 mm/year):**
- Rice is the obvious choice (grows in wet conditions).
- Other crops require excellent drainage (raised beds, ridging).

## Part 3: Soil-Crop Matching

### Soil Type and Crop Suitability

**Sandy soil:**
- **Best:** Rye, barley, beans, potatoes, turnips.
- **Challenging:** Wheat (prefers clay), rice (needs water retention).
- **Solution:** Add compost to improve water retention.

**Clay soil:**
- **Best:** Wheat, barley, oats, beans.
- **Challenging:** Potatoes (clay compaction causes deformities), beans (poor drainage).
- **Solution:** Add sand and compost to improve drainage.

**Loam (ideal):**
- **Everything grows well.** This is the easiest soil to work with.

**Acidic soil (pH &lt;6):**
- **Best:** Rye, oats, beans, potatoes.
- **Avoid:** Wheat, barley (prefer pH 6.5+).
- **Solution:** Add lime to raise pH if needed.

**Alkaline soil (pH &gt;7.5):**
- **Best:** Wheat, barley, lentils.
- **Challenging:** Potatoes (prefer pH 5.5-7).
- **Solution:** Add sulfur or acidifying compost if needed.

## Part 4: Multi-Year Crop Planning

### Year 1 (Emergency Year)

Focus entirely on calories. Plant:
- 70% grains (whatever grain grows best in your climate).
- 30% legumes (for nitrogen and protein).

**Avoid:** Fruit trees (won't produce for years), root crops needing storage (potatoes need 3 months to grow), anything that doesn't feed you this year.

**Goal:** Harvest enough to feed your settlement for 1 year + save seed for next year.

### Year 2-3 (Consolidation)

Add storage crops:
- 50% grains (reduce from year 1 as you have more security).
- 20% legumes.
- 20% potatoes or cassava (storage crops).
- 10% other crops (leafy greens, squash for nutrition).

**Goal:** Diversify food sources and build 1-2 years of grain storage.

### Year 4+ (Stability)

Once you have 2+ years of grain stored:
- 40% grains.
- 20% legumes.
- 20% storage crops (potatoes, turnips).
- 10% vegetables and leafy greens (nutrition).
- 10% fruit or specialty crops.

## Part 5: Diversity and Risk Management

### Why Crop Diversity Matters

Monoculture (growing one crop) is efficient but risky. If that crop fails (disease, pest, drought), the entire harvest is lost.

**Diversification strategy:**
- **Year-to-year:** Plant different crops each year (crop rotation, Vol 10, Ch 5).
- **Area-to-area:** Do not plant all wheat in one field. Split across multiple fields so a localized disease or pest affects only part of the crop.
- **Crop-to-crop:** Grow multiple crop types so if one fails, others sustain you.

### Pest and Disease Risk

Different crops have different pests:
- Wheat: Hessian fly, rust fungus.
- Beans: Bean beetles, rust fungus.
- Potatoes: Blight, Colorado potato beetle.
- Maize: Corn borers, smut fungus.

Growing multiple crops means pests affecting one crop do not affect all.

### Climate Risk

A year of drought may favor barley (drought-tolerant) but kill wheat (needs water). A year of excess rain may favor rice but cause mold on stored wheat.

Growing multiple crops hedges against climate variability.

### Seed Saving

Always save seed from this year's harvest for next year's planting. This ensures:
- Genetic adaptation to your specific climate and soil.
- Independence from external seed sources.
- Adaptation over generations (your seeds become better suited to local conditions).

**Seed saving strategy:** Save 10-20% of your best harvest as seed. Store in cool, dry place.

## Part 6: Labor Considerations

### Planting and Harvest Timing

All crops mature at once. This creates labor bottlenecks:
- **Peak planting:** Spring (everyone plants simultaneously for 2-4 weeks).
- **Peak harvest:** Fall (everyone harvests simultaneously for 4-8 weeks).

A settlement of 200 people can plant 50-100 hectares in a 4-week window with 50% of adults working full-time in fields.

**Planning:** Know how much land you can plant and harvest with your labor force, then grow that amount (not more).

### Long-Cycle vs. Short-Cycle Crops

**Long-cycle crops (4-6 months):** Wheat, maize, rice.
- Planted in spring/early summer, harvested in fall/winter.
- Require sustained field management (weeding, irrigation).
- Single harvest per year.

**Short-cycle crops (2-3 months):** Beans (snap varieties), peas, leafy greens, squash.
- Can be planted multiple times in one year (succession planting).
- Useful for filling gaps in the calendar.
- More labor-intensive (multiple plantings, harvests).

**Strategy:** Use long-cycle crops for bulk calories; use short-cycle crops for supplemental food and fresh eating.

## Part 7: Seed Sourcing and Varieties

### Seed Acquisition (Year 1 Post-Collapse)

Your first year, you need seeds from somewhere:
- **Existing stores:** If there are seed banks or agricultural suppliers, access them.
- **Salvage:** Seeds from abandoned gardens or grain stores.
- **Trade:** Exchange with other settlements for seeds.
- **Grow:** If you have time before the season, grow quick crops (beans, lettuce) for seed.

### Heirloom vs. Hybrid Seeds

**Heirloom:** Old varieties that breed true (seeds saved from this year produce next year's crop reliably).
- Advantage: Breed true, can be saved for next year.
- Disadvantage: Often lower yield, less uniform, may not be adapted to your climate.

**Hybrid:** Cross-bred varieties, very uniform, high-yield.
- Advantage: Often higher yield, good disease resistance.
- Disadvantage: Seeds saved from hybrids do not breed true (next generation is weaker). You must buy new seed each year.

**Post-collapse recommendation:** Prioritize heirloom varieties. You cannot buy seed every year; you must save your own.

### Variety Selection

For each crop, research 3-5 varieties:
- **Early-maturing:** Matures in shorter season (good if your season is short).
- **High-yield:** Produces more calories per hectare.
- **Disease-resistant:** Resistant to common diseases in your region.
- **Storage:** Stores well without spoiling (important for grains, potatoes).

Choose varieties that combine these traits, prioritizing for your climate.

## Verification

Your crop selection is sound if:
1. **Calorie crops:** 70%+ of planted area is high-calorie crops (grains + legumes + tubers).
2. **Climate match:** Crops match your frost-free season and rainfall.
3. **Soil suitability:** Crops are suited to your soil type (or you have amended the soil).
4. **Labor capacity:** Total planted area fits your settlement's planting and harvest labor.
5. **Diversity:** You are growing at least 3 different crop types.
6. **Seed security:** You save 10-20% of harvest as seed for next year.
7. **Multi-year plan:** You can articulate a 3-year crop plan with specific crops and areas.

## Common Mistakes

**Growing for flavor instead of calories.** Interesting food is a luxury. Prioritize calories; add flavor once survival is secure.

**Planting crops unsuited to your climate.** Maize in a 90-day frost-free season will not mature. Rice in a desert will not grow. Match crops to climate.

**Monoculture.** One crop fails, the entire harvest is lost. Grow diversity.

**Saving poor seed.** Save seed from your best, healthiest plants. Saving seed from weak plants creates a weak next generation.

**Ignoring labor capacity.** You cannot plant 200 hectares if you only have 50 people to plant. Know your limits.

**Not rotating crops.** Growing the same crop in the same field every year depletes soil and builds pest populations. Rotate.
