## Overview

Most post-collapse agriculture depends on rainfall. But rainfall is unpredictable. Droughts happen. Irrigation lets you grow crops during dry periods and increase yields during wet periods.

This chapter teaches you to:
1. **Assess water availability** — springs, streams, wells, ponds.
2. **Design irrigation systems** — channels, ditches, and pipes.
3. **Apply water efficiently** — surface, furrow, and drip methods.
4. **Schedule irrigation** — when to water, how much, and based on what.

Post-collapse irrigation is simple: water flows downhill from a source to crops. No electricity, no pumps, just gravity.

## Prerequisites

Understand soil (Vol 10, Ch 1). Understand water flow and hydraulics (Vol 28: Hydraulics & Water Engineering, if available).

## Part 1: Water Sources

### Types of Water Sources

**Surface water (streams, rivers):**
- Reliable if water flows year-round.
- Advantage: Large volume, gravity flow possible.
- Disadvantage: Other settlements may depend on the same stream; taking water may affect downstream users.

**Springs:**
- Water emerges from the ground naturally.
- Advantage: Continuous flow, often clean.
- Disadvantage: Smaller volume than streams; site-dependent.

**Shallow wells (hand-dug, 5-10 meters):**
- Dig a hole until water appears (groundwater table).
- Advantage: Can be dug by hand; water is available year-round.
- Disadvantage: Labor-intensive; requires regular maintenance.

**Deep wells (dug or drilled, 10+ meters):**
- Groundwater from deeper sources.
- Advantage: More reliable than shallow wells; less contamination risk.
- Disadvantage: Much harder to dig; may require specialized tools.

**Ponds and cisterns:**
- Collect and store water (rainfall, runoff).
- Advantage: Can be built at any location.
- Disadvantage: Water quality may be poor; seasonal (empty in dry season).

**Rainwater harvest:**
- Roof gutters and downspouts direct rain into cisterns.
- Advantage: Free water during rainy season.
- Disadvantage: Not reliable in dry season.

### Assessing Water Availability

**Year-round flow:** Is water available year-round, or only during rainy season?
- Year-round flow (springs, deep wells, streams) enables continuous irrigation.
- Seasonal water (ponds, cisterns) can irrigate only during/after rainy season.

**Volume:** How much water does the source provide?
- **Measurement:** Watch a stream for 1 hour. Measure the width, depth, and speed of flow.
- **Rough calculation:** Width (meters) × depth (meters) × speed (0.5-1.5 m/s for a moderate stream) = cubic meters per second.
- **Example:** A stream 2 meters wide, 0.5 meters deep, flowing at 1 m/s = 1 cubic meter per second = 86,400 cubic meters per day = enough for 20-50 hectares of irrigation (depending on crop and climate).

**Seasonal variability:** How much does flow change between wet and dry seasons?
- A stream flowing in wet season may be dry in summer.
- Springs usually remain constant.
- Plan irrigation systems for the minimum expected flow.

### Water Rights

Assess whether you have legal or customary rights to the water:
- **On your land:** Water on your land is yours (subject to local laws).
- **Adjacent streams:** Usually, you have limited rights (cannot divert entire stream, must leave water for downstream users).
- **Shared water:** If multiple settlements depend on the same source, negotiate usage (Vol 25: Trade, Money & Markets).

## Part 2: Irrigation Systems

### Gravity-Fed Channels

The simplest irrigation system uses gravity to move water from a high source to lower crops.

**Basic system:**
1. **Intake:** Where water enters the system (a small dam, a spring box, or a stream diversion).
2. **Channel:** A ditch or pipe that carries water downhill to the crops.
3. **Distribution:** Water is diverted from the channel to individual fields.
4. **Return:** Excess water drains to a pond or back to the stream.

**Channel construction:**
- **Gradient:** The channel must have a slight downward slope (1-2% gradient, or 1 meter drop per 50-100 meters of length).
- **Too steep:** Water flows too fast, erodes the channel, and is hard to control.
- **Too flat:** Water barely flows; channel fills with sediment.
- **Width and depth:** 0.5-1.5 meters wide, 0.3-0.5 meters deep (depends on water volume).
- **Material:** Earth (dug out), stone-lined, or wood-lined. Earth is cheapest but requires maintenance (cleaning sediment annually).

**Diversion structure:**
- At points where water enters a field, build a simple gate or valve to control flow.
- Gate: A board that slides into grooves, controlling water depth and flow.
- Valve: A wooden or clay plug with an opening (post-collapse, usually a board-and-groove gate).

**Example system (20-hectare farm):**
- Source: A spring at elevation 150 meters.
- Crops: At elevation 100 meters (50 meters drop).
- Channel: 5 km long (1% gradient = 50 meters drop over 5 km).
- Width: 1 meter; depth: 0.3 meters.
- Gates: 10 gates dividing the channel to supply 10 fields (2 hectares each).

### Surface Irrigation (Flooding)

Water flows across the entire field, soaking the soil.

**How it works:**
1. Water enters the field through a gate or ditch.
2. The entire field is flooded (or nearly flooded).
3. Water soaks into the soil and drains away.

**Best for:**
- Flat terrain (sloping fields are hard to flood evenly).
- Rice paddies (built with levees to hold water).
- Soil with slow drainage (clay, loam).

**Advantages:**
- Simple (no complex channels needed).
- Supplies large amounts of water quickly.
- Good for crops needing lots of water (rice).

**Disadvantages:**
- Wastes water (much runs off before soaking in).
- Flooding can cause waterlogging (too much water kills roots).
- Hard to control on sloping terrain.

### Furrow Irrigation

Water runs in shallow channels (furrows) between rows of crops.

**How it works:**
1. Before planting, plow furrows (shallow channels) between crop rows.
2. Water enters one end of the furrow and flows down the row.
3. Water soaks sideways into the root zone of crops.

**Best for:**
- Row crops (beans, corn, squash).
- Sloping terrain (easier to manage than flat fields).
- Soil with moderate drainage (loamy soil).

**Advantages:**
- More water-efficient than surface flooding (water stays in the furrow; less runoff).
- Easy to control (just turn water on/off at the top).
- Easy to extend (as many furrows as you have water for).

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires preparation (plowing furrows before each season).
- Uneven watering (water at the top of the furrow gets more water than the bottom).
- Doesn't work well on very steep slopes (water rushes down).

**Implementation:**
- Furrow length: 50-200 meters (longer furrows waste more water to runoff at the end).
- Spacing: 0.5-1 meter between furrows (depends on crop spacing).
- Water entry: A pipe or ditch brings water to the head of each furrow.

### Drip Irrigation (Low-Tech)

Water drips slowly from holes in a pipe, soaking the soil near plant roots.

**How it works:**
1. A main line carries water from the source.
2. Branch lines run along crop rows.
3. Small holes (1-2 mm) in the branch line release water slowly.
4. Water drips onto the soil, soaking in near the roots.

**Best for:**
- Vegetable gardens (small to medium scale).
- Water-scarce regions (drip is very efficient).
- Row crops and individual plants.

**Advantages:**
- Very water-efficient (little runoff, little evaporation).
- Can be very precise (water only where plants are).
- Works on slopes (water doesn't run off).

**Disadvantages:**
- More complex to set up than surface or furrow.
- Requires maintenance (holes clog with sediment).
- Pipes are permanent infrastructure.

**Low-tech drip system:**
- **Main line:** Gravity-fed pipe from the water source.
- **Branch lines:** Small bamboo or clay tubes running to each row.
- **Drip emitters:** Simple holes drilled in the tubes, or clay pots buried upside-down in the soil (clay slowly releases water as soil dries).

**Clay pot irrigation (simplest):**
- Unglazed clay pots (terracotta) are buried halfway into the soil.
- Water is poured into the pot daily.
- Clay slowly releases water to the soil as it dries.
- Works for individual plants or small gardens.
- Advantage: No manufactured materials needed; simple.
- Disadvantage: Only suitable for small-scale growing.

## Part 3: Calculating Water Needs

### Daily Water Requirement

Different crops need different amounts of water.

**Typical daily water need (in ideal climate):**
- **Rice paddies:** 5-10 mm per day (1 cm of standing water needs replacement frequently).
- **Wheat:** 2-4 mm per day.
- **Beans:** 2-3 mm per day.
- **Corn:** 3-5 mm per day.
- **Vegetables:** 2-4 mm per day.

**Calculation example (corn, 10 hectares):**
- Water need: 4 mm per day = 0.004 meters per day.
- 10 hectares = 100,000 square meters.
- Daily water: 100,000 m² × 0.004 m = 400 cubic meters per day.
- Hourly: 400 cubic meters ÷ 24 hours = 16.7 cubic meters per hour.
- Your spring must provide at least 16.7 cubic meters per hour for continuous irrigation.

### Seasonal Water Need

Water need varies by season. Plants need more water during hot months, less during cool months.

**Rough seasonal pattern:**
- Spring (cool): 60% of peak need.
- Summer (hot): 100% of peak need.
- Fall (cool): 70% of peak need.
- Winter (dormant): 0-20% of peak need.

**Planning:** Design your irrigation system for peak summer need. In spring and fall, you have excess water; in winter, you may have none.

### Irrigation Scheduling

**When to water:**
- **Soil moisture test:** Dig 10 cm into the soil. If it is dry, water. If it is moist, wait.
- **Plant signs:** Wilting indicates water stress (too dry). Yellowing indicates waterlogging (too wet).
- **Timing:** Water early morning (before heat) or evening (as temperature drops). Avoid midday (water evaporates before soaking in).

**How much to water:**
- **Sandy soil:** More frequent, smaller amounts (water drains quickly).
- **Clay soil:** Less frequent, larger amounts (water stays in soil longer).
- **Target:** Moisten the root zone (top 20-30 cm for vegetables; top 30-50 cm for grains).

**Frequency:**
- **Sandy soil:** Every 1-3 days in summer, less in spring/fall.
- **Clay soil:** Every 3-7 days in summer, less in spring/fall.
- **Observation:** Adjust based on plant health and soil moisture.

## Part 4: Water Management and Conservation

### Reducing Water Loss

**Evaporation:**
- Mulch crops (4-5 cm of straw, leaves) reduces evaporation by 50%.
- Shade structures reduce direct sun exposure (useful for vegetables in extreme heat).

**Runoff:**
- Build terraces on slopes to slow water flow.
- Use furrows or drip irrigation instead of surface flooding.

**Deep percolation (water going too deep):**
- Clay soil naturally prevents this.
- Sandy soil: Add mulch or increase organic matter to hold water.

**Seepage from channels:**
- Line channels with clay (4-5 cm), compacted.
- Or use cloth/plastic sheeting (if available).
- Unlined earth channels lose 20-30% of water to seepage.

### Sharing Water

If multiple settlers depend on the same water source:
- **Divide by time:** Each settler gets the water for part of the day/week (rotation system).
- **Divide by volume:** Each settler gets a percentage of flow (measured at the intake).
- **Seasonal priority:** In dry season, give priority to crops needing more water (rice before wheat).

**Documentation:** Keep records of who uses how much water and when. Disputes over water are common; clear rules prevent conflict.

## Part 5: Maintenance

### Channel Maintenance

**Seasonal tasks:**
- **Spring:** Clear sediment from channels (silt accumulates over time).
- **Summer:** Repair breaks/leaks.
- **Fall:** Remove dead plants, leaves.
- **Winter:** Plan upgrades, repairs.

**Annual maintenance:** 10-20 person-days per 5 km of channel (varies by size and condition).

### System Repairs

**Common problems:**
- **Leaks:** Find by walking the channel. Repair with clay, rocks, or compacted earth.
- **Breakage:** Channels fail during heavy rain or freeze-thaw. Repair breaks immediately.
- **Clogging:** Debris clogs gates and pipes. Clean regularly.

## Verification

Your irrigation system is sound if:
1. **Water source:** You have identified a reliable year-round source (spring, stream, well).
2. **Volume:** The source provides enough water for your crops in peak season.
3. **System design:** You have designed channels or pipes for gravity flow (1-2% gradient).
4. **Water efficiency:** You are using surface, furrow, or drip irrigation suited to your crops.
5. **Scheduling:** You water based on soil moisture (not just on a fixed schedule).
6. **Conservation:** You minimize losses (mulch, reduce runoff, line channels).
7. **Maintenance:** You have a plan for seasonal maintenance (cleaning, repairs).

## Common Mistakes

**Overwatering.** Too much water causes waterlogging (roots rot). Water only when soil is dry 10 cm deep.

**Ignoring water source limitations.** Your spring provides 5 cubic meters per day, but you try to irrigate 20 hectares needing 40 cubic meters per day. Size your crops to your water.

**Building channels too steep.** Fast-flowing water erodes the channel. Maintain 1-2% gradient.

**Not maintaining channels.** Channels fail if not maintained. Annual cleaning and repair are essential.

**Sharing water without rules.** Undefined water rights create conflict. Set clear rules and measure usage.

**Watering midday.** Water evaporates; very inefficient. Water early morning or evening.
