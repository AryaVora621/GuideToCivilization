## Overview

Pests destroy crops. Locusts, beetles, aphids, slugs, fungi, and diseases can wipe out a season's food. After collapse, chemical pesticides vanish. But humans have managed pests for millennia without them.

This chapter covers how to identify pests, prevent infestations through good farming practices, and control outbreaks using mechanical, biological, and cultural methods.

## Prerequisites

- Experience growing crops (you have tended a garden or field)
- Ability to identify common plants and insects
- Willingness to monitor crops regularly
- Access to basic farming tools

## Materials & Tools

- Observation and documentation skills
- Simple tools (hand-picking, netting, traps)
- Knowledge of beneficial insects and plants

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Identify Common Agricultural Pests

Know your enemy. Different pests require different responses.

**Insects:**

**Locusts/grasshoppers:**
- Appearance: Large jumping insects, green or brown, 3-5 cm long
- Damage: Eat entire leaves, strip plants bare
- Timing: Spring and summer (breeding season)
- Control difficulty: High (swarms are hard to stop)

**Beetles:**
- Appearance: Hard-bodied insects, often metallic, varied sizes
- Damage: Chew holes in leaves and fruits
- Timing: Spring through fall
- Control difficulty: Medium (mobile, but slower than locusts)

**Aphids:**
- Appearance: Tiny soft-bodied insects, green or brown, 2-3 mm
- Damage: Pierce plants and drink sap, weakening them; transmit diseases
- Timing: Spring through fall
- Control difficulty: Low (slow-moving, easily removed)

**Slugs and snails:**
- Appearance: Soft-bodied, slimy or shelled, 1-5 cm
- Damage: Chew large holes in leaves and seedlings
- Timing: Wet weather, spring and fall
- Control difficulty: Low (slow, easy to find)

**Diseases:**

**Fungal diseases (powdery mildew, rust, blight):**
- Appearance: White powder, orange/brown coating, dark spots
- Damage: Weakens plant; reduces yields; spreads rapidly
- Timing: Wet or humid weather
- Control difficulty: Medium (prevention better than cure)

**Bacterial diseases:**
- Appearance: Wilting, yellowing, spots, cankers
- Damage: Plant declines and dies
- Timing: Various
- Control difficulty: High (no mechanical cure; prevent only)

**Viral diseases:**
- Appearance: Mottled leaves, stunted growth, deformed fruits
- Damage: Severe yield loss
- Timing: Spread by insects
- Control difficulty: Very high (no cure; prevent by controlling insect vectors)

### Step 2: Monitor Crops Regularly

Prevention starts with early detection. Regular monitoring lets you catch infestations before they explode.

**Weekly monitoring routine:**

1. Walk through crops 2-3 times per week
2. Check both sides of leaves (pests hide underneath)
3. Look for damage: Holes, yellowing, wilting, spots
4. Count pests if present (how many aphids per leaf? How many beetles?)
5. Record observations (location, pest type, damage level, date)

**When to act:**

- Light infestation (1-2 pests per plant): Monitor; take action if worsening
- Medium infestation (5-10 pests per plant): Take action now
- Heavy infestation (20+ pests per plant): Emergency action; may lose crop

**Decision tree:**

```
See pest/damage?
├─ No → Continue monitoring
├─ Yes, light → Monitor; prepare interventions
├─ Yes, medium → Start mechanical control
└─ Yes, heavy → Intensive control; consider sacrificing crop
```

### Step 3: Use Cultural/Preventive Methods

Best pest control prevents infestations before they start.

**Crop rotation:**

Plant different crops in sequence. Pests specific to one crop cannot survive on another.

Example:
- Year 1: Plant beans in field A (beans attract bean beetles)
- Year 2: Plant wheat in field A (bean beetles cannot eat wheat; population crashes)
- Year 3: Plant beans again in field B; beans were in field A

**Why it works:** Many pests have specific host plants. Without the host plant, pest populations starve.

**Timing:**

Wait at least 2 years between planting the same crop in the same field. Some pests persist in soil; waiting longer is safer.

**Sanitation:**

- Remove crop residue (dead plants harbor pests)
- Destroy diseased plants (stop disease spread)
- Clean tools between fields (prevent disease transmission)

**Plant spacing:**

- Dense plantings trap moisture, promoting fungal disease
- Spacing allows air circulation, reducing humidity
- Isolated plants are easier for pests to find; some density deters pests
- Balance: Enough space for air flow; enough density for mutual protection

**Water management:**

- Overwatering creates wet conditions favoring fungal disease
- Water at soil level, not foliage (wet leaves get diseased)
- Water in early morning so plants dry quickly
- Reduce watering in humid weather

**Variety selection:**

- Choose pest-resistant varieties when available
- Local heirloom varieties are often adapted to local pests
- Multiple varieties spread risk (if one variety fails, others may survive)

### Step 4: Use Mechanical Control Methods

Remove pests by hand or simple tools.

**Hand-picking:**

For large pests (beetles, caterpillars, slugs):
1. Inspect plants carefully
2. Pick off pests (wear gloves if sensitive)
3. Kill picked pests (crush, drown, or feed to chickens)
4. Repeat daily during outbreak

Effectiveness: Very high for slow pests; impractical for tiny pests or large infestations.

Time cost: 30 min-2 hours per day depending on infestation.

**Netting:**

Protect seedlings or valuable plants with fine netting:
1. Cover plant or bed with fine mesh netting
2. Seal edges to ground (prevent pests from crawling under)
3. Remove netting once plant is established and more resistant
4. Reuse netting year after year

Effectiveness: Very high (physical barrier works)

Time cost: 1 hour to install; reusable

**Traps:**

**Yellow sticky traps:**
- Attract flying insects (whiteflies, some beetles)
- Sticky surface traps them
- Monitor traps to track pest presence
- Cost: Low (can make with sticky sap and yellow paper)

**Light traps:**
- Attract night-flying insects (moths, beetles)
- Insects fly into light source and die
- Most effective on dark nights
- Cost: Low to medium (need light source)

**Pitfall traps:**
- Dig holes with sloped sides (pests fall in, cannot escape)
- Effective for slugs, beetles, and crawling insects
- Placement: Around crop perimeter
- Cost: Free (just dig holes)

**Barriers:**

**Row covers:**
- Floating fabric covers crops, preventing pest access
- Allow water and light through
- Remove once plants flower and need pollination
- Effectiveness: Very high for flying pests
- Cost: Medium (reusable for years)

**Copper or sulfur dust:**
- Dust on leaves deters or kills soft-bodied pests and fungal diseases
- Reapply after rain
- Harmless to most plants (test on small area first)
- Cost: Low to medium (can make sulfur dust from pure sulfur)

### Step 5: Use Biological Control Methods

Harness natural predators and pathogens.

**Beneficial insects:**

**Ladybugs:**
- Eat aphids (one ladybug eats 50-100 aphids per day)
- Attract by planting flowering plants nearby (pollen provides food)
- Population builds over weeks; takes time to be effective

**Parasitic wasps:**
- Lay eggs in pest insects; larvae eat pest from inside
- Specific to certain pests (different wasp species for different pests)
- Attract by providing flowering plants
- Very effective once established

**Ground beetles:**
- Eat slugs, snails, and soft-bodied pests
- Attracted by mulch and ground cover (shelter)
- Nocturnal hunters

**How to encourage beneficial insects:**

1. Plant diverse flowers (alyssum, yarrow, buckwheat) to provide pollen
2. Provide water (shallow dish with pebbles)
3. Reduce use of any pesticides (even soap kills beneficial insects)
4. Leave some wild areas as shelter

**Pathogens:**

**Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt):**
- Naturally occurring bacterium that kills caterpillar pests
- Spray on plants (caterpillars eat treated leaves and die)
- Harmless to humans, other insects, and plants
- Can be cultured from infected insects if available

**Fungi:**
- Some fungi naturally infect pests
- Spraying with fungal spores can control soft-bodied pests
- Requires specific conditions (humidity, temperature)

### Step 6: Know When to Accept Losses

Not all pest damage is worth fighting.

**Economic threshold:**

- Cost of control vs. Value of crop lost
- If control costs more than crop is worth, accept the loss
- Example: Spending 100 hours hand-picking beetles may not be worth 50 kg of beans

**Acceptable damage:**

- 10-20% loss is often acceptable and sustainable
- Trying to eliminate all pests requires excessive effort
- Some pests are actually beneficial (they feed beneficial insects)

**Sacrifice strategy:**

- Plant extra crop expecting some losses
- Plant some crops as pest traps (attract pests away from main crop)
- Accept that some years will be bad; plan reserves for those years

### Step 7: Response to Severe Infestations

When infestation is out of control:

**Triage:**

1. Can the pest be controlled with available methods? If no → skip to step 3
2. Is control cost worth the crop value? If no → skip to step 3
3. Sacrifice the crop: Pull plants and burn or compost (if not diseased)
4. Clean field thoroughly (remove all plant material)
5. Plan crop rotation to prevent recurrence

**Containment:**

- If pest is spreading to other fields, isolate the infested field
- Prevent workers from moving between fields (avoid spreading pests)
- Destroy all affected plants to stop spread

**Documentation:**

- Record what happened (pest type, severity, damage)
- Record what you tried (did manual control work? Did netting help?)
- Use lessons to prevent next year (rotate crops, choose resistant varieties)

## Verification

**Successful pest control results in:**

- Early detection of pest infestations (regular monitoring working)
- Controlled pest populations (numbers stable or declining)
- Acceptable crop losses (less than 20%)
- Minimal pest damage visible on harvest

**If pest control is failing:**

- Pests suddenly appear in large numbers (monitoring too infrequent)
- Pest populations grow despite control efforts (intervention inadequate)
- Significant crop loss (30%+ damage; control started too late)
- Disease spreads rapidly (sanitation inadequate)

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1 - No monitoring.**

You plant crops and assume they are fine. Suddenly, pest outbreak appears. Monitor weekly to catch problems early.

**Mistake 2 - Waiting too long.**

You see a few pests and think it will be fine. Populations explode exponentially. Act when you see the first pests, not when infestation is severe.

**Mistake 3 - Single control method.**

You rely only on hand-picking. If infestation is heavy, hand-picking fails. Combine multiple methods: rotation, sanitation, beneficial insects, and mechanical control.

**Mistake 4 - No crop rotation.**

You grow the same crop in the same field every year. Pests build up. Rotation breaks pest cycles.

**Mistake 5 - Overwatering in wet weather.**

You water even when it is raining. Plants get too wet; fungal diseases explode. Reduce watering when humidity is high.

**Mistake 6 - Planting dense monocultures.**

All plants are the same variety, planted close together. Pests find them easily and spread fast. Diversity and spacing reduce pest spread.

**Mistake 7 - Ignoring early signs.**

Yellowing leaf, one insect, light wilting. You ignore it. Disease or pest explodes. Act on early warning signs.
