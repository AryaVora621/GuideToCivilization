## Overview

Soil is the foundation of agriculture. Good soil produces abundant crops. Poor soil produces starvation. This chapter teaches you to:

1. **Assess your soil** — type, texture, drainage, fertility.
2. **Improve soil** — add organic matter, amendments, nutrients.
3. **Maintain soil health** — prevent degradation, rotate crops, build long-term fertility.

Soil takes years to build but days to destroy. Treat it as your most valuable asset.

## Prerequisites

None. Foundational knowledge applicable to all agriculture.

## Part 1: Soil Assessment

### Soil Texture

Soil texture is the proportion of sand, silt, and clay.

**Assessment — Jar Test:**
1. Fill jar halfway with soil sample (from 15 cm deep).
2. Add water until full; add drop of dish soap.
3. Shake vigorously for 2 minutes.
4. Set down; wait 1 minute. Sand settles first (mark level).
5. Wait 4 hours. Silt settles (mark level).
6. Read clay layer (top, after 24 hours).

**Texture classification:**
- **Sandy soil:** &gt;70% sand. Drains fast; low fertility; easy to work.
- **Loam:** 40-60% sand, 20-30% silt, 10-27% clay. Ideal; balanced drainage and fertility.
- **Clay soil:** &gt;27% clay. Retains water; high fertility; hard to work.

### Drainage

**Assessment — Percolation Test:**
1. Dig hole 30 cm deep.
2. Fill with water; let it drain.
3. Fill again; measure how long water takes to drain completely.

**Rates:**
- &lt;1 hour: Excellent drainage (sandy).
- 1-4 hours: Good drainage (loam).
- 4-8 hours: Poor drainage (clay, needs amendment).
- &gt;8 hours: Very poor (likely needs raised beds or drainage).

### Fertility Indicators

**Visual signs of fertile soil:**
- Dark color (high organic matter).
- Crumbly structure (not compacted).
- Earthworms present (abundance indicates health).
- Plant growth vigorous (without additional fertilizer).

**Chemical assessment (if possible):**
- Nitrogen (N): Plant growth vigor.
- Phosphorus (P): Root development, flowering.
- Potassium (K): Disease resistance, overall vigor.

**Low-tech assessment:** Grow a test crop (beans or lettuce) and observe growth.

## Part 2: Soil Types and Management

### Sandy Soil

**Problems:** Drains too fast; loses nutrients; dries out.

**Solutions:**
- Add organic matter (compost, manure) — increases water retention by 2-4% per 5 cm of organic matter.
- Mulch heavily (retains moisture).
- Frequent watering (more often than clay).
- More frequent fertilizing (nutrients leach away).

### Clay Soil

**Problems:** Drains poorly; becomes waterlogged; hard to work when wet.

**Solutions:**
- Add organic matter and sand (improves drainage).
- Raise beds (4-6 inches elevates above water table).
- Avoid working when wet (compacts soil, destroys structure).
- Mulch (reduces crusting, prevents water loss at top).

### Loam

**Ideal:** Balanced texture; retains moisture; drains well; easy to work.

**Maintenance:** Add organic matter annually; avoid compaction; rotate crops.

## Part 3: Soil Amendments

### Organic Matter (Compost)

**What it does:**
- Improves structure (crumbly vs. compacted).
- Increases water retention (in sandy soil).
- Improves drainage (in clay soil).
- Adds nutrients (slow-release).
- Supports earthworms and beneficial microbes.

**How much:** Add 2-5 cm per year to maintain fertility.

**Sources:** Kitchen scraps, plant residue, manure, leaves.

### Manure

**Types:**
- **Fresh:** High nitrogen; can burn plants; compost first.
- **Aged:** Safer; nutrients more available.
- **Best:** Well-composted, 3-6 months old.

**Application:** 2-5 tons per acre, or 1-2 cm layer worked into top 15 cm.

### Lime (Calcium Carbonate)

**Purpose:** Raises pH (makes acidic soil less acidic).

**When needed:** If plants yellowing despite nutrients present; if soil is very acidic.

**Application:** Crushed oyster shells, eggshells, limestone powder. Use sparingly (too much locks up nutrients).

### Wood Ash

**Purpose:** Raises pH; adds potassium.

**Caution:** Excessive use (&gt;1 ton/acre) can harm soil structure and lock up micronutrients.

**Application:** 1-2 inches worked into soil.

## Part 4: Nutrient Management

### Nitrogen (N)

**Role:** Leaf growth, stem vigor.

**Deficiency signs:** Yellowing leaves; stunted growth.

**Sources:** Compost, manure, legume crops (beans, peas), grass clippings.

**Nitrogen-fixing crops:** Plant annually—nitrogen-fixing bacteria in root nodules convert atmospheric nitrogen to plant-available form. Plow under after harvest to add nitrogen to soil.

### Phosphorus (P)

**Role:** Root development, flowering, seed production.

**Deficiency signs:** Purple-red leaves; poor root development; delayed flowering.

**Sources:** Compost, manure, bone meal (if available), rock phosphate.

### Potassium (K)

**Role:** Disease resistance, drought tolerance, overall vigor.

**Deficiency signs:** Weak stems; leaf margins brown/scorched.

**Sources:** Compost, manure, wood ash, kelp (if available).

## Part 5: Long-Term Soil Health

### Crop Rotation

**Principle:** Different crops use and deplete different nutrients. Rotating breaks pest cycles.

**Simple 3-year rotation:**
- Year 1: Nitrogen-demanding crop (corn, wheat) + legume cover crop in winter.
- Year 2: Legume crop (beans, peas, clover) — replenishes nitrogen.
- Year 3: Root crops (potatoes, carrots) or other crops.
- Repeat.

### Cover Crops

**Purpose:** Protect soil over-winter; add organic matter; fix nitrogen.

**Crops:** Clover, vetch, rye, barley.

**Method:** Plant in fall after harvest; plow under in spring before main crop.

### Mulching

**Purpose:** Retains moisture; suppresses weeds; adds organic matter as it decomposes.

**Materials:** Straw, leaves, grass clippings, wood chips.

**Depth:** 5-10 cm; keep away from plant stems (prevents rot).

### Earthworms and Soil Life

**Significance:** Earthworms aerate soil, improve structure, process organic matter.

**Indicators of healthy soil:** Abundant earthworms (&gt;10 per shovel of soil).

**Support:** Add organic matter; avoid tilling (harms worms); avoid chemicals.

## Verification

**Soil health checklist:**
- Texture: Sandy/loam/clay identified and managed appropriately.
- Drainage: Water percolates in 1-4 hours (or improved with amendments).
- Organic matter: Crumbly structure; dark color; 2-5 cm added annually.
- Earthworms: Present in abundance (&gt;5 per shovel).
- Plant growth: Vigorous, without disease or nutrient deficiency signs.
- Fertility: Crops yield appropriately for soil type and amendments applied.

## Common Mistakes

**Ignoring soil type.** Different soils need different management. Sandy isn't "bad" if managed correctly.

**Adding lime excessively.** Over-liming locks up nutrients and harms soil structure.

**Tilling excessively.** Breaks soil structure; kills earthworms; promotes compaction after rain.

**Not rotating crops.** Same crop yearly depletes specific nutrients and builds pest populations.

**Neglecting organic matter.** Soil degrades without regular organic matter input.
