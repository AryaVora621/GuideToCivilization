## Overview

A settlement produces organic waste: crop residue, animal manure, kitchen scraps, dead vegetation. This waste is not garbage; it is soil amendment.

Composting is the process of decomposing organic matter into stable soil amendment (compost). A settlement needs compost to maintain soil fertility without synthetic fertilizer.

This chapter teaches:
1. **Hot compost:** Fast (months), large-scale, needs management.
2. **Windrows:** Moderate-scale, less management.
3. **Vermicompost:** Small-scale, uses worms, requires less active management.

Post-collapse, composting is how you close the nutrient cycle: waste becomes fertility becomes food becomes waste again.

## Prerequisites

Understand soil amendment (Vol 10, Ch 1). Understand crop rotation and nitrogen (Vol 10, Ch 5).

## Part 1: Why Compost?

### The Nutrient Cycle

A sustainable farm cycles nutrients:
- Crops grow, consume nutrients.
- Crops are harvested, nutrients leave the field (in the food).
- Animal manure and crop residue are returned to soil, replacing nutrients.
- Cycle repeats indefinitely.

Without this cycle, soil depletes and yields crash.

### Compost vs. Raw Manure

**Raw manure:**
- Fresh animal manure.
- High in nitrogen (can "burn" plants if too concentrated).
- Contains pathogens and weed seeds (not heated).
- Takes 6-12 months to decompose enough to be safe for crops.

**Compost:**
- Decomposed manure + other organic matter.
- Nitrogen is stabilized (won't burn plants).
- Pathogens and weed seeds are killed (if properly heated).
- Ready to use in 2-6 months (depending on method).

Composting converts raw waste into safe, usable soil amendment.

## Part 2: Hot Compost (Active Composting)

Hot compost is a pile of organic matter that heats up as microbes decompose it. Temperature reaches 50-70°C (120-160°F), killing pathogens and seeds. Done in 2-6 months.

### Building a Hot Compost Pile

**Materials (carbon + nitrogen balance):**
- **Carbon-rich (browns):** Dry leaves, straw, wood chips, sawdust, shredded paper.
- **Nitrogen-rich (greens):** Fresh manure, grass clippings, food scraps, green plant material.

**Ideal ratio:** 30:1 carbon to nitrogen by weight (roughly 3 parts browns to 1 part greens).

**Too much carbon:** Pile decomposes slowly; takes 6+ months.
**Too much nitrogen:** Pile smells (ammonia odor); nitrogen is lost as gas.

### Building the Pile

1. **Base:** Layer of coarse material (straw, wood chips) for air flow (10-15 cm).
2. **Layers:** Alternate 15-20 cm layers of browns and greens.
3. **Size:** At least 1 meter × 1 meter × 1 meter (smaller piles don't heat well; larger piles are hard to manage).
4. **Moisture:** Water each layer to roughly "wrung-out sponge" moisture (damp, not soaking).
5. **Finish:** Pile should be 1.5-2 meters tall.

### Managing the Pile

**Temperature:** The pile should heat up within 1-2 days (if ratio is right).
- **Week 1-3:** Very hot (50-70°C). Microbes are actively decomposing.
- **Week 4-6:** Cools as decomposition slows.

**Turning:** Every 1-2 weeks, turn the pile (flip it with a fork, moving outside material to the center).
- **Why:** Turning adds oxygen, speeds decomposition, ensures even heating.
- **Timing:** Turn when pile cools below 50°C (wait 2-3 weeks between turns).
- **Labor:** 1 person, 1-2 hours per turn (for a 1-meter cube pile).

**Finishing:** After 2-4 months of turning, the pile no longer heats up; decomposition is mostly done. Wait another 2-4 weeks (to ensure weed seeds are dead), then use.

**Total time:** 2-6 months (depending on climate, management, materials).

### Finished Compost Characteristics

- Dark brown or black color.
- Crumbly texture (not soggy).
- Earthy smell (no ammonia or rotten smell).
- Unrecognizable original materials (mostly decomposed).

## Part 3: Windrows

A windrow is a long, low pile of compost that decomposes more slowly than a hot pile but requires less active management (less turning).

### Building Windrows

**Shape:** Long rows, roughly triangular in cross-section.
- Width: 1.5-2 meters.
- Height: 1-1.5 meters.
- Length: As long as you want (30+ meters is typical).

**Materials:** Same as hot compost (30:1 carbon to nitrogen).

### Managing Windrows

**Turning:** Once every 2-4 weeks (less frequent than hot compost piles).
- Use a tractor-mounted turner (if available) or hand-fork.
- Each turning takes 2-4 hours per 30 meters of windrow (depending on size and tools).

**Moisture:** Keep damp (not soaking). In rainy climates, cover with tarps during heavy rain to prevent waterlogging.

**Time to compost:** 4-8 months (slower than hot piles, due to less frequent turning).

**Advantage:** Less labor-intensive than hot piles; good for large operations.
**Disadvantage:** Takes longer; piles must be very large to avoid cooling too quickly.

## Part 4: Vermicompost (Worm Composting)

Vermicompost uses earthworms to decompose organic matter. It is slower but requires very little active management.

### How Vermicompost Works

Earthworms eat organic matter and excrete "castings" (worm feces), which is finished compost.

**Decomposition time:** 3-6 months (depends on worm population and temperature).

**Advantages:**
- No turning needed (worms do the work).
- Less heat (works in cool climates).
- Very nutrient-rich end product.
- Can be done at small scale (bins) or large scale (beds).

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires earthworms (must acquire or breed them).
- Slower than hot piles.
- Sensitive to temperature (worms die if too hot or too cold).

### Setting Up a Vermicompost Bed

**Container:**
- Wooden box or raised bed, 1-2 meters long, 0.5-1 meter wide, 30-40 cm deep.
- Drainage holes in the bottom (but not so large that worms escape).
- Cover to keep moisture in and light out.

**Bedding:**
- Layer of shredded newspaper or cardboard (10-15 cm).
- Layer of compost or soil (5-10 cm) as inoculant.

**Worms:**
- Red wigglers (Eisenia foetida) are best for composting (not soil-dwelling).
- 500-1000 worms per square meter of surface area.
- Cost: Free if you breed your own (worms reproduce in 8-12 weeks); purchased if not available locally.

### Feeding the Worms

- Add food scraps (vegetable, fruit, coffee grounds) in thin layers (every 1-2 weeks).
- Avoid meat, oils, dairy (attracts pests, causes odor).
- Bury food scraps 5-10 cm under the bedding (prevents odor and pest attraction).
- Amount: 1-2 kg per square meter per week (roughly 1-2 meals' worth of scraps).

### Harvesting Vermicompost

After 3-6 months:
- The original bedding is converted to dark, crumbly vermicompost.
- Worms are still present (mixed in).

**Harvesting method:**
1. Stop feeding for 2 weeks (worms eat remaining material).
2. Push all material to one side of the bin.
3. Add fresh bedding to the empty side.
4. Move worms and castings from the old side to the new side (they migrate).
5. Remove finished compost from the old side.

**Yield:** 1 square meter of worm bin produces roughly 50-100 kg of finished compost per year.

## Part 5: Compost Scaling for a Settlement

### Small Settlement (50-100 people)

**Waste generated:**
- Manure: 2-5 tons per year (from cattle, goats, chickens).
- Crop residue: 5-10 tons per year.
- Kitchen scraps: 1-2 tons per year.
- Total: 8-17 tons per year.

**Composting system:**
- One hot pile (1.5 × 1.5 × 1.5 meters), turned every 2 weeks.
- Produces roughly 3-5 tons of finished compost per year.
- Plus: Vermicompost bin (4-6 square meters) produces 200-300 kg per year.

**Labor:** 2-4 hours per week (turning, feeding).

### Medium Settlement (100-300 people)

**Waste generated:**
- Manure: 10-30 tons per year.
- Crop residue: 20-50 tons per year.
- Kitchen scraps: 5-10 tons per year.
- Total: 35-90 tons per year.

**Composting system:**
- Two hot piles (rotating: one being filled, one decomposing), turned every 2 weeks.
- Or three windrows (30 meters each), turned monthly.
- Produces roughly 15-30 tons of finished compost per year.
- Plus: Vermicompost beds (20 square meters) produce 1-2 tons per year.

**Labor:** 1-2 person-days per week.

### Large Settlement (300+ people)

**Waste generated:**
- Manure: 30+ tons per year.
- Crop residue: 50+ tons per year.
- Kitchen scraps: 10+ tons per year.
- Total: 90+ tons per year.

**Composting system:**
- Four hot piles (rotating system).
- Or five to eight windrows (turned monthly or by tractor).
- Produces 40-80 tons of finished compost per year.
- Plus: Vermicompost operation (50+ square meters) produces 5-10 tons per year.

**Labor:** Full-time composting team (2-3 people).

## Part 6: Application Rates

### How Much Compost to Apply

**Maintenance:** After soil is established, add 2-5 cm (20-50 tons per hectare) annually to maintain fertility.

**Building:** If soil is depleted, add 10-15 cm (100-150 tons per hectare) over 2-3 years to rebuild.

**Calculation example (20-hectare farm):**
- Maintenance: 20 hectares × 30 tons per hectare = 600 tons per year.
- A settlement producing 50 tons of finished compost per year can maintain 1-2 hectares with that compost alone.
- Rest of fertility comes from crop rotation (legumes) and animal manure applied directly.

### Application Method

- **Broadcast:** Spread compost evenly over field (2-5 cm layer).
- **Incorporation:** Plow or dig it into top 15-20 cm.
- **Timing:** Fall or early spring (before planting).

## Part 7: Troubleshooting Compost

### Pile Smells Bad (Ammonia Odor)

**Problem:** Too much nitrogen (too many greens).
**Solution:** Add more browns (straw, leaves, wood chips).

### Pile Doesn't Heat Up

**Problem:** Too much carbon, too little nitrogen; OR pile is too small.
**Solution:** Add more greens (manure, grass clippings). Or make a larger pile.

### Pile Stays Wet

**Problem:** Poor drainage or too much rain.
**Solution:** Add coarser material (wood chips) for aeration. Cover with tarps in heavy rain.

### Worms Die in Vermicompost

**Problem:** Temperature too high or too low; OR too dry; OR pH too acidic.
**Solution:** 
- Move bin to cooler location (if too hot).
- Add bedding layers (if too cold).
- Increase moisture slightly.
- Add lime or wood ash to raise pH.

## Verification

Your composting system is adequate if:
1. **Scale:** You have hot piles, windrows, or vermicompost suited to your settlement size.
2. **Feedstock:** You collect manure, crop residue, and kitchen scraps (not wasting them).
3. **Carbon-nitrogen balance:** You balance browns and greens (30:1 ratio).
4. **Management:** Hot piles are turned regularly; windrows turned monthly; worm bins are fed weekly.
5. **Output:** You produce enough finished compost (2-5 tons per year minimum for a 50-person settlement).
6. **Quality:** Finished compost is dark, crumbly, and doesn't smell.
7. **Application:** You apply compost annually to maintain soil fertility.

## Common Mistakes

**Not composting at all.** Manure and crop residue are nutrients; wasting them wastes fertility.

**Too much nitrogen (smelly pile).** Add browns. Most people use too much manure without balancing it.

**Pile too small.** Piles under 1 meter cubed don't heat well and decompose slowly.

**Never turning.** Without turning, hot piles take 6+ months. Regular turning speeds it to 2-4 months.

**Applying fresh manure directly.** Raw manure can burn plants and spreads pathogens. Compost it first.

**Forgetting to cover worm bins.** Worms need darkness. Exposed bins have worms escaping or drying out.
