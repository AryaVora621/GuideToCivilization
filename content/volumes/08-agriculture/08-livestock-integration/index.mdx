## Overview

Livestock are not separate from agriculture; they are part of it. Animals convert crops and grass into manure, meat, milk, wool, hides, and draft power.

This chapter teaches:
1. **Animals for draft power:** Oxen, horses, mules for plowing and transport.
2. **Animals for manure:** Cattle, goats, chickens produce nutrient-rich manure.
3. **Animals for food:** Meat, milk, eggs on the farm.
4. **Integration:** How to rotate animals and crops so each supports the other.

A sustainable farm has both crops and animals. Each multiplies the productivity of the other.

## Prerequisites

Understand soil and nutrient cycling (Vol 10, Ch 1). Know animal health (Vol 11: Veterinary Medicine).

## Part 1: Why Livestock?

### Draft Power

Before tractors, animal power was essential for farming.

**Tasks requiring draft animals:**
- Plowing (turning soil, breaking ground).
- Harrowing (breaking up clods, preparing seed beds).
- Threshing grain (traction to separate grain from chaff).
- Transport (moving goods, grain, hay).

**Animals by strength:**
- **Oxen:** Strongest. Slow but powerful. Best for plowing hard ground.
- **Horses:** Faster than oxen. Less powerful but more versatile.
- **Mules:** Cross between horse and donkey. Tough, reliable, disease-resistant.
- **Donkeys:** Small, hardy, low maintenance. Suitable for light work and transport.

**Labor replacement:** One person + one ox can plow 0.5-1 hectare per day. Without oxen, plowing the same area by hand (spade) takes 10+ person-days of labor.

### Manure Production

Animals convert feed into manure, which is the primary soil amendment post-collapse.

**Manure production rates:**
- **Cattle:** 10-15 kg manure per day (fresh).
- **Horses:** 8-10 kg per day.
- **Goats:** 1-2 kg per day.
- **Chickens:** 0.05-0.1 kg per day (small but accumulates).

**Nutrient content:**
- Fresh manure: 0.5-1% nitrogen, 0.2% phosphorus, 0.3% potassium.
- Composted manure: 1-2% nitrogen, 0.5-1% phosphorus, 0.8-1% potassium.

A single cow produces enough manure per year (3-5 tons) to amend 0.5-1 hectare of crop land.

### Food Production

Livestock provide protein and calories.

**Meat yield:**
- **Cattle:** 200-300 kg meat per animal (can be 400-600 kg for large breeds).
- **Goats:** 15-25 kg meat per animal.
- **Chickens:** 1.5-2 kg meat per bird.
- **Pigs:** 50-80 kg meat per animal.

**Milk production (dairy breeds):**
- **Cattle:** 10-20 liters per day during lactation (8+ months per year).
- **Goats:** 1-3 liters per day.
- **Sheep:** 0.5-1.5 liters per day.

**Eggs:**
- **Chickens:** 200-300 eggs per year per bird (good layers).

## Part 2: Integrating Animals and Crops

### Rotational Grazing

Animals graze on pasture, consume grass, and deposit manure. This improves soil and moves nutrient from pasture to manure.

**System:**
1. Divide pasture into paddocks (fenced sections).
2. Graze animals intensively in one paddock for 1-2 weeks.
3. Move animals to the next paddock.
4. Leave the grazed paddock to recover (4-6 weeks).
5. Cycle back to the first paddock.

**Benefits:**
- Manure is spread evenly across pasture.
- Grazing controls weeds.
- Soil is not trampled excessively (given recovery time).
- Pasture productivity increases over years.

**Stocking density:** 
- Cattle: 1-2 per hectare (depending on grass quality).
- Goats: 3-5 per hectare (smaller animals, lighter impact).
- Sheep: 5-10 per hectare.

Too many animals deplete pasture; too few waste grass. Find the balance.

### Crop Residue + Livestock

After harvest, crop residue (stalks, leaves) becomes animal feed.

**Residues for animal feed:**
- **Grain straw:** Wheat, barley, rye straw. Low nutrition but fills the stomach.
- **Corn stalks:** Nutritious; animals will eat leaves and stalks.
- **Bean/pea residue:** Protein-rich; valuable feed.
- **Root crop tops:** Carrot, beet, turnip tops are nutritious greens.

**System:**
1. Harvest grain for human food.
2. Animals graze or are fed the straw/residue.
3. Animals convert residue into manure.
4. Manure is spread back on fields.

**Benefit:** Nothing is wasted. The same land feeds humans, then animals, then generates manure for next year.

### Pasture + Crop Rotation

Alternate pasture and crops on the same land.

**3-year rotation:**
- Year 1: Grain (wheat, barley).
- Year 2: Legume (beans, clover).
- Year 3: Pasture (animals graze; manure improves soil).

**Benefit:** Animals improve soil for next crop; crops provide feed for animals.

### Animals in Orchards/Vineyards

Grazing under fruit trees or vines.

**System:**
- Goats or sheep graze under the trees.
- They eat weeds and fallen fruit (preventing pests).
- Manure fertilizes the trees.
- No labor needed for weeding or fertilizing.

**Caution:** Do not allow animals to strip bark (can kill trees). Rotate grazing to prevent overgrazing.

## Part 3: Types of Livestock

### Cattle (Draft + Meat + Milk)

**Breeds:**
- **Draft breeds (oxen):** Large, strong. Slow but powerful. Examples: Oxen (castrated bulls), Draft horses-sized cattle.
- **Meat breeds:** Fast-growing, efficient converters. Examples: Shorthorn, Hereford.
- **Dairy breeds:** Optimized for milk production. Examples: Holstein, Jersey, Guernsey.

**Management:**
- 1 hectare of pasture per animal (rough estimate).
- Requires shelter (barn) in cold/wet climates.
- Annual calves (if breeding) replace herd gradually.

**Lifespan:** 10-15 years productive life.

### Goats (Milk + Meat + Fiber)

**Breeds:**
- **Dairy:** Alpine, Saanen, Nubian (1-3 liters/day).
- **Meat:** Boer, Spanish, Kiko.
- **Fiber:** Angora, Cashmere (produce fine fiber for textiles).

**Advantages:**
- Small (easy to handle).
- Prolific breeders (multiple kids per year).
- Eat brush, weeds, and tree leaves (not just grass).
- Cheap to feed.
- Hides useful for leather.

**Disadvantages:**
- Require secure fencing (clever escapes).
- Can be destructive (strip bark from trees).

**Management:**
- 0.5 hectare per animal (smaller than cattle, more grazing pressure).
- Does kid (give birth) yearly; requires breeding management.

### Chickens (Eggs + Meat + Pest Control)

**Breeds:**
- **Layers:** Rhode Island Red, Leghorn, Sussex (200-300 eggs/year).
- **Meat:** Cornish, Australorp, Brahma.
- **Dual-purpose:** Wyandotte, Plymouth Rock (eggs + meat).

**Advantages:**
- Minimal space (100-200 birds per hectare possible).
- Fast reproduction (chicks mature in 4-6 months).
- Eat insects, pests (reduce pest pressure on crops).
- Manure is potent fertilizer (high nitrogen).
- Feathers useful for insulation, pillows.

**Disadvantages:**
- Vulnerable to predators (require secure coop).
- Need grain supplementation (cannot survive on grass alone).
- Prone to diseases if crowded.

**Management:**
- 4-6 square meters of coop per bird (for winter shelter).
- 100+ square meters of run per bird (for daytime foraging).
- Replace layers every 2-3 years (productivity drops).

### Pigs (Meat + Fertilizer)

**Breeds:**
- **Large:** Yorkshire, Landrace, Duroc (fast-growing, large carcasses).
- **Heritage:** Guinea Hog, Gloucestershire Old Spots (slower-growing, flavorful meat).

**Advantages:**
- Efficient feed converters (fast meat production).
- Omnivorous (eat scraps, grain, roots).
- Manure is excellent for soil amendment.
- By-products (lard, hides) are valuable.

**Disadvantages:**
- Destructive (root up pasture, destroying grass).
- Require careful management (can become aggressive).
- High feed requirements during growth.

**Management:**
- Confine to pens with access to pasture (prevent pasture destruction).
- Rotate pens to prevent overuse.
- Slaughter at 6-12 months (before they become too large).

### Sheep (Meat + Wool + Milk)

**Breeds:**
- **Meat:** Dorper, Katahdin, Barbado (fast-growing, efficient).
- **Wool:** Merino, Rambouillet (fine wool for textiles).
- **Milk:** East Friesian, Lacaune (1+ liter/day).
- **Dual-purpose:** Most heritage breeds.

**Advantages:**
- Efficient grazers (extract nutrition from pasture others cannot).
- Multiple products (meat, wool, milk, hides).
- Relatively low-maintenance.
- Small enough for transport on foot.

**Disadvantages:**
- Vulnerable to predators (require protection).
- Susceptible to parasites (require management).
- Wool requires processing (shearing, carding, spinning).

## Part 4: Calculating Animal Capacity

### Land Requirement

**Pasture needed (hectares per animal per year):**
- Cattle: 0.8-1.2 hectares (depends on grass quality, rainfall).
- Goats: 0.4-0.6 hectares.
- Sheep: 0.2-0.4 hectares (efficient grazers).
- Chickens: 0.01-0.02 hectares (very small).
- Pigs: 0.2-0.3 hectares (if rotated).

**Example (50-hectare farm, 100-person settlement):**
- 20 hectares for grain crops (wheat, barley).
- 20 hectares for legumes (beans, clover).
- 10 hectares for pasture/grazing rotation.

**Animals that 10 hectares can support (continuous grazing):**
- 10 cattle, OR
- 20 goats, OR
- 30 sheep, OR
- 500 chickens, OR
- 50 pigs (rotated).

### Feed Calculation

**Daily feed requirement:**
- Cattle: 10-15 kg fresh forage per day (grass, hay).
- Goats: 2-4 kg per day.
- Sheep: 2-3 kg per day.
- Chickens: 0.1-0.15 kg grain per day.
- Pigs: 2-4 kg feed per day (grain, roots, scraps).

**Hay production (for winter):**
- 1 hectare of pasture produces ~5 tons of hay per year.
- 1 cow needs ~5 tons of hay for winter (6 months).
- So 1 hectare of pasture = enough hay for 1 cow for winter.

## Part 5: Seasonal Livestock Management

### Spring (Breeding/Birth)

- **Goats, sheep, cattle:** Breeding animals conceive in fall, give birth in spring.
- **Chickens:** Begin egg-laying (after winter rest); good time to hatch chicks.
- **Pigs:** Fatten piglets born in winter; prepare for slaughter in fall.

### Summer (Growth/Milk Production)

- **Pasture:** Peak grass growth; animals graze.
- **Dairy animals:** Peak milk production; milk cheese, butter, yogurt.
- **Young animals:** Grow rapidly on rich pasture.
- **Task:** Hay-making (cut grass, dry, store for winter).

### Fall (Harvest/Slaughter)

- **Grain harvest:** Feed animals crop residue.
- **Slaughter:** Kill animals destined for meat before winter (reduce feed needs).
- **Breeding:** Prepare breeding animals for fall breeding.

### Winter (Dormancy/Indoor)

- **Confinement:** Animals in barns, eating stored hay.
- **Reduced productivity:** No milk, no eggs (for some breeds), slow growth.
- **Expense:** Peak feed cost; burn through hay stores.
- **Breeding:** Mating season for cattle, goats, sheep (spring births).

## Verification

Your livestock integration is adequate if:
1. **Draft power:** You have 1+ oxen or horses for plowing and transport.
2. **Manure:** Animals produce 5+ tons annually (enough for soil amendment).
3. **Rotation:** Animals graze rotational pastures; land recovers between grazing.
4. **Feed:** Animals eat crop residue; crops benefit from manure.
5. **Diversity:** You have 2-3 types of livestock (reduces risk).
6. **Capacity:** Your land can support the animals you keep without degradation.
7. **Health:** Animals are healthy; vet care prevents disease.

## Common Mistakes

**Too many animals.** Overstocking degrades pasture and animals starve or become sick.

**No rotation.** Continuous grazing in one pasture destroys it.

**Inadequate shelter.** Animals in harsh climates need barns; exposure causes death.

**Poor breeding management.** Uncontrolled breeding leads to overpopulation or genetic problems.

**Not using crop residue.** Grain residue is animal feed; wasting it means buying feed.

**Neglecting animal health.** Sick animals are unproductive; prevent disease through management.
