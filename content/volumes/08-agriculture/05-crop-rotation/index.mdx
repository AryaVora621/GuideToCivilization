## Overview

Crop rotation is farming the same field with different crops each year. It is not random; it follows a plan.

Why rotate?
1. **Nitrogen management:** Legumes (beans, peas) add nitrogen; grain crops (wheat, corn) consume it. Rotating them maintains balance.
2. **Pest control:** Wheat pests do not eat beans. Rotating breaks pest cycles without chemicals.
3. **Disease prevention:** Many diseases are crop-specific. Rotating prevents buildup.
4. **Soil structure:** Different crops have different root depths and structures. Rotation maintains soil health.

Post-collapse, crop rotation is how you farm forever without synthetic fertilizer.

Understand nitrogen and soil fertility (Vol 10, Ch 1). Know which crops to grow (Vol 10, Ch 2).

## Part 1: The Nitrogen Cycle

### Why Nitrogen Matters

Nitrogen (N) is the primary nutrient for plant growth. Plants cannot make it themselves; they get it from the soil.

**Nitrogen sources:**
- Atmospheric nitrogen (N2 in the air): Plants cannot use it directly.
- Soil nitrogen (compounds like nitrate): Plants can use it.
- Organic matter (compost, manure): Decomposes, releasing nitrogen.
- Nitrogen fixation: Certain bacteria convert atmospheric N2 into plant-available nitrogen.

### Nitrogen Consumption by Crops

Different crops consume different amounts of nitrogen.

**High-nitrogen crops (consume 100-200 kg N per hectare per year):**
- Wheat, corn, rice, barley.
- Leafy vegetables (spinach, lettuce, kale).

**Medium-nitrogen crops (consume 50-100 kg N per hectare per year):**
- Potatoes, squash, beans.

**Nitrogen-fixing crops (add 50-200 kg N per hectare per year):**
- Legumes: beans, peas, lentils, clover, alfalfa.
- These crops host nitrogen-fixing bacteria in root nodules. The bacteria convert atmospheric N2 into nitrogen the plant uses.

### Nitrogen Depletion

If you grow high-nitrogen crops every year without adding nitrogen, soil nitrogen declines:
- Year 1: Wheat consumes 150 kg N. Yield is good.
- Year 2: Available N is lower (only 50 kg N in soil). Wheat yield drops.
- Year 3: Even less N. Wheat barely grows.
- By Year 5-7: Soil is so depleted that wheat fails entirely.

This is why rotation is essential. You cannot grow wheat every year without degrading soil.

## Part 2: Crop Rotation Plans

### Simple 3-Year Rotation

The most sustainable rotation for most settlements.

**Fields:** Divide your farmland into 3 sections (3A, 3B, 3C).

**Year 1:**
- Field 3A: Grain (wheat, barley, corn).
- Field 3B: Legume (beans, peas, or clover).
- Field 3C: Other crops (potatoes, squash, vegetables).

**Year 2:**
- Field 3A: Legume (replenishes nitrogen).
- Field 3B: Other crops (use nitrogen added by legume).
- Field 3C: Grain.

**Year 3:**
- Field 3A: Other crops.
- Field 3B: Grain.
- Field 3C: Legume.

**Year 4:** Return to Year 1 cycle.

**Result:** Each field produces all three crop types over 3 years. Nitrogen is balanced. Pests and diseases decline.

### 4-Year Rotation (More Intensive)

If you have fewer fields or need more food, use a 4-year rotation.

**Year 1: Grain** (wheat, barley, corn).
- Consumes nitrogen.
- Heavy feeder.

**Year 2: Legume** (beans, peas, alfalfa).
- Fixes nitrogen.
- Replenishes soil.

**Year 3: Root crop** (potatoes, carrots, turnips).
- Medium nitrogen use.
- Different pest cycles.

**Year 4: Leafy vegetable or light feeder** (cabbage, lettuce, or fallow/rest).
- If rest: Field recovers; no crop planted.
- If vegetable: Moderate nitrogen use.

**Sequence over 4 years:** Each field cycles through all types. Very sustainable.

### 2-Field System (Minimal)

For a small settlement with only 2 fields:

**Year 1:**
- Field A: Grain + underground cover crop (plant grain in spring, clover in fall after harvest).
- Field B: Legume (beans, peas).

**Year 2:**
- Field A: Legume.
- Field B: Grain + underground cover crop.

**Cover crop:** In late summer/fall, after harvesting grain, plant clover or other nitrogen-fixing plant. Let it grow over winter. Plow it under in spring (adds nitrogen). Then plant the next year's crop.

**Advantage:** Only 2 fields, very simple.
**Disadvantage:** Less diversity, higher pest risk.

## Part 3: Nitrogen-Fixing Crops (Legumes)

### Legume Basics

Legumes are crops where nodules on roots contain nitrogen-fixing bacteria.

**Common legumes:**
- **Beans:** Bush beans, pole beans, runner beans. Annual. Harvest in late summer.
- **Peas:** Green peas, snow peas, split peas. Annual. Harvest in early summer.
- **Lentils:** Small lens-shaped seeds. Annual. Harvest in late summer.
- **Clover:** White clover, red clover, crimson clover. Used as cover crop (plowed under for nitrogen).
- **Alfalfa:** Perennial (4-5 years). Cut for hay or grazed; plow under after several years for nitrogen.
- **Vetch:** Annual or winter vetch. Often mixed with grain as cover crop.

### Nitrogen Addition Rate

**Legume type and nitrogen addition (per hectare per year):**
- Beans (fresh harvest): 50-80 kg N.
- Peas (fresh harvest): 50-80 kg N.
- Lentils: 60-100 kg N.
- Clover (cover crop, plowed under): 100-150 kg N.
- Alfalfa (4-5 years, then plowed): 150-200 kg N total.

### Implementing Legume Rotations

**Year 1: Grain field**
- Grow wheat, corn, or barley (harvest for food).
- In late fall, after harvest, plant a cover crop (clover or vetch).

**Winter:** Cover crop grows (if mild) or lies dormant (if cold).

**Spring: Year 2**
- Plow under the cover crop (adds 100+ kg N to soil).
- Plant a legume for harvest (beans, peas, or lentils).

**Summer to fall:** Legume grows, fixes nitrogen.

**Harvest:** Collect beans or peas for food. Plow under plant residue (stems, roots).

**Year 3:** Plant grain again. Nitrogen is replenished.

### Mixing Crops with Legume Covers

You can grow grain + legume together (intercropping):

**Grain + legume mixture:**
- Plant wheat at normal density.
- Interplant beans or peas at lower density (so they don't shade grain excessively).
- Harvest both simultaneously (wheat as grain, beans as a second crop).
- Plow under all residue.

**Advantage:** Harvest 2 crops from 1 field in 1 year; nitrogen added to soil.
**Disadvantage:** More complex harvesting; lower grain yield (space used for legume).

## Part 4: Break Crops and Rotations

### What Are Break Crops?

A "break crop" is a crop that breaks the cycle of pests/diseases. Different crops have different pests.

**Example breaks:**
- **Wheat pests:** Hessian fly, Frit fly, various fungi.
- **Beans pests:** Bean beetles, bean rust fungus.
- **Potato pests:** Colorado potato beetle, late blight fungus.

Growing wheat → bean → potato → wheat, each pest's cycle is broken. Wheat pests disappear after 2-3 years without wheat to feed on.

### Common Crop Families

Crops in the same family share pests/diseases. Rotation should avoid planting the same family consecutively.

**Crop families:**
- **Grains (Poaceae):** Wheat, barley, rye, oats, corn, rice.
- **Legumes (Fabaceae):** Beans, peas, lentils, clover, alfalfa.
- **Root crops (multiple families):**
  - Potatoes, tomatoes (Solanaceae) — share pests.
  - Carrots, parsnips (Apiaceae) — share pests.
  - Beets, spinach, chard (Amaranthaceae) — share pests.
- **Brassicas (Brassicaceae):** Cabbage, broccoli, turnips, radishes — share pests.
- **Squash family (Cucurbitaceae):** Squash, pumpkins, cucumbers, melons — share pests.

### Rotation Strategy

Build your rotation to avoid planting the same family in the same field more than once every 3-4 years:

**Year 1: Grain (wheat).**
**Year 2: Legume (beans).** Different family; different pests.
**Year 3: Root crop (potatoes).** Different family again.
**Year 4: Different root crop (carrots) OR brassica (cabbage).** Different from potatoes within root crops.

By Year 5, you can return to grain; pest cycles are broken.

## Part 5: Fallow and Rest Fields

### What Is Fallow?

A fallow field is left unplanted for a year (or season). The field "rests."

**Benefits:**
- Soil structure rebuilds (roots decompose, microbes thrive).
- Weed seeds are depleted (weeds grow, then are removed; no crop to harvest, so farmers focus on weeding).
- Pests lose food source (pest populations crash).
- Soil moisture is restored (moisture accumulates without crop extraction).

### When to Use Fallow

**Necessary for:**
- Arid/semi-arid regions (low rainfall). Fallow allows soil moisture to accumulate for next year's crop.
- Very poor soil (depleted, exhausted). Fallow gives soil recovery time.
- Extreme pest pressure (if pests are overwhelming, fallow breaks the cycle).

**Optional in:**
- Well-watered regions (abundant rainfall). Crop rotation without fallow is sufficient.
- Fertile soil. Legume rotation maintains fertility; fallow is not necessary.

### Implementation

**1-in-3 system:**
- Divide fields into 3 sections.
- Year 1: Grain. Legume. Fallow.
- Year 2: Fallow. Grain. Legume.
- Year 3: Legume. Fallow. Grain.

**Advantage:** Every field rests 1 year in 3; soil recovers.
**Disadvantage:** 33% of land is unproductive each year.

## Part 6: Multi-Year Rotation Examples

### Temperate Climate (Cold Winters, Moderate Rain)

**Example: 4-field rotation (4 years per cycle)**

- **Field A Year 1:** Winter wheat (planted fall, harvested summer).
- **Field B Year 1:** Beans (planted spring, harvested fall).
- **Field C Year 1:** Potatoes (planted spring, harvested fall).
- **Field D Year 1:** Fallow (rest field).

- **Field A Year 2:** Beans (nitrogen replenished by previous year's fallow or cover crop).
- **Field B Year 2:** Potatoes.
- **Field C Year 2:** Fallow.
- **Field D Year 2:** Winter wheat.

- Continue rotating (each field moves to next type each year).

**Sustainability:** Nitrogen maintained by bean rotation; pest cycles broken; soil rests 1 year per 4.

### Semi-Arid Climate (Low Rain, Hot Summers)

**Example: 3-field rotation with fallow**

- **Field A Year 1:** Grain (wheat or barley).
- **Field B Year 1:** Legume (beans or lentils, if water available; otherwise, fallow).
- **Field C Year 1:** Fallow (soil moisture recovery).

- **Field A Year 2:** Legume.
- **Field B Year 2:** Fallow.
- **Field C Year 2:** Grain.

- **Field A Year 3:** Fallow.
- **Field B Year 3:** Grain.
- **Field C Year 3:** Legume.

**Sustainability:** Fallow allows moisture recovery critical in dry climate; nitrogen maintained by legume rotation.

### Wet Tropical Climate (High Rain Year-Round)

**Example: Continuous cropping (no fallow)**

With abundant rainfall and warmth, you can grow crops most of the year:

- **Field A:** Grain in dry season, legume in wet season (succession planting).
- **Field B:** Legume in dry season, grain in wet season.
- **Field C:** Root crops year-round (potatoes, cassava, yams).

**Rotation:** Crops rotate by season, not by year. Every field produces multiple crops per year.

**Sustainability:** No fallow needed; legume in every season replenishes nitrogen.

## Part 7: Managing Rotation in Practice

### Record-Keeping

Maintain a record of what was planted where each year:

**Rotation log:**
```
Year  Field A        Field B        Field C        Field D
2024  Wheat          Beans          Potatoes       Fallow
2025  Beans          Potatoes       Fallow         Wheat
2026  Potatoes       Fallow         Wheat          Beans
```

This simple log prevents mistakes and allows you to plan 3-5 years ahead.

### Seed/Seedling Allocation

Different crops need different seeding rates and labor. Plan ahead:

**Spring:**
- Grain (wheat, barley): Seed required.
- Potatoes: Seed potatoes required.
- Beans/peas: Seed required.

**Labor peak:** Spring (planting) and fall (harvest) are busiest.

### Soil Health Monitoring

Every year, assess soil:
- **Visual:** Color, crumbly texture, earthworms present (healthy) vs. compacted, pale, few worms (poor).
- **Plant signs:** Do crops grow well? Are they healthy or showing deficiency signs?
- **Nitrogen:** After a legume rotation, the next grain crop should grow exceptionally well (high nitrogen). If growth is poor, nitrogen is depleted; add compost or manure.

## Verification

Your crop rotation system is sound if:
1. **Plan exists:** You have a written 3-4 year rotation plan for each field.
2. **Nitrogen balance:** Grain crops alternate with legume crops (not back-to-back grains).
3. **Pest management:** Different crop families are rotated (not the same family year after year).
4. **Fallow (if needed):** In arid regions, fallow or cover crops are part of the rotation.
5. **Seeding/harvest:** You have seed and labor capacity for all planned crops.
6. **Monitoring:** You observe soil health and adjust rotation if needed.
7. **Sustainability:** Your rotation maintains or improves soil fertility over 5+ years without synthetic fertilizer.

## Common Mistakes

**Same crop every year.** Soil depletes; pests build up; yields crash by year 3-5.

**Grain every other year without legumes.** Nitrogen still depletes. Legumes MUST be part of the rotation.

**No record of what was planted.** You forget the plan and accidentally repeat the same sequence. Keep a log.

**Ignoring fallow in arid regions.** Low-rainfall areas need fallow to accumulate soil moisture.

**Legume monoculture.** Planting beans every year in the same field depletes potassium and other nutrients. Legumes must alternate with other crops.

**Expecting immediate results.** Crop rotation takes 3-5 years to show benefits. Have patience.
