## Overview

Seed saving is how a post-collapse settlement becomes self-sufficient. Each year, you harvest seeds from your best plants and save them for next year. Over generations, your seed stock adapts to your local climate and soil.

This chapter teaches:
1. **Which plants to save seed from.** Select the best plants; avoid saving seed from sick or weak plants.
2. **How to harvest and dry seeds.** Different plants require different harvesting and drying methods.
3. **How to store seeds.** Cool, dry storage extends seed viability for years.
4. **How much seed to save.** Calculate the quantity needed for next year's planting plus a safety margin.

Post-collapse, seed is currency. Control seed, and you control agriculture.

## Prerequisites

Understand which crops you are growing (Vol 10, Ch 2).

## Part 1: Which Seeds to Save

### Save Seeds From

**Your best plants:** Select seeds only from plants that:
- Grew vigorously (health and strength).
- Produced large yields (quantity of fruit/grain).
- Matured early (if your growing season is short).
- Were disease-resistant (no visible disease or pest damage).
- Produced quality fruit (size, flavor, texture).

**Why:** Selecting seeds from your best plants creates a self-improving seed stock. After 5-10 years, your locally-adapted varieties outperform the original seed.

### Do NOT Save Seeds From

**Weak or sick plants:** Do not save seed from plants that:
- Were diseased or pest-infested.
- Grew poorly or produced low yields.
- Were misshapen or abnormal.
- Came from volunteer plants (plants that grew accidentally from dropped seed).

**Why:** Weak seeds produce weak plants. You will degrade your seed stock.

**Hybrid varieties:** If you planted a hybrid seed (marked F1 on the packet), do not save seed from it. Hybrid seeds do not breed true; the next generation is weaker.

### Which Crops to Prioritize

**Easiest to save seed from (start here):**
- **Beans and peas:** Easy to harvest (dry pods), easy to store, highly productive.
- **Lettuce:** Easy to identify mature seeds, prolific seed production.
- **Radishes:** Easy to identify seed pods, prolific seed production.
- **Squash and pumpkins:** Easy to extract seeds, long-lived seeds.

**Moderate difficulty:**
- **Wheat, barley, rye:** Seed is just the grain itself; easy to store; requires threshing.
- **Tomatoes:** Seeds require fermentation to clean; then dry and store.
- **Carrots, beets:** Seeds require 2-year growing cycle (biennial).

**Difficult (avoid until you have experience):**
- **Corn:** Easily cross-pollinates; need multiple plants to avoid inbreeding. Requires careful isolation.
- **Cucumbers:** Cross-pollinate with squash and melons; require isolation.
- **Cabbage and broccoli:** Biennial (2 years); require careful overwintering.

## Part 2: Harvesting Seeds

### Timing — When to Harvest

Seeds are mature when:
- **Seed pods are dry and brown** (not green).
- **Seeds are hard** (cannot be dented with a fingernail).
- **Seeds rattle inside the pod** (indicates dryness).

**Harvest too early:** Seeds are immature; poor germination.
**Harvest too late:** Seeds may fall on the ground (lost) or begin to sprout.

### Harvesting Methods

**Dry-seed crops (beans, grains, lettuce, radishes):**
1. Leave plants in the field to fully dry (2-3 weeks after maturity).
2. Pull the entire plant or cut the seed-bearing portion.
3. Place in a dry location (barn, shed) to continue drying for 1-2 weeks.
4. Thresh (beat) the dried plant to separate seeds from chaff.
5. Winnow (blow away chaff with wind or a fan) to clean seeds.

**Example — Wheat:**
- Wait until wheat heads are brown and hard.
- Cut wheat stalks with a sickle or knife, bundle them (a sheaf).
- Hang bundles in a dry location for 2-3 weeks.
- Thresh by beating bundles with a flail or stick.
- Winnow to separate grain from chaff.
- Store dried grain.

**Wet-seed crops (tomatoes, cucumbers, squash):**
1. Let fruit fully ripen and mature on the plant.
2. Harvest the ripe fruit.
3. Extract seeds (cut open fruit, scoop out seeds with pulp).
4. For tomato/cucumber: ferment seeds in water for 3-5 days (fermentation removes the gel coating).
5. Rinse thoroughly.
6. Dry completely (see Part 3).

**Example — Squash:**
- Wait until squash skin is hard and color is fully developed.
- Cut squash from vine.
- Leave fruit in the field for 2-4 weeks to cure (harden skin).
- Cut squash in half, scoop out seeds and stringy pulp.
- Rinse seeds in water, remove any remaining pulp.
- Dry completely.

**Biennial crops (carrots, beets, cabbage):**
- These crops flower and seed in their second year.
- Year 1: Grow the plant, harvest roots (eat some, keep best few for seed).
- Year 2: Replant the saved roots; they flower, produce seeds.
- Harvest seeds after flowering.

**Example — Carrot:**
- In Year 1, grow carrots normally. Keep 5-10 of the best carrots.
- In Year 2, plant the saved carrots in spring (point up, crown at soil level).
- Carrots will flower in mid-summer.
- Flowers are umbels (clusters). Let them mature fully (1-2 weeks after flowering).
- Cut seed heads when they are brown and dry.
- Thresh and winnow to separate seeds.

### Amount to Harvest

**Rule of thumb:** Save 10-20% of your harvest as seed.

**Example (wheat):**
- You plant 1 hectare with 200 kg of seed.
- You harvest 6000 kg of grain.
- You save 600-1200 kg (10-20%) for next year's planting (2000 kg/hectare is a typical seeding rate).
- You consume the remaining 4800-5400 kg.

**Safety margin:** Always save more than you think you need. Stored seed loses viability over time; extra seed ensures you can replant if some seed germinates poorly.

## Part 3: Drying and Storing Seeds

### Drying Seeds

**Why dry?** Seeds must be dry to store without rotting. Moisture causes mold and premature germination.

**Target moisture content:** 10-12% moisture by weight (seeds are hard and rattle).

**Drying methods:**

**Sun drying (free, works well in dry climates):**
- Spread seeds thinly on cloth or a tray (5-10 cm deep).
- Leave in sun for 1-2 weeks, stirring daily.
- Seeds are dry when they rattle and are hard.
- Problem: Rain or high humidity prevents drying.

**Shade drying (slower, works in humid climates):**
- Spread seeds thinly in a dry shed or room (air circulation matters).
- Leave for 2-4 weeks, stirring occasionally.
- Slower than sun but works when sun is not available.

**Fire drying (fastest, works in any climate):**
- Build a low-temperature fire (no flame, just coals).
- Spread seeds on a drying rack 2-3 meters above the fire.
- Heat to 40-50°C (warm to touch, but not hot enough to burn you).
- Dry for 4-8 hours, stirring occasionally.
- Stop when seeds are hard and rattle.
- Caution: Too much heat kills seeds (above 60°C).

**Test for dryness:** Bite a seed. It should be hard and brittle, not soft or chewy.

### Storing Seeds

**Storage conditions:** Seeds last longest in cool, dry, dark conditions.

**Ideal storage:**
- **Temperature:** 0-5°C (refrigerator or cold room). Cool slows seed aging.
- **Humidity:** 10-12% moisture (dry). Use silica gel or dried rice to absorb excess moisture.
- **Light:** Dark (no light). Light can trigger germination.

**Container types:**

**Paper envelopes:**
- Breathable; good for short-term storage (1-2 years).
- Label clearly (crop name, variety, year, date saved).
- Stack in a box or jar.

**Glass jars:**
- Airtight; good for long-term storage (3-5 years).
- Add silica gel or dried rice inside to absorb moisture.
- Seal tightly.
- Label on the outside.

**Cloth bags:**
- Breathable; good for medium-term storage (1-3 years).
- Store in a cool, dry place.

**Vacuum sealing:**
- If you have the capacity, vacuum-sealed bags keep seeds viable for 5-10 years.
- Add desiccant (silica gel) before sealing.

### Seed Storage by Crop Type

**Grain crops (wheat, barley, rye, oats):**
- **Moisture:** 10-12%.
- **Storage life:** 5-10 years if stored cool and dry.
- **Container:** Glass jar or cloth bag.
- **Special needs:** Protect from rodents (vermin will eat grain).

**Legumes (beans, peas, lentils):**
- **Moisture:** 10-12%.
- **Storage life:** 3-5 years.
- **Container:** Glass jar or sealed envelope.
- **Special needs:** Some legumes attract beetles; store in cool conditions or use silica gel to prevent infestation.

**Vegetable seeds (tomato, cucumber, lettuce, radish):**
- **Moisture:** 8-10% (drier than grains).
- **Storage life:** 2-4 years.
- **Container:** Paper envelope in a glass jar, or sealed bag.
- **Special needs:** Very moisture-sensitive; use silica gel.

**Squash and pumpkin seeds:**
- **Moisture:** 8-10%.
- **Storage life:** 4-6 years.
- **Container:** Paper envelope or sealed bag.
- **Special needs:** Remove all pulp and stringy material (attracts mold).

**Carrot and beet seeds (biennials):**
- **Moisture:** 8-10%.
- **Storage life:** 2-3 years (shorter than most seeds).
- **Container:** Paper envelope.
- **Special needs:** Plant quickly; viability declines after 3 years.

## Part 4: Genetic Improvement Through Seed Saving

### Natural Selection

Each year you save seeds, you are naturally selecting for traits that thrive in your environment:
- **Year 1:** You plant commercial seed or heirloom seed.
- **Year 2:** You save seed from your best plants. These plants adapted best to your soil, climate, and pests.
- **Year 3:** You plant the saved seed. The new generation is slightly better adapted.
- **Year 10:** After 10 years of selection, your seed stock is highly adapted to your specific location.

### Traits to Select For

**Important (select for these):**
- **Early maturity:** Plants that mature earlier than others. Early-maturing varieties are valuable if your season is short.
- **Disease resistance:** Plants with no visible disease or pest damage.
- **High yield:** Plants that produce more grain/fruit than average.
- **Drought tolerance:** If water is scarce, select plants that thrived despite drought.

**Less important (do not over-select for these):**
- **Size:** Larger fruit is nice but not essential for calories.
- **Color:** Beauty is not necessary; function matters.
- **Flavor:** Important after survival is secure; not critical initially.

### Avoiding Inbreeding

Inbreeding (saving seed from only one or two plants) causes genetic problems (weak plants, poor yields, disease susceptibility).

**Prevention:**
- **Save seed from multiple plants:** Do not save seed from just your 1-2 best plants. Save from 10-20 good plants.
- **Mix pollen sources:** For crops that cross-pollinate (corn, squash), ensure pollen comes from multiple sources.
- **Rotate seed stock:** Every 5-10 years, introduce new seed (from another settlement or garden) to prevent inbreeding depression.

## Part 5: Special Cases

### Self-Pollinating vs. Cross-Pollinating Crops

**Self-pollinating crops (easier to save seed from):**
- Wheat, barley, rye, oats, beans, peas, tomatoes, lettuce.
- Naturally self-pollinate; little risk of cross-pollination.
- Can save seed from a single plant without inbreeding concerns (though 10+ plants is better).

**Cross-pollinating crops (harder to save seed from):**
- Corn, squash, pumpkins, cucumbers, melons, cabbage, broccoli.
- Pollinated by wind or insects; easily cross with related plants.
- Must isolate plants from other varieties (grow only 1 variety per season, or use distance isolation).
- Save seed from 10+ plants to avoid inbreeding.

### Biennial Crops

Crops that flower in their second year (carrots, beets, cabbage, broccoli, radishes):
- **Year 1:** Grow the plant for food.
- **Year 2:** Replant the best plants; they flower and produce seed.
- **Challenge:** These crops take 2 years for one seed cycle, making breeding slow.
- **Strategy:** Grow them for food primarily; save seed only from your best plants every other year.

### Hybrids vs. Heirlooms

**Heirloom varieties:** Old, stable varieties that breed true.
- **Advantage:** Seeds saved from this year produce identical plants next year.
- **Disadvantage:** Often lower yield than modern hybrids.

**Hybrid (F1) varieties:** Cross between two inbred lines; very uniform, high-yield.
- **Advantage:** High yield, uniform, often disease-resistant.
- **Disadvantage:** Seeds saved from hybrids produce weaker F2 plants (next generation is inferior).
- **Solution post-collapse:** Prioritize heirloom varieties or save seed only from open-pollinated varieties.

## Part 6: Seed Quantity Planning

### How Much Seed Per Hectare?

**Wheat, barley, rye:**
- 150-200 kg seed per hectare.
- 1 kg seed produces roughly 50 kg grain (so 1 hectare with 150 kg seed yields ~7500 kg grain).

**Beans:**
- 30-60 kg seed per hectare (lower planting rate than grain).
- 1 kg seed produces 10-15 kg beans.

**Potatoes:**
- 2000-3000 kg seed potatoes per hectare (yes, this is heavy; each plant is cut into pieces).
- 1 kg seed produces 5-10 kg potatoes.

**Squash/pumpkins:**
- 0.5-1 kg seed per hectare (very little; seeds are large and few are needed).
- Much lower seeding rate than grain.

### Calculation Example (200-person settlement)

**Goal:** Secure 1 year of calories for 200 people.

**Calculation:**
- Each person needs 150-200 kg grain per year (calories).
- 200 people × 150 kg = 30,000 kg grain needed.
- Wheat yield: 5000 kg/hectare (average).
- Hectares needed: 30,000 kg ÷ 5000 kg/hectare = 6 hectares.
- Seed needed: 6 hectares × 150 kg/hectare = 900 kg wheat seed.

**Seed saving** (for next year):
- Harvest: 6 hectares × 5000 kg/hectare = 30,000 kg.
- Save for seed: 10-20% = 3000-6000 kg (but only 900 kg is needed for replanting).
- Consume: 24,000-27,000 kg (enough for the year plus some stored).

**Extra seeds:** Save 6000+ kg (more than the 900 kg needed) as insurance against germination failure or replanting need.

## Verification

Your seed-saving system is working if:
1. **Selection:** You save seed only from your best plants (healthy, high-yield).
2. **Drying:** Seeds are fully dry before storage (hard, rattling).
3. **Storage:** Seeds are stored cool (0-15°C), dry, and dark.
4. **Quantity:** You save 10-20% of harvest as seed (safety margin above next year's planting need).
5. **Diversity:** You save seed from 10+ individual plants per crop (prevent inbreeding).
6. **Documentation:** You label all saved seeds with crop name, variety, and date.
7. **Germination test:** Before planting, test a sample of stored seed to confirm viability.

## Common Mistakes

**Saving seed from poor plants.** You will degrade your seed stock. Save only from your best plants.

**Saving seed from hybrids.** Hybrid F1 seeds do not breed true. Next generation is weaker.

**Storing wet seeds.** Moisture causes mold and premature germination. Dry seeds thoroughly before storing.

**Storing seeds in warm places.** Seeds age quickly at high temperature. Store cool (0-15°C if possible).

**Saving too little seed.** Save 10-20% of harvest as insurance. Too little seed leaves you vulnerable if germination is poor.

**Planting all seed-saving plants in one location.** If disease or pests hit that location, all your seed source is lost. Spread them around.
