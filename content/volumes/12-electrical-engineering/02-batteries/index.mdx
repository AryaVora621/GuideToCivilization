## Overview

A battery is a device that converts chemical energy into electrical energy. Unlike a power plant or generator (which produce electricity from mechanical motion or other sources), a battery stores energy in chemical form and releases it on demand.

Batteries are essential for:

1. **Portable lighting:** Flashlights, lanterns, and lamps powered by batteries work anywhere, without connection to a generator or water wheel
2. **Portable power:** Hand tools, portable communication devices, and mobile equipment depend on batteries
3. **Backup power:** Batteries provide power during power outages or when other sources are unavailable
4. **Storage:** Excess power from a generator or renewable source (water wheel, wind turbine) can be stored in batteries for later use
5. **Signaling:** Batteries power lights, bells, and horns for communication or alarms

In a post-collapse world with limited electrical infrastructure, the ability to make, maintain, and use batteries is valuable. This chapter covers battery chemistry, common battery types, how to build simple batteries, and how to maintain and troubleshoot them.

## Part 1: Battery Chemistry and Principles

### How Batteries Work: Electrochemistry

A battery works through oxidation-reduction reactions (redox reactions) between two different metals and an electrolyte.

**Key components:**

1. **Positive electrode (cathode):** A metal or compound that tends to accept electrons
2. **Negative electrode (anode):** A metal that tends to lose electrons
3. **Electrolyte:** A substance containing ions (charged particles) that allows electrons to flow internally

**How it works:**

1. Inside the battery, chemical reaction causes electrons to accumulate on the negative electrode
2. Electrons repel each other and want to move away
3. If an external circuit is connected (a light bulb, motor, or load), electrons flow from the negative electrode through the circuit to the positive electrode
4. Inside the battery, ions in the electrolyte complete the circuit by moving from one electrode to the other
5. The chemical reaction continues, pushing more electrons out of the negative electrode
6. Current flows as long as the chemical reactants are available

**Voltage:** The voltage (electrical potential) of a battery depends on the chemical reaction occurring. Different reactions produce different voltages:
- Zinc-copper reaction (Voltaic cell): approximately 1.1 volts
- Zinc-carbon reaction (Leclanché cell / dry cell): approximately 1.5 volts
- Lead-acid reaction: approximately 2 volts per cell (12-volt car battery = 6 cells in series)

**Current capacity:** The amount of current a battery can provide depends on its size and the conductivity of the electrolyte. A small AA battery might provide 1–2 amps; a large car battery can provide 100+ amps.

### Voltage and Current Relationships

**Voltage is the push; current is the flow.**

A battery's voltage is roughly constant (1.5V for a Leclanché cell) regardless of load, until the battery is depleted.

Current, however, depends on the load:

**Ohm's law:** Voltage = Current × Resistance
Or: Current = Voltage / Resistance

**Examples:**
- A 1.5V battery with a 10-ohm load produces current = 1.5 / 10 = 0.15 amps
- The same 1.5V battery with a 1-ohm load produces current = 1.5 / 1 = 1.5 amps
- A lower-resistance load draws more current

**Connecting batteries in series (to increase voltage):**
- One 1.5V battery: provides 1.5V
- Two 1.5V batteries in series: provide 3V
- Three 1.5V batteries in series: provide 4.5V

**Connecting batteries in parallel (to increase current capacity):**
- One 1.5V battery, 2 amp capacity: provides 1.5V, up to 2 amps
- Two 1.5V batteries in parallel: provide 1.5V, up to 4 amps total

### Battery Discharge and Lifespan

As a battery is used, the chemical reactants inside are consumed. Current decreases, and voltage drops.

**Discharge curve:** Plotting voltage versus time during discharge shows the typical behavior:
1. Initial voltage: full voltage (1.5V for new Leclanché cell)
2. Constant discharge: voltage drops slowly as chemical reactants are consumed
3. Final phase: voltage drops rapidly (battery is nearly empty)
4. Cutoff: voltage drops too low to power most devices

**Lifespan:** Depends on:
- Battery capacity (amount of chemical reactant stored)
- Discharge rate (how much current is drawn)
- Temperature (higher temperature accelerates reactions, shortening lifespan)
- Storage conditions (unused batteries slowly discharge)

**For intermittent use:** A D-size battery might power a flashlight for 10–15 hours. A small AA battery might power the same flashlight for 3–5 hours.

## Part 2: Common Battery Types

### Voltaic Cell (Galvanic Cell)

The simplest battery type, demonstrating basic principles.

**Construction:**
1. Take a glass jar or beaker
2. Fill with salt water or dilute acid (electrolyte)
3. Insert a zinc strip (negative electrode)
4. Insert a copper strip (positive electrode)
5. Connect wires to each electrode

**Voltage:** Approximately 1.1 volts (for zinc-copper)
**Current:** Moderate (depends on electrolyte conductivity and electrode size; typically 0.1–1 amp)
**Advantages:** Simple, easy to demonstrate, easy to understand chemistry
**Disadvantages:** Voltage drops quickly, not reliable for practical use, electrolyte can spill

**Voltaic cells are used for learning, not practical applications.** However, a farmer without access to commercial batteries could build multiple Voltaic cells and connect them in series to power low-voltage lights or communication devices.

### Leclanché Cell (Dry Cell)

The standard single-cell battery used in flashlights and small devices.

**Construction:**

1. **Zinc casing:** A zinc metal cylinder serves as the negative electrode and container
2. **Carbon rod:** A carbon rod at the center serves as the positive electrode
3. **Electrolyte:** A moist paste containing ammonium chloride (and sometimes manganese dioxide)
4. **Manganese dioxide:** Packed around the carbon rod; serves as a depolarizer (reduces polarization effects that limit current)

**Assembly process (simplified):**
1. Prepare a cylindrical zinc casing (or use salvaged casing)
2. Create a mixture: manganese dioxide powder + ammonium chloride + water (creating a moist paste)
3. Pack the paste around the carbon rod (the rod should be centered)
4. Seal the casing at the top with an insulating layer (pitch, wax, or plastic)
5. Provide terminals for connecting external circuit

**Voltage:** Approximately 1.5 volts
**Current capacity:** Depends on size; typical AA battery: 1–2 amps for short bursts; AAA battery: 0.5–1 amp
**Advantages:** Sealed (no spillage), reliable, portable, widely manufactured
**Disadvantages:** Single-use only (non-rechargeable dry cells), voltage declines gradually, unsuitable for very high current loads

**Modern improvements:** Alkaline dry cells use different chemistry and last 3–5x longer than older carbon-zinc cells.

### Lead-Acid Battery

Used in vehicles and backup power systems.

**Construction:**

1. **Lead plates:** Positive plates (lead dioxide, brown) and negative plates (pure lead, gray)
2. **Separator:** Porous material between plates, allowing ionic flow but preventing physical contact
3. **Electrolyte:** Dilute sulfuric acid (H2SO4 + water)
4. **Container:** Hard plastic or lead-sealed case, divided into cells

**Cell structure:** A typical car battery has 6 cells, each providing approximately 2 volts. Total: 12 volts.

**Chemistry:**
- Discharge reaction: Pb + PbO2 + 2H2SO4 → 2PbSO4 + 2H2O
- This reaction creates electric current and consumes the lead plates and sulfuric acid

**Recharging:** Lead-acid batteries are rechargeable. Applying reverse voltage (charging) reverses the reaction, restoring lead and sulfuric acid.

**Voltage:** 2 volts per cell (12 volts total for 6 cells)
**Current capacity:** Large; a car battery can provide 400–1000 amps briefly
**Advantages:** Rechargeable, high current capacity, reliable, long lifespan (10+ years)
**Disadvantages:** Heavy, contains corrosive acid, cannot be discharged completely (sulfation damages plates), requires ventilation during charging (hydrogen gas evolution)

## Part 3: Building and Maintaining Batteries

### Building a Simple Leclanché Cell

**Materials:**
- Zinc casing (salvaged from old battery or made from zinc sheet, 5 cm diameter, 8 cm height)
- Carbon rod (graphite or salvaged from flashlight battery, 1 cm diameter, 7 cm length)
- Manganese dioxide powder: 50 grams
- Ammonium chloride powder: 20 grams
- Zinc chloride (optional, improves performance): 10 grams
- Water: 30 milliliters
- Paper separator: thin, porous
- Wires and terminals for electrical connection

**Assembly (simplified):**

1. **Prepare the electrolyte mixture (15 minutes):**
   - Mix ammonium chloride + zinc chloride + manganese dioxide powder
   - Add water slowly, stirring, until the mixture reaches a moist paste consistency (not soggy, not dry)

2. **Insert separator and electrode (15 minutes):**
   - Cut a strip of porous paper and line the inside of the zinc casing
   - Insert the carbon rod at the center, ensuring it is straight and centered

3. **Pack the electrolyte (15 minutes):**
   - Carefully pack the moist paste around the carbon rod
   - Avoid pressing too hard (pack firmly but loosely enough that ionic flow is not impeded)
   - The paste should surround the carbon rod completely

4. **Seal the battery (15 minutes):**
   - Cover the top with insulating material (pitch, wax, or epoxy)
   - Ensure no electrolyte seeps out
   - Create terminals for positive (connected to carbon rod) and negative (connected to zinc casing)

5. **Test (15 minutes):**
   - Measure voltage: should be approximately 1.5V
   - Connect a small load (light bulb or resistor) and verify current flows
   - If voltage is correct, the battery is ready for use

**Yield and performance:**
- A single cell provides 1.5V
- Multiple cells in series: 3 cells = 4.5V, 4 cells = 6V, 6 cells = 9V, 8 cells = 12V
- A hand-built Leclanché cell has lower performance than commercial cells but is functional
- Lifespan: 20–40 hours of intermittent use (less than commercial batteries, but adequate for basic needs)

### Maintaining and Testing Batteries

**Visual inspection:**
- Check for corrosion (white or green powdery coating on terminals)
- Look for cracks or bulging (indicates internal pressure; do not use)
- Ensure no electrolyte is leaking

**Voltage test:**
- Use a multimeter to measure open-circuit voltage (voltage when no load is connected)
- A healthy battery should read within 10% of rated voltage
- A 1.5V battery should read 1.35–1.65V
- A 12V battery should read 10.8–13.2V

**Load test:**
- Connect a known load (resistor or light bulb) and measure voltage under load
- Voltage should drop only slightly (10–20%) when load is applied
- If voltage drops more than 50%, the battery is weak or dying

**Current test (for checking a battery's ability to deliver current):**
- Connect a variable load resistance
- Increase the resistance gradually, monitoring current
- A healthy battery maintains current even at low resistance
- If current drops quickly as resistance decreases, the battery is failing

**For lead-acid batteries (advanced tests):**

1. **Specific gravity test:**
   - Use a hydrometer (specialized tool) to measure electrolyte density
   - Healthy battery: specific gravity 1.25–1.30
   - Low specific gravity indicates sulfation or discharge

2. **Cell voltage test:**
   - Test each of the 6 cells individually
   - Healthy cells: 2.0–2.1V per cell
   - A weak cell (under 1.5V) indicates internal damage

### Troubleshooting Common Battery Problems

**Problem 1: Battery does not provide voltage**

Causes:
- Internal short circuit
- Dead cell (lead-acid only)
- Corroded terminals (open circuit)

Solution:
- Clean terminals with a wire brush; retry
- Test individual cells (lead-acid)
- If internal short is suspected, discard (not repairable)

**Problem 2: Voltage is correct but no current flows**

Causes:
- Open circuit (wire disconnected or broken)
- Corroded terminals (high resistance connection)
- Weak cell (cannot supply current)

Solution:
- Check all connections; ensure wires are secure
- Clean corroded terminals
- Test with high-resistance load (if current flows, the battery is weak but functional)

**Problem 3: Battery becomes hot during use**

Causes:
- Excessive current draw (resistance too low)
- Battery being overcharged (lead-acid)
- Internal short circuit

Solution:
- Reduce current draw by increasing load resistance
- Stop charging (lead-acid) and allow cooling
- If heat continues, discard the battery (risk of explosion or fire)

**Problem 4: Electrolyte leaks from battery**

Causes:
- Casing damage (crack, corrosion)
- Overpressure inside (gas generation)

Solution:
- Stop using immediately (leaking electrolyte is caustic)
- Clean up spilled electrolyte with water
- Discard battery and replace

### Building and Using Battery Banks

For applications requiring higher voltage or current:

**Battery bank in series (to increase voltage):**
- 4 × 1.5V batteries in series = 6V
- Same current capacity as a single battery
- Use for devices requiring higher voltage (small motors, bright LEDs)

**Battery bank in parallel (to increase current capacity):**
- 4 × 1.5V batteries in parallel = 1.5V
- 4× the current capacity of a single battery
- Use for devices drawing high current but operating at standard voltage

**Mixed series-parallel (to increase both):**
- 2 groups of 3 batteries in series (3 × 1.5V = 4.5V), connected in parallel
- Provides 4.5V at 2× current capacity
- More complex but flexible for custom applications

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Powering a Flashlight

**Situation:** You have a flashlight that requires 3V and draws 0.5 amps. You have AA batteries (1.5V, 1 amp capacity).

**Solution:**
- Two AA batteries in series = 3V
- Current capacity: 1 amp (limited by the single battery)
- The flashlight draws 0.5 amps, which is within the battery's capacity
- Lifespan: A typical AA battery with 2 amp-hour capacity would last 4 hours at 0.5 amp draw

**Setup:** 
- Connect two AA batteries in series (positive of first to negative of second)
- Connect the flashlight between the open positive (first battery) and open negative (second battery)

### Example 2: Charging a Lead-Acid Battery

**Situation:** A 12V car battery has been drained (voltage reads 10.5V). You need to recharge it using a generator producing 14V at 5 amps.

**Process:**

1. **Connect the charger:**
   - Positive terminal of generator → Positive terminal of battery
   - Negative terminal of generator → Negative terminal of battery

2. **Monitor charging:**
   - Voltage will rise toward 14V
   - Current will decrease as the battery charges (high current initially, dropping toward zero)
   - Charging continues until voltage reaches 13.5–14V and current approaches zero

3. **Duration:**
   - Battery capacity: typically 40–50 amp-hours for a car battery
   - Charging at 5 amps: approximately 8–10 hours
   - Faster charging (higher current) reduces duration but generates more heat

4. **Stop charging:**
   - When voltage reaches 14V and current drops below 0.5 amps, charging is complete
   - Over-charging risks damage (excessive heat, hydrogen gas evolution, water loss)

### Example 3: Building a 6V Power Supply for Remote Equipment

**Situation:** Remote monitoring equipment requires 6V at up to 2 amps for 48 hours continuously.

**Design:**

1. **Voltage requirement:** 6V → Use 4 × 1.5V batteries in series

2. **Current and duration:**
   - Current draw: 2 amps continuous for 48 hours
   - Total amp-hours needed: 2 amps × 48 hours = 96 amp-hours
   - Typical AA battery capacity: 2 amp-hours
   - Batteries needed per set: 96 / 2 = 48 AA batteries

3. **Series-parallel configuration:**
   - 4 batteries in series (for 6V)
   - 12 parallel strings (48 batteries / 4) = 12 amp capacity
   - This configuration provides 6V at 12 amp capacity
   - For a 2-amp draw, the battery bank can supply current for 48 hours (12 amps / 2 amps draw = 6 hour-unit = 96 hours, but accounting for discharge curve, approximately 40–50 hours realistic)

4. **Storage and rotation:**
   - Build multiple battery banks
   - When one bank is depleted, switch to the next
   - Rotate depleted batteries back to a collection point for recycling or refurbishment

## Common Mistakes

1. **Connecting batteries with opposite polarities:** If you reverse one battery in a series circuit, voltages cancel. Always ensure positive and negative terminals are correctly aligned.

2. **Over-discharging lead-acid batteries:** Discharging below 10.5V causes sulfation (lead sulfate crystals that cannot be reversed). This permanently damages the battery. Use charge controllers that prevent deep discharge.

3. **Mixing old and new batteries:** Old (weak) batteries in series with new batteries can cause reverse current through the old batteries, damaging them. Use matched sets of batteries from the same batch/age.

4. **Exposing batteries to extreme heat:** Heat accelerates chemical reactions, reducing lifespan. Store batteries in cool conditions. Do not leave batteries in direct sunlight or near heat sources.

5. **Using the wrong battery type for the load:** A dry cell cannot supply high currents continuously. A lead-acid battery is too heavy for a flashlight. Match battery type to application.

6. **Ignoring battery terminal corrosion:** Corrosion (white or green coating) creates high resistance and reduces effective voltage. Clean terminals regularly with a wire brush and apply a thin coat of grease to prevent oxidation.

7. **Assuming all 1.5V batteries are equivalent:** Different battery types (Leclanché, alkaline, lithium) have different internal resistance and current capacity. Alkaline batteries last 3–5x longer than carbon-zinc batteries.

8. **Shorting the battery (connecting + and - directly):** This creates maximum current draw, heating the battery dangerously. A shorted battery can explode or catch fire. Always use a load (resistor or device).

9. **Recharging non-rechargeable batteries:** Dry cells (Leclanché) should not be recharged. Attempting to recharge can cause leaks or rupture. Only rechargeable batteries (lead-acid, nickel-cadmium, lithium) should be charged.

10. **Not storing batteries properly:** Batteries self-discharge in storage. Lead-acid batteries can sulfate if stored in a drained state. Store batteries fully charged, in cool conditions, and check them periodically.
