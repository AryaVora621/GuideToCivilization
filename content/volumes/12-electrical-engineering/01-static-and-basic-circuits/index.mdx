## Overview

Electricity is the movement of charged particles (electrons) through a material. Understanding electricity requires understanding three concepts:

1. **Static electricity:** Charges at rest, building up on surfaces (friction, induction).
2. **Current:** The flow of charges through a conductor in a circuit.
3. **Circuits:** Paths through which current flows, doing useful work (lighting, heating, moving machines).

In a post-collapse world, electricity is limited but still valuable. A hand-crank generator, a battery salvaged from a car, or a water wheel powering a generator can provide power for lighting, heating, communication, or small machinery. Understanding basic circuits allows you to:

- Build and troubleshoot simple power systems
- Prevent waste and damage (overloads, short circuits)
- Safely work with voltage and current
- Understand power distribution and storage

This chapter covers the fundamentals: how static electricity works, the difference between conductors and insulators, how current flows in a circuit, and how to build a simple circuit safely.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of atoms and electrons (electrons are negatively charged particles orbiting atomic nuclei)
- Ability to identify and measure with simple tools
- Comfort with basic math (multiplication, division, simple ratios)
- Familiarity with metals (copper, iron, aluminum) and common materials

## Part 1: Static Electricity

### Understanding Charge

All materials are made of atoms. Atoms contain:
- **Protons** (positively charged, in the nucleus)
- **Neutrons** (neutral, in the nucleus)
- **Electrons** (negatively charged, orbiting the nucleus)

In a neutral atom, the number of electrons equals the number of protons, so the net charge is zero.

**Charge imbalance:**
- If an atom gains electrons, it becomes negatively charged (anion)
- If an atom loses electrons, it becomes positively charged (cation)

Static electricity is the accumulation of charge on a surface.

### How Static Charge Builds: Friction

When two materials rub together, electrons can transfer from one to the other:

1. **Rubbing wool with a balloon:** Wool atoms lose electrons to the rubber balloon. The balloon becomes negatively charged (excess electrons). The wool becomes positively charged (deficit of electrons).

2. **Rubbing a comb through hair:** Hair atoms lose electrons to the plastic comb. The comb becomes negatively charged. The hair becomes positively charged.

The result: both objects now carry static charge.

### Step 1: Observe and Measure Static Charge

**Experiment 1: Attracting paper with a charged object**

1. Rub a plastic comb through your hair vigorously (30 seconds)
2. Hold the comb a few millimeters above small pieces of paper or pith balls
3. Observe: The paper pieces jump toward the comb (attraction)

Why this happens:
- The comb has a negative charge (excess electrons)
- The paper is neutral, but the negative charge on the comb repels electrons in the paper's surface, leaving the surface positively charged (relatively)
- Opposite charges attract; the paper is pulled toward the comb

**Experiment 2: Repulsion between similarly charged objects**

1. Charge two balloons by rubbing them with wool (both balloons gain electrons, both become negatively charged)
2. Hang both balloons from strings suspended close to each other
3. Observe: The balloons push away from each other (repulsion)

Why this happens:
- Both balloons have excess electrons (both negatively charged)
- Like charges repel; the balloons push apart

**Key observation:**
- Opposite charges attract (positive attracts negative)
- Like charges repel (negative repels negative; positive repels positive)

### Step 2: Understand the Direction and Strength

Static charge accumulates on surfaces and exerts forces on nearby charged objects.

The **electric field** is an invisible region of influence around a charged object:
- A positive charge creates a field that pushes other positive charges away and pulls negative charges toward it
- A negative charge creates a field that pushes other negative charges away and pulls positive charges toward it

The strength of the field decreases with distance. A strongly charged object exerts a stronger force on nearby objects.

### Step 3: Discharge (Removing Static Charge)

Charge dissipates when a path of electrons forms between the charged object and ground:

1. **Slow discharge:** Touch a charged balloon to a conductor (metal). Electrons flow from the balloon to the conductor and to ground (Earth). The balloon loses its charge gradually.

2. **Rapid discharge (spark):** If the voltage is high enough and the gap is small enough, electrons jump through the air in a visible arc. This is a spark or static discharge.

Lightning is a massive static discharge between clouds and ground.

## Part 2: Conductors and Insulators

### Definition and Properties

**Conductors** are materials that allow electrons to move freely through them.

- Electrons in atoms are loosely bound to nuclei
- When voltage is applied, electrons move easily
- Examples: copper, aluminum, iron, silver, seawater, salt water

**Insulators** are materials that resist electron movement.

- Electrons are tightly bound to atoms
- Even with voltage applied, electrons move very slowly (almost not at all)
- Examples: rubber, plastic, glass, dry wood, ceramic, paper, air

**Semiconductors** (not covered here, but important) are materials between conductors and insulators. Silicon and germanium are semiconductors; they conduct under certain conditions.

### Comparing Conductors

Different conductors have different **resistivity**—a measure of how much they resist current flow.

**Common conductors ranked by resistivity (lowest to highest, best to worst):**
1. Silver (lowest resistivity, best conductor)
2. Copper (slightly higher, but excellent and cheaper than silver)
3. Aluminum (about 1.5x copper)
4. Iron (about 6x copper)
5. Nichrome (about 60x copper; used in heating elements because it resists current and produces heat)

For most electrical systems, copper is the standard choice: it conducts well and is affordable.

### Step 1: Identify Conductors and Insulators in Your Environment

Examine common materials:

- **Copper wire:** Conductor (easily)
- **Rubber coating on wire:** Insulator (protects your hand from the conductor inside)
- **Glass:** Insulator (electricity cannot flow through it; it is used to insulate high-voltage lines)
- **Dry wood:** Insulator (electrons are trapped in the wood's structure)
- **Wet wood:** Partly conductive (water contains dissolved ions that carry charge; wet conditions allow current to flow where dry conditions would not)
- **Salt water:** Conductor (dissolved ions carry charge)

**Key insight:** Water purity matters. Pure distilled water is a poor conductor. Tap water with minerals is a better conductor. Salt water is an excellent conductor.

### Step 2: Understand Why Conductivity Varies

Conductors work because they have **free electrons**—electrons that are not tightly bound to atoms and can move when voltage is applied.

Metals have many free electrons because:
- Atoms in metals are packed tightly
- Electrons in the outer shell of metal atoms are weakly bound
- When voltage is applied, these electrons move

Insulators have few free electrons because:
- Electrons are tightly bound to atoms
- Electrons cannot move even when voltage is applied

This difference in electron availability determines conductivity.

### Step 3: The Importance of Insulation

Electrical systems must be insulated to:

1. **Prevent shock:** Insulation around a conductor prevents your skin from touching the conductor and receiving an electric shock
2. **Prevent fires:** Bare conductors touching flammable materials can cause arcs and ignition
3. **Prevent short circuits:** Insulation between conductors prevents unintended paths for current
4. **Prevent loss:** Insulation around wires prevents current from leaking into surroundings

## Part 3: Basic Circuits

### Circuit Anatomy

A circuit is a closed path through which current flows. All circuits have:

1. **A power source** (battery, generator, solar panel): pushes current
2. **A load** (light bulb, resistor, motor): uses the energy
3. **Conductors** (wires): carry current from source to load and back
4. **A switch** (optional but practical): opens and closes the circuit to turn it on and off

Current flows from the positive terminal of the power source, through the load (where it does work), and back to the negative terminal. This forms a complete loop.

### Step 1: Build a Simple Series Circuit

A series circuit is one where components are connected in a line: current flows from the battery through one component, then the next, and back to the battery.

**Materials:**
- 1.5V battery (AA or similar)
- Light bulb with base rated for 1.5V
- Two pieces of copper wire (gauge 14–18 AWG, about 6 inches each)
- Switch (optional; a loose wire can turn the circuit on and off)

**Assembly:**

1. Identify the battery terminals: positive (+) and negative (−)
2. Strip 0.5 inches of insulation from the end of each wire
3. Connect one wire from the positive terminal of the battery to one terminal of the light bulb base
4. Connect the second wire from the other terminal of the light bulb to the negative terminal of the battery
5. If using a switch: disconnect one wire from the battery and insert the switch between them

When the circuit is complete (all connections touching), current flows and the bulb lights. When you break the connection (open the switch), current stops and the bulb goes dark.

**What is happening:**
- Electrons flow from the negative terminal of the battery through the wire to the light bulb
- Inside the bulb, electrons push through a thin filament (a conductor designed to resist current and produce heat and light)
- Electrons exit the filament and return through the wire to the positive terminal of the battery

### Step 2: Understand Series vs. Parallel

**Series circuit (one path):**
- Components are connected in a line
- Current flows through one component, then the next
- If one component fails (breaks), the circuit opens and no current flows (all components go dark)
- Example: old Christmas lights (one burned-out bulb darkened the whole string)

**Parallel circuit (multiple paths):**
- Components are connected side-by-side, each with its own path to the battery
- Current can flow through one component or another (or both)
- If one component fails, others continue to work
- Example: outlets in a room; if one device stops, others keep working

For practical systems, parallel circuits are preferred because failure of one component does not stop the whole system.

### Step 3: Understand Voltage Drop and Load

When current flows through a component, some of the electrical energy is converted to another form (heat, light, motion). The voltage decreases as current flows through the component. This is called **voltage drop**.

Example:
- A 1.5V battery powering a light bulb
- Voltage at the positive terminal: 1.5V
- Voltage at the negative terminal: 0V
- Voltage drop across the bulb: 1.5V (almost all of it; the rest is lost in wires and connections)

If you use a multimeter to measure voltage across the bulb, you will read approximately 1.5V. This is the voltage available to power the bulb.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Choosing the Right Battery

You have a light bulb rated at 3V. You have:
- A 1.5V AA battery
- Two AA batteries connected in series (1.5V + 1.5V = 3V total)
- A 12V car battery

**Correct choice:** Two AA batteries in series (3V)

**Why:**
- One AA battery (1.5V) produces too little voltage; the bulb will be dim or not light at all
- Two AA batteries in series produce 3V, matching the bulb's rating; the bulb will light normally
- A 12V battery produces too much voltage; it will damage the bulb (filament burns out), the bulb fails, and you have wasted a valuable battery

**Lesson:** Match voltage. Insufficient voltage: dimness or failure. Excessive voltage: destruction.

### Example 2: Identifying a Short Circuit

You connect a 9V battery to a light bulb with wire that touches the wrong terminal. The circuit appears complete, but the bulb does not light and the wire becomes very hot.

**What happened:** A short circuit. Current is flowing directly from the positive terminal back to the negative terminal, bypassing the bulb. All the voltage drops are in the wire (which heats up), and no voltage is left for the bulb.

**Fix:** Disconnect immediately. Identify where the wires are touching unexpectedly. Reroute them or add insulation.

**Prevention:** Use insulated wire, route wires carefully, and use a switch rather than hand-connecting wires.

### Example 3: Extending a Circuit

You want to power a light bulb in a room using a battery in another room. You have 30 feet of copper wire (gauge 12 AWG).

**Process:**
1. Measure the distance from the battery to the light (30 feet to the bulb, 30 feet back = 60 feet total)
2. Estimate voltage drop: copper wire gauge 12 carries current with low resistance. Over 60 feet with moderate current (under 5A), voltage drop is typically under 0.1V (acceptable)
3. Connect the battery to one end of the wire
4. Connect the bulb to the other end
5. Complete the circuit with a return wire

The bulb should light; voltage drop in a short run of thick wire is minimal.

## Verification

**You understand basic circuits and static electricity when:**

1. You can explain why static builds up (friction transfers electrons) and how it discharges (electrons flow to ground)
2. You can identify conductors and insulators in your environment and explain why they behave differently
3. You can build a simple series circuit with a battery, bulb, and switch, and predict what happens when you open/close the switch
4. You understand voltage drop and can choose the correct battery voltage for a device
5. You can identify a short circuit and explain why it is dangerous (excessive current, heating, fire risk)
6. You can design a simple parallel circuit and explain why it is better for powering multiple devices

## Common Mistakes

1. **Assuming all electricity is the same:** Static electricity, current in circuits, and lightning are all the same phenomenon (charge movement), but they differ in scale, speed, and how they are generated. Understanding these differences is crucial for safety.

2. **Underestimating static electricity:** Static discharge can damage sensitive electronics and ignite flammable materials. Treat static carefully, even if it seems weak.

3. **Confusing voltage, current, and resistance:** These are three separate properties. Voltage is the push (potential difference). Current is the flow (amount of charge per second). Resistance is the opposition. This is covered in the next chapter, but do not assume they are the same.

4. **Ignoring polarity:** Batteries have positive and negative terminals. Reversing them changes the direction of current. For some devices (motors, LEDs), reversed polarity causes failure. For others (resistive loads like bulbs), direction does not matter.

5. **Oversizing conductors:** Using thicker wire than necessary wastes material. Use the appropriate gauge for the current. Too thin: overheating and fire. Too thick: wasted cost and weight.

6. **Assuming water is always conductive:** Pure water is a poor conductor. Tap water, salt water, and mineral water conduct well. This matters for safety (wet hands on a bare conductor are dangerous).

7. **Neglecting insulation:** Insulation is not optional. Bare conductors cause shocks, fires, and short circuits. Always cover conductors with appropriate insulation.

8. **Forgetting to open the switch:** Always open the switch before working on a circuit. Unexpected current is the leading cause of electrical injury.
