A generator is the single most important machine for rebuilding electrical civilization. Every light bulb, radio, charging circuit, and electric tool depends on a source of electricity, and outside of batteries and solar panels there is only one practical way to make it in quantity: spin a magnet near a coil of wire. Master this machine and you can convert the energy of falling water, wind, steam, or muscle into usable power.

This chapter teaches you to build a generator from first principles. You will learn why a generator produces electricity, how to choose between the major designs, how to wind coils and arrange magnets, and how to verify and troubleshoot the finished machine. The same principles, run in reverse, build an electric motor, so this knowledge unlocks half of all electrical machinery.

## Why a Generator Works

A generator exploits **electromagnetic induction**: when a magnetic field through a loop of wire changes, a voltage appears across the ends of the wire. The faster the field changes, and the more loops of wire you have, the higher the voltage.

There are two ways to change the field through a coil:

1. **Move a magnet** past a stationary coil.
2. **Move a coil** through a stationary magnet's field.

Both work. The voltage produced depends on three things you control:

- **Magnetic field strength** (stronger magnets, smaller air gaps)
- **Number of turns** in the coil (more turns, more voltage)
- **Speed of change** (faster rotation, more voltage)

This is the core equation in words: voltage is proportional to turns multiplied by the rate of change of magnetic flux. You do not need calculus to use it. You need to remember that to get more voltage you add turns or spin faster, and to get more current you use thicker wire and stronger magnets.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding that a moving magnet induces voltage in a nearby wire
- Ability to identify magnetic poles and test magnet strength
- Basic circuit knowledge: voltage, current, resistance, and how to measure them
- Ability to wind wire neatly and count turns
- Access to a rotating power source (hand crank, treadle, waterwheel, or windmill)

## Materials & Tools

**Essential:**
- **Magnets:** Permanent magnets are the easiest start. Scavenge them from old loudspeakers, hard disk drives (very strong neodymium magnets), microwave ovens (large ferrite magnets), and motors. Stronger is better.
- **Copper wire:** Insulated magnet wire is ideal. Salvage it from transformers, dead motors, or old appliances. You will need tens to hundreds of meters depending on design.
- **Iron core material:** Soft iron, mild steel laminations, or transformer cores. Iron concentrates the magnetic field and dramatically increases output.
- **A shaft and bearings:** A steel rod for the axle, plus bearings (salvaged ball bearings, or hardwood blocks with grease as plain bearings).
- **A frame:** Wood, metal, or a combination, rigid enough to hold the air gap constant.

**For winding and finishing:**
- A bobbin or form to wind coils on
- Fine sandpaper or a flame to strip enamel insulation from wire ends
- A multimeter or, failing that, a small light bulb or compass for testing
- Diodes (for rectification, if you want DC output) salvaged from any electronics

**Optional but valuable:**
- A hand drill or lathe to help wind coils evenly
- Epoxy, shellac, or varnish to lock windings in place

## Part 1: Choosing a Generator Design

Three designs are practical to build by hand. Choose based on your materials and skill.

### Design A: Axial Flux Disc Generator (recommended for beginners)

Two discs face each other. One disc (the rotor) carries magnets arranged in a ring with alternating poles facing out. The other disc (the stator) carries flat coils. As the magnet disc spins, each coil sees the field reverse repeatedly, generating alternating current.

**Advantages:** No iron core required in the simplest version, easy to scale by adding magnets and coils, tolerant of imperfect construction, ideal for low-speed sources like windmills.

**Disadvantages:** Needs many magnets, and the air gap must be held precisely.

### Design B: Radial Drum Generator (the classic dynamo)

Magnets or coils sit on a cylindrical rotor that spins inside a ring of coils or magnets. This is the shape of most salvaged motors and car alternators.

**Advantages:** Compact, strong output, the familiar shape you can copy from any dead motor.

**Disadvantages:** Tighter tolerances, harder to wind coils into slots.

### Design C: Salvaged Motor Conversion (fastest result)

Almost any permanent-magnet DC motor becomes a generator if you spin its shaft. Stepper motors and brushed DC motors from printers, drills, and treadmills work immediately. Spin the shaft and measure voltage at the terminals.

**Advantages:** Near-zero construction, proven design, built-in bearings.

**Disadvantages:** Output limited by the donor motor, and you do not learn to wind coils.

Start with Design C to prove your power source works, then build Design A for serious output.

## Part 2: Permanent Magnet vs. Field Coil

A generator needs a magnetic field. You get it one of two ways:

- **Permanent magnets:** Simple, reliable, no extra wiring, produce power the instant the rotor turns. The right choice when you have good magnets.
- **Field coils (electromagnets):** Wire wound around iron, energized by some of the generator's own output. This is how large industrial generators work, and it lets you control output voltage by adjusting field current. It requires a small starting current (residual magnetism in the iron usually provides enough to "self-excite").

For a first build, use permanent magnets. Move to field coils only when you need adjustable output or cannot get strong magnets.

## Part 3: Winding the Coils

The coils are where electricity is born. Wind them carefully.

### Step 1: Choose Wire Gauge

Thicker wire carries more current but takes more space and gives fewer turns per coil. Thinner wire gives more turns (more voltage) but higher resistance (more heat, less current). For a small generator charging batteries, a medium gauge (roughly 0.6 to 1.0 mm copper diameter) is a sound compromise.

### Step 2: Decide Turns Per Coil

More turns means more voltage at a given speed. For a hand-cranked or wind-driven generator turning a few hundred RPM, plan on 100 to 200 turns per coil to reach useful voltages near 12 to 24 volts. Test and adjust: if voltage is below your target, add turns or spin faster.

### Step 3: Wind Neatly

1. Wrap wire around your form in tight, even layers.
2. Count every turn. Keep a tally; do not guess.
3. Wind all coils in the same direction and with the same turn count so their outputs add correctly.
4. Leave both wire ends long enough to connect later.
5. Lock the finished coil with varnish, epoxy, or tightly wrapped thread so vibration cannot loosen it.

### Step 4: Connect Coils in the Right Phase

In a multi-coil generator, coils must be wired so their voltages add rather than cancel. As the rotor turns, adjacent coils see opposite magnet poles, so adjacent coils must be wound or connected in opposite sense. The simple test: connect two coils, spin the rotor, and measure output. If combined voltage is roughly the sum of the individual coils, the phasing is correct. If it is near zero, reverse the connections on one coil.

## Part 4: Arranging the Magnets

1. **Alternate the poles.** Around the rotor, magnets must alternate north-out, south-out, north-out. This makes the field through each coil reverse as the rotor turns, which is what generates voltage.
2. **Match magnet count to coil count.** A common, forgiving ratio is 4 magnets to 3 coils per phase, but a simple beginner build can use equal numbers. Equal counts produce single-phase output; offset ratios produce smoother multi-phase output.
3. **Secure magnets firmly.** Spinning magnets that come loose are dangerous projectiles. Glue them into recesses and, if possible, wrap the rotor edge with strong tape or a band.
4. **Keep the air gap small and even.** The distance between magnet face and coil is the single biggest factor in output after magnet strength. Aim for the smallest gap that still clears safely as the rotor spins, often 1 to 3 mm. A wobbling rotor that needs a large gap will rob you of most of your power.

## Part 5: Assembling the Machine

1. **Mount the bearings** in the frame so the shaft spins freely with no side play.
2. **Fix the rotor** (magnet disc or drum) to the shaft, balanced so it does not vibrate.
3. **Mount the stator** (coils) at the chosen air gap, rigidly, so it cannot shift under magnetic pull (the attraction between rotor and stator can be strong).
4. **Route the coil leads** out to a terminal block where you can connect a load.
5. **Spin by hand first** to confirm the rotor clears the stator everywhere with no scraping.

## Part 6: AC, DC, and Rectification

A spinning generator naturally produces **alternating current (AC)**: the voltage swings positive, then negative, as poles pass each coil. AC is fine for some uses and is what the wider power grid uses, but charging batteries and running most salvaged electronics needs **direct current (DC)**.

Convert AC to DC with a **rectifier**, a ring of diodes that lets current pass only one way:

- A **single diode** gives crude half-wave DC (wastes half the output).
- A **bridge of four diodes** gives full-wave DC and is the standard choice. For a three-phase generator, use six diodes.

Add a **capacitor** across the DC output to smooth the pulsing into steadier voltage. To charge a battery, feed the rectified output through a blocking diode so the battery cannot drive current back into the generator when it stops.

## Worked Example: A Windmill Battery Charger

Goal: charge a 12-volt battery from a small windmill turning about 300 RPM.

1. **Design:** Axial flux disc, 12 magnets on the rotor, 9 coils on the stator (a 4:3 ratio, three-phase).
2. **Coils:** 150 turns each of 0.8 mm magnet wire, wound and varnished.
3. **Magnets:** Salvaged neodymium magnets from hard drives, alternating poles, set into a wooden disc, air gap about 2 mm.
4. **Output:** Three phases rectified by a six-diode bridge, smoothed with a salvaged capacitor, fed to the battery through a blocking diode.
5. **Result at 300 RPM:** Roughly 14 to 16 volts open-circuit, enough to push charge into a 12-volt battery. If voltage comes in below 14 volts, the fix is more turns per coil or a smaller air gap; if the windmill struggles to turn under load, reduce turns or accept lower current.

This single machine, multiplied and scaled, is the backbone of off-grid electricity.

## Verification Criteria

Your generator works correctly when:

- **It spins freely** by hand with a steady, even air gap and no scraping.
- **Voltage rises with speed.** Spin faster, measure higher voltage. If voltage does not change with speed, the magnets are not alternating or a coil connection is wrong.
- **Output reaches your target voltage** at the working speed of your power source.
- **It delivers current under load.** Connect a small bulb; it should light, and the rotor should become noticeably harder to turn (you are now feeling the electrical power you produce).
- **DC output is steady** after rectification and smoothing, with correct polarity for charging.

## Common Mistakes

- **Air gap too large.** The most common cause of weak output. Tighten and true the rotor so you can shrink the gap.
- **Coils wired out of phase.** Voltages cancel and output stays near zero. Test coils in pairs and reverse connections until they add.
- **Counting turns carelessly.** Mismatched coils produce uneven, partly cancelling output. Tally every turn.
- **Weak or non-alternating magnets.** Verify each magnet's polarity before mounting, and use the strongest magnets you have.
- **Loose magnets or windings.** At speed these fail violently. Glue, band, and varnish everything.
- **Forgetting the blocking diode.** Without it, a connected battery drains back through the generator when it stops turning.
- **Writing the air gap as a broken value.** When recording specs, write "under 3 mm" rather than the broken-tag form, so your notes survive copying into other documents.
