## Overview

The ocean is not static. Water moves in two primary ways:

1. **Tides:** Regular vertical (up-and-down) movements of water level, driven primarily by the moon's gravity, with secondary effects from the sun
2. **Currents:** Horizontal movements of water, caused by wind, density differences, and tidal flows interacting with land

For maritime civilizations, understanding tides and currents is essential for:

1. **Navigation:** Currents can push a ship off course. Tides create safe and dangerous water states in harbors and narrow channels.
2. **Harbors and settlements:** A harbor with extreme tidal range exposes boats to stranding at low tide or flooding at high tide. Understanding tidal range is critical for planning harbors and seawalls.
3. **Fishing:** Tides drive fish movements. Fishing is more productive at certain tidal states.
4. **Power:** Tidal currents and water level differences can be harnessed for power generation (mills, turbines).
5. **Safety:** Extreme currents near headlands, in narrow channels, or during unusual tidal events can swamp boats. Predicting and avoiding these situations saves lives.

This chapter covers the mechanics of tides and currents, how to predict them using basic observations, and how to navigate safely in tidal waters.

## Part 1: Understanding Tides

### The Cause of Tides: Gravity and Orbital Mechanics

Tides are caused by the gravitational pull of the moon (and, to a lesser extent, the sun) on Earth's water.

**Key principle:** The moon's gravity pulls on Earth's oceans, creating a bulge of water on the side of Earth facing the moon and (counterintuitively) also on the opposite side.

**Why both sides bulge:**

The moon pulls on the entire Earth, but pulls more strongly on the side of Earth facing the moon (closer) and less strongly on the far side. This difference in gravitational strength (called tidal force) stretches Earth, creating water bulges on both sides:

1. **Near side (facing the moon):** The water is pulled toward the moon more strongly than the solid Earth beneath it. Result: water bulges toward the moon.
2. **Far side (opposite the moon):** The moon pulls on the solid Earth more strongly than on the distant water. Result: the Earth is pulled away from the water, leaving water behind (bulge).

**As Earth rotates:** Each location on Earth rotates through the tidal bulges. In most places, this means two high tides (one facing the moon, one opposite) and two low tides per day (approximately 24 hours 50 minutes, accounting for the moon's orbital motion).

### Tidal Range and Frequency

**Tidal range:** The difference in water level between high tide and low tide.

**Typical values:**
- Microtidal regions (under 2 meters range): Most open ocean, tropical areas
- Mesotidal regions (2–4 meters): Moderate coasts
- Macrotidal regions (over 4 meters): Bay of Fundy (up to 16 meters), parts of Europe and Asia

**Frequency:** In most locations, tides occur twice per lunar day (approximately 24 hours 50 minutes).

**Lunar day:** The time between successive passages of the moon overhead. This is not the same as a solar day (24 hours) because the moon orbits Earth, moving eastward against the background of stars.

**Implication:** High tide occurs about 50 minutes later each day. A high tide at noon on day 1 occurs at 12:50 PM on day 2, 1:40 PM on day 3, and so on.

### Spring and Neap Tides

Tidal range varies throughout the lunar month:

**Spring tides (larger range):**
- Occur around full moon and new moon
- The sun, Earth, and moon are aligned (sun and moon on the same side of Earth or opposite sides)
- Gravitational pulls combine, creating larger tidal bulges
- Range can be 20–50% larger than average

**Neap tides (smaller range):**
- Occur around quarter moons (first quarter and third quarter)
- The sun and moon are at right angles (from Earth's perspective)
- Gravitational pulls partially cancel, creating smaller tidal bulges
- Range can be 20–50% smaller than average

**Predictability:** Spring and neap tides follow the lunar cycle (approximately 29.5 days). Knowing the current lunar phase allows prediction of whether the next tide will be larger or smaller than average.

### Step 1: Observe and Record Tides

**Method 1: Tidal staff (simplest)**

1. Choose a fixed location (harbor, jetty, or shore) that is accessible during both high and low tide
2. Install a measuring pole (or mark one at the observation site), graduated in centimeters, with zero at a known reference height (e.g., the lowest water level ever recorded, or a rock that is always exposed)
3. Record water level at regular intervals: once per hour, or at high tide and low tide times
4. Record the date, time, lunar phase, and any weather observations
5. Repeat for several weeks, tracking the pattern

**Key observations:**
- Two high tides per lunar day (approximately 12 hours 25 minutes apart)
- Two low tides per lunar day
- Tidal range changes from week to week (larger during spring tides, smaller during neap tides)

**Method 2: Visual observation (no instruments)**

1. Choose a harbor or shoreline with visible tidal change (rocks that are exposed at low tide, or a beach that widens and narrows)
2. Observe the water level visually and estimate (e.g., "water is at the second large rock" or "water is at the middle of the beach")
3. Record observations at regular times
4. Track patterns over weeks

This method is less precise but still useful for understanding general patterns.

### Step 2: Predict Tides from Patterns

**Simple prediction method (for locations with regular, predictable tides):**

1. Observe the times of high and low tides for several weeks
2. Calculate the average time between successive high tides (usually about 12 hours 25 minutes)
3. Calculate the average tidal range (high tide water level minus low tide water level)
4. Note how the range changes with lunar phase (larger during spring tides, smaller during neap tides)

**Prediction formula (rough):**
- If today's high tide is at 2:00 PM, tomorrow's high tide will be at approximately 2:50 PM
- After 2–3 days, account for cumulative delay: 50 min × 2–3 days = 100–150 minutes (1.5–2.5 hours later)

**For more accurate predictions:** Use a tidal atlas (if available) or historical records from your location. These provide detailed tables of high tide and low tide times and heights, allowing precise planning.

### Step 3: Account for Seasonal Variations

Tidal range and timing vary seasonally due to:

1. **Sun's declination:** The sun's position relative to the equator changes throughout the year. When the sun's declination aligns with the moon's orbital plane, tidal effects are stronger.

2. **Earth's orbital position:** Earth is closer to the sun in January (perihelion) and farther in July (aphelion). This affects solar tidal influence.

3. **Perigee and apogee:** The moon's orbit is elliptical. When the moon is closest to Earth (perigee), tidal range is larger. When farthest (apogee), range is smaller.

**Implication:** Over a full year, tidal range varies by 10–30%, depending on location. In planning harbors or navigation, account for this variation.

## Part 2: Ocean Currents

### Types of Currents

**Wind-driven currents:**
- Caused by persistent winds (trade winds, westerlies)
- Form large-scale circular patterns in oceans (gyres)
- Can transport water thousands of kilometers
- Speed: typically 0.5–2 meters per second (1–4 knots)

**Tidal currents:**
- Caused by tidal flows in bays, harbors, and narrow channels
- Can be very fast in narrow passages
- Change direction with tidal stage
- Speed: variable, but can exceed 5 meters per second (10+ knots) in extreme cases

**Density currents:**
- Caused by differences in water temperature and salinity
- Cold, salty water is denser and sinks below warmer, fresher water
- Create sustained currents (e.g., the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation)
- Slow-moving (centimeters per second) but persistent

**Rip currents (localized):**
- Caused by waves pushing water against shore, creating a return flow
- Narrow, fast jets of water flowing seaward
- Dangerous to swimmers and small boats
- Form in certain beach configurations

### Predicting Currents

**Wind-driven currents:**
- Move in the direction of persistent winds
- Speed increases with wind strength
- Can be estimated from historical wind data or sailing experience

**Tidal currents:**
- Follow a predictable cycle linked to tidal stage
- Often, maximum current occurs near mid-tide (halfway between high and low)
- Slack water (no current) occurs near high tide and low tide
- In narrow channels, currents can be strong and fast

**Method for predicting tidal current:**

1. Observe current direction and speed at different tidal stages (high tide, low tide, mid-tide)
2. Record the direction and speed for several tidal cycles
3. Note that direction often reverses between flood tide (water rising) and ebb tide (water falling)
4. Use pattern to predict future currents

**Example:**
- At high tide: current flows north at 0.5 knots
- At mid-tide (falling): current flows north at 2 knots (maximum)
- At low tide: current is slack (nearly zero)
- At mid-tide (rising): current flows south at 2 knots
- At high tide: current flows south at 0.5 knots (then reverses)

This pattern repeats every tidal cycle (approximately 12 hours 25 minutes).

### Step 1: Measure Current Speed and Direction

**Method 1: Float observation (simple, rough)**

1. Throw a floating object (cork, piece of wood) into the water upstream
2. Note the time (or count seconds) as the float drifts downstream
3. Measure the distance the float travels in a fixed time (e.g., 60 seconds)
4. Calculate speed: distance (in meters) / time (in seconds) = speed in meters per second
5. Repeat for several floats to average out variations

**Method 2: Leadline observation (more accurate)**

1. Drop a weighted line (leadline) with a marker (knot or colored tape) to the water bottom
2. Note the direction the line angles (indicates current direction)
3. From a boat moving at constant speed (or fixed position), measure the line's angle deviation from vertical
4. Use trigonometry (beyond this basic level) or visual estimation to judge current strength

**Method 3: Observation of water surface (qualitative)**

1. Look for ripples, eddies, or foam lines on the water surface
2. Fast currents create visible surface patterns (white caps, whirlpools, foam)
3. Slow currents may be invisible but detectable by observing floating debris

### Step 2: Integrate Current into Navigation

**Planning a passage against a current:**
- A boat traveling at 5 knots against a 1-knot current makes progress at 4 knots
- A journey that would take 10 hours with no current takes 12.5 hours against current

**Planning a passage with a current:**
- A boat traveling at 5 knots with a 1-knot current makes progress at 6 knots
- The same journey takes 8.3 hours with a helpful current

**Avoiding dangerous currents:**
- Near headlands and capes, currents can form whirlpools or standing waves (overfalls)
- Narrow channels can amplify current speed (water squeezed through a narrow opening moves faster)
- Plan routes to avoid strongest currents, especially in bad weather

## Part 3: Application to Harbor and Settlement Planning

### Assessing a Potential Harbor Site

**Criteria for a safe harbor:**

1. **Shelter from waves and storms:** Mountains, headlands, or barrier islands protect the harbor
2. **Adequate water depth:** Minimum 2–3 meters (for trading vessels) or 1–1.5 meters (for small boats)
3. **Good holding ground:** Soft mud or sand for anchors to grip (avoid rock or hard clay)
4. **Manageable tidal range:** Under 4 meters is preferred; over 6 meters is challenging (requires seawalls or long tidal channels)
5. **Moderate currents:** Fast currents in harbors can cause anchored boats to swing and collide
6. **Fresh water and supplies:** A river or stream for drinking water; access to food sources

### Evaluating Tidal Range Impact

**High tidal range consequences:**
- At low tide, harbor dries out (boats are stranded on mud)
- Approach channels must be deep enough to accommodate low-tide water levels
- Seawalls must be tall enough to resist high tide (potential flooding)

**Low tidal range advantages:**
- Water level is more stable
- Smaller seawalls and dikes are sufficient
- Boats remain afloat at all tides

**Example calculations:**
- Harbor 1: Tidal range of 2 meters (microtidal)
  - Low tide water depth: 5 meters
  - High tide water depth: 7 meters
  - All boats can enter at any tidal state
  
- Harbor 2: Tidal range of 10 meters (extreme macrotidal)
  - Low tide water depth: 2 meters (shallow, only small boats)
  - High tide water depth: 12 meters (deep, large ships possible)
  - Seawall must be 10+ meters tall
  - Access channels require dredging to lower-tide depths

### Planning Approaches and Seawalls

**Approach channels:**
- Dredge channels deep enough to accommodate ships at low tide
- For a harbor with 10-meter tidal range and ships requiring 3 meters draft, dredge to 13+ meters

**Seawalls and tidal gates:**
- Build seawalls to contain high tide (typically designed for 1.5× predicted maximum tide height to account for storms)
- Install gates (tidal gates or sluice gates) to prevent flooding at high tide while allowing drainage at low tide

**Anchorages and moorings:**
- Calculate swing radius: an anchored boat swings in a circle as tide and wind change
- Provide space equal to boat length × 3 (for swing radius in calm conditions) × 1.5 (safety margin)

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Predicting Tide Times at a New Location

**Situation:** A settlement is considering a harbor site on an unfamiliar coast. No tide tables are available. Predict high and low tide times for planning purposes.

**Process:**

1. **Observe for 2–3 weeks:**
   - Record high tide time and water level daily
   - Note lunar phase
   - Average the observed high tide times

2. **Calculate pattern:**
   - Average time between successive high tides: 12 hours 25 minutes
   - High tide today: 1:00 PM
   - High tide tomorrow: 1:50 PM
   - High tide day after: 2:40 PM
   - (approximately; small variations occur)

3. **Account for spring and neap tides:**
   - During spring tide (around full moon or new moon): range is 20% larger
   - During neap tide (around quarter moon): range is 20% smaller
   - Current lunar phase: determine if approaching spring or neap tide

4. **Prediction for next week:**
   - Day 1 (today): High tide 1:00 PM, range 3 meters
   - Day 2: High tide 1:50 PM, range 3.1 meters (approaching spring)
   - Day 3: High tide 2:40 PM, range 3.2 meters (spring tide)
   - Day 4: High tide 3:30 PM, range 3.1 meters (past spring tide peak)

### Example 2: Navigating a Strong Tidal Current

**Situation:** A sailing ship must cross a bay with a strong tidal current. The current flows at 2 knots during mid-tide. The ship travels at 4 knots in still water.

**Problem:**
- If the ship aims directly across, the current will push it 2 knots sideways
- The ship will end up 2 nautical miles downstream instead of directly across

**Solution:**
- Aim upstream at an angle such that the ship's actual track (course over ground) is directly across
- Use trigonometry or visual estimation:
  - Ship's heading: 20–30 degrees upstream of the desired track
  - This allows the current to push the ship sideways while the ship moves forward, resulting in motion directly across

**Alternative:**
- Time the crossing to coincide with slack water (near high or low tide), when current is minimal
- Cross then, rather than fighting current at mid-tide

### Example 3: Harbor Design for Macrotidal Region

**Situation:** A port is being designed in a macrotidal region with 9-meter tidal range.

**Design parameters:**

1. **Water depth at low tide:** 2 meters (very shallow)
   - Approach channel must be dredged to 4+ meters to accommodate 2-meter-draft cargo ships at low tide

2. **Seawall height:**
   - Built for 1.5× spring tide maximum (10.5 meters)
   - Add 2 meters freeboard for safety: total 12.5 meters tall

3. **Tidal gate system:**
   - Install gates at harbor entrance to hold water in during low tide
   - Gates open at high tide to prevent excessive pressure
   - Gates close during transition to prevent drainage

4. **Mooring arrangements:**
   - Ships swing in a circle of 50–100 meters radius (depending on ship size and holding ground)
   - Provide separated moorings for multiple ships
   - Use deep anchors or fixed moorings because boats rise and fall 9 meters through the day

5. **Labor and maintenance:**
   - Dredging costs are high (9-meter tidal range means frequent maintenance)
   - Gates require regular inspection
   - Worth the investment for a major trading hub, but challenging for small settlements

## Common Mistakes

1. **Confusing true solar day with lunar day:** High tide occurs about 50 minutes later each day, not at the same time. Failure to account for this causes missed tides and stranded ships.

2. **Assuming tides are the same everywhere:** Tidal range varies from place to place. Open ocean has small tides (under 1 meter). Bays and channels can have extreme tides (over 15 meters). Always observe local conditions.

3. **Underestimating current strength:** A 2-knot current doesn't sound fast, but it can push a small boat kilometers off course over hours. Always estimate current effects on navigation.

4. **Ignoring tidal currents in narrow channels:** Channels and straits can have currents exceeding 5 knots (faster than many sailing ships can travel). Timing passage to avoid strong currents is essential.

5. **Thinking tides are predictable everywhere:** Tides are regular in open ocean and most coasts, but some bays or shallow areas have irregular, chaotic tidal patterns due to complex bathymetry. Local knowledge is essential.

6. **Neglecting seawall design:** A seawall designed for average tide will be overtopped by spring tide or storm surge. Always account for maximum expected water level + margin.

7. **Anchoring in unsafe ground:** Sand and mud are good holding grounds; rock and shale are poor. An anchor dragging at night due to poor holding ground can result in the boat drifting aground.

8. **Underestimating swing radius:** An anchored boat swings in a large circle as wind and tide change direction. Providing insufficient anchor space causes collisions with other boats or the shore.

9. **Forgetting that tidal current reverses:** In channels, the direction of the current completely reverses between flood and ebb tide. A current flowing north at mid-flood can flow south at mid-ebb. Always account for direction changes.

10. **Ignoring seasonal variations:** Tidal range can vary by 30% seasonally. Design for the maximum expected conditions (usually late autumn or early spring) to ensure year-round safety.
