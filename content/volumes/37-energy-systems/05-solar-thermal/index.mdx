## Overview

Every square meter of sunlit ground receives roughly a kilowatt of solar power on a clear day. That energy arrives free, every day, with no fuel to burn and no smoke. Solar thermal technology captures this energy as heat: it heats water for washing and bathing, cooks food, dries crops for storage, distills clean water, and at large scale can even drive engines to generate electricity. Unlike solar electric panels, which need semiconductor manufacturing, solar thermal devices can be built from simple, available materials — dark metal, glass, and insulation — making them ideal for a society rebuilding its energy base.

The core idea is simple. Dark surfaces absorb sunlight and turn it into heat. Glass lets sunlight in but traps the heat (the greenhouse effect). Insulation keeps that heat from escaping. Reflectors concentrate sunlight to reach higher temperatures. By combining these four elements in different ways, you build everything from a simple solar water heater to a furnace hot enough to melt metal.

This chapter explains the principles of solar heat capture and walks through building the most useful devices: a solar water heater, a solar cooker, and a solar still, with notes on concentrating systems for higher temperatures.

## Materials & Tools

**Common materials across solar thermal devices:**

- **Absorber:** dark metal sheet, black-painted surface, or a dark container. Copper and aluminum conduct heat well; any dark metal works.
- **Glazing:** glass or clear plastic to let light in and trap heat
- **Insulation:** straw, wool, sawdust, foam — anything that traps still air, placed behind and around the absorber
- **Reflectors:** polished metal, mirror, or foil-covered board to direct extra sunlight onto the absorber
- **A frame or box** to hold the assembly and angle it toward the sun
- **For water heating:** pipes or tubing, a storage tank, and connecting plumbing

## Part 1: The Four Principles

### 1. Absorption

Dark, matte surfaces absorb the most sunlight and convert it to heat. A black-painted metal plate can absorb over 90 percent of the light hitting it. Shiny or light surfaces reflect light away and stay cool. So every solar heat collector starts with a dark absorber.

### 2. The Greenhouse Effect (Glazing)

Glass and clear plastic are transparent to incoming sunlight (visible light) but largely opaque to the infrared heat that warm objects radiate. So sunlight passes through the glazing, strikes the dark absorber, and becomes heat — but that heat cannot easily radiate back out through the glass. Heat builds up inside. This is why a closed car in the sun gets so hot, and it is the single most important trick for reaching useful temperatures.

### 3. Insulation

Heat escapes by conduction and convection wherever the hot absorber touches cooler surroundings. Insulation — any material full of trapped, still air — slows that loss. Putting insulation behind and around the absorber (everywhere except the sun-facing glazed side) keeps the captured heat where you want it.

### 4. Concentration

A flat absorber reaches modest temperatures (enough for hot water or cooking). To reach higher temperatures — for melting, high-temperature cooking, or power generation — you concentrate sunlight from a large area onto a small target using mirrors or polished reflectors. A curved (parabolic) reflector can focus sunlight to a point hot enough to melt metal.

## Part 2: Building a Solar Water Heater

A solar water heater is the most practical first project: it pays back constantly by saving fuel you would otherwise burn to heat water.

### The Thermosiphon Design

The simplest reliable design uses no pump. It relies on the fact that hot water rises:

1. **Collector:** A shallow, glazed, insulated box containing dark pipes or a dark tank. Cold water enters the bottom; sun heats it.
2. **Storage tank:** Placed above the collector. As water in the collector heats, it rises into the top of the tank, drawing cool water from the bottom of the tank into the bottom of the collector. This sets up a natural circulation loop with no moving parts.

### Step-by-Step

1. Build an insulated box, open on the sun-facing side. Line the back and sides with insulation.
2. Mount a dark absorber: black-painted copper or steel pipes, or a flat dark tank, inside the box. Connect pipes so water can flow through.
3. Cover the open side with glass or clear plastic, sealed to trap heat.
4. Mount a storage tank above and behind the collector. Plumb cold supply to the collector bottom and the collector top to the tank top.
5. Angle the collector toward the midday sun (roughly equal to your latitude, facing the equator).
6. Fill with water and let the sun work. By afternoon you will have hot water rising into the tank.

A well-built collector this way easily reaches 50 to 70 degrees Celsius — ample for washing, bathing, and cleaning.

## Part 3: Building a Solar Cooker

### The Box Cooker

A box cooker is an insulated, glazed box with a dark interior and a reflective lid:

1. Build a box, insulate the walls and floor, and paint the inside dark.
2. Place a dark cooking pot inside.
3. Cover the top with glass or clear plastic.
4. Add a hinged reflective lid (foil on board) angled to bounce extra sunlight into the box.

A box cooker reaches 100 to 150 degrees Celsius — slow but steady, good for stews, grains, and baking. It cannot burn food and needs no tending, but it cooks slowly and only when the sun shines.

### The Parabolic Cooker

A curved reflective dish focuses sunlight onto a single point where you place the pot. This reaches far higher temperatures — enough to fry and boil quickly — but the focus must be aimed at the sun and adjusted as the sun moves, and the intense focal point can burn skin or eyes. Use with care.

## Part 4: The Solar Still (Clean Water)

A solar still uses the sun to purify water by distillation — turning dirty or salty water into clean drinking water:

1. Place contaminated water in a shallow black-lined basin inside an enclosure.
2. Cover with a sloped sheet of glass or clear plastic.
3. The sun evaporates water from the basin; vapor (leaving salt and contaminants behind) condenses on the cool underside of the glazing.
4. The condensed droplets run down the slope into a collection channel.

The output is modest — a few liters a day per square meter — but it produces genuinely clean water from sources you could not otherwise drink, including seawater. This is a survival-critical capability.

## Part 5: Toward Higher Temperatures and Power

By concentrating sunlight with parabolic troughs or dishes, you can generate steam. Steam can drive an engine or turbine to make mechanical power or electricity (concentrated solar power). At large scale this is how solar thermal power plants work. At small scale, a parabolic dish can heat a small boiler to run a simple steam engine. These systems are complex and are covered in a dedicated chapter, but the principle is the same four ideas pushed to their limit: absorb, trap, insulate, and above all concentrate.

## Worked Example: Sizing a Household Water Heater

A family wants warm water for daily washing.

- **Demand:** Say they want about 100 liters of water heated from 20 to 60 degrees Celsius each day. Heating 100 liters by 40 degrees takes a known amount of energy (about 4.6 kilowatt-hours).
- **Sun available:** On a clear day, a square meter of collector might deliver 4 to 6 kilowatt-hours of usable heat.
- **Collector size:** Accounting for losses (a flat collector is perhaps 50 percent efficient), roughly 2 square meters of collector should comfortably heat that 100 liters on a sunny day.
- **Tank:** A 100-liter insulated tank mounted above the collector stores the day's hot water for evening use.

This is a modest, buildable system that eliminates the daily fuel cost of heating wash water.

## Verification Criteria

- **It gets hot.** A working collector's absorber should be noticeably hot to the touch (carefully) within an hour of strong sun; water output should reach 50 degrees Celsius or more.
- **Circulation works (thermosiphon).** In a water heater, the tank top should warm during the day without any pump — proof the natural loop is flowing.
- **The cooker cooks.** A box cooker should hold above 90 degrees Celsius in good sun, enough to cook grains and stews over a few hours.
- **The still produces clean water.** Output from a solar still should be clear and salt-free; taste and clarity confirm purification.
- **Heat is retained.** A well-insulated, glazed device stays hot well after a passing cloud; rapid cooling means insufficient insulation or air leaks.

## Common Mistakes

- **Skipping the glazing.** Without glass to trap heat, an absorber loses heat to the wind almost as fast as it gains it. The greenhouse effect is essential.
- **Poor insulation.** Heat leaks out the back and sides if uninsulated. Insulate everywhere except the sun-facing glazed surface.
- **Wrong angle.** A collector facing the wrong way or at a poor angle captures far less. Aim it toward the midday sun.
- **Light-colored absorber.** A shiny or pale absorber reflects the energy you are trying to capture. Make it dark and matte.
- **Air gaps in glazing seals.** Leaks let trapped hot air escape; seal the glazing well.
- **Ignoring focal-point hazards.** Parabolic concentrators create intensely hot, blinding focal points. Protect eyes and skin and never leave them aimed unattended near flammable material.

Solar thermal technology is among the most accessible forms of renewable energy: no exotic materials, no moving parts in its simplest forms, and fuel that arrives free every sunny day. Master the four principles — absorb, trap, insulate, concentrate — and you can heat water, cook food, purify water, and dry crops using nothing but sunlight and simple materials you can build by hand.
