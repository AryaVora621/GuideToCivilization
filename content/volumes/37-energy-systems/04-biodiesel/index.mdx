## Overview

A diesel engine will run on more than refined petroleum. With the right processing, the oils and fats you can grow or render — vegetable oil, used cooking oil, animal tallow — become biodiesel, a fuel that performs almost identically to petroleum diesel. Rudolf Diesel himself demonstrated his engine on peanut oil. The catch is that raw vegetable oil is too thick (viscous) to burn well in a normal diesel engine for long. Biodiesel is the product of a simple chemical reaction that thins the oil into a clean-burning fuel.

For a civilization rebuilding its energy base, biodiesel is precious because diesel engines are rugged, efficient, and run generators, pumps, tractors, and vehicles. If you can grow oilseed crops or collect waste fats, you can keep diesel machinery running without petroleum. The process, called transesterification, uses a catalyst (lye) and an alcohol (methanol or ethanol) to transform thick oil into thin fuel plus a useful byproduct, glycerin.

This chapter is rated advanced because it involves caustic and flammable chemicals. Done carefully it is safe and reliable; done carelessly it can cause serious chemical burns or fire. Read the safety sections closely.

## Materials & Tools

**Chemicals:**

- **Oil or fat:** fresh vegetable oil, waste cooking oil (filtered), or animal tallow
- **Alcohol:** methanol is standard; ethanol can work but is trickier. Roughly 20 percent of the oil volume.
- **Catalyst:** lye — sodium hydroxide (NaOH) or potassium hydroxide (KOH). KOH is easier to dissolve and gives a more usable glycerin byproduct.

**Equipment:**

- A sealed mixing reactor (a container that resists the chemicals; HDPE plastic or steel)
- A way to heat the oil gently and a thermometer
- A scale for weighing the catalyst accurately
- Goggles, chemical gloves, apron — non-negotiable
- A settling vessel for separating fuel from glycerin
- Containers for washing and storing the fuel

## Part 1: The Chemistry of Transesterification

### What the Reaction Does

Vegetable oils and fats are triglycerides: three fatty-acid chains attached to a glycerol backbone. This bulky structure is what makes oil thick. Transesterification swaps the glycerol backbone for three small alcohol molecules. The result is three separate fatty-acid esters (the biodiesel — thin, free-flowing molecules) plus one molecule of glycerol (glycerin), which settles out.

> triglyceride + alcohol → biodiesel (esters) + glycerin

The lye catalyst makes the reaction go. It is consumed in side reactions but mostly enables the swap. The reaction is reversible and incomplete unless you use excess alcohol, which is why recipes call for more methanol than the strict chemical ratio.

### Why Not Just Burn the Raw Oil?

Raw vegetable oil works briefly but is too viscous: it does not atomize properly in the injectors, leaving deposits that foul the engine and dilute the lubricating oil over time. Some people run raw oil in heated "two-tank" systems, but converting to biodiesel is the durable solution because biodiesel matches petroleum diesel's viscosity and burns cleanly in an unmodified engine.

## Part 2: The Critical First Step — Titration

### Why It Matters

Waste oils contain **free fatty acids** (FFAs) formed when oil breaks down from cooking. These acids react with your lye catalyst and turn it into soap instead of fuel, ruining the batch (you get a thick, gloppy mess that will not separate). The dirtier the oil, the more FFAs, the more extra lye you must add to compensate.

Fresh, clean oil has almost no FFAs and needs only the base amount of catalyst. Used oil needs a titration test to measure the FFAs so you can calculate the right extra catalyst.

### How to Titrate (Simplified)

1. Dissolve a small, precisely weighed amount of lye in distilled water to make a reference solution of known strength.
2. Dissolve a measured small sample of your oil in pure alcohol with a pH indicator (such as phenolphthalein, which turns pink in base).
3. Add the reference lye solution drop by drop, counting drops, until the indicator just changes color (stays pink).
4. The number of drops tells you how much extra catalyst the oil needs per liter, beyond the base amount.

This sounds fiddly, but it is the difference between fuel and a barrel of soap. For fresh oil you can often skip titration and use the standard base dose; for waste oil, always titrate.

## Part 3: Making Biodiesel

### Step 1: Prepare the Oil

Filter the oil to remove food particles. Heat it to drive off any water (water also causes soap formation), holding it warm and watching for it to stop bubbling. Then bring it to the reaction temperature, around 50 to 55 degrees Celsius.

### Step 2: Make the Methoxide

This is the most dangerous step. Carefully dissolve the measured lye into the methanol, making **sodium (or potassium) methoxide**. This mixture is extremely caustic and the methanol is toxic and flammable.

- Work outdoors or in strong ventilation.
- Wear goggles, gloves, and an apron.
- Add lye slowly to alcohol in a sealed, chemical-resistant container, swirling until dissolved. Never breathe the fumes; never let it touch skin.

### Step 3: React

Add the methoxide to the warm oil and mix vigorously for about an hour, keeping the temperature near 50 degrees Celsius. Mixing is essential to bring the reactants together. The mixture will gradually thin as the reaction proceeds.

### Step 4: Settle and Separate

Let the mixture sit for 8 hours or overnight. Glycerin, being denser, sinks to the bottom as a dark, thick layer; the biodiesel floats on top as a clearer, amber liquid. Drain off the heavy glycerin from the bottom. Keep the glycerin — it is useful for soap-making.

### Step 5: Wash

Fresh biodiesel still contains traces of methanol, leftover catalyst, and soap. Wash it gently with water: water carries away the contaminants and sinks, leaving cleaner fuel on top. Repeat washing until the wash water comes off clear. Then dry the fuel by gently heating to remove residual water, or let it settle clear over time.

## Part 4: Testing Your Fuel

Before pouring biodiesel into an engine you depend on, test it.

- **The wash test:** Properly reacted fuel washes clean without turning into an emulsified mess. Soap-laden fuel emulsifies badly.
- **The clarity test:** Finished, dried biodiesel should be clear and bright, not cloudy. Cloudiness means water or incomplete reaction.
- **The methanol wash / settling test:** Mix a sample of fuel with a larger volume of methanol; fully converted biodiesel dissolves completely with no sediment dropping out. Unconverted oil falls to the bottom, revealing a bad batch.
- **The burn test:** A small sample should burn cleanly. (Do this safely, away from your stock.)

## Part 5: Using and Storing Biodiesel

Biodiesel runs in standard diesel engines, often with no modification. A few cautions:

- **It is a solvent.** When first used, biodiesel cleans out deposits left by petroleum diesel, which can clog fuel filters initially. Carry spare filters when first switching.
- **It gels in the cold.** Biodiesel thickens at higher temperatures than petroleum diesel. In cold climates, blend it with petroleum diesel or warm the fuel system.
- **It degrades rubber.** Older engines with natural-rubber fuel lines may need those lines replaced with biodiesel-compatible material.
- **Store it clean and dry.** Keep water out and store in sealed containers; biodiesel can grow microbes and oxidize over long storage.

### The Glycerin Byproduct

Each batch leaves a layer of glycerin mixed with soap and excess catalyst. This is not waste: it can be processed into soap (Vol on chemistry), used as a degreaser, or composted carefully. Do not pour it carelessly into waterways — it is caustic until neutralized.

## Worked Example: A Batch from Waste Cooking Oil

You have collected 10 liters of used frying oil.

1. **Filter and dry:** Strain out food bits, heat to drive off moisture.
2. **Titrate:** Test a sample. Suppose it needs 2 extra grams of lye per liter beyond the base dose.
3. **Calculate catalyst:** Base dose plus the titration correction, multiplied by 10 liters, gives the total lye to weigh out.
4. **Make methoxide:** Carefully dissolve the weighed lye into about 2 liters of methanol (roughly 20 percent of the oil volume), wearing full protection.
5. **React:** Warm the oil to 50 degrees Celsius, add the methoxide, mix one hour.
6. **Settle:** Let it sit overnight; drain the glycerin from the bottom.
7. **Wash and dry:** Gently wash with water until clear, then dry.
8. **Test:** Confirm clarity and run the methanol settling test before fueling.

The reward: roughly 9 to 10 liters of usable diesel fuel from oil that was otherwise waste.

## Verification Criteria

- **Clean separation.** A clear glycerin layer settles to the bottom and a distinct fuel layer floats on top. No thick, uniform sludge (which means soap formation failed the batch).
- **Clear, bright fuel.** Finished, dried biodiesel is transparent and free of cloudiness.
- **Passes the settling test.** A fuel sample fully dissolves in excess methanol with no oily sediment dropping out.
- **Runs the engine cleanly.** The engine starts and runs smoothly; exhaust is not excessively smoky.
- **Wash water runs clear.** After enough washes, the rinse water comes off colorless.

## Common Mistakes

- **Skipping titration on waste oil.** Free fatty acids will turn your batch into soap. Always titrate used oil.
- **Wet oil.** Water in the oil causes soap formation. Dry the oil thoroughly first.
- **Careless methoxide handling.** Lye-in-methanol causes severe burns and toxic, flammable fumes. Full protection and ventilation, every time.
- **Under-mixing.** Poor mixing leaves the reaction incomplete and the fuel full of unconverted oil. Mix vigorously for the full time.
- **Skipping the wash.** Unwashed fuel carries catalyst and soap that harm the engine.
- **Running it cold without blending.** Biodiesel gels in cold weather and can starve the engine.

The chemistry is straightforward once understood, but the caustic and flammable materials demand respect. Master titration, work clean and dry, protect yourself, and test every batch, and you can keep diesel machinery alive on fuel you make yourself.
