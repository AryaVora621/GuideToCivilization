## Overview

Ethanol is the alcohol in beer, wine, and spirits, but it is also a clean-burning liquid fuel, a powerful disinfectant, a solvent, and a preservative. It is made by a process older than recorded history: yeast, a single-celled fungus, consumes sugar and excretes ethanol and carbon dioxide. That fermentation gives you a dilute alcohol solution. Distillation, which separates liquids by boiling point, then concentrates it.

For a rebuilding civilization, ethanol is enormously valuable because you can make it from crops you grow yourself: sugarcane, sugar beets, fruit, corn, potatoes, or any starchy or sugary plant. Unlike fossil fuels, it is renewable and producible with simple equipment. A working still and a supply of sugary mash give you fuel for lamps and stoves, antiseptic for wounds, solvent for extracting medicines and making inks, and a fluid that stores energy compactly and burns cleanly.

This chapter teaches the two-stage process: fermentation to make alcohol, and distillation to concentrate it. It also covers the safety issues, because distillation involves flammable vapor and, if done carelessly, can produce toxic results.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding that yeast is a living microbe that ferments sugar (Vol 27)
- Comfort heating liquids and handling flammable materials safely
- Access to a sugary or starchy crop
- Basic ability to build or seal a sealed vessel and condenser

## Materials & Tools

**For fermentation:**

- A fermentation vessel (a clean barrel, crock, or sealed bucket)
- An airlock or a loose cover that lets carbon dioxide escape but keeps air out
- Sugar source: cane juice, crushed fruit, or cooked starchy crops
- Yeast (baker's yeast, wild yeast, or saved yeast from a previous batch)
- Clean water
- Optional: a hydrometer to measure sugar and track fermentation

**For distillation:**

- A still: a sealed boiler, a vapor line, and a condenser (a coiled tube cooled by water)
- A heat source you can control gently
- A collection vessel
- A thermometer (very helpful for separating fractions)
- Cooling water supply for the condenser

## Part 1: The Science of Fermentation

### What Yeast Does

Yeast metabolizes simple sugars (mainly glucose and fructose) in the absence of much oxygen and produces ethanol and carbon dioxide. The simplified reaction is:

> sugar → ethanol + carbon dioxide

The carbon dioxide bubbles off; the ethanol stays in the liquid. Fermentation continues until the sugar runs out or the alcohol concentration gets so high (around 12 to 15 percent) that it poisons the yeast itself. This natural limit is why fermented drinks rarely exceed about 15 percent alcohol without distillation.

### Sugar Sources and the Starch Problem

Yeast can only eat simple sugars. Some crops give you sugar directly:

- **Sugarcane and sugar beet:** crush and extract the sweet juice
- **Fruit:** grapes, apples, berries — crush to release the juice
- **Honey:** dissolve in water (this makes mead)

Other crops store energy as **starch**, a long chain of sugar units that yeast cannot eat directly. You must first break the starch into sugars, a step called **conversion** or **saccharification**:

- **Corn, wheat, barley, potatoes, cassava:** cook to gelatinize the starch, then add an enzyme source.
- The traditional enzyme source is **malt** (sprouted grain), which contains amylase that chops starch into sugar. Sprout barley, dry it, grind it, and mix it into the warm cooked mash. Saliva and some molds also contain amylase and have been used historically.

Once the starch is converted to sugar, fermentation proceeds as with any sugary crop.

## Part 2: Making the Mash and Fermenting

### Step 1: Prepare the Sugar Solution

For a sugary crop, extract the juice and dilute it to a sugar concentration the yeast can handle (a hydrometer reading is useful here; aim for a solution that will ferment to about 10 to 12 percent alcohol). For a starchy crop, cook it to a porridge, cool it to about 65 degrees Celsius, mix in ground malt, and hold it warm for an hour or two while the enzymes convert the starch. Then cool further.

### Step 2: Pitch the Yeast

Cool the mash to a comfortable warm temperature (around 20 to 30 degrees Celsius — too hot kills yeast). Add the yeast and stir. Within hours you should see bubbling as carbon dioxide forms.

### Step 3: Ferment

Cover the vessel with an airlock or a cloth that keeps insects and air out while letting gas escape. Keeping air out matters: with oxygen present, other microbes turn alcohol into vinegar. Ferment in a warm, stable place for several days to two weeks. Fermentation is finished when bubbling stops and, if you have a hydrometer, the sugar reading has dropped and stabilized.

The result is a "wash" or "wine" of roughly 8 to 14 percent alcohol. This is drinkable-strength but too dilute for fuel or disinfectant. To concentrate it, you distill.

## Part 3: Distillation

### The Principle

Ethanol boils at 78 degrees Celsius; water boils at 100 degrees Celsius. If you heat the wash, the vapor coming off is richer in alcohol than the liquid, because the lower-boiling ethanol evaporates more readily. By condensing that vapor back to liquid, you collect a more concentrated alcohol. Repeating or refining the process raises the concentration further.

### The Equipment

A basic pot still has three parts:

1. **The boiler (pot):** holds the wash and is heated.
2. **The vapor path / column:** carries rising vapor toward the condenser. A taller column with packing gives better separation.
3. **The condenser:** a tube, often coiled, cooled by water (a "worm" running through a tub of cold water). Vapor entering the cold tube condenses back to liquid and drips out the end.

Every joint must be sealed against vapor leaks, traditionally with a paste of flour and water.

### Step-by-Step Distillation

1. Fill the boiler no more than about two-thirds full of wash.
2. Seal the still and begin heating gently. Start the cooling water flowing through the condenser.
3. Watch the temperature. As the liquid approaches 78 degrees Celsius, alcohol-rich vapor begins to come over and drip from the condenser.
4. **Discard the foreshots.** The very first liquid to come out (the "foreshots") can contain methanol and other volatile, toxic compounds with lower boiling points. Always discard the first small fraction. This is a critical safety step.
5. **Collect the hearts.** The main run, coming over while the temperature sits near 78 to 85 degrees Celsius, is the good ethanol. Collect this.
6. **Cut the tails.** As the temperature climbs toward 95 to 100 degrees Celsius, you are mostly boiling water and heavier compounds with an oily smell. Stop collecting, or set these "tails" aside to redistill later.

### Raising the Strength

A single pass through a simple pot still yields perhaps 40 to 60 percent ethanol. Redistilling that product raises it further. With a packed column ("reflux still"), you can reach the high 80s to low 90s in percent. You cannot exceed about 95.6 percent ethanol by distillation alone, because at that point ethanol and water boil together as a fixed mixture (an azeotrope). To go higher (for blending with gasoline, which requires near-anhydrous ethanol), you must use a drying agent such as quicklime or molecular sieve.

## Part 4: Using Ethanol

### As Fuel

Ethanol burns cleanly with a pale blue flame. At 70 percent and above it lights readily and is excellent for stoves and lamps. Pure or near-pure ethanol can run modified engines. Because it absorbs water, store fuel ethanol in sealed containers. Denature fuel ethanol (make it undrinkable and tax-exempt where relevant) by adding a small amount of a poison like methanol if you must distinguish it from drinking alcohol — but never confuse denatured fuel with potable spirit.

### As Disinfectant and Antiseptic

Ethanol around 60 to 80 percent is one of the best general disinfectants available, killing most bacteria and many viruses. Notably, pure ethanol is a worse disinfectant than 70 percent ethanol, because some water is needed to penetrate microbial cells. For wound antisepsis and sterilizing instruments, 70 percent is the target.

### As Solvent and Preservative

Ethanol dissolves many substances that water cannot, making it valuable for extracting medicinal compounds from plants (tinctures), making inks and dyes, and preserving biological specimens.

## Worked Example: From Corn to Fuel

Suppose you have a sack of corn and want lamp fuel.

1. **Cook:** Grind the corn, mix with water, and cook to a thick porridge to gelatinize the starch.
2. **Convert:** Cool to about 65 degrees Celsius, stir in ground malted barley, and hold warm for two hours. The mash turns sweeter as starch becomes sugar.
3. **Ferment:** Cool to about 25 degrees Celsius, pitch yeast, cover with an airlock, and wait one to two weeks until bubbling stops. You now have a wash of roughly 8 to 10 percent alcohol.
4. **Distill:** Heat in the still, discard the foreshots, collect the hearts near 78 to 85 degrees Celsius. One pass yields a moderate-strength spirit; a second pass concentrates it to lamp-fuel strength.
5. **Use:** Burn the result in a lamp. The leftover spent mash ("distillers' grains") is a nourishing animal feed — nothing is wasted.

## Verification Criteria

- **It bubbles, then stops.** Active fermentation produces visible carbon dioxide; a clean stop means the sugar is consumed.
- **The distillate burns.** Collected hearts should light with a clean blue flame. A flame that sputters or will not light means the strength is too low — redistill.
- **You discarded the foreshots.** Confirm you set aside the first fraction every time. This is non-negotiable for any ethanol that might be ingested.
- **Strength check.** If you have a hydrometer or alcoholmeter, the hearts should read well above the wash strength; fuel-grade should read 70 percent or higher.
- **No vapor leaks.** During distillation, no alcohol smell should escape from the still's joints (a leak is both a loss and a fire hazard).

## Common Mistakes

- **Keeping the foreshots.** The first fraction can contain methanol and acetone. Always discard it, especially for drinking alcohol.
- **Letting air into fermentation.** Oxygen lets vinegar bacteria spoil the batch into sour acetic acid.
- **Overheating the mash before pitching yeast.** Yeast dies above roughly 40 degrees Celsius; always cool to a warm, comfortable temperature first.
- **Boiling too hard during distillation.** A violent boil carries water over with the alcohol and lowers purity; heat gently and steadily.
- **Open flames near the still.** Ethanol vapor is highly flammable. Keep the heat source controlled and the area ventilated; never distill near an open flame on the vapor path.
- **Storing fuel ethanol in open containers.** Ethanol absorbs water from air, weakening it over time. Seal it.

Ethanol production ties together agriculture, biology, and chemistry into one of the most useful processes a community can master. From a crop you grow, you produce fuel, medicine, and a versatile solvent — renewably, repeatably, and with equipment you can build by hand. Master fermentation first, then distillation, and respect the fire and toxicity hazards, and you will have a reliable source of one of civilization's most valuable liquids.
