## Overview

When organic matter rots in the absence of air, microbes break it down and release a flammable gas that is roughly 60 percent methane. This is the same gas that powers a natural-gas stove. A biogas digester is a sealed tank that traps this gas so you can burn it for cooking, lighting, or running an engine. What goes in is waste: animal manure, food scraps, crop residue, even human sewage. What comes out is fuel plus a nutrient-rich slurry that makes an excellent fertilizer.

In a world without pipelines or fuel deliveries, a digester is one of the most practical energy machines you can build. It runs on materials you already produce every day, it needs no rare parts, and it solves two problems at once: it gives you cooking fuel and it safely processes waste that would otherwise breed disease and attract pests. A single household digester fed with the manure from a few animals can supply most of a family's cooking needs.

This chapter teaches you how anaerobic digestion works, how to size and build a simple digester, how to feed and maintain it, and how to use the gas and the leftover slurry safely.

## Prerequisites

- Basic understanding that microbes cause decomposition (Vol 27)
- Comfort working with simple sealed containers and plumbing
- Access to a steady supply of organic waste (manure is ideal)
- A reasonably warm climate or a way to keep the digester warm
- Patience: a new digester takes two to four weeks to start producing usable gas

## Materials & Tools

**For a small household digester:**

- A sealed tank or drum for the digestion chamber (a 200-liter drum scales to one person; larger families need 1000 liters or more)
- A gas-tight gas holder (a second floating drum, or a strong plastic gas bag)
- Inlet pipe (for feeding slurry in) and outlet pipe (for spent slurry to flow out)
- A gas outlet pipe with a valve, plus tubing to your stove or lamp
- A simple pressure relief or water trap to prevent dangerous pressure buildup
- Sealant: rubber gaskets, silicone, or pitch to make joints gas-tight

**Feedstock and starter:**

- Fresh animal manure (cow, pig, or chicken) as the main feedstock
- Water for mixing slurry to the right consistency
- A "starter" culture: a bucket of slurry from an active digester, or fresh manure, which already contains the right microbes

**Tools:**

- A bucket and stick for mixing slurry
- A simple thermometer if available (helps you track temperature)
- A flame and soapy water for leak testing

## Part 1: How Anaerobic Digestion Works

### The Microbial Team

Anaerobic digestion ("anaerobic" means without oxygen) is carried out by several groups of microbes working in sequence. You do not need to manage them individually, but understanding the chain helps you avoid mistakes.

1. **Hydrolysis:** Bacteria break large molecules (fats, proteins, carbohydrates) into smaller, soluble pieces like sugars and amino acids.
2. **Acidogenesis:** Other bacteria ferment those pieces into simple acids, alcohols, hydrogen, and carbon dioxide.
3. **Acetogenesis:** A further group converts those products into acetic acid, hydrogen, and carbon dioxide.
4. **Methanogenesis:** Finally, specialized microbes called methanogens consume the acids, hydrogen, and carbon dioxide and produce methane gas.

The whole point is that last step. Methanogens are slow-growing and fussy: they hate oxygen, they hate sudden temperature swings, and they hate acidic conditions. Most digester problems come down to making the methanogens unhappy.

### What the Gas Is

The gas a digester makes, called biogas, is a mixture:

- About 55 to 70 percent **methane** (the part that burns)
- About 30 to 45 percent **carbon dioxide** (does not burn, dilutes the fuel)
- Small amounts of **hydrogen sulfide** (smells like rotten eggs, is corrosive and toxic)
- Traces of water vapor and other gases

Because of the carbon dioxide, biogas has less energy per liter than pure natural gas, but it still burns cleanly with a blue flame and is perfectly good for cooking and lighting.

### The Conditions Methanogens Need

- **No oxygen.** The tank must be sealed. Even small air leaks slow gas production and, worse, can create an explosive mixture inside the holder.
- **Warmth.** Digesters work best between 30 and 40 degrees Celsius. Below 15 degrees, production nearly stops. This is why warm climates or insulated/heated tanks matter.
- **Near-neutral acidity.** A pH between 6.8 and 7.2 is ideal. If you overfeed, acids build up faster than methanogens can eat them, the contents turn sour, and gas production crashes.
- **The right food balance.** Microbes need a carbon-to-nitrogen ratio around 25 or 30 to 1. Manure is close to ideal. Too much fresh green material (high nitrogen) or too much dry straw (high carbon) throws this off.

## Part 2: Sizing Your Digester

Before building, estimate how much feedstock you have and how much gas you need.

### Gas Demand

A rough guide: cooking for one person uses about 150 to 300 liters of biogas per day. A family of five might need 1000 to 1500 liters daily. A biogas lamp uses roughly 100 to 150 liters per hour.

### Gas Supply

Fresh cow manure yields roughly 30 to 40 liters of biogas per kilogram of dry matter. One cow produces enough manure per day to make somewhere around 1000 to 1500 liters of gas in a well-run digester. Pigs and chickens produce richer manure per kilogram but in smaller quantities.

### Tank Volume and Retention Time

The slurry must stay in the tank long enough to be fully digested, typically 20 to 40 days (the "retention time"). In a warm climate, aim for around 25 days; in cooler conditions, longer.

To size the tank: multiply the daily slurry input by the retention time. If you add 20 liters of slurry per day and want a 25-day retention, you need a digestion chamber of at least 500 liters, plus extra space at the top for gas. As a rule, size the chamber so daily input is no more than about 5 percent of its volume.

## Part 3: Building a Simple Floating-Drum Digester

The floating-drum design is the easiest to understand and the most forgiving for a beginner. Gas collects under an inverted drum that floats and rises as gas accumulates, giving you a visible gauge of how much gas you have and providing steady pressure.

### Step 1: The Digestion Chamber

Use a sealed tank or a buried, lined pit. A 200-liter steel or plastic drum works for one person; weld or seal a larger tank for a family. The chamber holds the slurry.

### Step 2: Inlet and Outlet

Fit an inlet pipe near the top that runs down into the slurry, so new feed enters below the surface without disturbing the gas space. Fit an outlet pipe (an overflow) at a level that lets spent slurry leave automatically when fresh slurry is added. The inlet and outlet should be on opposite sides so fresh feed flows across the tank before leaving.

### Step 3: The Gas Holder

Take a second, smaller drum and invert it over the chamber so it floats on the slurry surface, open end down. As gas collects, the drum rises. A guide pole or frame keeps it from tipping. The weight of the drum sets the gas pressure: heavier drum, higher pressure.

### Step 4: Gas Outlet

Fit a pipe and valve into the top of the floating drum. Run tubing from there to your stove or lamp. Install a simple water trap (a U-shaped section of pipe holding water) in the line to catch condensation and act as a pressure safety; if pressure gets too high, gas bubbles out through the water trap instead of bursting a seam.

### Step 5: Seal Everything

Every joint that touches the gas space must be gas-tight. Use gaskets, silicone, or pitch. After assembly, test for leaks (see verification below).

## Part 4: Starting and Feeding

### Charging the Digester

1. Mix fresh manure with water at roughly equal parts by volume to make a slurry the consistency of thin paint. Too thick and it will not flow or mix; too thin and microbes wash out.
2. Add a starter culture: a bucket of slurry from a working digester, or simply extra fresh manure, to seed the methanogens.
3. Fill the chamber, leaving the designed gas space at the top.
4. Seal it and wait. The first gas produced is mostly carbon dioxide and will not burn. Vent the first batch or two. After two to four weeks, the gas will light. This is normal startup behavior.

### Daily Feeding

Once running, feed a fixed amount of fresh slurry each day. Each addition pushes an equal volume of digested slurry out the overflow. Keep the feed steady; methanogens hate sudden changes. Do not dump in a huge load at once, and never add toxic materials.

### What to Feed and What to Avoid

**Good feedstock:** animal manure, food scraps, vegetable waste, crop residue (chopped small), and human sewage in properly designed systems.

**Avoid or limit:**

- Large amounts of woody or fibrous material (digests very slowly)
- Soap, disinfectants, antibiotics, or chemically treated water (these kill the microbes)
- Too much citrus or very acidic waste (drops the pH)
- Meat and fats in large quantities (can sour the tank, though small amounts are fine)

## Part 5: Using the Gas and the Slurry

### Burning Biogas Safely

Biogas burns with a clean blue flame in a stove with a slightly enlarged jet (the gas is less energy-dense than bottled gas, so burners often need a larger orifice). For lighting, use a biogas mantle lamp. The hydrogen sulfide in raw biogas is corrosive and mildly toxic; for cooking it is tolerable in ventilated spaces, but if you run an engine on biogas you should scrub the gas through iron filings or similar to remove the sulfide.

**Safety is critical.** Methane mixed with air between roughly 5 and 15 percent is explosive. Never use an open flame to check for leaks near the holder. Always ventilate the cooking area. Store gas only in purpose-built holders, never in sealed rigid containers that could burst.

### The Digestate: Free Fertilizer

The slurry that flows out the overflow, called digestate, is one of the best fertilizers you can make. Digestion preserves most of the nitrogen and converts it to forms plants absorb easily, while killing many weed seeds and pathogens. Let it settle or compost briefly, then apply it to crops. This closes the loop: waste becomes fuel becomes fertilizer becomes food becomes waste again.

## Worked Example: Sizing for a Small Family

Suppose a family of four cooks two meals a day and keeps two cows.

- **Gas need:** Roughly 250 liters per person per day for cooking, so about 1000 liters daily.
- **Manure supply:** Two cows produce enough manure to generate around 2000 to 3000 liters of gas per day in a healthy digester. Supply comfortably exceeds demand.
- **Slurry input:** Mixing the daily manure with equal water gives perhaps 40 liters of slurry per day.
- **Tank size:** With a 25-day retention, the chamber must hold at least 40 × 25 = 1000 liters of slurry, plus gas space on top. A 1200- to 1500-liter chamber is appropriate.

This family could meet all its cooking needs and have surplus gas for a lamp, while producing a steady stream of fertilizer for the garden.

## Verification Criteria

You know your digester is working when:

- **The gas burns.** Collect a sample and light it at the burner; a steady blue flame means good methane content. (Vent and discard the first batches, which will not light.)
- **The holder rises daily.** A floating drum that climbs each day shows active gas production.
- **No leaks.** Brush soapy water over every joint; bubbles reveal escaping gas. Fix all leaks before relying on the system.
- **Steady output.** Once mature, production should be roughly constant day to day with steady feeding.
- **Healthy slurry.** Digestate flowing out should be dark, smooth, and far less smelly than the raw input.

## Common Mistakes

- **Letting it go cold.** Production falls sharply below 20 degrees Celsius. Insulate or warm the tank in cold weather.
- **Overfeeding.** Dumping in too much feedstock sours the tank and stops gas production. Feed steady, modest daily amounts.
- **Air leaks.** A leaky seal both wastes gas and risks an explosive mixture. Seal and test thoroughly.
- **Adding toxins.** Soap, bleach, or antibiotic-laced manure can wipe out the microbes. Keep these out.
- **Expecting instant gas.** A new digester needs two to four weeks to mature. Do not give up early.
- **Using a flame to find leaks.** Always use soapy water, never an open flame near the gas holder.
- **Ignoring the water trap.** Without a pressure-relief water trap, a blocked line can let pressure build dangerously.
