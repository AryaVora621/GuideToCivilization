## Overview

Science tells you: *the Earth is 4.6 billion years old, gravity pulls downward, vaccines work*. But why should you believe these claims? Because scientists measured evidence? But how do you know the measurements are valid? Because other scientists replicated them? But what if all scientists are wrong?

Philosophy of science asks: What is knowledge? How do we know anything? What are the foundations of scientific belief?

This chapter teaches:

1. **Epistemology:** What is knowledge and how is it acquired?
2. **The scientific method's assumptions:** What does science assume to be true?
3. **Empiricism vs. rationalism:** Observation vs. logic as sources of truth
4. **Falsifiability:** What makes a claim testable?
5. **Limits of science:** What science cannot answer

Understanding philosophy of science makes you a better scientist and a more critical thinker.

## Part 1: What Is Knowledge?

### Three Elements of Knowledge

Most philosophers agree knowledge requires three things:

**1. Belief:** You must believe the claim (not just guess randomly).

**2. Truth:** The claim must actually be true (not just seem true).

**3. Justification:** You must have good reasons for believing it (evidence, logic, etc.).

Example: "The sun rises in the east."
- Belief: You believe this ✓
- Truth: It's actually true (Earth rotates, sun appears to rise) ✓
- Justification: You've observed it repeatedly ✓
- Result: This is knowledge.

Counterexample: "The sun is a god in a chariot."
- Belief: You believe this ✓
- Truth: It's false (the sun is a star) ✗
- Justification: You have reasons (observation, tradition) ✓
- Result: This is not knowledge (fails truth test).

### Sources of Knowledge

**Observation (Empiricism):** Knowledge comes from sensing the world. "I see the tree, therefore I know the tree exists."

**Logic (Rationalism):** Knowledge comes from reason. "All humans are mortal. Socrates is human. Therefore, Socrates is mortal."

**Authority:** Knowledge comes from trusted sources. "My teacher says this, so it's true."

**Intuition:** Knowledge comes from immediate understanding. "I just know this is right."

Science primarily uses empiricism + logic (observation + reasoning).

## Part 2: Assumptions Science Makes

Science rests on assumptions that are not *proven* but rather *assumed*:

### Assumption 1: The Physical World Exists Independently

Science assumes an objective reality exists regardless of what anyone thinks. The Earth was round before humans discovered it.

Why? Without this, science cannot make claims about nature—only about our perceptions.

### Assumption 2: The World Is Orderly and Lawful

Science assumes that patterns in nature repeat. If an experiment works once, it will work again under the same conditions.

Why? Without this, prediction and generalization are impossible. Everything would be random.

### Assumption 3: We Can Observe and Measure the World

Science assumes our senses and instruments give us reliable information about reality.

Why? Without this, evidence is meaningless. We'd have no access to the world.

### Assumption 4: Simpler Explanations Are Better

**Occam's Razor:** When two explanations fit the data equally, prefer the simpler one.

Example: "Plants grow because of sunlight" (simple) vs. "Plants grow because of sunlight and invisible plant-fairies and the phase of the moon" (complex). Choose the simple one.

Why? Simpler explanations are easier to test and have fewer hidden assumptions.

### Assumption 5: Causation Is Possible

Science assumes that some events cause others. A causes B, not just coincidence.

Why? Without causation, there's no mechanism, no explanation, just random correlation.

## Part 3: Empiricism and Rationalism

### Empiricism: Knowledge from Observation

**Claim:** All knowledge comes from sensory experience. You know fire is hot because you've felt it.

**Strength:** Direct connection to reality; testable claims.

**Weakness:** Senses deceive (optical illusions, hallucinations). Experience is limited (you can't experience distant galaxies directly).

### Rationalism: Knowledge from Logic

**Claim:** Some knowledge comes from pure reason. You know 2+2=4 without observing apples.

**Strength:** Logical truths are universal and certain.

**Weakness:** Logic alone doesn't tell you what the world is like. You must observe first, then reason.

### Science's Approach: Empirical Rationalism

Science combines both:
1. **Observe:** Gather data through experiment
2. **Reason:** Use logic to interpret the data
3. **Predict:** Use reasoning to make new predictions
4. **Test:** Observe again to verify predictions

Neither observation alone nor reason alone is sufficient.

## Part 4: Falsifiability

### Popper's Criterion

**Falsifiability:** A claim is scientific only if it's possible to prove it false through observation.

Example (scientific): "If I drop a rock, it will fall" → Falsifiable (if the rock floats, the claim is false).

Example (not scientific): "God exists" → Not falsifiable (any evidence can be explained by "God works in mysterious ways").

### Why Falsifiability Matters

A claim that cannot be disproven is not testable. If no observation could ever refute it, it's not a scientific claim—it might be true, but it's not science.

### Limits of Falsifiability

Pure falsifiability is too strict. Some scientific claims are hard to directly falsify (the age of the Earth, or the composition of a distant star). Science uses **indirect** falsifiability: theories that would predict observations that could be falsified.

## Part 5: What Science Cannot Answer

### Questions Science Cannot Answer

**"What is good/evil?"** (Ethics)
- Science can describe what people value, but cannot prescribe what they *should* value.

**"Does life have meaning?"** (Philosophy)
- Science can explain biological meaning, but not existential meaning.

**"Why is there something rather than nothing?"** (Metaphysics)
- Science explains how things work, but starts by assuming *something* exists.

**"What should I do?"** (Ethics/Values)
- Science can predict consequences, but cannot decide what's right.

### Why These Limits Exist

Science asks "how" and "what." It does not ask "why" in the deepest sense or "should" in the moral sense.

To answer these questions, you need philosophy, ethics, personal reflection—not just science.

## Part 6: Skepticism and Trust

### Scientific Skepticism

Science assumes **skepticism:** Require evidence for claims. Don't believe something just because an authority says so.

But skepticism has limits. Requiring proof of *everything* is impractical. You trust that atoms exist (indirect evidence) without personally observing them.

### Justified Trust

In practice, you trust scientists because:
1. They follow methods that anyone can verify
2. Their work is checked by others (peer review)
3. Claims are tested repeatedly across cultures and time
4. When errors are found, they're corrected

This is rational trust, not blind faith.

## Part 7: Worked Examples

### Example 1: Evaluating a Medical Claim

**Claim:** "This herbal supplement cures cancer."

**Philosophical analysis:**
- Is it testable? (Falsifiable?) Yes—if the supplement works, cancer patients should recover better than controls.
- Is it empirical? It should be tested through observation and experiment.
- Does evidence exist? Check peer-reviewed studies.
- Could it be wrong? Yes. If most users don't recover, the claim is false.

**Conclusion:** This is a scientific claim that can be tested. Evaluate it based on evidence.

### Example 2: Evaluating a Metaphysical Claim

**Claim:** "The universe has a purpose."

**Philosophical analysis:**
- Is it testable? (Falsifiable?) No—any observation is consistent with "the universe has a purpose" or "it doesn't."
- Can science answer this? Not directly. Science describes how the universe works, not whether it has purpose.

**Conclusion:** This is a philosophical question, not a scientific one. Answer it through philosophy, not science.
