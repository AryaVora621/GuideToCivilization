## Overview

You observe that people get sick when the weather turns cold. You conclude: "Cold causes sickness." But is this true? Maybe people stay indoors in cold weather and share germs. Maybe vitamin D deficiency (from reduced sun) causes illness. Maybe it is coincidence—sickness happens to occur in winter.

Your reasoning error: You jumped from correlation (cold and sickness occur together) to causation (cold causes sickness) without investigating alternative explanations.

This chapter teaches:

1. **Common reasoning errors:** Patterns we all fall into
2. **How to recognize them:** Specific questions that reveal flawed thinking
3. **How to correct them:** Strategies for better reasoning
4. **How to evaluate others' arguments:** Spotting errors in what you read or hear
5. **Why errors are normal:** Our brains are pattern-seekers, not logic machines

Reasoning errors are not failures; they are human. Recognizing them is how we improve.

## Part 1: Correlation vs. Causation

### The Core Error

**Correlation:** Two things occur together or change together.

**Causation:** One thing directly causes the other.

Correlation does not prove causation. This is the most common reasoning error.

### Example 1: Ice Cream and Drowning

**Observation:** In summer, ice cream sales and drowning deaths both increase.

**Flawed reasoning:** "Ice cream causes drowning."

**Reality:** Summer heat causes both. Heat makes people buy ice cream and makes people swim (and some drown). Ice cream does not cause drowning.

**How to recognize the error:** Ask, "Is there a more plausible common cause?" (Yes: summer heat causes both.)

### Example 2: Sleep Duration and Academic Performance

**Observation:** Students who sleep 8 hours per night have higher test scores than students who sleep 6 hours per night.

**Flawed reasoning:** "More sleep causes higher test scores."

**Alternative explanation:** Disciplined students sleep more and study more. Discipline (not sleep) causes high scores. Or: Stressed, anxious students sleep less and perform worse. Anxiety (not sleep) causes the difference.

**How to recognize the error:** Ask, "Could a third factor explain both?" (Yes: discipline, or anxiety, or socioeconomic factors affecting both sleep and test access.)

### How to Test Causation

To claim causation, you need:

1. **Correlation:** A and B occur together (or change together)
2. **Temporal order:** A occurs before B (not after)
3. **No plausible alternative explanation:** You have ruled out other causes

Example: "Sleep causes higher test scores."
- Correlation: ✓ More sleep correlates with higher scores
- Temporal order: ✓ Sleep happens before the test
- No alternative: ? Could discipline, wealth, or genetics explain both? Yes. So causation is not proven.

## Part 2: Confirmation Bias

### The Error

You believe something. You then seek evidence that supports your belief and ignore evidence that contradicts it. This is **confirmation bias**.

### Example

**Your belief:** "Herbal tea cures headaches."

You notice:
- Three times you drank herbal tea and your headache improved (confirming evidence)
- You ignore: Five times you drank herbal tea and your headache did not improve (contradicting evidence)
- You ignore: Three times your headache improved without tea (contradicting evidence)

**Result:** You are more confident in your belief, even though the evidence does not support it.

### How to Recognize It

Ask yourself:
- "What evidence would prove me wrong?"
- "Am I actively looking for contradicting evidence, or just supporting evidence?"
- "Are there times when my belief was not true?"

If you struggle to find contradicting evidence, you likely have confirmation bias.

### How to Correct It

**Method 1: Seek opposing views**
Listen to someone who disagrees with you. Ask them for evidence against your position. Really listen—do not just wait to respond.

**Method 2: Actively look for counterexamples**
Make a list of times your belief was wrong. If you cannot, you are not looking hard enough.

**Method 3: Play devil's advocate**
Argue against your own position. What would a skeptical person say? What evidence would convince them you are wrong?

**Method 4: Use a checklist**
Before concluding, ask:
- Do I have any evidence against this?
- Could I be wrong?
- What would change my mind?

## Part 3: Availability Heuristic

### The Error

You judge how common something is based on how easily examples come to mind. If examples are easily remembered, you overestimate frequency. If examples are hard to remember, you underestimate frequency.

### Example 1: Shark Attacks

Shark attacks are rare. But shark attack stories are memorable and often in the news. People overestimate the danger of sharks (availability heuristic: easy-to-recall examples lead to overestimation of frequency).

In reality, you are more likely to be killed by lightning than by sharks. But lightning deaths are less memorable, so fewer people fear them.

### Example 2: Causes of Death

People overestimate deaths from rare, dramatic causes (murder, plane crashes, terrorism) because these are memorable and in the news. People underestimate deaths from common, mundane causes (heart disease, diabetes, accidents) because these are less memorable.

**Reality:**
- Heart disease: ~600,000 deaths/year in the US
- Murder: ~20,000 deaths/year in the US
- Terrorism: ~100 deaths/year in the US

Yet people often perceive murder and terrorism as greater threats.

### How to Correct It

**Method 1: Gather actual statistics**
Do not rely on memory. Count or research actual frequencies.

**Method 2: Distinguish dramatic from common**
Events that are dramatic (memorable) are not necessarily frequent. Common events are often mundane and forgettable.

**Method 3: Ask, "How many examples do I actually know of?"**
You might remember three shark attacks (because they were dramatic). But do you know anyone who was killed by a shark? Probably not. Few people are. This contradiction reveals the bias.

## Part 4: Hasty Generalization

### The Error

You observe a few examples and conclude a general rule.

**Example 1:** You meet three people from a distant town who are unfriendly. You conclude: "People from that town are unfriendly."

But you have met only 3 out of thousands. Your sample is tiny and might not be representative. Other samples might contradict your conclusion.

**Example 2:** A farmer tries a new crop and it fails. He concludes: "This crop will not grow in our climate." But he tried it once, in one location, in one year. Variable conditions (soil quality, rainfall, pest pressure) might explain the failure. Different conditions might succeed.

### How to Recognize It

Ask yourself:
- "How many examples do I have?"
- "Could the examples be unusual or unrepresentative?"
- "Would different examples lead to a different conclusion?"

If you are concluding from fewer than 10 examples, you should be suspicious of hasty generalization.

### How to Correct It

**Method 1: Require larger samples**
Before generalizing, gather more examples. Ideally, at least 20–30 examples, randomly selected.

**Method 2: Look for exceptions**
If you claim "all X are Y," find one counterexample and your claim is false. "All people from town X are unfriendly" is disproven by one friendly person from town X.

**Method 3: Acknowledge uncertainty**
Instead of "The crop will not grow here," say "The crop failed in my trial. More testing is needed." This is less confident but more accurate.

## Part 5: Appeal to Authority

### The Error

You believe something because an authority figure says it, without evaluating the evidence yourself.

**Example 1:** "The village elder says this herb cures fever, so it must be true."

But the elder might be wrong. Age does not equal knowledge. Authority should be based on relevant expertise and evidence, not just title.

**Example 2:** "A famous person says the Earth is flat. I will believe them."

But fame does not equal expertise. A famous actor is not a geographer. Believe claims based on evidence and expertise, not fame.

### How to Recognize It

Ask yourself:
- "Is this person actually an expert in this domain?"
- "Would I believe this claim if it came from someone without authority?"
- "What is the evidence behind the authority's claim?"

If you are relying on authority rather than evidence, you have an appeal to authority bias.

### How to Correct It

**Method 1: Verify claims independently**
Do not take an expert's word for it. Ask: What is their evidence? Can I verify it myself?

**Method 2: Distinguish between domains**
An expert in one domain is not an expert in all domains. A skilled blacksmith is not an expert in medicine just because they are respected.

**Method 3: Look for dissenting experts**
If multiple experts disagree, none of them can claim absolute authority. Evidence, not authority, should decide.

## Part 6: Sunk Cost Fallacy

### The Error

You have invested time, effort, or resources into something. You continue investing even though it is clearly not working, because you do not want to "waste" what you have already invested.

This is the **sunk cost fallacy**: treating past investments as a reason to continue investing, even when the investment is failing.

### Example

**A farmer invests 2 months and significant labor into a failing crop.** 

The crop is clearly not growing (poor soil, pest damage, wrong climate). The rational decision is to stop growing this crop and plant something else.

But the farmer thinks: "I have already spent 2 months. If I quit now, those 2 months are wasted. Let me continue and hope it improves."

This is the fallacy. The 2 months are already gone. Continuing to invest will not recover them. The question is: "What should I do going forward?" not "Can I recover the past investment?"

### How to Recognize It

Ask yourself:
- "Am I continuing because this is the best option going forward?"
- "Or am I continuing because I do not want to admit defeat?"
- "If I had not already invested in this, would I choose to start now?"

If the answer to the last question is "No," you are likely in a sunk cost fallacy.

### How to Correct It

**Method 1: Forget the past investment**
Make decisions based on future value, not past cost. If the crop will not grow, stop growing it. The past is gone; focus on the future.

**Method 2: Ask, "What is my best option going forward?"**
Ignore sunk costs. What choice maximizes your future benefit?

**Method 3: Recognize that quitting is not failure**
Quitting a failing project is rational, not failure. Continuing a failing project hoping to recover past investment is the real failure.

## Part 7: False Dilemma

### The Error

You present only two options when more exist. You force a choice between false alternatives, limiting thinking.

**Example 1:** "You are either with us or against us."

But someone could be neutral, skeptical, or disagree about specific points while agreeing on others. The world is rarely binary.

**Example 2:** "Either we adopt this new farming method, or our crops will fail."

But there might be other options: improve the old method, adopt a different new method, adjust other factors. More than two options usually exist.

### How to Recognize It

Ask yourself:
- "Are there more than two options?"
- "Am I forced into this binary, or am I choosing it?"
- "What options am I not considering?"

If you can list three or more alternatives, the dilemma is false.

### How to Correct It

**Method 1: List all options**
Before deciding, explicitly list every option you can think of, not just two.

**Method 2: Challenge binary language**
When you hear "either...or," ask "What about...?" to explore other options.

**Method 3: Recognize spectrum instead of binary**
Many things exist on a spectrum, not as opposites. Instead of "good or bad," consider varying degrees of goodness.

## Part 8: Worked Examples

### Example 1: Analyzing a Flawed Argument

**Claim:** "This amulet protects from disease. Last winter, I wore it and did not get sick."

**Reasoning errors:**
1. **Correlation vs. causation:** The amulet might not be the cause. You stayed healthy due to isolation, good nutrition, or luck.
2. **Confirmation bias:** You remember the times you wore the amulet and stayed healthy. You might ignore times you wore it and got sick.
3. **Hasty generalization:** One winter is not enough to conclude the amulet works universally.
4. **Availability heuristic:** You remember this one winter prominently, so you overestimate the amulet's power.

**Correct reasoning:**
To prove the amulet works, you would need a controlled experiment: one group wears it, one group does not, both exposed to the same disease risk, and you compare illness rates.

### Example 2: Evaluating a Doctor's Claim

**Doctor's claim:** "This herbal remedy cures arthritis. I have treated 50 patients, and 40 improved."

**Evaluate:**
- **Authority:** Is the doctor a real expert? (Matters for credibility)
- **Sample size:** 50 patients is reasonable
- **Comparison:** But did 40 improve because of the remedy or due to placebo effect, natural recovery, or lifestyle changes? You need a control group (people with arthritis who do NOT take the remedy) to compare
- **Confirmation bias:** The doctor remembers the 40 who improved and forgets the 10 who did not
- **Correlation vs. causation:** Improvement correlated with the remedy, but did the remedy cause it?

**Conclusion:** The claim is not proven. You need a randomized controlled trial (half take remedy, half take placebo, neither knowing which they have, and compare outcomes).

## Part 9: Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Thinking You Are Immune to Bias

Everyone has reasoning errors. Experts have them. Smart people have them. Recognizing this is the first step to improving.

### Mistake 2: Dismissing an Argument Entirely Because It Has One Error

An argument might have an error but still be largely correct. Address the specific error; do not dismiss the whole argument.

### Mistake 3: Assuming "I Personally Experienced X" Proves a General Rule

Personal experience is valid data, but it is a sample of one. It is prone to all the biases in this chapter. Balance personal experience with other evidence.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Disconfirming Evidence

When you find evidence against your belief, do not dismiss it. Address it. You might change your mind, or you might strengthen your belief with better evidence.

### Mistake 5: Confusing Logical Fallacy with Being Wrong

A fallacy is a flaw in reasoning. A claim can be true even if it is argued with a fallacy. A claim can be false even if it is argued logically. Separate the reasoning from the claim.

## Verification

You understand reasoning errors when:

1. You can distinguish between correlation and causation
2. You can identify confirmation bias in yourself and others
3. You can recognize when you are generalizing from too few examples
4. You can evaluate authority claims critically
5. You can spot sunk cost fallacy reasoning
6. You can identify false dilemmas and find alternative options
7. You can explain why our minds are prone to these errors (we are pattern-seekers; quick intuitions served us well evolutionarily but mislead us now)
