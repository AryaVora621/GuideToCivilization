## Overview

Observation leads to questions. Questions suggest possible answers. A hypothesis is a *proposed* answer—a prediction about how something works. But a proposal is not proof. The only way to test a hypothesis is through experiment: deliberately arranging conditions to see if your prediction matches reality.

This chapter teaches you how to:

1. **Form a testable hypothesis** from an observation or question
2. **Design an experiment** that fairly tests the hypothesis
3. **Control variables** so your experiment answers the right question
4. **Collect data** systematically
5. **Analyze results** and draw conclusions
6. **Recognize when an experiment fails** and what to do about it

Experiments are not guesses or hopes. They are deliberate, repeatable tests. If two people perform the same experiment in different places at different times and get the same result, that result is trustworthy. This is how knowledge is built.

## Prerequisites

- Ability to observe carefully and ask questions (from Ch 1)
- Understanding of cause and effect (if A causes B, changing A should change B)
- Willingness to be wrong (the best experiments are those that disprove your guess)
- Ability to measure or count (quantitative data is stronger than qualitative)
- Patience: experiments take time; rushing ruins results

## Materials & Tools

**For designing and conducting experiments:**
- Notebook or journal (to record hypothesis, method, and results)
- Measuring instruments (ruler, thermometer, scale, watch, measuring cup)
- Materials specific to your hypothesis (varies by experiment)
- Ability to control conditions (temperature, light, time, access to resources)

**For analysis:**
- Ability to count, measure, and calculate
- Optional: graph paper or simple charting (to visualize results)

## Part 1: What Is a Hypothesis?

### Hypothesis vs. Guess

A **guess** is a random shot in the dark. A **hypothesis** is an educated prediction based on what you already know.

A hypothesis:
- Is based on observation or existing knowledge
- Makes a specific, testable prediction
- Proposes a cause-and-effect relationship
- Can be proven false

Examples of testable hypotheses:
- "Plants in sunlight grow taller than plants in shade"
- "Warm water dissolves salt faster than cold water"
- "Mold grows faster on bread with added sugar than on bread without sugar"
- "Fish survive longer in moving water than in still water"

Examples of *untestable* statements (not hypotheses):
- "Nature is beautiful" (opinion, not testable)
- "Life exists on other planets" (too broad; cannot be directly tested in a lab)
- "Some plants are stronger than others" (too vague; what does "stronger" mean?)

### The Form of a Hypothesis

A strong hypothesis follows this pattern:

**"If [I change X], then [Y will change] because [reason]."**

Examples:
- "If I increase the temperature of water, then salt will dissolve faster, because heat increases the motion of molecules."
- "If I add sugar to bread dough, then mold will grow faster on it, because sugar feeds fungal cells."
- "If I remove oxygen from a container of water, then fish will die, because fish need dissolved oxygen to breathe."

Notice:
- The hypothesis identifies the **independent variable** (the thing you change: temperature, sugar, oxygen)
- The hypothesis identifies the **dependent variable** (the thing that changes as a result: dissolution rate, mold growth, fish survival)
- The hypothesis gives a **reason** (not required, but helpful for understanding)

### Why Hypothesis Matters

A hypothesis *focuses* your experiment. Without one, you wander. With one, every measurement serves a purpose. The hypothesis is a prediction you will test, not a prediction you expect to be correct. If your hypothesis is wrong, that is still valuable knowledge.

## Part 2: Designing an Experiment

### Step 1: Identify Your Variables

Every experiment involves three types of variables:

**Independent variable:** The thing YOU change.
- Example: temperature of water (you heat it or cool it)
- You control this directly

**Dependent variable:** The thing that CHANGES AS A RESULT.
- Example: how fast salt dissolves (you measure this)
- You measure this to see if your hypothesis is correct

**Control variables:** Everything else that might affect the result (you keep these constant).
- Example: amount of salt, type of salt, size of salt crystals, type of water, container size, stirring method
- You keep these the same so you know changes in the dependent variable are caused only by the independent variable

### Step 2: Design a Fair Test

A **fair test** changes only the independent variable while keeping all other factors constant.

Unfair test: "I'll test if warm water dissolves salt faster by heating the water, stirring it more, and using finer salt crystals." (Three independent variables—you cannot tell which one caused the result.)

Fair test: "I'll heat the water to different temperatures (independent variable) while keeping the amount of salt, type of salt, container size, and stirring method identical (control variables). I'll measure dissolution time (dependent variable)."

### Step 3: Decide How Many Tests You Need

**Replication:** Repeat your experiment at least three times (more is better).

Why?
- Random chance can skew one trial
- Three trials reveal if results are consistent
- If all three trials give similar results, the result is more trustworthy

Example: Testing if sunlight increases plant growth.
- Plant A: sunlight, measure height after 4 weeks
- Plant B: sunlight, measure height after 4 weeks
- Plant C: sunlight, measure height after 4 weeks
- Plant D: no sunlight, measure height after 4 weeks
- Plant E: no sunlight, measure height after 4 weeks
- Plant F: no sunlight, measure height after 4 weeks

Results: If plants A, B, C are all taller than D, E, F, the result is reliable.

## Part 3: Conducting the Experiment

### Step 1: Plan the Details

Write down *exactly* what you will do:
- What materials will you use (and how much)?
- What are the exact conditions (temperature, light, time)?
- How often will you measure?
- What will you measure or count?
- How will you record data?

Example plan for "Does salt dissolve faster in warm water?":
1. Prepare three cups of water at 10°C (cold), three at 25°C (room temperature), three at 50°C (warm)
2. Measure 10 grams of salt into each cup
3. Stir each cup at a constant rate (20 stirs per minute)
4. Record the time until all salt dissolves
5. Calculate the average dissolution time for each temperature

### Step 2: Control the Environment

Minimize unexpected changes:
- Keep ambient temperature steady (or heat/cool deliberately)
- Keep light constant (or change it deliberately)
- Keep all containers the same size
- Use the same tool for measuring
- Stir the same way each time
- Repeat at the same time of day (if possible)

Small uncontrolled changes = unreliable results.

### Step 3: Collect Data Systematically

Record everything:
- Date and time
- Exact conditions (temperature, light, humidity)
- Measurements (numbers are better than descriptions)
- Observations (what you see)
- Anything unexpected

Keep a **data table**:

| Temperature | Trial 1 (seconds) | Trial 2 (seconds) | Trial 3 (seconds) | Average |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------------|-------------------|---------|
| 10°C        | 145               | 152               | 148               | 148.3   |
| 25°C        | 95                | 92                | 98                | 95      |
| 50°C        | 45                | 48                | 46                | 46.3    |

Numbers in a table are easier to analyze than scattered notes.

### Step 4: Avoid Bias

**Confirmation bias:** You expect salt to dissolve faster in warm water, so you unconsciously record times that support this. (Solution: record the actual time, not what you expect.)

**Observer effect:** Your presence changes the result. (Solution: use a timer; do not guess by watching.)

**Placebo effect:** Not applicable here, but the principle: do not *assume* a result before you measure.

## Part 4: Analyzing Results

### Step 1: Calculate Averages

If you ran three trials, calculate the average for each condition:

Average = (Trial 1 + Trial 2 + Trial 3) / 3

From the table above:
- Cold (10°C): Average = 148.3 seconds
- Room (25°C): Average = 95 seconds
- Warm (50°C): Average = 46.3 seconds

### Step 2: Look for Patterns

- Cold → takes longest
- Warm → takes shortest
- Pattern: warmer temperature → faster dissolution

This matches the hypothesis: "If temperature increases, dissolution time decreases."

### Step 3: Calculate the Magnitude of Change

How much faster?
- Cold to room: 148.3 → 95 seconds (36% faster)
- Room to warm: 95 → 46.3 seconds (51% faster)

The change is substantial and consistent.

### Step 4: Consider Error and Uncertainty

Are the differences real or due to measurement error?

- Trial-to-trial variation in cold water: 145, 152, 148 (range: 7 seconds)
- Trial-to-trial variation in warm water: 45, 48, 46 (range: 3 seconds)

The variation is small relative to the differences between temperatures (cold vs. warm differ by over 100 seconds). The result is **reliable**.

If variations were large (e.g., 100-second swings), you would need more trials or better controls.

## Part 5: Drawing Conclusions

### Conclusion from the Example Experiment

"In three trials each, salt dissolved faster in warmer water:
- At 10°C: average 148.3 seconds
- At 25°C: average 95 seconds
- At 50°C: average 46.3 seconds

The hypothesis is **supported**: temperature affects dissolution rate. Warmer water dissolves salt 3× faster than cold water.

The reason (from chemistry): Warmer molecules move faster, colliding with salt crystals more frequently, breaking the bonds that hold the salt together."

### When a Hypothesis Is Disproven

You test: "If I add acid to chalk, it will dissolve."

Result: Nothing happens. Chalk does not dissolve in water, acid, or salt water.

Conclusion: "The hypothesis is **not supported**. Chalk does not dissolve in acid under the tested conditions."

This is valuable. You now know chalk is resistant to acid—useful for choosing materials in a survival or building context.

## Part 6: Worked Examples

### Example 1: Plant Growth and Light

**Hypothesis:** "If plants receive more sunlight, they will grow taller."

**Independent variable:** Hours of sunlight per day (0, 4, 8, 12 hours)

**Dependent variable:** Plant height after 8 weeks (measured in cm)

**Control variables:** Same soil, same water amount, same pot size, same plant species, same starting size, same temperature, same humidity

**Results:**
- 0 hours sun: average height 8 cm (weak, pale)
- 4 hours sun: average height 15 cm
- 8 hours sun: average height 22 cm
- 12 hours sun: average height 24 cm

**Conclusion:** More sunlight correlates with greater height. The hypothesis is supported. (Note: light saturates around 12 hours; more is not always better.)

### Example 2: Salt Content and Ice Formation

**Hypothesis:** "If water contains salt, it will freeze at a lower temperature than pure water."

**Independent variable:** Salt concentration (0%, 5%, 10%, 20% by weight)

**Dependent variable:** Temperature at which water freezes (measured in °C)

**Control variables:** Same water volume, same salt type, same cooling method, same container

**Results:**
- 0% salt: freezes at 0°C
- 5% salt: freezes at -2.5°C
- 10% salt: freezes at -5.5°C
- 20% salt: freezes at -16°C

**Conclusion:** Salt lowers the freezing point. This explains why salting roads melts ice (salt water has a lower freezing point and remains liquid at temperatures where pure ice would form).

## Verification

You understand hypothesis and experiment when:

1. You can form a testable hypothesis from an observation
2. You can identify independent, dependent, and control variables in an experiment
3. You can design a fair test that isolates one variable
4. You can collect data systematically without bias
5. You can calculate averages and identify patterns
6. You can distinguish between correlation (two things change together) and causation (one thing causes another)
7. You understand why replication matters
8. You can draw a conclusion supported by data, not by hope

---

## Common Mistakes

1. **Testing multiple independent variables at once:** "I'll heat the water AND stir it faster AND use finer salt." Now you do not know which caused faster dissolution. Test ONE variable at a time.

2. **Forgetting control variables:** You change temperature but also change the stirring speed. Different stirring = different dissolution time. Control variables isolate cause.

3. **Running too few trials:** One trial is luck. Two trials might be coincidence. Three or more trials reveal the true pattern.

4. **Recording vague data:** "Salt dissolved quickly" is useless. "Salt dissolved in 47 seconds" is useful. Measure, do not guess.

5. **Confusing correlation with causation:** "Ice cream sales increase in summer; therefore, ice cream causes hot weather." No—both correlate with summer heat (a third cause). Experiments isolate cause by controlling other variables.

6. **Bias toward your hypothesis:** You predict X will happen; you subconsciously record results that support X. Measure objectively. Let data surprise you.

7. **Ignoring failed experiments:** A failed hypothesis is not a waste. It teaches you what does NOT work. Record it.

8. **Not repeating experiments:** Repeating someone else's experiment (even with the same materials) is valid science. Replication builds trust in results.
