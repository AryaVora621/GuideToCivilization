## Overview

You conduct an experiment. You measure. You get results. But do your results mean anything? Or did you get lucky?

Statistics answers this question: How confident should you be in your data?

This chapter teaches:

1. **Average, median, and variation:** How to summarize what your numbers tell you
2. **Normal distribution:** Why most measurements cluster around a typical value
3. **Confidence and uncertainty:** How much error is acceptable
4. **Comparing groups:** When is a difference real vs. random chance
5. **Data visualization:** How to show your results clearly

A single measurement tells you little. Many measurements, analyzed statistically, tell you truth.

## Prerequisites

- Ability to add, divide, and calculate simple arithmetic
- Understanding that measurements vary (no two measurements are identical)
- Experience conducting an experiment (from Ch 2)
- Willingness to learn that reality is messier than it appears

## Part 1: Describing Your Data

### Why Numbers Need Summaries

You conduct an experiment 10 times and get these results:

**Dissolution times (in seconds):** 45, 47, 46, 44, 48, 46, 45, 46, 47, 45

What do these numbers *mean*? Saying "it took 45, 47, 46..." is useless. You need a summary.

### The Average (Mean)

The **average** (mean) is the sum of all values divided by the count of values.

Calculation:
(45 + 47 + 46 + 44 + 48 + 46 + 45 + 46 + 47 + 45) / 10 = 459 / 10 = 45.9 seconds

**What it means:** If you did the experiment once, you would expect about 45.9 seconds.

**Strength:** The average accounts for all measurements and is easy to calculate.

**Weakness:** The average ignores whether measurements are tightly clustered (45, 46, 45, 46) or wildly scattered (10, 85, 50, 45).

### The Median

The **median** is the middle value when you arrange all measurements from smallest to largest.

Arrange your 10 values:
44, 45, 45, 45, 45, 46, 46, 46, 47, 47, 48

The median is the value in the middle. With 10 values (even number), take the average of the two middle values:
Median = (45 + 46) / 2 = 45.5 seconds

**What it means:** Half the measurements were below this value; half were above.

**Strength:** The median is not affected by extreme outliers (if one measurement was 500 seconds, it would not shift the median much).

**Weakness:** The median ignores some of your data.

### The Mode

The **mode** is the most frequently occurring value.

In your data: 45 appears 4 times, 46 appears 3 times, others appear once or twice.
Mode = 45 seconds

**What it means:** The most typical result.

**Strength:** Simple; shows what actually happened most often.

**Weakness:** Not useful if no value repeats or if all values repeat equally.

## Part 2: Understanding Variation

### The Range

The **range** is the difference between the highest and lowest values.

Highest: 48 seconds
Lowest: 44 seconds
Range = 48 - 44 = 4 seconds

**What it means:** All measurements fell within a 4-second span.

**Strength:** Shows how spread out your data is.

**Weakness:** A single extreme outlier can inflate the range.

### Standard Deviation (Informal)

**Standard deviation** measures how much typical measurements deviate from the average. A small standard deviation means measurements cluster tightly. A large standard deviation means measurements are scattered.

Informal calculation (sufficient for most purposes):

1. Calculate the average: 45.9 seconds
2. For each measurement, calculate how far it is from average:
   - 45 is 0.9 below average
   - 47 is 1.1 above average
   - 46 is 0.1 above average
   - (and so on)
3. Average these differences: roughly 0.8 seconds

**What it means:** Most measurements are within 1 second of the average (45.9 ± 1 second = 44.9 to 46.9 seconds).

### Why Variation Matters

Two experiments, both with an average of 45.9 seconds:

**Experiment A:** 45, 45, 46, 46, 45, 46, 46, 45, 46, 46 (tight clustering, range = 1)

**Experiment B:** 20, 25, 45, 50, 70, 80, 10, 50, 40, 39 (scattered, range = 70)

Same average. Experiment A is **reliable** (you get similar results each time). Experiment B is **unreliable** (results swing wildly). Variation reveals this.

## Part 3: Normal Distribution

### The Bell Curve

If you conduct a measurement many times under identical conditions, results form a pattern: most values cluster around an average, with fewer outliers on either side.

Visualize this:

```
      | frequency
      |     *
      |    ***
      |   *****
      |  *******
      | *********
      |***********
      |_____________
        average
```

This shape is called a **normal distribution** or **bell curve**.

Examples of normally distributed measurements:
- Plant heights in a population (most are average height; some are taller, some shorter)
- Rainfall amounts across years (some years are wet, some dry, most are average)
- Reaction times in experiments (most people react in similar times; some faster, some slower)
- Error in measurements (most measurements are close to true value; some are slightly off)

### The 68-95-99.7 Rule

In a normal distribution:
- 68% of measurements fall within 1 standard deviation of the average
- 95% fall within 2 standard deviations
- 99.7% fall within 3 standard deviations

Example: Plant height with average = 30 cm, standard deviation = 2 cm.

- 68% of plants are 28–32 cm tall (1 std dev)
- 95% are 26–34 cm (2 std devs)
- 99.7% are 24–36 cm (3 std devs)

This means:
- If you measure a plant at 28 cm, it is not unusual (within 1 std dev of average)
- If you measure a plant at 50 cm, it is highly unusual (5 std devs from average, indicating a different variety or conditions)

## Part 4: Comparing Groups

### When Is a Difference Real?

You test: "Does salt dissolve faster in warm water?"

- Warm water: average 45.9 seconds
- Cold water: average 52.3 seconds

The difference is 6.4 seconds. But is this difference real, or just random variation?

If you repeat the experiment many times:
- If warm water is *always* faster, the difference is **real**.
- If warm water is faster *most of the time*, the difference is likely **real**.
- If warm water is sometimes faster and sometimes slower, the difference might be **random chance**.

### Checking Significance

Look at the ranges:

**Warm water results:** 43, 44, 45, 46, 47, 48, 49 (range: 43–49, average 45.9)

**Cold water results:** 50, 51, 52, 53, 54, 55, 56 (range: 50–56, average 52.9)

The ranges do *not* overlap. Every warm measurement is lower than every cold measurement. The difference is **real**.

Now imagine:

**Warm:** 40, 45, 50, 55, 60 (range: 40–60, average 50)

**Cold:** 45, 50, 55, 60, 65 (range: 45–65, average 55)

Ranges overlap significantly. It is unclear whether warm water is truly faster or if the difference is random variation. This difference is **uncertain**.

### Rule of Thumb

If the ranges (or standard deviations) of two groups do not overlap, the difference is likely real. If they overlap significantly, the difference is uncertain.

## Part 5: Data Visualization

### Why Graphs Matter

Numbers in a table are hard to interpret. A graph shows patterns instantly.

### Types of Graphs

**Line graph** (how something changes over time):
```
Temperature (°C) vs. Time (hours)
|
|        ___
|       /   \
|      /     \___
|     /
|____/_____________
     time
```
Use this when you measure the same variable repeatedly over time.

**Bar graph** (comparing categories):
```
Dissolution time (seconds)
|
| ___
| |   |
| |   |
| |___| ___
| |   | |   |
| |___| |   | ___
| |     |___| |   |
| |           |   |___
|_|_____|_____|_______|
  10C   25C   50C
  (temperature)
```
Use this to compare independent groups (warm vs. cold, with salt vs. without salt).

**Histogram** (distribution of measurements):
```
Frequency
|        *
|       ***
|      *****
|     *******
|    *********
|   ***********
|_____________
  45 46 47 48
  (dissolution time in seconds)
```
Use this to show the spread of many measurements.

### Good Graph Practices

1. **Title:** "Dissolution Time vs. Water Temperature"
2. **Axis labels:** X-axis: "Temperature (°C)", Y-axis: "Time (seconds)"
3. **Data points:** Plot actual values
4. **Scale:** Start at 0 (unless there is a reason not to); use equal intervals
5. **Legend:** If multiple lines or bars, label them

## Part 6: Worked Examples

### Example 1: Plant Growth (Summarizing Data)

You grow 20 plants under identical conditions and measure height after 8 weeks:

Measurements (in cm): 18, 19, 20, 19, 21, 18, 20, 19, 22, 20, 19, 18, 21, 20, 19, 20, 18, 19, 20, 21

**Average:** (18+19+20+...+21) / 20 = 398 / 20 = 19.9 cm

**Median:** Arrange: 18, 18, 18, 18, 19, 19, 19, 19, 19, 19, 20, 20, 20, 20, 20, 20, 21, 21, 21, 22. Median = (19 + 20) / 2 = 19.5 cm

**Mode:** 19 and 20 (both appear 6 times)

**Range:** 22 - 18 = 4 cm

**Interpretation:** Plants average 19.9 cm, with most between 18–21 cm. Variation is small (4 cm range), suggesting consistent growing conditions.

### Example 2: Comparing Water Temperatures

You measure salt dissolution time in three temperatures:

**Cold (10°C):** 48, 50, 49, 51, 50 (average 49.6 sec, range 48–51)

**Warm (50°C):** 22, 24, 23, 25, 24 (average 23.6 sec, range 22–25)

Ranges do not overlap (48–51 vs. 22–25). Warm water dissolves salt faster. The difference (49.6 - 23.6 = 26 seconds) is **real**.

## Part 7: Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Using Average Alone

Two experiments with average = 50:
- A: 48, 49, 50, 51, 52 (consistent)
- B: 10, 30, 50, 70, 90 (scattered)

The average hides the difference. Always report range or standard deviation too.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Outliers

You measure 10 times and get: 45, 46, 45, 46, 1000. The average (208) is useless. Investigate the outlier. Did you make a recording error? Did something go wrong? Remove or explain it.

### Mistake 3: Confusing Categories

You compare "tall" vs. "short" plants without defining what "tall" and "short" mean. Use numbers (height in cm), not descriptions.

### Mistake 4: Drawing a Line Through Scattered Points

If you plot points (45, 47), (46, 48), (48, 49), a straight line through all three is misleading. The points show variation, not a perfect relationship. Draw a **trend line** (average direction) instead, or simply plot the points without connecting them.

### Mistake 5: Using Too Few Data Points

One or two measurements are not statistics; they are guesses. Conduct at least 10 trials (preferably 20+) to trust your summary.

### Mistake 6: Assuming Average = Typical

You have 100 people with incomes: 99 earn $30,000/year; 1 earns $1,000,000. Average income = $39,900. But the median ($30,000) is more "typical." Use both average and median.

## Verification

You understand statistics when:

1. You can calculate average, median, and range from a set of measurements
2. You understand what standard deviation means (spread around the average)
3. You recognize a normal distribution and can apply the 68-95-99.7 rule
4. You can compare two groups and determine if a difference is real or random
5. You can create a bar graph or line graph to show results
6. You understand that variation is normal and expected
7. You can explain why one experiment with 10 trials is more trustworthy than an experiment with 1 trial
