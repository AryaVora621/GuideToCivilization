## Overview

Calculus is the mathematics of change. It answers questions like: How fast is something changing? What is the maximum or minimum value? How much accumulates over time?

After collapse, calculus helps with resource management: How much water accumulates in a cistern as rain falls? What angle maximizes arrow range? How fast does a disease spread?

This chapter covers practical calculus concepts without formal derivation.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of geometry and algebra
- Ability to think about rates and change
- Willingness to use approximations

## Materials & Tools

- Paper and pen for calculations
- Understanding of graphs (x-axis, y-axis)
- Tables of values (created by experimentation)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Understand Rates of Change

Many things change over time.

**Examples:**

- Water filling a cistern: gallons per minute
- Disease spreading: new cases per day
- Arrow slowing: meters per second lost per meter traveled
- Crop growth: centimeters per week

**Rate = change in quantity / change in time**

Example: Cistern fills 10 gallons in 2 minutes. Rate = 10/2 = 5 gallons per minute.

### Step 2: Recognize When Rates Change

The rate itself can change.

**Constant rate:**

Water flows at steady 5 gallons per minute. After 10 minutes: 50 gallons. Linear growth.

**Changing rate:**

Arrow velocity starts at 50 m/s but decreases. After 10 meters: 40 m/s. After 20 meters: 30 m/s. Nonlinear change.

Disease starts infecting 2 people per day, but as more people are infected, rate increases (exponential growth). Day 1: 2 cases. Day 2: 4 cases. Day 3: 8 cases.

### Step 3: Find Maximum and Minimum Values

Many decisions involve optimization.

**Example:** Arrow range varies with launch angle.
- 10 degrees: 50 meters range
- 30 degrees: 95 meters range
- 45 degrees: 100 meters range (maximum)
- 60 degrees: 90 meters range
- 80 degrees: 45 meters range

At 45 degrees, range is maximum. This is the optimum angle.

**How to find maximum without calculus:**

1. Test multiple values
2. Plot on graph
3. Find the highest point
4. Record value at that point

### Step 4: Understand Accumulation

Total accumulated value equals sum of incremental changes.

**Example:** Rainfall accumulation.
- Hour 1: 2 mm rain
- Hour 2: 3 mm rain
- Hour 3: 1 mm rain
- Total after 3 hours: 2 + 3 + 1 = 6 mm

**More complex:** If rain falls at 1 mm per hour for 5 hours, total = 5 mm. But if rate varies hourly (1, 2, 2, 1, 0.5 mm), total = 6.5 mm.

### Step 5: Use Slope to Predict Change

If you know current rate, you can estimate near-future value.

**Example:** Cistern at 100 gallons, filling at 5 gallons per minute.

Estimate after 10 minutes: 100 + (5 × 10) = 150 gallons.

This works if rate stays constant. If rate changes, estimate becomes less accurate.

### Step 6: Recognize Diminishing Returns

Some processes show diminishing returns.

**Example:** More workers make more bricks, but not proportionally.

- 1 worker: 100 bricks per day
- 2 workers: 180 bricks per day (not 200, because they interfere)
- 3 workers: 250 bricks per day (marginal gain of 70, vs 80 before)
- 4 workers: 310 bricks per day (marginal gain of 60, decreasing)

After some point, adding workers helps less and less.

### Step 7: Understand Exponential Growth and Decay

Some quantities multiply each time period.

**Exponential growth (disease, population):**

Initial: 10 cases
Each person infects 2 others

- Day 0: 10 cases
- Day 1: 10 × 2 = 20 cases
- Day 2: 20 × 2 = 40 cases
- Day 3: 40 × 2 = 80 cases

Doubles each day. Growth accelerates.

**Exponential decay (radioactivity, confidence in rumor):**

Initial: 100 people believe a rumor
Each day, half stop believing

- Day 0: 100 believers
- Day 1: 50 believers
- Day 2: 25 believers
- Day 3: 12 believers

Halves each day. Growth slows.

### Step 8: Use Graphs to Visualize Change

Plot data to see patterns.

**Example:** Crop yield vs. water amount

Plot points:
- 10mm water: 50 kg yield
- 20mm: 90 kg
- 30mm: 120 kg
- 40mm: 140 kg
- 50mm: 155 kg

Graph shows increasing yield, but rate of increase slows. Beyond 50mm, more water does not help (reaches optimum).

### Step 9: Apply to Decision-Making

Use these concepts to make better decisions.

**Example: Allocate workers to tasks**

Task A: 1 worker makes 100 units. 2 workers make 180 units (marginal gain: 80).
Task B: 1 worker makes 60 units. 2 workers make 105 units (marginal gain: 45).

Assign first extra worker to Task A (better return).

**Example: Manage epidemic**

Disease spreading exponentially. Daily doubling.

Today: 10 cases. If no action taken:
- Day 5: 320 cases
- Day 10: 10,240 cases (epidemic)

Intervention: Reduce transmission to 1.5× (instead of 2×)
- Day 5: 76 cases (much slower)
- Day 10: 380 cases (manageable)

Early intervention prevents exponential explosion.

## Verification

Successful use of calculus concepts results in:
- Accurate rate predictions
- Good optimization decisions
- Recognition of exponential growth/decay
- Better resource allocation

If inadequate:
- Estimates are significantly wrong
- Optimization misses true maximum/minimum
- Exponential growth surprises you (seems to suddenly explode)

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1 - Assuming constant rates.**

Rate changes, but you assume it stays the same. Prediction is off.

**Mistake 2 - Ignoring exponential growth.**

Disease is doubling. You think it is growing linearly. Reality surprises (catastrophic).

**Mistake 3 - Confusing average with current rate.**

Average water flow over 10 days is 5 gallons/min, but current flow is 3 gal/min. Estimate is wrong.

**Mistake 4 - Missing diminishing returns.**

You keep adding workers assuming each adds same output. Actually, marginal gain decreases.

**Mistake 5 - Not collecting data.**

You guess at rates without measuring. Estimates are inaccurate. Always measure.

**Mistake 6 - Oversimplifying.**

Real systems are complex. Single-factor models miss important interactions.
