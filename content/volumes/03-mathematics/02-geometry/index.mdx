## Overview

Geometry is the mathematics of shapes and space. It is essential for building, surveying land, and navigating. A crooked wall wastes materials and looks bad. A misaligned field boundary causes disputes. Incorrect angle calculations lead to collapsed structures.

This chapter covers practical geometry: how to measure angles without instruments, calculate areas and volumes, use triangulation for surveying, and ensure structures are square and level.

## Prerequisites

- Comfort with basic arithmetic (addition, multiplication, fractions)
- Practical experience with building or land measurement
- Ability to work with approximate calculations

## Materials & Tools

- Rope and stakes (for surveying)
- Sticks or straight edges (for angles)
- Measuring tape or marked rope (for distances)
- Right angle tools (carpenters square, or make one)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Understand Basic Geometric Shapes

Shapes have properties you can measure and calculate.

**Triangle:**
- Three sides, three angles
- Sum of angles = 180 degrees
- Area = (base × height) / 2
- Right triangle: One angle is 90 degrees (Pythagorean theorem: a² + b² = c²)

**Rectangle:**
- Four sides, four 90-degree angles
- Opposite sides equal length
- Area = length × width
- Perimeter = 2 × (length + width)

**Circle:**
- All points equidistant from center
- Radius: Distance from center to edge
- Diameter: Distance across (2 × radius)
- Circumference = 2π × radius ≈ 6.28 × radius
- Area = π × radius² ≈ 3.14 × radius²

**Sphere:**
- 3D circle
- Volume = (4/3) × π × radius³
- Surface area = 4π × radius²

**Cylinder:**
- Circle with height
- Volume = π × radius² × height
- Surface area = 2π × radius² + 2π × radius × height

**Cube/rectangular solid:**
- Volume = length × width × height
- Surface area = 2 × (length × width + width × height + height × length)

### Step 2: Measure and Create Right Angles

A 90-degree angle is essential for building.

**Using the 3-4-5 triangle:**

The Pythagorean theorem tells us: 3² + 4² = 5² (9 + 16 = 25).

A triangle with sides 3, 4, and 5 units always has a 90-degree angle opposite the longest side.

**How to create a right angle:**

1. Mark a point (corner)
2. Measure 3 units along one direction, mark a point
3. Measure 4 units at an angle from the corner, mark a point
4. The distance between the two marked points should be 5 units
5. If it is, the angle at the corner is 90 degrees
6. If not, adjust the angle until it is

**Using a carpenter's square:**

- Place the square at the corner
- Align one arm along one wall
- Align other arm perpendicular
- The corner is now 90 degrees

**Scaling up:**

Use multiples of 3-4-5:
- 6-8-10 (twice as big)
- 9-12-15 (three times as big)
- 30-40-50 (ten times as big)

Use these for larger structures (fields, buildings).

### Step 3: Measure Distances Accurately

You cannot build correctly without accurate measurements.

**Pacing:**

Count your steps to estimate distance. Measure your pace (average distance per step), then pace × number of steps = distance. Accurate to about ±5%.

**Rope measurement:**

Mark a rope with knots every 1 meter (or standard unit). Use to measure distances. Accurate to ±1%.

**Triangulation:**

Measure distance to a point you cannot reach:
1. Create a baseline (measure distance between two points A and B)
2. Measure angles from A and B to the target point C
3. Use trigonometry (or graphically, draw to scale on paper) to find distance

Example: Distance across a river.
- Baseline: 100 meters along river bank
- Angle from point A to opposite bank: 45 degrees
- Angle from point B to opposite bank: 45 degrees
- Calculate distance (in this case, also 100 meters due to geometry)

### Step 4: Calculate Areas and Volumes

Critical for planning resource use.

**Area of irregular shapes:**

Break into triangles and rectangles:
- Measure dimensions of each piece
- Calculate area of each piece
- Sum = total area

Example: Field shape is irregular. Divide into 4 rectangles, calculate each, sum.

**Volume for storage:**

Calculate how much a container holds.

Example: Storage pit. Rectangular. 4m long, 2m wide, 2m deep.
Volume = 4 × 2 × 2 = 16 cubic meters. Can store 16,000 liters or 16 metric tons of water.

**Common conversions:**
- 1 cubic meter = 1,000 liters
- 1 cubic meter of water ≈ 1,000 kg

### Step 5: Use Angles for Surveying and Navigation

Angles allow measurement at distance.

**Measuring angles without protractor:**

**Using your hands:**
- At arm's length, your fist ≈ 10 degrees
- Your thumb ≈ 2 degrees
- Your pinky ≈ 1 degree
- Hold at arm's length and estimate angle to target

**Using a carpenter's angle:**
- Place angle tool at your eye
- Sight to target
- Read angle off the tool

**Using the sun:**
- Sun moves 360 degrees in 24 hours = 15 degrees per hour
- 1 hour of time corresponds to 15 degrees of direction
- Know time of sunrise/sunset to estimate angles

**Cardinal directions without compass:**

**Sunrise:** East
**Sunset:** West
**North Star (at night):** Find Polaris; it is approximately due north
**Shadow method:** Stick in ground casts shadow; shadow points roughly west at sunrise, east at sunset

### Step 6: Understand Scaling and Proportion

Enlarge or reduce designs while maintaining proportions.

**Scale factor:**

If you scale by factor 2, all dimensions double (but area quadruples, volume octuples).

Example: Design a room 4m × 6m. Scale model is 40cm × 60cm. Scale factor is 1/10.

**Maintaining proportions:**

If original is 4m × 6m (ratio 2:3), scaled version must also be 2:3.
- 40cm × 60cm: ratio 2:3 ✓
- 40cm × 70cm: ratio 4:7 ✗ (wrong proportions)

### Step 7: Create Floor Plans and Layouts

Use geometry to plan structures.

**Step 1:** Measure the land and record dimensions

**Step 2:** Create a scale drawing
- Choose scale (e.g., 1cm on paper = 1m on ground)
- Draw rectangles representing buildings
- Mark doors, windows, paths

**Step 3:** Verify right angles
- All corners should be 90 degrees (use 3-4-5 triangle at each corner)
- Walls should be parallel (opposite sides equal length)

**Step 4:** Mark dimensions on drawings
- All distances labeled
- All angles marked
- Workers can use plan to build accurately

### Step 8: Understand Slope and Gradient

Important for drainage, roads, and building foundations.

**Slope = rise / run**

- Slope of 1:10 means 1 unit up for every 10 units forward
- Slope of 1:3 means 1 unit up for every 3 units forward (steeper)

**Angle relationship:**

- Slope 1:10 ≈ 6 degrees
- Slope 1:5 ≈ 11 degrees
- Slope 1:3 ≈ 18 degrees
- Slope 1:1 = 45 degrees

**Measuring slope:**

- Measure vertical height (rise) and horizontal distance (run)
- Slope = rise / run
- For 1-meter horizontal, measure vertical and that is the rise

### Step 9: Apply to Common Building Problems

**Making a level floor:**

- Use a level tool (bubble level, or water in a tube)
- Measure height at multiple points
- High spots are higher than low spots by measured amount
- Fill low spots or remove high spots until level

**Making walls plumb (vertical):**

- Use a plumb bob (weight on string)
- Hang from top of wall
- String should be parallel to wall surface
- If not, wall leans and must be fixed

**Making corners square:**

- Use 3-4-5 triangle method
- Check all four corners
- If not square, walls will not fit together properly

**Spacing posts or columns evenly:**

- Total distance ÷ number of spaces = distance between each post
- Example: 20m distance, 5 posts. 20 ÷ 5 = 4m apart

## Verification

**Good geometry knowledge results in:**

- Buildings with straight walls and square corners
- Accurate land surveys
- Correct calculations of area and volume
- Buildings that stand straight and stable

**If geometry is inadequate:**

- Crooked walls (wastes materials, looks bad)
- Boundaries incorrectly placed (causes disputes)
- Structures unstable (could collapse)
- Miscalculations (run out of materials or waste them)

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1 - Assuming by eye.**

"That looks level" or "that looks square." Verify with tools. Eyes deceive.

**Mistake 2 - Rounding too much.**

You measure 4.3m but round to 4m. Over 10 measurements, error accumulates and becomes significant.

**Mistake 3 - Forgetting scale.**

You draw a 1:10 scale plan, but builder uses it as 1:1. Everything is 10 times too big.

**Mistake 4 - Not checking right angles.**

You assume the baseline is square to the boundary. It is not. All subsequent measurements are off.

**Mistake 5 - Confusing diameter and radius.**

You need a 2-meter diameter circle. You measure 2 meters as the radius. Circle is too big.

**Mistake 6 - Ignoring slope.**

You design a flat floor in a sloped area. Water pools. You did not account for slope.

**Mistake 7 - Using inaccurate tools.**

Your measuring rope stretches when wet. Your carpenter's square is damaged. Results are wrong.
