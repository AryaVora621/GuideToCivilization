## Overview

Probability and statistics are the mathematics of uncertainty. Probability predicts future events based on theory; statistics analyzes past events based on data. Together they enable rational decision-making when faced with incomplete information.

## Prerequisites

- Arithmetic and basic algebra
- Understanding of fractions and percentages

## Part 1: Probability Fundamentals

### What is probability?

Probability is the likelihood of an event occurring. It ranges from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain).

**Probability = (favorable outcomes) / (total possible outcomes)**

### Example: Rolling a die

A six-sided die has 6 possible outcomes. The probability of rolling a 4 is 1/6 ≈ 0.167 or 16.7%.

### Rule: Addition

If events are mutually exclusive (cannot both occur), the probability of one or the other is the sum of their individual probabilities.

**Example:** Probability of rolling either a 4 or a 5 on a die = 1/6 + 1/6 = 2/6 = 1/3.

### Rule: Multiplication

If events are independent (one does not affect the other), the probability of both occurring is the product of their probabilities.

**Example:** Probability of rolling two sixes in a row = 1/6 × 1/6 = 1/36 ≈ 0.028 or 2.8%.

## Part 2: Basic Statistics

### Describing data

**Mean (average):** Sum all values, divide by the count.

**Example:** Test scores: 70, 80, 90. Mean = (70+80+90)/3 = 80.

**Median (middle value):** Sort values, find the middle.

**Example:** Scores 70, 80, 90. Median = 80.

**Mode (most frequent):** The value that appears most often.

**Example:** Heights 5ft, 6ft, 5ft, 5ft. Mode = 5ft.

**Range:** Highest minus lowest value.

**Example:** Scores 70, 80, 90. Range = 90-70 = 20.

**Standard deviation:** Measures how spread out data is.

A small standard deviation means values cluster near the mean. A large standard deviation means values are scattered.

### Representing data

**Frequency table:** Lists values and how often they occur.

| Value | Frequency |
|-------|-----------|
| 70    | 2         |
| 80    | 5         |
| 90    | 3         |

**Histogram:** A chart showing frequency distribution.

**Example:** A bar chart with test scores on x-axis, number of students on y-axis.

## Part 3: Distributions

### Normal distribution (bell curve)

Many natural phenomena follow a normal distribution: heights, test scores, crop yields.

**Properties:**
- Symmetrical around the mean
- Most values cluster near the mean
- Rare values appear at the extremes
- About 68% of values fall within 1 standard deviation of the mean
- About 95% fall within 2 standard deviations

### Using the normal distribution

If crop yields follow a normal distribution with mean 100 bushels and standard deviation 10, then:
- About 68% of crops yield between 90 and 110 bushels
- About 95% yield between 80 and 120 bushels
- Only about 2.5% yield below 80 bushels

This predicts which yields are likely and which are unusually poor or good.

## Part 4: Hypothesis Testing

### The idea

Compare an observed result to what we expect by chance. If the difference is larger than random chance would predict, conclude something real is happening.

### Steps

1. **State the hypothesis:** "This seed variety produces higher yields."
2. **Collect data:** Grow both varieties, record yields.
3. **Calculate statistics:** Mean yield of each variety, standard deviation, sample size.
4. **Assess significance:** Are the results different enough to conclude the seed really is better, or could they differ by random chance?

### Example

**Hypothesis:** Fertilizer A increases crop yield.

**Experiment:** Grow 50 plots with fertilizer A, 50 without.
- With A: mean 110 bushels, std dev 10
- Without A: mean 100 bushels, std dev 10

The difference (10 bushels) is larger than the standard deviation, suggesting the fertilizer likely works.

### Null hypothesis

The "null hypothesis" assumes no difference: "Fertilizer A has no effect." If the data strongly contradicts the null hypothesis, we accept the alternative: "Fertilizer A does increase yield."

## Part 5: Confidence and Error

### Confidence interval

A range of values that likely contains the true value. For example: "The average yield is 105 bushels with a 95% confidence interval of 100–110."

This means: if we repeated the experiment many times, 95% of the confidence intervals would contain the true average.

### Types of error

**Type I error:** Rejecting a true null hypothesis (false positive). Concluding a treatment works when it doesn't.

**Type II error:** Accepting a false null hypothesis (false negative). Concluding a treatment doesn't work when it does.

A significance level (often 0.05 or 5%) sets the acceptable risk of Type I error. This means a 5% chance of concluding something works when it doesn't.

## Part 6: Practical Applications

### Medical trials

A new medicine is tested on 200 patients. 160 recover (80%). Without medicine, 60% typically recover. Is the difference significant?

With 80% recovery vs 60% expected, and a large sample size, this is likely significant (not random chance). The medicine probably works.

### Agricultural decisions

Comparing seed varieties: Does variety A yield more than variety B? Compare means, standard deviations, sample sizes. If variety A mean is significantly higher, plant variety A.

### Quality control

Manufacturing: if 2 out of 1000 items are defective, is this normal variation or a sign of a problem?

Understanding probability and statistics is demonstrated by:

1. **Calculating basic probabilities:** Correctly finding probability of events.
2. **Computing statistics:** Accurately calculating mean, median, standard deviation.
3. **Interpreting data:** Understanding what statistics reveal about real-world situations.
4. **Making evidence-based decisions:** Using data to choose between options.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Confusing correlation with causation:** Just because two variables are related does not mean one causes the other.
2. **Cherry-picking data:** Selecting only data that supports your conclusion.
3. **Ignoring sample size:** Small samples are less reliable than large samples.
4. **Assuming independence:** Events that seem independent may be related.
