## Overview

Trigonometry is the mathematics of triangles and angles. It solves the problem: Given some angles and one distance, find the unknown distances.

Examples:
- How tall is that tower? Measure the angle; calculate height.
- How far away is the enemy camp? Measure two angles; calculate distance.
- What angle should the siege ladder be? Calculate from height.

This chapter covers practical trigonometry: the three main functions (sine, cosine, tangent) and how to use them for surveying and navigation.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of geometry and right triangles
- Comfort with angles (degrees)
- Ability to work with ratios and decimals

## Materials & Tools

- A simple angle-measuring tool or protractor
- Measuring tape for baseline distances
- Paper and pencil for calculations
- Table of sine/cosine/tangent values (or approximate from memory)

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Understand the Right Triangle Setup

Trigonometry works with right triangles (one 90-degree angle).

**Parts of a right triangle:**

```
        |
        | opposite
        |
--------|----
      adjacent
```

**Relative to an angle:**
- Opposite: Side across from the angle
- Adjacent: Side next to the angle
- Hypotenuse: Longest side, opposite the 90-degree angle

**Example:**

Looking at a tower from ground level at a 30-degree angle upward:
- Angle of elevation: 30 degrees
- Opposite side: Height of tower (unknown)
- Adjacent side: Horizontal distance to tower (measurable)
- Hypotenuse: Line of sight (calculable)

### Step 2: Memorize or Reference Sine, Cosine, Tangent

These ratios relate angles to side lengths.

**Sine (sin):** opposite / hypotenuse

**Cosine (cos):** adjacent / hypotenuse

**Tangent (tan):** opposite / adjacent

**Mnemonic:** SOH-CAH-TOA (Sine = Opposite/Hypotenuse, Cosine = Adjacent/Hypotenuse, Tangent = Opposite/Adjacent)

**Common values (approximate):**

- sin 0° = 0; cos 0° = 1; tan 0° = 0
- sin 30° = 0.5; cos 30° = 0.87; tan 30° = 0.58
- sin 45° = 0.71; cos 45° = 0.71; tan 45° = 1
- sin 60° = 0.87; cos 60° = 0.5; tan 60° = 1.73
- sin 90° = 1; cos 90° = 0; tan 90° = undefined

### Step 3: Calculate Height of a Tall Object

Common problem: How tall is the tower?

**Procedure:**

1. Measure horizontal distance from tower base (let's say 50 meters)
2. Measure angle of elevation (looking up angle): 30 degrees
3. Use tangent: tan 30° = height / distance
4. Rearrange: height = distance × tan 30°
5. height = 50 × 0.58 = 29 meters

**Formula:** height = distance × tan(angle)

**Real example:**

Looking at a 100-meter cliff from 100 meters away:
- Angle of elevation: tan⁻¹(100/100) = tan⁻¹(1) = 45 degrees

### Step 4: Calculate Distance to a Distant Object

Problem: How far away is the enemy camp?

**Using baseline triangulation:**

1. Create a baseline (measure distance between two observation points, e.g., 100 meters)
2. From first point, measure angle to target: 40 degrees
3. From second point, measure angle to target: 60 degrees
4. Use trigonometry to calculate distance to target

**Approximate method (if angles are simple):**

If both angles are 45 degrees and baseline is 100 meters, then distance is approximately 100 meters (forms isosceles right triangle).

**More precise method:**

Use law of sines: a/sin(A) = b/sin(B) = c/sin(C)

Where a, b, c are sides and A, B, C are opposite angles.

This requires more calculation but is more accurate.

### Step 5: Calculate Range for Archery or Siege Weapons

Problem: At what angle should siege catapult fire to hit target 200m away?

**Assuming projectile travel in parabolic arc:**

At 45-degree angle, projectile travels farthest. For lower or higher angles:
- 30 degrees: Shorter range, higher arc
- 45 degrees: Maximum range
- 60 degrees: Shorter range, very high arc

**Practical approach:**

- Measure distance to target (200 meters)
- Estimate angle based on experience (40-50 degrees usually good)
- Adjust based on feedback (hits short/long)

**Using trigonometry for elevation:**

If target is on a hill 50 meters higher and 200 meters away:
- Angle to horizontal target: tan⁻¹(0/200) = 0 degrees
- Angle to elevated target: tan⁻¹(50/200) = tan⁻¹(0.25) ≈ 14 degrees
- Adjust aim upward by 14 degrees

### Step 6: Navigate Using Angles

Problem: Reach a target location without a compass.

**Using angle and distance:**

1. Measure angle to target (using sun, stars, or angle-measuring tool)
2. Measure or estimate distance to target
3. Travel in that direction, checking angle periodically to correct course

**Using triangulation to locate yourself:**

1. From unknown location, measure angles to two known landmarks
2. On map, draw lines at those angles from landmarks
3. Where lines intersect is your location

### Step 7: Measure Underwater Depth or Distance

Problem: How deep is the well/river?

**Using angle:**

1. Look at object underwater at an angle (e.g., 45 degrees from horizontal)
2. Measure distance to object horizontally (e.g., 5 meters)
3. At 45 degrees: depth ≈ distance = 5 meters

**More generally:**

Depth = distance × tan(angle)

This is approximate because water refraction bends light, but close enough for practice.

### Step 8: Understand Inverse Functions

Given a ratio, find the angle.

**Inverse functions:**

- sin⁻¹: Inverse sine (arcsin)
- cos⁻¹: Inverse cosine (arccos)
- tan⁻¹: Inverse tangent (arctan)

**Example:**

If opposite/hypotenuse = 0.5, then angle = sin⁻¹(0.5) = 30 degrees

**Using without calculator:**

Estimate from known values. If ratio is 0.5, you know sin 30° = 0.5, so angle is 30 degrees.

If ratio is between known values, estimate:
- sin⁻¹(0.6) is between sin⁻¹(0.5)=30° and sin⁻¹(0.71)=45°, so approximately 37°

### Step 9: Apply to Surveying a Field

Problem: Measure a field boundary using angles.

**Procedure:**

1. Set up baseline (measure 100m along known direction)
2. At each endpoint, measure angle to distant corner of field
3. Calculate position of corner using trigonometry
4. Repeat for all corners
5. Calculate area using geometry (sum of triangles)

**Result:** Accurate field boundary without direct measurement.

## Verification

**Successful trigonometry use results in:**

- Accurate height measurements
- Correct distance estimates
- Effective aiming (weapons and siege equipment)
- Proper surveying with minimal ground work

**If trigonometry is failing:**

- Heights are significantly off (tens of percent error)
- Distance estimates are wildly wrong
- Aiming requires multiple adjustments
- Surveying requires repeated measurements

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1 - Confusing angle types.**

Angle of elevation (up from horizontal) vs. angle of depression (down from horizontal). They are supplementary; use correct one.

**Mistake 2 - Forgetting to convert angles.**

Using degrees when tables give radians, or vice versa. Always check units.

**Mistake 3 - Wrong function.**

Need opposite/adjacent (tangent), but use opposite/hypotenuse (sine). Always pick correct trig function.

**Mistake 4 - Ignoring refraction.**

Light bends in water. Underwater distance appears closer than it is. Adjust estimates accordingly.

**Mistake 5 - Assuming flat Earth.**

Over very long distances (kilometers), Earth curvature matters. For surveying a field or fortress, negligible.

**Mistake 6 - Rounding angles.**

You measure 31.3 degrees but round to 30 degrees. Over long distances, error compounds. Keep precision.

**Mistake 7 - Forgetting baseline distance.**

You measure angles but forget to record baseline distance. Cannot calculate actual distance without it.
