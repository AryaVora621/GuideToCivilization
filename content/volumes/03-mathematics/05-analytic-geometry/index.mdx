## Overview

Analytic geometry combines algebra and geometry using coordinate systems. Instead of measuring angles and distances directly, you assign coordinates to points and calculate distances mathematically.

After collapse, this becomes a tool for mapping settlements, surveying land, and navigating. A coordinate system allows precise location recording without needing compass or theodolite.

## Prerequisites

- Understanding of geometry and trigonometry
- Comfort with algebra (variables, equations)
- Ability to create and read maps with grid systems

## Materials & Tools

- Grid paper or parchment
- Ruler and measuring tools
- Stakes for establishing coordinate origin
- Measuring rope or tape marked with units

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Establish a Coordinate System

A coordinate system assigns numbers to locations.

**Cartesian coordinates (x, y):**

Two perpendicular axes (horizontal = x, vertical = y). Every point has coordinates (x, y).

**Setting up in the field:**

1. Choose origin point (0, 0) — a recognizable landmark
2. Establish x-axis (east-west direction) — measure distances along it
3. Establish y-axis (north-south direction) — perpendicular to x-axis
4. Mark intervals (every 10 meters, or scaled unit)

**Example:**

Origin at settlement center. X-axis points east (positive) and west (negative). Y-axis points north (positive) and south (negative).

Well is at coordinates (50, 30) = 50 meters east and 30 meters north of center.

### Step 2: Record Positions Using Coordinates

Instead of "tower is northeast of settlement," you record exact coordinates.

**Recording a building:**

1. Measure distance from origin along x-axis: 80 meters east
2. Measure distance along y-axis: 60 meters north
3. Record: Building is at (80, 60)

**For irregularly shaped buildings:**

Record all corners:
- Corner A: (80, 60)
- Corner B: (85, 60)
- Corner C: (85, 65)
- Corner D: (80, 65)

Four corners define the building exactly.

### Step 3: Calculate Distance Between Two Points

Given two coordinates, find distance.

**Formula:** Distance = √[(x₂-x₁)² + (y₂-y₁)²]

**Example:**

Point A: (0, 0) — settlement center
Point B: (30, 40) — watchtower

Distance = √[(30-0)² + (40-0)²] = √[900 + 1600] = √2500 = 50 meters

Note: This is the 3-4-5 triangle scaled up (30:40:50 = 3:4:5).

### Step 4: Calculate Slope and Angle

Given two points, find the slope (steepness).

**Slope = (y₂-y₁) / (x₂-x₁)**

**Example:**

Point A: (0, 0)
Point B: (100, 20)

Slope = 20/100 = 0.2 (rise 20 for every 100 forward)

This is a gentle slope (~11 degrees).

### Step 5: Map a Territory

Create accurate map using coordinates.

**Procedure:**

1. Establish coordinate system with origin at a fixed point
2. Go to each significant location (building, water source, boundary marker)
3. Measure coordinates for that location
4. Record on paper grid

**Result:** Accurate map scalable to any size

**Map scale:**

If you measure in meters but draw on grid where 1 square = 10 meters:
- Point at (100, 50) in meters draws at (10, 5) on map

### Step 6: Determine Line Equations

A straight line can be expressed as an equation.

**Slope-intercept form: y = mx + b**

Where m = slope, b = y-intercept (where line crosses y-axis)

**Example:**

Line passes through (0, 10) and (10, 30)

Slope m = (30-10)/(10-0) = 20/10 = 2

Y-intercept b = 10 (crosses y-axis at y=10)

Equation: y = 2x + 10

**Using the equation:**

To find y-value for any x:
- x = 5: y = 2(5) + 10 = 20

To find x-value for any y:
- y = 50: 50 = 2x + 10, so x = 20

### Step 7: Find Intersection Points

Where do two lines or paths cross?

**Example:**

Road 1: y = 2x + 10
Road 2: y = -x + 40

Set equal: 2x + 10 = -x + 40

Solve: 3x = 30, so x = 10

Substitute back: y = 2(10) + 10 = 30

Intersection point: (10, 30)

**Real application:**

Border between two territories. Road A enters at (0, 20). Road B enters at (40, 0). Where do they meet? Find intersection.

### Step 8: Calculate Area Using Coordinates

Given vertices of a shape, find area.

**For a polygon with vertices (x₁,y₁), (x₂,y₂), ... (xₙ,yₙ):**

Shoelace formula: Area = |Σ(xᵢ×yᵢ₊₁ - xᵢ₊₁×yᵢ)| / 2

**Example: Triangle with vertices (0,0), (4,0), (2,3)**

Area = |[(0×0 - 4×0) + (4×3 - 2×0) + (2×0 - 0×3)]| / 2
     = |[0 + 12 + 0]| / 2
     = 12 / 2
     = 6 square units

### Step 9: Navigate Using Coordinates

Find your way to a destination using coordinates.

**Procedure:**

1. Know your current location coordinates (or determine via triangulation)
2. Know destination coordinates
3. Calculate direction (angle) toward destination
4. Calculate distance
5. Travel in that direction for that distance

**Angle calculation:**

From current position (x₁, y₁) to destination (x₂, y₂):

Angle = arctan[(y₂-y₁)/(x₂-x₁)]

(Remember: 0° is east, 90° is north, 180° is west, 270° is south)

**Example:**

Current: (0, 0)
Destination: (30, 40)

Angle = arctan(40/30) = arctan(1.33) ≈ 53°

Travel northeast at 53° from east = 37° from north.

Distance = 50 meters (calculated earlier).

### Step 10: Create Territorial Boundaries

Use coordinate lines to define borders.

**Example:**

Settlement territory is bounded by:
- West: x = -100 (west-east line at x=-100)
- East: x = 100
- South: y = -80
- North: y = 120

Territory is a rectangle from (-100,-80) to (100,120).

Area = 200 × 200 = 40,000 square units (16 square kilometers if units are meters)

## Verification

Successful analytic geometry results in:
- Accurate maps of territory
- Precise location records
- Correct distance calculations
- Efficient navigation using coordinates

If analytic geometry is inadequate:
- Map locations do not match reality
- Distances calculated are significantly wrong
- Boundary disputes due to unclear coordinates

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1 - Wrong axis direction.**

You define x-axis as north-south instead of east-west. All coordinates are rotated.

**Mistake 2 - Inconsistent scale.**

Some measurements in meters, some in paces. Coordinates are meaningless.

**Mistake 3 - Wrong formula.**

Using distance = (x₂-x₁) + (y₂-y₁) instead of √[(x₂-x₁)² + (y₂-y₁)²]. Distance is wrong.

**Mistake 4 - Forgetting negative coordinates.**

You only use positive coordinates. Points west or south of origin cannot be recorded.

**Mistake 5 - Misreading slope.**

Slope 2 means steep (rise 2 for every 1 forward). Slope 0.2 means gentle (rise 0.2 for every 1 forward). Easy to confuse.

**Mistake 6 - Not checking intersection.**

You calculate intersection of borders. It falls in a river. Borders are impractical; redesign.
