## Edible & Poisonous Plant Identification Reference

This appendix provides identification tables and safety guides for distinguishing edible plants from toxic species. Use these tables alongside visual inspection and community knowledge when foraging.

## Common Edible vs. Poisonous Comparison Table

| Plant Family | Edible Species | Poisonous Look-Alike | Key Difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| Apiaceae (Carrot family) | Wild carrot, parsnip, fennel | Poison hemlock, water hemlock | Poison varieties: purple blotches on stems, musty smell when crushed; edible: no blotches, carrot-like smell |
| Solanaceae (Nightshade) | Tomato, pepper, eggplant | Deadly nightshade, black nightshade berries | Edible: all fruit ripens red/yellow; toxic: black berries, dull leaves |
| Brassicaceae (Cabbage) | Kale, cabbage, mustard greens | False garlic mustard, charlock | Edible: mild aroma, smooth leaves; toxic: peppery smell, hairy stems |
| Rosaceae (Rose family) | Berries (strawberry, raspberry), rose hips, apple seeds (moderation) | Unknown berries on plants with thorns | Apple seeds contain cyanogenic compounds—avoid in quantity; edible berries firm, sweet smell |
| Lamiaceae (Mint family) | Mint, basil, oregano, thyme | None commonly confused | Mint family: square stems, aromatic, opposite leaves. Safest family for beginners. |
| Amaranthaceae | Amaranth greens, lamb's quarters | None commonly poisonous | Cooked amaranth safe; oxalate content reduced by boiling. Use young leaves. |

## Root and Bulb Identification Key

### Edible Roots (by appearance)

**Cluster bulbs / Grouped segments:**
- Wild garlic (ramps): 2–3 broad leaves, white base, garlic smell (crush to verify)
- Wild onion: narrow leaves, round bulb, onion smell when cut
- Camas: purple bulbs in clusters, lily-like leaves, flowers blue/purple

**Single taproot / Round bulb:**
- Wild carrot: thin orange/pale root, feathery leaves, carrot smell when crushed
- Parsnip: pale root, celery-like leaves, sweet smell
- Burdock: long dark root, large dock-like leaves, mild burdock taste when cooked

### Poisonous Roots (Warning Signs)

| Root Type | Appearance | Smell / Taste Test | Toxicity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Poison hemlock root | Pale, parsnip-like | Musty, unpleasant; DO NOT taste | All parts highly toxic; skin contact causes burns |
| Water hemlock root | White, chambered | Parsnip-like smell BUT musty undertone | Most toxic plant in north temperate zone; lethal dose: small amount |
| Foxglove root | Small, pale, fibrous | Sweet smell initially, then bitter | Digitalis toxin; affects heart; no antidote |
| Deadly nightshade root | Tan, fibrous, deep | Sweet smell when fresh | Tropane alkaloids; hallucination and death |

## Leaf Identification Tables

### Green Leafy Plants (Cooked or Raw)

| Plant | Leaf Shape | Stem Color | Preparation | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dandelion | Deeply lobed, jagged edges | Pale/white latex in stem | Raw (young) or cooked | Slightly bitter; rich in iron and nutrients |
| Chickweed | Oval, opposite pairs, small leaves | Hairless, pale green | Raw (salads) or cooked | Mild flavor; tender when young |
| Plantain | Oval with pronounced ribs | Reddish base | Cooked (tough when raw) | Mucilaginous; used in broths and soups |
| Wood sorrel | Clover-like, three leaflets | Red/pink tinges | Raw or cooked | Sour taste from oxalic acid; moderation recommended |
| Lamb's quarters | Diamond-shaped, waxy coating | Pale green, grooved | Cooked (spinach-like) | Mild flavor; cook to reduce oxalates |
| Nettles | Oval with serrated edges | Covered in stinging hairs | Cooked (gloves required) | Hairs disappear when cooked; very nutritious |
| Watercress | Small rounded leaflets | Hollow, floating | Raw or cooked | Peppery flavor; ensure water source is clean |

## Berry and Fruit Identification Chart

### Safe Berries by Color and Habit

**Red berries:**
- Strawberry (wild): small, soft, seeds on outside, early summer
- Raspberry/blackberry (cultivated or wild): aggregate fruit, bramble thorns, summer–fall
- Rowan berries: clustered, tart, fall (cook before eating)
- Wild rose hips: orange-red, seed-filled, fall (remove interior hairs before use)

**Black/dark berries:**
- Blackberry: aggregate, thorny canes, summer–fall
- Blueberry/huckleberry: round, blue-gray coating, mid-summer
- Elderberry: clustered, flat-topped clusters, late summer (cook before eating)
- Chokeberry: small, dark, astringent, fall

**Yellow/orange berries:**
- Sea buckthorn: bright orange, tart, fall
- Mulberry: elongated, sweet, early summer (birds prefer, harvest early)
- Apricot/peach (cultivated): stone fruit, summer

### Poisonous Berries (Warning Chart)

| Berry | Appearance | Plant Habit | Why Dangerous |
|---|---|---|---|
| Deadly nightshade | Black berries, shiny, grape-like | Herbaceous, 2–3 feet tall | Tropane alkaloids; no safe dose; causes hallucinations, death |
| Baneberry | White or red berries, small | Woodland herb, 1–2 feet | Cardiac glycosides; berries look like small pearls |
| Pokeweed berries | Dark purple-black, grape clusters | Tall weed, 3–6 feet | All parts toxic; saponins cause burning in throat |
| Yew berries | Red arils, small seed inside | Evergreen shrub/tree | Taxine in seed; cardiac poison; red part is harmless but seed fatal |
| Hemlock berries | Small, ribbed, brown | Tall plant, 3–10 feet, purple blotches | Alkaloids; even touching can cause burns; never handle |
| Datura/Nightshade berries | Spiky pod or berry, various colors | Large shrub | Tropane alkaloids in fruit, seeds, all parts; extreme toxicity |

## Mushroom Quick-Reject Rules (Avoid Entirely)

For foraging beginners, **reject any mushroom with these features:**

1. **Ring or skirt** on the stem near the cap
2. **Cup (volva)** at the base (partially buried; dig to check)
3. **White gills** AND a white stem (many deadly species share this)
4. **Umbrella-like cap** (many deadly Amanita species)
5. **Any mushroom you cannot identify with 100% certainty** — safer to skip

**Never** eat mushrooms based on a single field guide image. Consult 2–3 sources and an experienced forager.

## Toxic Plant Avoidance Rules

### Plants NEVER to Touch or Eat (Certain Fatality Risk)

| Plant | Why Avoid | Symptom Onset |
|---|---|---|
| Poison hemlock (all parts) | Furanocoumarins cause photosensitive burns; oral toxins in roots | Minutes (burns); hours (oral) |
| Water hemlock (all parts) | Cicutoxin; extremely potent neurotoxin | Minutes to under 1 hour |
| Deadly nightshade (all parts) | Tropane alkaloids; no antidote; unpredictable absorption | 30 minutes to 2 hours |
| Ricinus (castor bean) | Ricin protein; lethal in tiny amounts | 1–3 days (after ingestion) |
| Oleander (all parts) | Cardiac glycosides; cumulative toxin | Hours to days |
| Foxglove (all parts) | Digitalis; affects heart rhythm | Hours (cumulative effect) |
| Datura (all parts) | Tropane alkaloids; unpredictable potency | 30 minutes to hours |
| Stinging tree (Gympie-gympie) | Formic acid and silica spines; causes extreme pain | Seconds to minutes |

### Symptoms of Plant Poisoning (Emergency Response)

| Symptom | Likely Toxin Class | First Aid | Do NOT |
|---|---|---|---|
| Burning mouth/throat, excessive salivation | Oxalates, calcium crystals | Rinse mouth; drink milk/calcium | Don't swallow more; don't induce vomiting |
| Dilated pupils, hallucinations, rapid heart | Tropane alkaloids | Seek emergency care immediately | Do not attempt home treatment |
| Irregular heartbeat, chest pain | Cardiac glycosides | Emergency services; monitor pulse | Do not exert; lay down |
| Tremors, seizures, difficulty breathing | Neurotoxins (hemlock, water hemlock) | Emergency care immediately | No home remedy exists |
| Severe diarrhea, vomiting, abdominal pain | Saponins, irritant glycosides | Clear fluids, rest; seek care if severe | Avoid medications that mask symptoms |
| Skin burns/blisters (contact) | Furanocoumarins (hemlock, giant hogweed) | Wash immediately with soap; avoid sun | Do not scratch; cover blisters |

## Foraging Safety Checklist

**Before eating any wild plant:**

1. ✓ Identify with certainty using multiple sources and cross-reference with visual specimens
2. ✓ Ensure no toxic look-alikes in your region (vary by climate zone)
3. ✓ Harvest from clean areas (away from roads, pesticides, polluted water)
4. ✓ Wash thoroughly; cook when in doubt (cooking reduces most toxins)
5. ✓ Eat small amounts first; observe for allergic reaction over 24 hours
6. ✓ Keep a sample in case medical attention becomes necessary
7. ✓ Never rely on "taste tests" or folk beliefs as substitutes for identification
8. ✓ Consult an experienced local forager when uncertain

## Oxalate Content in Common Wild Edibles

Some safe plants become problematic in large quantities due to oxalates (which bind calcium and can cause kidney stones):

| Plant | Oxalate Level | Safe Preparation | Daily Limit (Adult) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Wood sorrel | High | Raw in salads, limited quantities | Under 1 cup raw |
| Lamb's quarters | Medium | Cooked (boiling reduces oxalates by 80%) | 2–3 cups cooked |
| Spinach (wild) | High | Cooked with calcium (milk, cheese) | 1 cup cooked |
| Rhubarb leaves | Extremely high | NEVER eat leaves; toxins beyond oxalates | None — poison |
| Buckwheat greens | Medium | Cooked or limited raw | 1 cup cooked |
