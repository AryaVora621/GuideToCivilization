## Paper, Ink & Bookbinding

Quick-reference recipes, ratio tables, and step keys for making paper, ink, and bound books from scavenged and grown materials. All ratios are by weight unless marked "by volume". Temperatures in degrees Celsius. Where a range is given, start at the middle value and adjust.

---

## 1. Fiber Sources for Paper

Ranked by ease and quality. "Yield" is rough dry paper per dry fiber input.

| Fiber source | Quality | Prep difficulty | Cook needed | Yield | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cotton/linen rag | Excellent | Low | No (just beat) | High | Best fiber; pre-made cellulose. The classic. |
| Flax (retted stalk) | Excellent | High | Yes | Medium | Strong, durable, archival. |
| Hemp | Excellent | High | Yes | Medium | Very strong; long fibers. |
| Inner tree bark (mulberry, kozo) | Very good | Medium | Yes | Medium | Long fiber, flexible paper. |
| Straw (wheat, rice) | Fair | Low | Yes | Low | Abundant; weak, brittle paper. |
| Grass / reeds | Fair | Low | Yes | Low | Bulk filler fiber. |
| Recycled paper waste | Good | Very low | No | High | Fastest route; just pulp it. |
| Wood (softwood) | Poor without chemistry | High | Yes (strong) | Medium | Needs lignin removal; acidic, yellows. |

**Rule:** rag and recycled paper need no cooking — just soak and beat. All plant stalks/bark must be cooked to dissolve lignin and pectin before beating.

## 2. Pulping (Cooking) Recipe — Plant Fibers

Alkaline cook breaks down lignin so fibers separate.

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Alkali | Wood-ash lye or slaked lime |
| Lye strength | Floats an egg to a coin-size patch at surface |
| Fiber : water | 1 : 20 by volume |
| Alkali : dry fiber | about 1 : 5 (lime) / 1 : 4 (ash lye) |
| Cook temperature | 90–100 (gentle boil) |
| Cook time, soft straw | 2–4 hours |
| Cook time, bark/flax/hemp | 4–8 hours |
| Done test | Fiber pulls apart between fingers into threads |

**After cook:** rinse thoroughly in clean water until rinse runs clear and no longer feels slippery (removes alkali). Unrinsed alkali rots paper over time.

### Beating (all fibers)

| Method | Time to pulp | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Hand mortar/mallet | 1–3 hours | Coarse, strong |
| Stamping mill (water-powered) | 2–6 hours | Even, fine |
| Hollander beater (geared) | 1–4 hours | Best, controllable |

Beat until a pinch dropped in water disperses into a cloud with no visible bundles. Over-beaten pulp drains slowly and gives hard, translucent paper; under-beaten gives weak, fluffy paper.

## 3. Sheet Forming & Sizing

| Step | Spec |
|---|---|
| Vat consistency | 1 part pulp : about 100 parts water (thin "tea") |
| Mould mesh | Fine cloth or wire screen; deckle frame on top |
| Pull | Dip vertical, scoop level, lift flat, shake to lock fibers |
| Couching | Flip wet sheet onto wool felt; stack with felts between |
| Pressing | Heavy press / lever; squeeze out free water |
| Drying | Hang or brush onto smooth board; air dry |

### Sizing (stops ink feathering)

| Size type | Recipe | Application |
|---|---|---|
| Gelatin (animal) | Soak hide/bone glue, warm to thin liquid | Dip dried sheet, re-dry |
| Starch | 1 part starch : 20 water, boil to thin paste | Brush or dip |
| Rosin + alum | Rosin soap + alum, added to vat | Internal (in pulp) |

Unsized paper = blotter (absorbs, feathers). Always size paper meant for ink writing.

## 4. Ink Recipes

### 4a. Iron Gall Ink (black, permanent, writing) — the standard

| Ingredient | Part (by weight) | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Tannin (oak galls, or bark/tea) | 3 | Reacts with iron |
| Iron(II) sulfate (green vitriol / copperas) | 2 | Darkening agent |
| Gum arabic | 2 | Binder, suspension |
| Water | 30 | Solvent |

**Method:** steep crushed galls in warm water 1–2 days; strain; dissolve gum in the liquid; add iron sulfate last. Goes on pale grey-blue, darkens to black over hours as it oxidizes. Slightly acidic — over centuries can eat paper; keep iron ratio modest.

### 4b. Carbon / Lampblack Ink (black, stable, non-corrosive)

| Ingredient | Part | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Soot / lampblack | 1 | Pigment |
| Gum arabic or hide glue | 1 | Binder |
| Water | 8–10 | Solvent |

**Method:** grind soot into the binder solution to a smooth, lump-free paste; thin to flow. Add a drop of ox gall or honey to wet and smooth. Permanent, will not fade, non-acidic. The basis of "India ink" and stick ink.

### 4c. Quick Colored Inks / Pigment Notes

| Color | Source | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Brown | Walnut husk boiled down | Easy, light-fades some |
| Sepia | Cuttlefish ink sac | Warm brown |
| Red | Madder root / cochineal insect / ochre | Mordant with alum |
| Blue | Indigo / woad (vat dye) | Hard; needs reduction vat |
| Yellow | Weld, turmeric, ochre | Turmeric fades fast |
| White | Chalk, lead white (toxic) | Cover/opaque |

All colored inks need a binder (gum arabic) and many need a mordant (alum) to fix.

### Binder ratios quick key

| Use | Binder : pigment |
|---|---|
| Thin writing ink | 1 : 1 binder-heavy |
| Drawing ink | 1 : 2 |
| Paint (covering) | 1 : 3 to 1 : 4 |

## 5. Pens & Writing Tools

| Tool | Make | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| Reed pen | Cut hollow reed, split nib | Bold, large script |
| Quill | Flight feather, hardened, cut nib | Fine writing |
| Brush | Animal hair in quill/ferrule | Painting, East-Asian script |
| Metal nib | Forged/rolled spring steel, slit | Durable, fine, late-tech |

**Nib cut key:** trim tip square, split center slit upward, scrape thin. Re-cut when ink flow stutters.

## 6. Bookbinding Methods

### Method comparison

| Method | Durability | Difficulty | Lies flat? | Best use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pamphlet (single signature) | Low | Very low | Yes | Booklets, under 40 pages |
| Saddle stitch | Low | Low | Yes | Thin booklets |
| Coptic (chain stitch) | High | Medium | Yes (fully) | Notebooks, journals |
| Sewn-on-cords (case bound) | Very high | High | Partly | Permanent library books |
| Long-stitch | Medium | Low | Yes | Field journals |

### Core vocabulary

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Leaf | One sheet = 2 pages |
| Folio | Sheet folded once |
| Signature / gathering | Several folded sheets nested |
| Spine | Bound edge |
| Text block | All sewn signatures together |
| Endpapers | Sheets joining block to cover |
| Headband | Reinforcing band at spine ends |

### Page imposition (folds per sheet)

| Fold | Name | Leaves/sheet | Pages/sheet |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 fold | Folio | 2 | 4 |
| 2 folds | Quarto | 4 | 8 |
| 3 folds | Octavo | 8 | 16 |

A common signature is 4–6 folded sheets. More than 8 sheets per signature splays and sews poorly.

## 7. Case-Bound Book — Step Quick-Reference

1. **Fold & gather** sheets into signatures (4–6 sheets each).
2. **Press** signatures flat under weight overnight.
3. **Mark sewing stations** on spine folds: punch 4–6 evenly spaced holes; keep end holes about 15 mm from head and tail.
4. **Sew** each signature to the next through the stations (Coptic or onto raised cords).
5. **Glue the spine** of the sewn block with flexible paste (wheat paste or hide glue); let dry under light press.
6. **Round the spine** gently with a hammer for a convex curve (optional, aids durability).
7. **Add endpapers** front and back; attach headbands at spine ends.
8. **Line the spine** with a strip of cloth (mull) extending onto front/back for hinge strength.
9. **Make boards:** cut two cover boards plus a spine strip; leave a hinge gap of about 6 mm each side of the spine piece.
10. **Cover** boards with cloth or leather; turn edges in and paste down.
11. **Case-in:** paste the text block's endpapers and cloth flaps to the inside of the boards.
12. **Press** the finished book under heavy weight 24 hours.

## 8. Adhesives Quick Table

| Adhesive | Recipe | Flexible? | Reversible? | Use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Wheat starch paste | 1 part flour : 5 water, cook to custard | Yes | Yes (water) | Spine lining, paste-downs |
| Hide / bone glue | Soak, warm to liquid; apply hot | Medium | Yes (heat/water) | Spine, boards |
| Gum arabic | Dissolve in water | Brittle | Yes | Light tipping, ink binder |
| Rice paste | As wheat, finer | Yes | Yes | Fine repair |

**Avoid** thick brittle glues on spines — books must flex. Paste thin layers, multiple coats.

## 9. Troubleshooting Key

| Problem | Likely cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Paper weak, falls apart | Under-beaten or under-cooked fiber | Beat/cook longer |
| Paper hard, glassy, slow to drain | Over-beaten pulp | Add fresh lightly-beaten pulp |
| Ink feathers / spreads | Paper unsized | Apply gelatin or starch size |
| Ink flakes off | Too little binder | Increase gum/glue ratio |
| Iron gall ink eats through page | Iron excess / too acidic | Reduce iron, buffer with chalk |
| Pages won't lie flat | Over-tight sewing or stiff spine glue | Loosen tension, flexible paste |
| Spine cracks when opened | Brittle adhesive | Re-back with cloth + flexible paste |
| Sheets yellow over time | Residual lignin or alkali | Better rinse; remove wood lignin |

## 10. Master Ratio Cheat Sheet

| Mixture | Ratio |
|---|---|
| Lye cook, fiber : water | 1 : 20 vol |
| Lime : dry fiber | 1 : 5 |
| Vat, pulp : water | 1 : 100 vol |
| Starch size | 1 : 20 |
| Iron gall (tannin:iron:gum:water) | 3 : 2 : 2 : 30 |
| Carbon ink (soot:binder:water) | 1 : 1 : 9 |
| Wheat paste (flour:water) | 1 : 5 |
| Writing ink binder:pigment | about 1 : 1 |
| Paint binder:pigment | about 1 : 3 |
