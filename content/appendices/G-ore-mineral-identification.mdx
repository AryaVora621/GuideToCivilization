## Ore & Mineral Identification

A field reference for identifying minerals and the ores that yield useful metals. Identification rests on a small set of physical tests anyone can perform with simple tools: hardness, streak, luster, color, cleavage, and density. No single property identifies a mineral; you confirm by combining several. Use this appendix as a lookup key — run the tests, then match the results against the tables below.

## The Identification Toolkit

You need only a few cheap tools to identify most minerals in the field:

| Tool | Used for | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| Steel knife / nail | Hardness (about 5.5) | Scratches anything softer |
| Copper coin | Hardness (about 3.5) | Intermediate reference |
| Glass plate | Hardness (about 5.5) | Mineral that scratches glass is 5.5 or harder |
| Fingernail | Hardness (about 2.5) | Softest field reference |
| Unglazed porcelain tile ("streak plate") | Streak (powder color) | The single most reliable test |
| Hand lens (10x) | Crystal form, luster, cleavage | Reveals structure |
| Small magnet | Magnetism | Identifies magnetite and iron-rich ore |
| Dilute acid (vinegar works weakly) | Carbonate test | Fizzes on calcite, limestone |

## Mohs Hardness Scale

Hardness is a mineral's resistance to scratching, ranked 1 (softest) to 10 (hardest). A mineral scratches anything softer than itself and is scratched by anything harder. This is the most diagnostic single test.

| Hardness | Reference mineral | Field test |
|----------|-------------------|------------|
| 1 | Talc | Crushed by fingernail; greasy feel |
| 2 | Gypsum | Scratched by fingernail (2.5) |
| 3 | Calcite | Scratched by copper coin (3.5) |
| 4 | Fluorite | Easily scratched by a knife |
| 5 | Apatite | Barely scratched by a knife |
| 5.5 | (knife / glass) | Mineral that scratches glass is 5.5+ |
| 6 | Orthoclase feldspar | Scratches glass; a file barely marks it |
| 7 | Quartz | Scratches glass and steel easily |
| 8 | Topaz | Scratches quartz |
| 9 | Corundum (ruby, sapphire) | Scratches topaz |
| 10 | Diamond | Hardest natural material |

**How to test:** try to scratch the unknown with each reference in turn (fingernail 2.5, copper 3.5, knife/glass 5.5, quartz 7). The hardness lies between the hardest thing that does NOT scratch it and the softest thing that does.

## Streak: Powder Color

Drag the mineral across an unglazed porcelain tile. The color of the powder left behind — the **streak** — is far more reliable than the surface color, because surface color varies with impurities and weathering while streak is consistent. Streak only works for minerals softer than the tile (about 7); harder minerals just scratch it.

| Streak color | Common minerals |
|--------------|-----------------|
| Black | Magnetite, hematite (some), graphite |
| Red-brown | Hematite (the key iron ore test) |
| Yellow-brown | Limonite, goethite (rust ores) |
| Greenish-black | Chalcopyrite, pyrite (faint) |
| White / colorless | Quartz, calcite, feldspar, most silicates |
| Gray-black | Galena (lead ore) |
| Pale green / blue | Malachite, azurite (copper ores) |

A telltale example: **hematite always gives a red-brown streak** even when the lump looks gray or black — the single most useful ore-identification fact for finding iron.

## Luster

Luster describes how a mineral reflects light. First decide **metallic vs. non-metallic**, then refine.

| Luster | Description | Examples |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| Metallic | Shines like polished metal | Galena, pyrite, magnetite, chalcopyrite |
| Adamantine | Brilliant, gem-like | Diamond, cerussite |
| Vitreous | Glassy | Quartz, feldspar, fluorite |
| Resinous | Like resin or amber | Sphalerite, sulfur |
| Pearly | Iridescent sheen | Talc, mica, gypsum |
| Greasy | Looks oily | Some quartz, nepheline |
| Earthy / dull | No shine, chalky | Limonite, kaolinite, weathered ores |

## Cleavage and Fracture

**Cleavage** is the tendency to break along flat planes; **fracture** is breakage along irregular surfaces. Count the cleavage directions and their angles.

| Pattern | Description | Examples |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| 1 direction | Splits into sheets | Mica, graphite |
| 2 directions at 90° | Breaks into right-angle blocks | Feldspar, galena (cubic) |
| 3 directions at 90° (cubic) | Perfect cubes | Galena, halite (salt) |
| 3 directions, not 90° (rhombohedral) | Leaning blocks | Calcite |
| Conchoidal fracture | Smooth curved shell-like break | Quartz, obsidian, flint |
| Hackly fracture | Jagged, like torn metal | Native copper, native metals |

## Density (Specific Gravity)

Heft is diagnostic. Metallic ores feel noticeably heavier than common rock for their size. Specific gravity (SG) is weight relative to water; rock-forming minerals are about 2.6–3.0, metal ores much higher.

| Mineral | Approx. SG | Heft impression |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| Quartz, feldspar | 2.6 - 2.7 | Ordinary "rock" weight |
| Calcite | 2.7 | Ordinary |
| Pyrite | 5.0 | Distinctly heavy |
| Magnetite | 5.2 | Heavy + magnetic |
| Hematite | 5.3 | Heavy |
| Galena | 7.5 | Very heavy (lead) |
| Native copper | 8.9 | Very heavy |
| Gold | 19.3 | Extremely heavy — the key gold test |

## Major Ore Minerals (Metal Sources)

The ores that matter most to a developing civilization, with their diagnostic properties.

| Ore mineral | Metal | Hardness | Streak | Luster / color | Key tests |
|-------------|-------|----------|--------|----------------|-----------|
| Hematite | Iron | 5 - 6 | Red-brown | Gray-black, earthy or metallic | Red-brown streak |
| Magnetite | Iron | 6 | Black | Black, metallic | Strongly magnetic |
| Limonite/goethite | Iron | 4 - 5.5 | Yellow-brown | Brown, dull/earthy | Yellow-brown streak (rust) |
| Chalcopyrite | Copper | 3.5 - 4 | Greenish-black | Brassy gold, tarnishes iridescent | Softer than pyrite; brittle |
| Malachite | Copper | 3.5 - 4 | Pale green | Bright green, banded | Green; fizzes in acid |
| Azurite | Copper | 3.5 - 4 | Pale blue | Deep blue | Blue; fizzes in acid |
| Native copper | Copper | 2.5 - 3 | (metallic copper) | Copper-red metal | Malleable; hackly |
| Cassiterite | Tin | 6 - 7 | White to pale brown | Brown-black, adamantine | Very heavy (SG ~7) |
| Galena | Lead | 2.5 | Gray-black | Silvery, metallic | Cubic cleavage; very heavy |
| Sphalerite | Zinc | 3.5 - 4 | Pale yellow-brown | Brown-yellow, resinous | Resinous luster |
| Bauxite | Aluminum | 1 - 3 | White-ish | Pale, pisolitic (pea texture) | Earthy, clay-like lumps |
| Cinnabar | Mercury | 2 - 2.5 | Red | Bright red | Red; very heavy |
| Native gold | Gold | 2.5 - 3 | Golden-yellow | Yellow metal | Malleable; SG ~19; will not tarnish |

## The Pyrite vs. Gold Test ("Fool's Gold")

Pyrite is constantly mistaken for gold. Distinguish them:

| Property | Gold | Pyrite ("fool's gold") |
|----------|------|------------------------|
| Hardness | 2.5 - 3 (soft, dents) | 6 - 6.5 (scratches glass) |
| Streak | Golden-yellow | Greenish-black |
| Behavior when struck | Malleable, flattens | Brittle, shatters |
| Crystal form | Irregular grains, nuggets | Sharp cubes, pyritohedra |
| Color in shade | Stays gold | Looks duller, brassier |

If it scratches glass and gives a black streak, it is not gold.

## A Simple Identification Key

Work through these branching questions in order:

1. **Does it have metallic luster?**
   - Yes → go to 2.
   - No → go to 4.
2. **Is it magnetic?** Yes → likely **magnetite** (iron). No → go to 3.
3. **Streak color?** Black/red-brown and hard → **hematite**. Gray-black + cubic + very heavy → **galena** (lead). Brassy + black streak + scratches glass → **pyrite**. Brassy + softer + greenish streak → **chalcopyrite** (copper).
4. **Is it green or blue?** Green that fizzes in acid → **malachite** (copper). Blue that fizzes → **azurite** (copper).
5. **Does it fizz in acid (non-metallic, fairly soft about 3)?** Yes → **calcite / limestone** (carbonate).
6. **Does it scratch glass (hardness 7+) with no cleavage, conchoidal fracture?** → **quartz**.
7. **Earthy, dull, yellow-brown streak, rusty?** → **limonite** (iron).
8. **Soft (under 2.5), greasy or sheet-like?** → **talc, mica, or graphite** (graphite marks paper, is gray-black).

## Where Ores Are Found (Quick Geology)

| Setting | What to look for |
|---------|------------------|
| Quartz veins in rock | Gold, copper sulfides, often with pyrite |
| Iron-stained ("gossan") outcrops | Rusty red/brown caps over buried sulfide ore |
| Riverbed gravels (placer) | Gold, cassiterite, dense grains concentrated by water |
| Limestone contact zones | Lead, zinc, copper replacement ores |
| Weathered surface zones | Malachite, azurite, limonite (oxidized ores) |

## Safety Notes

- **Cinnabar (mercury ore)** is toxic — do not heat, grind dry, or inhale dust.
- **Galena** is lead ore — wash hands after handling, never use near food.
- **Arsenic-bearing ores** (some shiny silvery-white minerals) release poison when heated; smelt only with care and ventilation.
- Always **test streak and hardness on a fresh surface**, not a weathered crust.

Identification is always cumulative: a brassy mineral could be pyrite, chalcopyrite, or gold, but hardness, streak, and heft together resolve it instantly. Carry a streak plate, a knife, a magnet, and a hand lens, test methodically, and most economically important minerals can be named in the field within a minute.
